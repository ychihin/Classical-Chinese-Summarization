Xiang Ji, also known as Xiang Yu, was a man from Xiaxiang who began his military career at age twenty-four. His uncle was Xiang Liang, son of Chu general Xiang Yan, who was killed by Qin general Wang Jian. The Xiang family had served as Chu generals for generations and were granted the title of Xiang. As a child, Xiang Ji failed at learning calligraphy and fencing, dismissing them as insufficient for facing ten thousand enemies. He studied the art of war under Xiang Liang but refused to master it completely. After Xiang Liang killed someone, they fled to Wuzhong, where Xiang Liang organized military and funeral services, secretly assessing people's abilities. When Qin Shi Huang visited Kuaiji, Xiang Ji declared he could replace the emperor, prompting Xiang Liang to cover his mouth and warn against such talk. Xiang Liang then recognized Xiang Ji's exceptional nature. Xiang Ji was over eight feet tall, could lift a cauldron, and was feared by the youth of Wuzhong.

In July of Qin II's first year, Chen She rebelled in Daze Township. In September, Yin Tong, the governor of Kuaiji, proposed raising troops with Xiang Liang and Huan Chu. Xiang Liang, knowing Huan Chu's whereabouts, had Xiang Ji wait with a sword. After entering the governor's chambers, Xiang Liang called Xiang Ji in, who beheaded the governor. Xiang Yu killed over a hundred guards, and the rest submitted. Xiang Liang raised an army of 8,000 elite soldiers from Wuzhong, appointing local heroes as officers. He became governor of Kuaiji, with Xiang Ji as general. Zhao Ping of Guangling urged Xiang Liang to lead troops west to attack Qin. Xiang Liang crossed the river with 8,000 men, joined forces with Chen Ying of Dongyang, and gained 60,000-70,000 troops at Xiapi. He defeated Qin Jia, who had betrayed King Chen, and merged his army. After defeating Qin forces at Dong'a, Xiang Liang grew arrogant, ignoring Song Yi's warnings. Qin reinforced Zhang Han, who defeated and killed Xiang Liang at Dingtao.

After Xiang Liang's death, King Huai of Chu appointed Song Yi as supreme general to rescue Zhao, with Xiang Yu as second-in-command. Song Yi stalled at Anyang for 46 days. Xiang Yu killed him for incompetence and took command, leading the army across the river after sinking ships and burning camps. They defeated the Qin army at Julu, killing Su Jiao and capturing Wang Li. The Chu army's victory made Xiang Yu the commander of all allied forces. Zhang Han, facing pressure from Zhao Gao, surrendered to Xiang Yu after negotiations. Xiang Yu appointed Zhang Han as King Yong but later executed over 200,000 Qin soldiers at Xin'an due to fears of rebellion.

Xiang Yu entered Hangu Pass and camped at Hongmen, where Pei Gong (Liu Bang) had already taken Xianyang. Fan Zeng urged Xiang Yu to kill Pei Gong, but Xiang Bo warned Pei Gong, who apologized at the Hongmen feast. Xiang Zhuang's sword dance failed to assassinate Pei Gong, who escaped with Fan Kuai's help. Xiang Yu later entered Xianyang, killed Ziying, burned the Qin palace, and collected treasures. He divided the empire, making Pei Gong King of Han and himself Hegemon-King of Western Chu. He moved Emperor Yi to Chen County and had him killed. Conflicts arose as Tian Rong rebelled in Qi and Chen Yu attacked Changshan. The King of Han conquered the Three Qins and marched east, leading to a prolonged war with Xiang Yu.

The war saw numerous battles, including the Han defeat at Pengcheng and the siege of Xingyang. Xiang Yu's forces were weakened by defections and food shortages. After a stalemate at Guangwu, Xiang Yu agreed to divide the empire at the Hong Canal, releasing the King of Han's family. However, the King of Han broke the treaty and pursued Xiang Yu. At Gaixia, Xiang Yu's army was surrounded, and hearing Chu songs, he realized defeat was imminent. He fled with 800 cavalry, but only 28 remained at Dongcheng. After killing hundreds of Han soldiers, he committed suicide, refusing to cross the Wu River. His body was divided among five Han officers for rewards. The city of Lu surrendered only after seeing his head, and he was buried with the rites of a duke. The Han king did not kill the Xiang clan, granting some titles. Sima Qian noted Xiang Yu's rise from obscurity to dominance in three years but criticized his arrogance, abandonment of Guanzhong, and failure to learn from the past, attributing his downfall to his own faults rather than divine will.
