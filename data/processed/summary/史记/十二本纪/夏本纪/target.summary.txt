Xia Yu, also known as Wen Ming, was the great-great-grandson of Huangdi and grandson of Emperor Zhuanxu. His father Gun and great-grandfather Changyi served as ministers rather than emperors. During Emperor Yao's reign, devastating floods surrounded mountains and covered hills, causing great distress. Yao sought flood control experts, and despite his reservations about Gun's character, appointed him based on ministers' recommendations. After nine years of failed efforts, Shun, who had been appointed to manage government affairs, executed Gun at Yushan—a decision widely approved—and appointed Gun's son Yu to continue the work.

Yu, described as intelligent, diligent, and morally upright, accepted Shun's appointment alongside Yi and Hou Ji. Grieving his father's execution, Yu worked tirelessly for thirteen years, passing his home multiple times without entering. He lived frugally, invested resources in water conservancy, and used various transportation methods to survey terrain. Yu distributed rice seeds, provided relief to the needy, and facilitated food transfers between regions. His flood control journey began in Jizhou, where he managed rivers and lakes, classified soil types, and established tax and tribute systems based on land fertility.

Yu's efforts spanned nine provinces, each with distinct characteristics. In Jizhou, white soft soil was taxed at upper-first class with middle-fifth class fields. Yanzhou featured black fertile soil with sixth-class fields and ninth-class taxes. Xuzhou had red sticky fertile soil with second-class fields and fifth-class taxes. Yangzhou's moist soil ranked ninth-class fields with seventh-class taxes. Jingzhou's moist soil had lower-middle eighth-class fields and upper-lower third-class taxes. Yuzhou's soft fertile soil featured upper-middle fourth-class fields and upper-middle second-class taxes. Liangzhou's blue-black soil had lower-upper seventh-class fields and lower-middle eighth-class taxes. Yongzhou's yellow soft fertile soil had upper-first class fields and middle-lower sixth-class taxes. Each province contributed specific tributes, including metals, fabrics, stones, and exotic goods, transported via designated waterways.

Yu opened nine mountain roads and channeled nine major rivers, including the Yellow River, Yangtze River, Huai River, and Wei River, directing waters to the sea. This unified the nine provinces, enabling peaceful habitation, road construction, and tributary systems. Emperor Shun recognized Yu's achievements by granting him a black jade symbol. Yu, Boyi, and Gaotao discussed governance before Shun, with Gaotao emphasizing moral cultivation, understanding people, and appeasing the populace through nine virtues. Yu recounted his diligent efforts in flood control, food distribution, and land management. Shun stressed the importance of capable ministers and moral leadership, warning against decadence like Danzhu's. Yu noted his own sacrifices, including marrying Tushan's daughter and leaving his son Qi unraised, to complete water control.

After Shun's death, Yu initially yielded to Shun's son Shangjun but ascended the throne when all princes paid homage to him, establishing the Xia dynasty with the surname Si. Yu appointed Gaotao as successor, but Gaotao died before inheriting. Yu later granted state affairs to Yi. Ten years into his reign, Yu died during an eastern inspection at Kuaiji. Yi yielded to Yu's son Qi, who became Emperor Qi of Xia after gaining universal support. Qi suppressed the Youhu clan's rebellion, issuing the "Gan's Oath" to enforce discipline. Subsequent Xia rulers included Taikang, who lost his country due to neglect; Zhongkang; and others down to Jie, the final emperor. Jie's tyranny led to his defeat by Tang, who replaced the Xia dynasty. Tang enfeoffed Xia descendants, who later received Qi in the Zhou dynasty. Sima Qian noted Yu's surname Si, with descendants adopting various clan names, and Confucius' correction of the Xia calendar. Yu's burial site at Kuaiji Mountain, meaning "accounting," commemorates his assessment of princes' merits.
