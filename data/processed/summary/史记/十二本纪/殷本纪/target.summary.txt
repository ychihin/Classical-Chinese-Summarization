Yin Qi's mother, Jian Di, was the daughter of the You'u family and the second concubine of Emperor Ku. While bathing in a river with three others, she swallowed an egg dropped by a black bird, became pregnant, and gave birth to Qi. Qi grew up to assist Yu in flood control. Emperor Shun appointed him as Situ to promote the education of the five ethics, sealed him in Shang Land, and gave him the surname Zi. Qi's achievements during the eras of Tang Yao, Yu Shun, and Xia Yu brought stability to the people. After his death, his descendants succeeded him in a long lineage, culminating in Chengtang (Tang), who moved the capital eight times.

Chengtang, a Fangbo under the Xia Dynasty, first conquered Ge Bo for failing to offer sacrifices. He wrote "Tang Zheng" to record this. Yi Yin (Aheng) served Chengtang, either as a married servant or after being hired following five invitations. Yi Yin advised Chengtang on governance, left for the Xia capital, and returned to Bo, writing "Nv Jiu" and "Nv Fang." Chengtang's kindness was shown when he ordered a net set on all sides to be reduced to three, allowing animals to escape, which impressed the princes. Xia Jie's tyranny led Chengtang to raise troops, with Yi Yin following. Chengtang issued the "Tang Oath," defeated Xia Jie at Mingtiao, and conquered Sanke. He replaced the Xia social god but wrote "Xia She" to explain why it was not replaced. After the princes submitted, Chengtang ascended the throne, wrote "Tang Gao" to warn the princes, and revised the calendar to favor white.

After Chengtang's death, his son Taiding died before ascending, so Waibing and then Zhongren ruled briefly. Yi Yin then supported Taijia, Chengtang's grandson. Taijia's tyranny led Yi Yin to exile him to Tong Palace for three years, during which Yi Yin governed. Taijia repented, returned to power, and practiced virtuous government, earning the title Taizong. After Taizong's death, Woding succeeded, and Yi Yin died. Subsequent rulers like Tai Geng, Yong Ji, and Tai Wu saw decline, but Tai Wu's appointment of Yi Zhi and response to a strange mulberry tree event revived Yin, earning him the title Zhongzong. Later rulers like Zhong Ding, Wai Ren, He Dan Jia, and Zu Yi saw further decline and revival, with Wu Xian playing a key role.

From Zhong Ding onward, succession disputes among brothers and sons caused nine generations of chaos, weakening Yin. Pan Geng moved the capital to Bo, Chengtang's former residence, and revived Yin by following Chengtang's decrees. After Pan Geng's death, Xiao Xin and Xiao Yi ruled weakly. Wu Ding, seeking to revive Yin, dreamed of a sage named Shuo, found him as a slave in Fu Xian, and appointed him prime minister, bringing prosperity. A pheasant crowing on a tripod during a sacrifice led Zu Ji to advise Wu Ding on morality, further strengthening Yin. Wu Ding was later called Gaozong.

After Wu Ding's death, Zu Geng and Zu Jia ruled, with Zu Jia's promiscuity causing decline. Subsequent rulers like Lin Xin, Geng Ding, and Wu Yi saw further decline. Wu Yi's cruelty, including making a puppet and shooting a blood-filled pouch, led to his death by lightning. His son Tai Ding and then Yi ruled, with Yi's son Xin (Zhou) succeeding. Zhou was talented but tyrannical, indulging in debauchery with Daji, increasing taxes, and inventing the Pao Ge torture. He killed Jiu Hou and E Hou, imprisoned Xibo (later King Wen), who was released after bribes. Xibo practiced virtuous governance, attracting princes. Zhou's minister Bigan was killed for advising, and Jizi was imprisoned. King Wu of Zhou led a revolt, defeated Zhou at Muye, and Zhou burned himself to death. King Wu executed Daji, released Jizi, and enfeoffed Zhou's son Wu Geng. Later, Wu Geng rebelled, and King Cheng made Wei Zi the king of Song to continue Yin's lineage.
