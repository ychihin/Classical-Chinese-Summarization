# Summary of the Historical Text on Emperor Gaozu of Han

Emperor Gaozu, born Liu Ji in Peixian County, was the son of Taigong and Liu Yu. His mother became pregnant after dreaming of meeting a god while resting by a lake, during which a dragon appeared on her body. Gaozu possessed distinctive physical features including a high nose, dragon-like forehead, beautiful beard, and seventy-two black moles on his left leg. He was generous, charitable, and broad-minded, with lofty ambitions that set him apart from ordinary people.

As an adult, Gaozu served as the chief of Sishui Pavilion, where he became familiar with local officials and enjoyed drinking and women. He frequently borrowed wine from local establishments, where shopkeepers observed a dragon above him when he was drunk. During a corvée trip to Xianyang, he saw Qin Shihuang and expressed his desire to achieve similar greatness. Lu Gong, impressed by Gaozu's appearance at a gathering, married his daughter to him despite his wife's objections. This daughter became Empress Lu, mother of Emperor Xiaohui and Princess Lu Yuan.

While serving as pavilion chief, Gaozu encountered an old man who predicted that Empress Lu and their children would become noble, and that Gaozu's own appearance was beyond description. When escorting servants to Mount Li, many fled, so Gaozu released the remaining servants and became a fugitive. Drunk one night, he killed a large snake blocking his path. An old woman later claimed the snake was the son of the White Emperor, killed by the son of the Red Emperor, which made Gaozu secretly proud and increased his followers' awe of him.

During the Qin II period, Chen Sheng's rebellion inspired the magistrate of Peixian County to consider revolt. Following advice from Xiao He and Cao Shen, Fan Kuai was sent to summon Liu Ji. After the magistrate reneged, the elders killed him and welcomed Liu Ji, who became Duke of Pei after initially declining. They raised two to three thousand soldiers, attacked nearby areas, and returned to defend Fengyi. When Yong Chi betrayed Pei Gong by surrendering to Wei, Pei Gong failed to recapture Fengyi and sought help from Xiang Liang, who provided additional troops.

Pei Gong joined forces with Xiang Yu, and together they achieved military successes while suffering setbacks. After Xiang Liang's death, King Huai of Chu appointed Pei Gong as governor of Dang County and ordered him to march west into Guanzhong, promising kingship to whoever entered first. Despite Xiang Yu's desire to join, King Huai's advisors favored Pei Gong's reputation as a tolerant elder. Pei Gong collected scattered troops, defeated Qin forces, and through strategic decisions including sparing the governor of Nanyang County, gained widespread surrenders.

Pei Gong's army arrived at Bashang in October of the first Han year, where the King of Qin surrendered. After initially wanting to rest in the palace, Pei Gong heeded advice to seal the treasures and return to camp. He established the three-chapter law code, abolished harsh Qin laws, and won the people's support by refusing their offerings. When Xiang Yu's forces approached, Pei Gong's subordinate Cao Wushang informed Xiang Yu of his intentions, but Xiang Bo's intervention and Pei Gong's apology at Hongmen prevented conflict.

Xiang Yu later broke the agreement, making Pei Gong King of Han in Ba, Shu, and Hanzhong. The King of Han used Han Xin's strategy to march eastward, attacking and defeating King Yong Zhanghan. Over subsequent years, the Han and Chu forces engaged in prolonged warfare with shifting fortunes. The King of Han survived assassination attempts, built alliances, and eventually defeated Xiang Yu at Gaixia, where Xiang Yu's forces heard Chu songs and were routed.

After establishing the Han Dynasty, Gaozu faced rebellions from various kings including Zang Tu, Han Xin, Chen Xi, and Yingbu. He personally led campaigns against them, executing or defeating the rebels. During a visit to his hometown Peixian County, Gaozu composed the famous Song of the Great Wind and exempted the county from corvée service. He died from an arrow wound sustained during the campaign against Yingbu, refusing medical treatment by declaring his fate determined by heaven. Before death, he designated successors for key positions, including Cao Shen, Wang Ling, Chen Ping, and Zhou Bo. Gaozu was buried in Changling with the posthumous title Emperor Gao, having established the Han Dynasty from humble beginnings.
