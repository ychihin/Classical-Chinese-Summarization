# Summary of the Five Emperors' Records

Huangdi (Yellow Emperor), a descendant of the Shaodian tribe with the surname Gongsun and name Xuanyuan, displayed remarkable intelligence from birth. During his rise to power, the Shennong clan had declined, and princes were fighting violently. Xuanyuan trained his army, conquered rebellious princes, and eventually all submitted except the violent Chiyou. After Emperor Yan attacked the princes, they turned to Xuanyuan, who cultivated virtue, organized his forces, and defeated Emperor Yan at Banquan. He later captured and killed Chiyou at Zhuolu, after which the princes recognized him as emperor, replacing Shennong.

The Yellow Emperor traveled extensively—east to Mount Tai, west to Kongtong, south to the Yangtze River, and north驱逐 the Hunzhou tribe. He had no fixed residence, establishing military camps wherever he went. He appointed officials named after clouds, created supervisory positions, and received a divine tripod for calendar-making. He observed celestial movements, calculated seasons, and appointed ministers to govern. Following the laws of heaven and earth, he studied yin and yang, explained life and death, cultivated grains, domesticated animals, and collected resources for public use. Because of the earth attribute's auspicious sign, he was called the Yellow Emperor. He had twenty-five sons, fourteen of whom established surnames. After his death, his grandson Gaoyang ascended as Emperor Zhuanxu.

Emperor Zhuanxu Gaoyang was calm, resourceful, and knowledgeable. He cultivated crops, calculated seasons, followed ghosts and gods to establish rituals, and educated the people. His domain extended north to Youling, south to Jiaozhi, west to Liusha, and east to Panmu, pacifying all creatures. After his death, his nephew Gao Xin became Emperor Ku. Gao Xin was born with great aura, benevolent and dignified, understanding both divine will and people's concerns. He governed impartially, and all under heaven submitted. After his death, his son Zhi ruled without achievement, so his brother Fangxun became Emperor Yao.

Emperor Yao was kind as heaven and wise as God, rich but not proud. He united his clan, inspected officials, and ordered the Xi and He families to create a calendar based on celestial observations. He appointed officials to oversee spring, summer, autumn, and winter farming, establishing a 366-day year with leap months. When Yao sought a successor, his son Danzhu was deemed unworthy, and Gonggong was rejected for insincerity. Despite warnings, Gun was appointed to control floods but failed after nine years. The Four Mountains recommended Yu Shun, a filial man living among commoners. Yao tested Shun by marrying his daughters to him, observing his virtue, and assigning him various duties, which Shun performed excellently.

Shun accepted Yao's abdication and began governing. He established rituals, standardized measurements, and conducted inspections every five years. He divided the world into twelve states, dredged rivers, and implemented a penal system with reduced punishments. He exiled the four evil ministers—Gonggong, Huandou, Sanmiao, and Gun—satisfying the world. After Yao's death, Shun initially yielded to Danzhu, but the people universally supported Shun, who then ascended the throne.

Shun's lineage traced back to Emperor Zhuanxu, though his immediate ancestors were commoners. Despite his father Gusou, stepmother, and brother Xiang repeatedly attempting to kill him, Shun remained filial and respectful. His virtue attracted people, and wherever he lived, communities grew. Yao tested him thoroughly before entrusting him with governance. Shun appointed worthy descendants of ancient families—the Eight Kai and Eight Yuan—to manage agriculture and education, while exiling four evil families. He appointed Yu to control floods, Gaotao as judge, and other capable ministers. Yu's contributions were greatest: opening mountains, dredging rivers, and delineating nine provinces. The territory expanded five thousand miles, and all regions submitted. Shun died while touring south and was buried at Jiuyi Mountain. His son Shangjun being unworthy, Shun recommended Yu to heaven. After Shun's death, Yu ascended the throne, continuing the pattern of abdication.

The historian notes that while records of the Five Emperors are ancient and sometimes unclear, careful study of various texts and oral traditions confirms their authenticity. This account selects the most elegant and correct materials as the first chapter of the historical record.
