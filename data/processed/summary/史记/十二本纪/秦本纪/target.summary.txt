# Summary of the Historical Text on the Ancestors and Early Rulers of Qin

The ancestor of Qin originated from Nuxiu, a granddaughter of Emperor Zhuanxu's descendant. While weaving, Nuxiu swallowed a swallow's egg and gave birth to Daye. Daye married Nuhua, who gave birth to Dafei. Dafei assisted Yu in flood control, and Emperor Shun rewarded him, granting him the surname Ying and a wife. Dafei's descendants included Feilian and Elai, who served King Zhou of Yin. When King Wu of Zhou defeated Zhou, Elai was killed, while Feilian died later and was buried on Huotai Mountain.

Feilian's son Jisheng began a lineage that produced Zaofu, who was favored by King Mu of Zhou for his driving skills. Zaofu drove King Mu westward and later rushed back to suppress a rebellion, earning the city of Zhao as his fief, establishing the Zhao family. Feizi, a descendant, lived in Quanqiu and excelled at raising horses. King Xiao of Zhou appointed him to manage horses, and later granted him the land of Qin as a vassal state, known as Qin Ying. Qin Ying's descendants included Qin Hou, Qin Zhong, and Zhuang Gong. Qin Zhong was killed by Xirong, but his son Zhuang Gong, with Zhou's help, defeated Xirong. Zhuang Gong's son Xiang Gong succeeded him.

Duke Xiang rescued the Zhou dynasty from Quan Rong attacks and escorted King Ping of Zhou eastward to Luoyi. King Ping granted Duke Xiang the title of prince and the land west of Qishan, making Qin a vassal state. Duke Xiang died in battle against Xirong. His son Wen Gong expanded territory, built cities, established rituals, and set up historians. Wen Gong's descendants included Ning Gong, Wu Gong, De Gong, Xuan Gong, Cheng Gong, and Mu Gong.

Duke Mu of Qin was a notable ruler. He married a daughter of Jin, captured the virtuous minister Baili Xi, and appointed him along with Jian Shu. During a famine, Qin aided Jin despite previous betrayals. When Jin attacked Qin, Duke Mu was nearly captured but was rescued by 300 men who had previously eaten his horse and been forgiven. Duke Mu captured Jin's ruler but released him after negotiations. He later helped Chong'er become Duke Wen of Jin. Duke Mu attacked Zheng but withdrew after learning of Zheng's preparedness. The Qin army was defeated by Jin at Miao Mountain. Duke Mu later adopted Youyu's strategies, attacked the Rong king, annexed twelve countries, and dominated Xirong. He died and was buried with 177 people, including three virtuous ministers.

After Duke Mu, his descendants included Kang Gong, Gong Gong, Huan Gong, Jing Gong, Ai Gong, Hui Gong, Dao Gong, Li Gong, Cao Gong, Huai Gong, Ling Gong, Jian Gong, Hui Gong, Chu Zi, and Xian Gong. During this period, Qin frequently changed rulers, and Jin grew stronger, taking Qin's land west of the Yellow River. Duke Xian abolished live burials and built cities. His son Duke Xiao ascended the throne at age 21.

Duke Xiao issued an order to recruit talents to strengthen Qin. Wei Yang (Shang Yang) came from Wei and implemented reforms, establishing laws, promoting agriculture, and enforcing strict rewards and punishments. Despite opposition, the new laws were adopted. Qin built Xianyang, established counties, and expanded territory. Duke Xiao died, and his son Huiwen Jun executed Wei Yang.

Huiwen Jun's reign saw further expansion. Zhang Yi served as prime minister. Qin attacked Shu, captured Hanzhong, and defeated Chu. King Wu succeeded Huiwen Jun but died after a weightlifting accident. His half-brother Zhaoxiang Wang became king, with Queen Mother Xuan as regent. Zhaoxiang Wang's reign was marked by extensive military campaigns. Bai Qi defeated Han and Wei at Yique, killing 240,000. Qin attacked Chu, occupied Ying, and established Nanjun. Bai Qi defeated Zhao at Changping, killing 400,000 surrendered soldiers. Qin attacked the Western Zhou, whose king surrendered. The nine tripods were moved to Qin, and the Zhou dynasty was destroyed.

Zhaoxiang Wang died, and his son Xiaowen Wang reigned only three days before dying. Zhuang Xiang Wang succeeded him, attacking Eastern Zhou and merging its lands. He died, and his son Ying Zheng became king, later known as Qin Shi Huang. He united the world, established thirty-six counties, and became the First Emperor. After his death, his son Hu Hai became the Second Emperor. In the third year of the Second Emperor's reign, princes rebelled. Zhao Gao killed the Second Emperor and made Ziying emperor. Ziying was killed by princes, and Qin was destroyed.

The Grand Historian noted that Qin's ancestors were surnamed Ying, with descendants scattered across various states, taking their fiefs as surnames. Qin was granted Zhao City due to Zaofu, so they were also called the Zhao family.
