The First Emperor of Qin, son of King Zhuangxiang of Qin, was born Zheng in Handan in the first month of the forty-eighth year of King Zhao of Qin. At thirteen, he succeeded as King of Qin after his father's death. The Qin state had already expanded significantly, annexing territories like Ba, Shu, Hanzhong, and establishing commanderies. Lu Buwei served as prime minister, with Li Si as a retainer and Meng Ao, Wang Jue, and others as generals. Due to the king's youth, state affairs were managed by ministers.

In the early years of his reign, Qin faced rebellions and military campaigns. Meng Ao suppressed a revolt in Jinyang and later attacked Wei, capturing twenty cities. The king faced challenges including a locust plague, famine, and a coalition attack from five states. In the eighth year, Lao Ai was granted the title of Marquis Changxin and given extensive authority. The following year, his conspiracy to rebel was discovered, leading to a battle in Xianyang. Lao Ai was captured, and his followers were executed or exiled. Lu Buwei was removed as prime minister due to his involvement.

The king implemented policies to strengthen Qin, including expelling guest officials, though Li Si's counsel led to the revocation of this order. Wei Liao advised bribing enemy officials, a strategy the king adopted. Military campaigns continued, with Wang Jian and others attacking Zhao and capturing cities. In the seventeenth year, Qin conquered Han, establishing Yingchuan Commandery. The conquest of Zhao followed, with the king personally executing those who had wronged his mother's family in Handan.

The assassination attempt by Jing Ke of Yan failed, leading to Qin's attack on Yan. Wang Jian and others captured Yan's capital and obtained Prince Dan's head. Wei was conquered by diverting rivers to flood Daliang. Jing (Chu) was attacked, with Wang Jian capturing King Jing. Xiang Yan's rebellion was suppressed, and by the twenty-fifth year, Yan, Dai, and Qi were all conquered, unifying China.

The king declared himself the First Emperor, abolishing posthumous titles and establishing a system of succession for ten thousand generations. He adopted water virtue, with black as the color, six as the standard, and harsh laws. The empire was divided into thirty-six commanderies, with unified laws, weights, measures, and writing. Wealthy families were moved to Xianyang, and palaces were built imitating those of conquered states.

The First Emperor toured extensively, erecting stone tablets praising his achievements. He sought immortality, sending Xu Shi with boys and girls to find the elixir. He burned books and buried Confucian scholars alive, enforcing strict laws. The construction of the Epang Palace and the Lishan tomb involved hundreds of thousands of workers. The emperor's death was concealed, and a forged edict made Hu Hai the Second Emperor, forcing Fusu to commit suicide.

The Second Emperor's reign was marked by cruelty and rebellion. Chen She's uprising spread, and Zhang Han suppressed it with convicts. Zhao Gao gained power, killing officials and princes. The Second Emperor was eventually forced to commit suicide by Zhao Gao's agents. Ziying, made king, killed Zhao Gao but soon surrendered to Liu Bang. Xiang Yu killed Ziying and the Qin clan, burning Xianyang. The Qin dynasty ended after 610 years from Duke Xiang to the Second Emperor.
