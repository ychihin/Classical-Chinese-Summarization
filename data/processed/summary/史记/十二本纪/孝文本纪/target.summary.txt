Emperor Xiaowen, the eldest son of Emperor Gaozu, was made King of Dai after Chen Xi's defeat in the spring of Gaozu's eleventh year, with his capital at Zhongdu. In the eighth year of Emperor Gao's reign, Empress Gao died, and the Lu family plotted rebellion. The ministers annihilated them and summoned the king to become emperor. The king consulted his ministers; Zhang Wu advised caution, suspecting the ministers' intentions, while Song Chang argued that the Liu family's rule was divinely ordained, the people were loyal, and the king, as the eldest surviving son of Emperor Gaozu, was the rightful choice. After divination showed a favorable omen, the king sent his uncle Bo Zhao to verify the ministers' sincerity. Confirmed, he traveled to Chang'an, where he was greeted by officials. He initially refused the throne three times but eventually accepted, becoming Emperor Xiaowen.

Upon ascending the throne, the emperor granted amnesty, rewarded officials, and restored lands to Qi and Chu. He abolished the law of punishing innocent family members, stating it harmed the people and contradicted good governance. He initially hesitated to appoint a crown prince, suggesting his uncles or brothers instead, but officials insisted on his son, citing tradition. He agreed, and his eldest son became prince. He also established a queen and rewarded the poor and elderly. He honored meritorious officials from Dai, including Song Chang, and granted lands to followers of Emperor Gaozu.

The emperor faced natural disasters, interpreting solar eclipses as divine warnings. He urged officials to correct his faults, reduced expenses, and promoted agriculture. He abolished laws against defamation to encourage free speech. He dealt with rebellions, such as that of the King of Jibei, and conflicts with the Xiongnu, often opting for defense over aggression. He showed leniency to the King of Huainan, exiling him instead of executing him, and later honored his sons. He abolished corporal punishment after Tiying's plea for her father, emphasizing education over harsh penalties.

The emperor's reign was marked by frugality; he avoided building a terrace costing 100 pounds of gold, wore simple clothes, and forbade extravagance. He maintained peace with Nanyue and the Xiongnu through diplomacy, and tolerated critical ministers. His policies fostered moral education and stability. He died in Weiyang Palace in the seventh year of his reign, leaving a will urging simple mourning, minimal burial, and the release of harem women. He was honored as Emperor Xiaowen, and his temple was designated Taizong, with the "Zhaode Dance" created to commemorate his virtues. Historians praised his benevolence, humility, and effective governance, noting that his reign brought prosperity and moral clarity to the Han dynasty.
