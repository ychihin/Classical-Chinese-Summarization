# Summary of the Wei Family Historical Records

The Wei family traces its ancestry to Bi Gonggao, a descendant of the Zhou royal family. After King Wu of Zhou defeated the Shang, Gao was granted the territory of Bi and adopted Bi as his surname. His descendants eventually became commoners, with some living in the Central Plains and others among the Yi and Di peoples. One descendant, Bi Wan, served Duke Xian of Jin and was granted the territory of Wei after military victories. A diviner predicted that Bi Wan's descendants would prosper greatly, and indeed the Wei family grew increasingly powerful within the state of Jin.

Wei Wuzi followed Chong'er into exile and later served him as Duke Wen of Jin. The family continued through generations: Wei Daozi, Wei Jiang, Wei Ying, and Wei Xianzi. During Wei Jiang's service to Duke Mourning of Jin, he killed a servant of the duke's brother who had caused disorder, initially angering the duke but eventually earning his trust. Duke Mourning credited Wei Jiang with eight years of successful alliances and peace with the Rong and Di tribes. As the Jin state declined, the six ministers grew stronger. Wei Xianzi served as a minister of Jin alongside Zhao Jianzi, Zhongxing Wenzi, and Fan Xianzi. Later generations including Wei Chi, Wei Huanzi, and eventually Wei Wenhou continued the family's rise.

Wei Wenhou's reign marked the formal establishment of Wei as a state. He studied under Zixia, treated Duan Qianmu as a guest, and gained praise from the princes for his respect toward worthy men. When selecting a prime minister, he consulted Li Ke, who advised examining five aspects of candidates: whom they associate with, whom they recommend when powerful, what they avoid when frustrated, and what they reject when poor. Li Ke determined that Wei Chengzi was the better choice over Zhai Huang, noting that Wei Chengzi spent nine-tenths of his income on employing worthy men like Bu Zixia, Tian Zifang, and Duan Qianmu.

After Wei Wenhou's death, his son Wei Wuhou succeeded him. The state faced various military campaigns against Qi, Zhao, Qin, and Chu. Following Wuhou's death, a succession dispute arose between Ziyi and Gongzhong Xu. The states of Han and Zhao disagreed on how to handle the situation, which ultimately saved Wei from partition. King Hui of Wei suffered repeated military defeats and sought to recruit worthy men, attracting Zou Yan, Chunyu Kun, and Meng Ke. Meng Ke advised the king that a ruler should focus on benevolence and righteousness rather than profit.

The later reigns of King Xiang, King Ai, King Zhao, King Anli, King Jingmin, and King Wei saw Wei's gradual decline. The Qin state repeatedly attacked Wei, capturing cities and territories. Despite efforts by figures like Lord Xinling Wuji, who successfully led a coalition of five states against Qin, Wei continued to lose ground. In the third year of King Wei's reign, the Qin army diverted water from the chasm to flood Daliang, capturing King Wei and destroying the Wei state, which was then established as prefectures and counties.

The Grand Historian concluded that while some blamed King Wei's failure to employ Lord Xinling for Wei's destruction, he believed the Qin state was destined by Heaven to unify the realm, and even wise ministers could not have prevented Wei's fall.
