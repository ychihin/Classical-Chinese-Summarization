The text chronicles the lineage and history of the State of Chen, founded by Duke Hu, a descendant of Emperor Shun. After Shun’s death, his descendants held intermittent titles until King Wu of Zhou enfeoffed Guiman (Duke Hu) in Chen to continue Shun’s sacrifices. The succession of Chen rulers is detailed, including Dukes Shen, Xiang, Xiao, You, Li, Wu, Yi, Ping, Wen, Huan, Li, Zhuang, Xuan, Mu, Gong, Ling, Cheng, Ai, Hui, Huai, and Min. Key events include Duke Li’s son Wan fleeing to Qi after fearing persecution, where his descendants later rose to power. During Duke Xuan’s reign, internal strife led to the killing of Crown Prince Yu Kou. Duke Ling’s adultery with Xia Ji and subsequent murder by her son Xia Zhengshu prompted King Zhuang of Chu to invade Chen, initially annexing it but later restoring it after advice from Minister Shen. Chen later broke its alliance with Chu, leading to repeated Chu invasions. Duke Ai’s death involved a succession crisis where his brother Zhao killed the crown prince, leading to Chu’s destruction of Chen under King Ling. King Ping of Chu later restored Chen by enfeoffing Wu (Duke Hui). The state was finally destroyed by King Hui of Chu during Duke Min’s reign. The text also briefly covers the Qi state (descendants of Yu), noting its fall 34 years after Chen. It concludes with a reflection on the enduring legacy of Shun’s descendants, contrasting their prosperity with the decline of Yu’s line, and mentions other ancient states like Teng, Xue, and Zou as minor and unrecorded.
