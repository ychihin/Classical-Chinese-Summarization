# Summary of Historical Records on Imperial Consorts and Their Influence

The text discusses the crucial role of marriage and imperial consorts in the rise and fall of dynasties. It begins by noting that founding emperors and orthodox monarchs often relied on their relatives for support. The Xia Dynasty rose through the Tushan family's daughter and fell with Moxi; the Yin Dynasty rose through the Nu family and fell due to Daji; the Zhou Dynasty rose through Jiang Yuan and Tai Ren, while King You's downfall involved Bao Si. The text emphasizes that husband-wife relationships are the most important ethical bond, and marriage is the most cautious ritual application.

The narrative then details several Han Dynasty empresses and their families. Empress Lu, wife of Emperor Gaozu, lost favor in her later years as Lady Qi gained prominence. After Gaozu's death, Empress Lu executed the Qi family and killed Prince Ruyi. She tried to secure succession through her daughter's child but failed, eventually elevating her Lu relatives. After her death, the Lu clan plotted rebellion but were eliminated by ministers, who installed Emperor Xiaowen.

Empress Dowager Bo's story follows. Her mother had an affair with a Wei clan member during Qin times, producing Bo Ji. A fortune-teller predicted she would bear an emperor. Wei Bao, hearing this, betrayed the King of Han but was defeated. Bo Ji entered the harem but remained unfavored for over a year. Through friends' intervention, she gained the king's favor, dreamed of a blue dragon in her belly, and bore a son. After Gaozu's death, because she was rarely favored, she escaped Empress Lu's persecution and followed her son to the Kingdom of Dai, becoming Queen Mother of Dai. After Empress Lu's death, ministers chose her son as Emperor Xiaowen.

Empress Dowager Dou was selected for palace service during Empress Lu's reign. When palace maids were distributed to princes, she wanted to go to Zhao but was mistakenly sent to Dai. There, the King of Dai favored her, and she bore two sons. After the queen and her sons died, Dou Ji's eldest son became crown prince, and she became empress. Her brother Dou Guangguo, sold as a child, later identified himself through shared memories and was reunited with her. The Dou family received honors, and Empress Dowager Dou promoted Huang-Lao teachings.

The text also covers Empress Wang, whose mother Zang'er forcibly took her daughter from her husband Jin Wangsun and sent her to the prince's womb. She dreamed of the sun falling into her arms and bore a son who became Emperor Wu. Lady Li Ji, mother of the eldest son, refused the eldest princess's marriage proposal for her daughter, leading to conflict. Through manipulation, Mrs. Wang's son became crown prince instead.

Wei Zifu, a singer from Princess Pingyang's household, caught Emperor Wu's attention. After initial neglect, she became pregnant and gained favor, eventually becoming empress after Empress Chen was deposed for witchcraft. Her brother Wei Qing became a general, and her family produced five marquises.

The text concludes with Emperor Wu's later consorts and his controversial execution of Lady Gou Yi, mother of his youngest son. He explained that young monarchs with living mothers caused chaos, citing Empress Lu as an example. All mothers of Emperor Wu's children were executed to prevent such problems.
