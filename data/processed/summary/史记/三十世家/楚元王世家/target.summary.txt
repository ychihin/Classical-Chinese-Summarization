Liu Jiao, King of Chu Yuan, was the younger half-brother of Emperor Gaozu. As a child, Gaozu often brought guests to his sister-in-law’s house for meals. She resented him and once scraped the pot to pretend no soup was left, causing guests to leave. Gaozu later saw soup remained, fostering resentment. When he became emperor, he enfeoffed his brothers but initially excluded his eldest brother’s son, citing the mother’s unworthiness, though he later granted the son the title of Genjiehou. His second brother Zhong was made acting king. In the sixth year of his reign, after arresting King Han Xin of Chu, Gaozu made Jiao King of Chu, with his capital at Pengcheng. Jiao reigned 23 years, succeeded by his son Ying, then grandson Wang Wu. Wu, during mourning for Empress Dowager Bo, committed a private crime, losing Donghai County. The next spring, Wu conspired with the King of Wu to rebel. His prime minister Zhang Shang and Taifu Zhao Yiwu opposed him; Wu killed them and marched west with the King of Wu, attacking Liang and capturing Jibi. At Changyi, they fought General Zhou Yafu. The Han army cut off Wu and Chu’s food routes; starving, the King of Wu was defeated, and the King of Chu committed suicide. Their armies surrendered. Emperor Xiaojing initially wanted to restore their descendants, but Empress Dowager Dou opposed it for the King of Wu. Only the King of Chu’s descendant, Li, then Zongzheng, was named King Wen of Chu. He reigned three years, succeeded by An Wangdao, then Jian Wangzhu, then Wang Chun. In the second year of Dijie, eunuchs accused Chun of rebellion; he committed suicide, and Chu’s territory became Pengcheng County.

Zhao Wang Liu Sui, a son of Gaozu, was posthumously named You. King You died of sorrow. Empress Gao made Lu Lu King of Zhao; after her death, ministers executed the Lu clan and made King You’s son king. In Emperor Xiaowen’s second year, Sui’s younger brother Piqiang became King Wen of Hejian. He reigned 13 years, succeeded by Ai Wangfu, who died heirless, ending the kingdom. Sui ruled Zhao for 26 years. During Emperor Xiaojing’s reign, Chao Cuo deprived him of Changshan County. When Wu and Chu rebelled, King Zhao conspired with them. His prime minister Jian De and internal history Wang Han resisted; he burned them to death and stationed troops on Zhao’s western border, awaiting the Wu army. He also sought Xiongnu aid. The Han sent Quzhou Hou Liji to attack Zhao. King Zhao defended Handan for seven months. After Wu and Chu were defeated, Xiongnu withdrew. Luan Bu, returning from Qi, joined Liji to divert water against Zhao’s capital. The city fell; King Zhao committed suicide, and his line ended.

Tai Shigong remarked: A rising nation shows auspicious signs, with valued gentlemen and dismissed villains. A perishing nation sees wise men retreat and treacherous officials rise. Had King Wu of Chu heeded Duke Shen, or King Zhao appointed Mr. Fang Yu, they might have avoided usurpation and death. A king without inherent virtue cannot use wise men. The nation’s safety depends on decrees and appointments—this is profoundly true.
