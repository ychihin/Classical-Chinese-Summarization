The text chronicles the history of the Yan state, beginning with its founder, Zhao Gongxi, a member of the Zhou royal family granted the title in Northern Yan by King Wu. During King Cheng's reign, Zhao Gong governed west of Shaanxi, while the Duke of Zhou governed the east. Suspicious of the Duke of Zhou's regency, Zhao Gong was appeased by the Duke's historical references to virtuous Yin ministers. Zhao Gong governed the western region with great popularity, famously adjudicating under a Tangli tree, which the people later honored in the poem "Gantang" after his death.

The succession of Yan rulers is then detailed, from Huihou through numerous generations, with key events interspersed: the flight of King Zhou Li, the rise of Qin as vassals, and the reign of King Xuan. The narrative covers Yan's interactions with other states, including attacks on King Hui of Zhou, invasions by Shan Rong (aided by Duke Huan of Qi), and participation in alliances against Qin. Internal strife emerges under King Kuai, who ceded power to his minister Zizhi, leading to chaos, Qi's invasion, and the death of King Kuai. King Zhao of Yan restored stability by recruiting talents like Le Yi, Zou Yan, and Ju Xin, leading to a prosperous period and a successful campaign against Qi, capturing its capital Linzi.

After King Zhao's death, King Hui's distrust of Le Yi led to Qi's recovery under Tian Dan. Subsequent rulers faced defeats by Zhao, including a failed attack advised by Ju Xin, resulting in heavy losses. The state weakened as Qin expanded, destroying Han and Zhao. Prince Dan of Yan's failed assassination attempt on Qin's king via Jing Ke prompted Qin's invasion, capturing Ji and forcing King Yan to flee to Liaodong. Eventually, Qin captured Liaodong, ending the Yan state. The historian concludes that Yan's survival for eight or nine hundred years, despite external pressures and near destruction, was due to Zhao Gong's virtuous legacy.
