After the death of the great minister Huo Qubing, he submitted a memorial to Emperor Shu, expressing gratitude for the emperor's favor that allowed him to serve in the army. He stated that he should focus on border defense but dared to discuss other matters because he saw the emperor worrying about the world and the people, reducing his own food, music, and servants. Huo Qubing noted that the princes had grown up and could perform rites, yet they lacked official titles. He urged the emperor to appoint the prince as soon as possible on an auspicious midsummer day.

Following Huo Qubing's death, the emperor issued an edict to the censor. On the day of Yihai in the third month, Huo Guang, the censor and minister, sent a report to Weiyang Palace. The emperor ordered it handed to the censor. On Wushenshuo in the third month of the sixth year of Yuanshou, the censor Huo Guang, along with other officials, received the document. The prime minister Zhuang Qingzhai, censor Zhang Tang, and others submitted a memorial echoing Huo Qubing's request to title the princes, specifically Liu Hong, Liu Dan, and Liu Xu, as kings.

The emperor responded by citing the Zhou dynasty's enfeoffment of eight hundred princes, all with the surname Ji, and the "Book of Rites" stating that branch children cannot sacrifice in the ancestral temple. He questioned the proposal to merge vassal states, claiming he had never heard of it. The emperor argued that his virtue was shallow and he could not harmonize the country, let alone make an unlearned prince a king. He ordered a re-discussion.

On Bingzi day of March, another report was sent to Weiyang Palace. Prime Minister Chen Qingzhai and Censor Zhang Tang risked their lives to advise, discussing with princes and officials. They referenced the Zhou dynasty's enfeoffment and the examples of Uncle Kang and Bo Qin, who were granted titles due to their ancestors' virtues. They argued that the emperor's actions in honoring the virtuous and supporting the rise of declining states were commendable. However, they insisted that making the prince a king would disrupt the order of superiority and inferiority, and they requested that Liu Hong, Liu Dan, and Liu Xu be made princes and kings.

The emperor again cited Uncle Kang and the Duke of Zhou, emphasizing the difference between the wise and unworthy. He stated that this was enough to suppress unvirtuous princes and make them kings. On Wuyin day in April, another report was submitted, reiterating the request. The emperor repeated his previous reasoning.

On Guiwei day in April, the memorial was left in the palace without instructions. Prime Minister Qingzhai, Grand Servant Gongsun He, and others made another speech, referencing Huo Qubing's earlier memorial. They argued that Emperor Gao founded the world and named his descendants kings, and the laws of the late emperor should not be changed. They requested the emperor to order the historian to choose an auspicious day and prepare rituals.

The emperor issued an edict saying "Okay." On Bingshen day in April, a report was made, and the emperor decreed that Liu Hong be made King of Qi, Liu Dan King of Yan, and Liu Xu King of Guangling. On Dingyou day in April, the orders were entered into Weiyang Palace. On Yisi day in April, the emperor sent Zhang Tang to inform the temple and establish the princes as kings, issuing specific policies for each.

Tai Shigong commented that the king divided land to build countries and enfeoffed children to praise relatives, ensuring the royal family's stability. Chu Shaosun added that he obtained the seal policy books from elders and compiled them to pass down the wise master's will. He noted that Emperor Wu consecrated three sons as kings on the same day, writing policies to warn them. He also recounted the story of Mrs. Wang, who wished for her son Liu Hong to be king in Luoyang, but Emperor Wu chose Qi instead. Liu Hong died young, and the country was abolished. Chu Shaosun explained the ritual of receiving soil and the warnings given to each king, emphasizing the importance of following the emperor's instructions. He concluded by detailing the fates of the kings: Liu Xu plotted rebellion and committed suicide, and Liu Dan also conspired and died, leading to the abolition of their states.
