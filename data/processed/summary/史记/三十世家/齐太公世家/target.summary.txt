# Summary of the Historical Records of Qi State

Taigong Wang, originally named Lu Shang and surnamed Jiang, was a descendant of officials who assisted Yu in flood control during ancient times. In his old age, he met Zhou Xibo (later King Wen of Zhou) while fishing on the Wei River. Xibo, having received a divination that he would find an assistant to become overlord, recognized Lu Shang as the sage his ancestors had predicted would come to Zhou. He called him "Taigong Wang" and respected him as military advisor. Some accounts say Lu Shang had previously served King Zhou of Shang but left due to his unruliness, while others claim he was a recluse recruited by Xibo's ministers to help rescue Xibo from imprisonment. Regardless of the legends, all agree he became teacher and assistant to both King Wen and King Wu.

Taigong secretly planned with King Wen to implement virtue and overthrow Shang rule, employing military tactics that later generations would honor. After King Wen's death, King Wu succeeded him. In the ninth year of King Wu's reign, he campaigned eastward with Taigong as his commander, gathering eight hundred princes at Mengjin. Two years later, despite unfavorable divination and a storm, Taigong persuaded King Wu to march. They defeated Shang Zhou at Muye, and King Zhou was killed. King Wu then distributed Shang's wealth to the poor, honored loyal ministers, and established Zhou governance—all following Taigong's advice.

King Wu enfeoffed Taigong with the state of Qi at Yingqiu. When Taigong traveled slowly to his fief, a lodging house keeper warned him that opportunities are easily lost. He immediately hurried and arrived just as the Lai people attacked for the territory. Taigong governed by simplifying customs, opening commerce, and developing fishery and salt industries, making Qi a great power. King Cheng later granted Taigong authority to conquer any rebellious nobles within vast territories. Taigong died over one hundred years old.

The succession continued through Dinggong, Yigong, Guigong, and Aigong, whom the Marquis of Ji falsely accused, leading to his execution by boiling. His brother Jing became Duke Hu but was killed by Aigong's brother Shan, who became Duke Xian and moved the capital to Linzi. Subsequent rulers included Duke Wu, Duke Li (killed by the people for his tyranny), Duke Wen, Duke Cheng, and Duke Zhuang. During Duke Zhuang's reign, the Zhou royal family moved east to Luoyi after Quan Rong killed King You.

Duke Li was succeeded by Duke Xiang, who had conflicts with his cousin Wuzhi and committed adultery with his sister, Lady Lu of Lu. When Duke Huan of Lu discovered this, Duke Xiang had him killed. Later, Duke Xiang was killed by Wuzhi and conspirators after failing to replace garrison troops as promised. Wuzhi was then killed by the people of Yonglin.

After Duke Xiang's death, his brothers competed for the throne. Prince Xiaobai, with support from the Gao and Guo families, returned first and became Duke Huan of Qi. He initially wanted to kill Guan Zhong, but Bao Shuya convinced him that Guan Zhong was essential for becoming overlord. Duke Huan appointed Guan Zhong as minister, and together with Bao Shu, Xi Peng, and Gao Xi, they reformed Qi's government, implemented military systems, developed commerce and fisheries, and relieved the poor, bringing happiness to the people.

Duke Huan became the first overlord of the Spring and Autumn period. He kept his promise to return territory to Lu after Cao Mo's kidnapping, earning trust among princes. He rescued Yan from Shanrong, gave Yan territory, and restored Zhao Gong's governance. He defended Wei against Di invaders and built a city for them. He attacked Cai and Chu for failing to pay tribute, though he eventually made peace with Chu. He convened multiple alliances, including the famous Kuiqiu meeting, where King Xiang of Zhou rewarded him. Despite Guan Zhong's opposition, Duke Huan wanted to perform the fengshan sacrifices but eventually abandoned the idea.

After Guan Zhong's death, Duke Huan ignored his advice and appointed Yi Ya, Shu Diao, and Kaifang, who seized power. Duke Huan had six favored concubines bearing sons, and after his death, his sons fought for succession. His body lay unburied for sixty-seven days, with maggots crawling outside the door. Five of his sons eventually became rulers: Wugui, Xiaogong, Zhaogong, Yigong, and Huigong.

The state experienced continued turmoil through various rulers. Duke Yi was killed by Bingrong and Yongzhi after mutilating them. Duke Hui was established after Yigong's death. Duke Qing faced invasion from Jin after his mother laughed at Jin envoy Chuke. Duke Zhuang was killed by Cui Zhu for adultery with his wife. Cui Zhu and Qingfeng then controlled the government until the Tian family rose to power.

Tian Chang eventually killed Duke Jian and established Duke Ping, becoming prime minister and dividing Qi's territory for the Tian family. The Tian family ultimately took full possession of Qi, and when Tian He became a prince, the state continued under the Tian clan until King Wei of Qi ruled as a hegemon.

Sima Qian observed that Qi's fertile land and broad-minded people, combined with Taigong's foundation and Duke Huan's virtuous governance, naturally led to its position as overlord among the princes.
