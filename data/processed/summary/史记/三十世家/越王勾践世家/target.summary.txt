# Summary of the Historical Text on Yue and Wu

King Gou Jian of Yue was a descendant of Xia Yu, son of a concubine of Emperor Shaokang of Xia. Emperor Shaokang's son was enfeoffed at Kuaiji, where he maintained Xia Yu's sacrifices. His descendants tattooed their bodies, cut their hair short, developed wasteland, and built cities. After more than twenty generations, rule passed to Yunchang, who had conflicts with King Helu of Wu. After Yunchang's death, his son Gou Jian became king.

In Gou Jian's first year, King Helu of Wu attacked Yue. Gou Jian sent death-defying warriors who lined up in three rows and rushed into Wu's position, shouting they would kill themselves. The stunned Wu soldiers were attacked by Yue forces, defeated at Jianli, and Helu was wounded. Dying, Helu warned his son Fucha never to forget Yue.

Three years later, Gou Jian learned Fucha was training soldiers to avenge Wu. Despite Fan Li's warning that weapons were dangerous and attacking immoral, Gou Jian attacked Wu. Fucha mobilized elite troops, defeated Yue at Fujiao, and surrounded Kuaiji with only 5,000 Yue soldiers remaining. Gou Jian regretted ignoring Fan Li's advice. Fan Li advised humility and generous gifts to Wu. Gou Jian sent official Zhong to beg for peace, offering himself as a slave and his wife as a concubine. Wu's Zixu advised against accepting, but Dazai Pi, bribed with treasures, persuaded King Wu to pardon Yue. Zixu warned that Gou Jian was a wise monarch with capable ministers who would cause chaos if allowed to return, but King Wu ignored him.

After returning home, Gou Jian worked tirelessly, hanging gall where he could taste it, asking himself if he had forgotten Kuaiji's humiliation. He farmed personally, his wife wove cloth, ate no meat, wore no fine clothes, treated worthy men with courtesy, helped the poor, and shared hardships with the people. He entrusted government to Zhong while Fan Li served as hostage in Wu for two years.

Seven years after returning, Gou Jian wanted revenge. A counselor advised patience, noting Wu was attacking Qi and Jin while lacking morality. Yue should befriend Qi, Chu, and Jin while treating Wu favorably. Two years later, when Wu prepared to attack Qi, Zixu warned that Gou Jian shared hardships with his people and would become Wu's worry, urging attack on Yue first. King Wu refused, defeated Qi, and scolded Zixu. Yue's Zhong tested Wu by requesting grain; King Wu lent it despite Zixu's objections. Dazai Pi slandered Zixu, and when Zixu entrusted his son to the Bao family while on a mission to Qi, King Wu sent him a sword to commit suicide. Zixu laughed, saying he should have his eyes hung on Wu's east gate to see Yue's army enter.

Three years later, Gou Jian asked Fan Li about attacking Wu. Fan Li said no. The next spring, while Wu's elite troops were at Huangchi with King Wu, Yue attacked, killed Wu's prince, and defeated Wu's forces. King Wu hid the news and sought peace. Yue agreed, unable to destroy Wu. Four years later, Yue attacked again, besieged Wu's capital for three years, and trapped King Wu on Gusu Mountain. King Wu sought peace, reminding Gou Jian of Kuaiji. Gou Jian wavered, but Fan Li insisted Wu must be destroyed, recalling God had given Yue to Wu before. Fan Li drove away Wu's envoy. King Wu committed suicide, covering his face, ashamed to see Zixu. Yue's king buried Wu's king and killed Dazai Pi.

After pacifying Wu, Gou Jian marched north, met with Qi and Jin princes at Xuzhou, and paid tribute to Zhou. King Yuan of Zhou rewarded him. Gou Jian donated land to Chu, Song, and Lu. The Yue army marched unimpeded, and Gou Jian became overlord. Fan Li then left, sending a letter to Zhong from Qi, saying: "When birds are gone, the good bow is hidden; when cunning rabbit dies, running dogs are cooked. You can only share adversity with the king, not happiness." Zhong claimed illness but was slandered as rebellious. The king sent him a sword, saying he had used three of Zhong's seven strategies against Wu and should try the remaining four with the late king. Zhong committed suicide.

Gou Jian died, and his descendants ruled for generations until King Wuqiang. During King Wei of Chu's reign, Yue attacked Qi. Qi's envoy persuaded Yue to attack Chu instead, arguing that Chu's forces were dispersed. Yue abandoned Qi and attacked Chu. King Wei of Chu defeated Yue, killed King Wuqiang, and seized all land from Wu to Zhejiang. Yue disintegrated, with various descendants competing for power, some claiming kingship, living south of the Yangtze and paying tribute to Chu. Seven generations later, Fujian Junyao helped princes overthrow Qin. Emperor Gao of Han restored Yue's kingship. Dongyue and Minjun were both Yue descendants.

Fan Li served Gou Jian for over twenty years, destroying Wu and making Gou Jian hegemon. Recognizing that great fame was difficult to sustain and Gou Jian could not share happiness, Fan Li left by boat with valuables, never returning. He changed his name, farmed in Qi, accumulated wealth, became prime minister, then resigned, distributed his property, and moved to Taodi, calling himself Tao Zhugong. He farmed and traded, accumulating millions. His second son killed someone in Chu. He sent his youngest son with gold to bribe his old friend Zhuang Sheng, but his eldest son insisted on going. The eldest son delivered the gold but later retrieved it when he thought amnesty would free his brother. Zhuang Sheng, ashamed, told the Chu king that the amnesty was due to bribery. The king killed Zhu Gong's son first, then issued amnesty. Zhu Gong had expected this, knowing his eldest son valued money while his youngest would spend freely. Fan Li died of old age in Taodi.

Tai Shigong commented: Xia Yu's contributions were great, channeling rivers and stabilizing Kyushu. His descendant Gou Jian worked hard, destroyed Wu, and became overlord—truly virtuous, likely Xia Yu's legacy. Fan Li moved three times, leaving a glorious reputation.
