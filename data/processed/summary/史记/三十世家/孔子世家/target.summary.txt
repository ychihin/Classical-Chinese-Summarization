Confucius was born in Zouyi, Changping Township, Lu State, to Shu Lianghe and a woman named Yan after they prayed at Niqiu Mountain. His head was concave at birth, so he was named Qiu, with the courtesy name Zhongni and surname Kong. His father died shortly after his birth and was buried in Fangshan, but his mother did not reveal the location. As a child, Confucius played at setting up sacrificial vessels and learning ritual movements. After his mother died, he temporarily placed her coffin at the Five Fathers' Road until a neighbor told him his father's burial place, allowing him to bury them together. At seventeen, Meng Lizi, a senior official, warned his son Yizi that Confucius, a descendant of saints, should be taken as a teacher. After Lizi's death, Yizi and Nangong Jingshu learned etiquette from Confucius.

Confucius' family was poor and of low status. He worked as a clerk managing the Ji family's warehouse and pastures, where he was fair and efficient, leading to his promotion to Sikong. He later left Lu, was ostracized in Qi, expelled in Song and Wei, besieged between Chen and Cai, and finally returned to Lu. At nine feet six inches tall, he was called "The Long Man." Nangong Jingshu requested Duke Zhao of Lu to allow Confucius to travel to Zhou to learn etiquette from Lao Tzu, who advised him to avoid speaking of others' faults and to act without selfish motives. After returning, Confucius gained many disciples.

At thirty, Confucius advised Duke Jing of Qi that Qin's success came from its ambitious governance and use of talents like Baili Xi. At thirty-five, after political turmoil in Lu, Confucius went to Qi, where he studied music and advised Duke Jing on governance, emphasizing proper roles and frugality. However, Yan Ying dissuaded the duke from employing Confucius, criticizing Confucian rituals as impractical. Confucius left Qi and returned to Lu.

At fifty, Confucius was appointed governor of Zhongdu, then promoted to Sikong and later to Grand Sikou. In the tenth year of Duke Ding of Lu, he organized a meeting at Jiagu between Lu and Qi, where he confronted Qi's disruptive performances and had performers executed. Qi returned invaded lands to Lu. In the thirteenth year, Confucius ordered the demolition of the city walls of Ji, Meng, and Shu families to curb their power, but the Meng family resisted, and the city of Cheng could not be captured.

At fifty-six, Confucius acted as chief minister, killing the official Shaozhengmao. Within three months, Lu's markets became honest, men and women walked separately, and lost items were not taken. Qi, fearing Lu's rise, sent eighty beautiful women and 120 horses to Lu. Ji Huanzi accepted them, neglecting state affairs. Confucius left Lu after the suburban sacrifice was mishandled, traveling to Wei, where he received a salary of 60,000 dou but left after slander. He was besieged in Kuang, mistaken for Yang Hu, but escaped. He returned to Wei, met Nanzi, and left after Duke Linggong's behavior disgusted him.

Confucius traveled to Song, where Sima Huan tried to kill him, then to Zheng, where he was described as a lost dog. In Chen, he identified arrows from the Sushen tribe. After three years, he left due to invasions. At Pu, he was detained but escaped after a covenant. He returned to Wei, but Duke Linggong did not use him. He considered going to Jin but turned back after learning of the deaths of two virtuous officials. He returned to Wei, then left for Chen after Duke Linggong ignored him.

At sixty, Confucius was in Chen. After Ji Huanzi's death, Ji Kangzi recalled Ran Qiu, and Confucius hoped to return. He moved to Cai, then to Ye, where he advised on governance. He met hermits Chang Ju and Jie Ni, who criticized his efforts. He was besieged between Chen and Cai, running out of food, but continued teaching. His disciples questioned their plight, but Confucius maintained his principles. King Zhao of Chu planned to grant him land, but Zixi dissuaded him, and the king died soon after. Confucius returned to Wei, then to Lu after fourteen years.

In Lu, Confucius was not appointed to office. He compiled the "Shu Zhuan" and "Book of Rites," corrected poetry and music, and studied the "Book of Changes," wearing out the binding cords. He taught about 3,000 disciples, with 72 mastering the six arts. He educated in knowledge, conduct, loyalty, and faith, avoiding profit unless linked to fate and benevolence. He was humble in his hometown but eloquent in court. He avoided spoiled food and improper seating, and showed sympathy to mourners and the blind.

In his later years, Confucius compiled the "Spring and Autumn Annals" based on Lu's history, covering twelve rulers. He used subtle writing to praise and criticize, making rebellious officials fearful. He died at seventy-three in the sixteenth year of Duke Ai of Lu. Duke Ai mourned him, but Zigong criticized the eulogy. Confucius was buried north of Lucheng. Disciples mourned for three years, with Zigong staying six years. His tomb became a site of annual worship, and his former residence was turned into a temple, preserved for over 200 years. Emperor Liu Bang of Han sacrificed to him. Confucius' descendants continued his lineage, including his son Boyu, who died before him, and grandson Zisi, who wrote "The Doctrine of the Mean." The historian concluded that Confucius, though a commoner, became the supreme sage, with his teachings enduring for generations.
