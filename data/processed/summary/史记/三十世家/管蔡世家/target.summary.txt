The text details the lineage and history of the sons of King Wen of Zhou, focusing on Guan Shuxian and Cai Shudu, younger brothers of King Wu. King Wu had ten brothers from the same mother, Taisi. Among them, only King Wu and Zhou Gongdan were virtuous and talented, leading King Wen to make Fa (King Wu) crown prince. After King Wu conquered the Yin, he enfeoffed lands to his brothers: Shuxian received Guan, Shudu received Cai, and they were made ministers to Wu Geng, son of the Yin king. Zhou Gongdan was enfeoffed in Lu and served as prime minister. Other brothers received Cao, Cheng, and Huo. Kang Shu and Ran Jizai were too young for titles.

After King Wu died, King Cheng succeeded as a child, and Zhou Gongdan ruled as regent. Guan Shu and Cai Shu, suspecting Zhou Gong's intentions, rebelled with Wu Geng. Zhou Gong crushed the rebellion, executing Wu Geng and Guan Shu, and exiling Cai Shu. Cai Shu died in exile, but his son Hu reformed and was later enfeoffed in Cai by Zhou Gong's recommendation. The text then traces the succession of Cai rulers through generations, including conflicts with Chu and Qi. Key events include Cai Aihou's capture by Chu, Cai Miao Hou's conflict with Duke Huan of Qi over a marriage dispute, and Cai's eventual destruction by King Hui of Chu after 33 years.

The text also covers the lineage of Cao Shu Zhenduo, another brother, whose descendants ruled Cao until its destruction by Song. It notes that Guan Shu left no descendants, while Zhou Gongdan's descendants ruled Lu, and Kang Shu's descendants ruled Wei. The historian concludes that Guan and Cai's rebellion was unremarkable, but Zhou Gong's regency stabilized the Zhou dynasty. The dream of a Cao man predicting the state's fall is recounted, ending with Cao's destruction due to poor governance under Boyang and Gongsun Qiang.
