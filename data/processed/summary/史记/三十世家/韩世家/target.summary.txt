The ancestor of Korea shared the surname Ji with Emperor Zhou. His descendants were enfeoffed in Jin State at Han Yuan, becoming known as Han Wuzi. After three generations, Han Jue emerged, and his fief was called Han Weishi. During Duke Jinggong’s third year in Jin, Tu'anjia sought to kill Zhao Shuo, son of the deceased Zhao Dun. Han Jue tried to stop him but failed, then warned Zhao Shuo, who begged him not to let the Zhao line end. Han Jue agreed. When Tu'anjia massacred the Zhao family, Han Jue feigned illness. Cheng Ying and Gongsun Jiu hid the orphan Zhao Wu, which Han Jue knew. In the eleventh year, Han Jue and Chuke led 800 chariots to defeat Qi, capturing Feng Chou’s father. Jin then established six ministers, with Han Jue first, titled Xianzi. In the seventeenth year, Jinggong fell ill; divination blamed Daye’s descendants. Han Jue praised Zhao Shui’s deeds, prompting Jinggong to restore Zhao Wu to the Zhao line. Han Xianzi grew old and died, succeeded by his son Xuanzi, who moved to Zhouyi. In the fourteenth year of Jin Ping Gong, Jizha of Wu predicted Jin’s power would go to Han, Wei, and Zhao. Later, Han Xuanzi, Zhao, and Wei divided Qi and Yangyu counties, then attacked Fan and Zhonghang families. Xuanzi died, succeeded by Sadako, who moved to Pingyang. Subsequent rulers included Jianzi, Zhuangzi, Kangzi, and Wuzi. In Wuzi’s second year, Korea attacked Zheng, killing King Zheng Youhui. After Wuzi died, Jinghou reigned, attacking Zheng and occupying Yongqiu. In the sixth year of Jinghou, Han, Zhao, and Wei were recognized as vassal states. Later rulers included Liehou, Wenhou, Aihou, Yihou, Zhaohou, King Xuanhui, King Xiang, King Li, King Huanhui, and finally King Han, who was captured by Qin in the ninth year of his reign, ending Korea. Sima Qian noted that Han Jue’s act of restoring Zhao Wu was a rare virtue, and the Han family’s rise to princely status over ten generations was deserved.
