Wei Zikai, eldest son of Emperor Yi of Yin and half-brother of King Zhou, repeatedly advised King Zhou against his dark, licentious rule but was ignored. Fearing Yin's downfall, Wei Zi consulted the Grand Master and Junior Master, who advised him to leave when the kingdom became irredeemable. He then departed. Jizi, a relative of King Zhou, lamented King Zhou's extravagance with ivory chopsticks, foreseeing ruin. After King Zhou ignored his remonstrations, Jizi feigned madness and became a slave rather than publicize the king's evil. Prince Bigan, another relative, bluntly admonished King Zhou, who killed him and ripped out his heart to check for seven orifices. Wei Zi concluded that ministers may leave a king who refuses repeated advice.

King Wu of Zhou defeated Yin, and Wei Zi surrendered, bearing sacrificial vessels. King Wu restored Wei Zi's title and appointed Wu Geng, son of Shang Zhou, to continue Yin sacrifices, with Guan Shu and Cai Shu as assistants. King Wu visited Jizi, who explained the nine great laws of heaven: the Five Elements, Five Matters, Eight Political Rules, Five Epochs, Imperial Principles, Three Virtues, Divination, Various Signs, and Five Blessings with Six Extremes. King Wu granted Jizi Korea, no longer treating him as a subject. Jizi later composed a poem mourning Yin's ruins.

After King Wu's death, Zhou Gongdan, acting as regent, suppressed a rebellion by Guan Shu, Cai Shu, and Wu Geng. He killed Wu Geng, exiled Cai Shu, and appointed Wei Zikai to rule Yin, later enfeoffing him in Song. Wei Zi's benevolent rule endeared him to the people. His successors included Wei Zhong, Song Gongji, Ding Gongshen, Min Gong, Yang Gongxi, Li Gong, Li Gongju, Hui Gong, Ai Gong, Dai Gong, Wu Gong, Xuan Gongli, and Mu Gong. Mu Gong, honoring his brother Xuan Gong's wish, passed the throne to Xuan Gong's son Yi (Duke Shang) instead of his own son.

Duke Shang's reign saw turmoil: Hua Du killed Kong's father and Duke Shang, installing Duke Zhuang. Subsequent rulers included Min Gong, Huan Gong, Xiang Gong, Cheng Gong, Zhao Gong, Wen Gong, Gong Gong, Ping Gong, Yuan Gong, Jing Gong, Zhao Gong, Duke Du, Xiu Gongtian, Pi Gong, Ticheng, and Yan. Yan, known as "Jie Song," indulged in cruelty and debauchery, shooting advisors. The princes allied with Qi to attack Song, killing Yan and dividing its territory in his forty-seventh year.

Tai Shigong concluded that Confucius recognized three benevolent men of Yin: Wei Zi, Jizi, and Bigan. The "Spring and Autumn" noted Song's instability began when Duke Xuan deposed the prince for his brother. Duke Xiang's benevolent attempts at leadership were praised, though he was defeated at Hongshui; gentlemen commended his adherence to ritual propriety amidst the era's lack of etiquette.
