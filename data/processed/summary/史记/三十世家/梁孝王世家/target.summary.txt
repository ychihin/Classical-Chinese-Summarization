Liu Wu, King Xiao of Liang, was the son of Emperor Xiaowen and half-brother to Emperor Xiaojing, with Queen Mother Dou as his mother. Emperor Xiaowen had four sons: the eldest became Emperor Xiaojing; Liu Wu was the second, followed by Liu Shen and Liu Mingsheng. Liu Wu was initially made acting king, later King of Huaiyang, and eventually King of Liang in the twelfth year of Emperor Xiaowen's reign. He ruled for eleven years from his first title as King of Dai to becoming King of Liang. During his reign, he frequently entered the court, staying in the capital for extended periods. Emperor Jing once casually promised to pass the throne to him, which secretly pleased Liu Wu and the Queen Mother.

In the spring of the rebellion of the seven kingdoms, Wu and Chu attacked Liang, killing tens of thousands. King Xiao defended Suiyang with generals like Han Anguo, blocking Wu and Chu from advancing westward. After three months of stalemate with Taiwei Yafu, Wu and Chu were destroyed, with Liang's casualties and captures roughly equal to the imperial court's. The following year, a prince was established. Due to his merits and relation to the emperor, King Xiao was granted a large, fertile fiefdom with over forty cities. He was deeply favored by Empress Dowager Dou, receiving countless rewards. He built Dongyuan over 300 li in radius, expanded Suiyang City to 70 li, and constructed palaces and elevated passages over 30 li long. His splendor rivaled an emperor's, with banners, thousands of attendants, and hunting expeditions. Heroes and lobbyists from across the country, including Yang Sheng and Gongsun Gui, flocked to Liang. Gongsun Gui, given a thousand pieces of gold and a lieutenant position, was called General Gongsun. Liang forged many weapons and amassed nearly a trillion in money, plus countless jewels.

In October of the 29th year, King Xiao visited Emperor Jing, who sent an imperial carriage to greet him. He requested to stay in the capital, which was granted due to the Queen Mother's love. He accompanied the emperor in chariots and hunted at Shanglin Garden. Liang's ministers could enter the palace freely. In November, after the prince was deposed, the Queen Mother wanted King Xiao as successor, but ministers like Yuan An dissuaded Emperor Jing, blocking the motion. The matter remained secret. King Xiao then returned to his state. In April, the emperor made King Jiaodong the crown prince. Resentful, King Xiao plotted with Yang Sheng and Gongsun Gui to assassinate Yuan An and over ten other ministers. The court failed to find the murderer initially but eventually suspected King Xiao. After capturing the assassins, the emperor sent envoys to Liang, arresting Gongsun Gui and Yang Sheng, who hid in the harem. Liang's officials urged the king to have them commit suicide, which he did. The emperor remained resentful, but after Han Anguo pleaded for forgiveness through the eldest princess, King Xiao was forgiven. He later entered the capital in disguise, hiding in the eldest princess's garden, causing the Queen Mother to weep, thinking the emperor had killed him. King Xiao then confessed, and brotherly relations were restored, though the emperor gradually alienated him.

In the winter of the 35th year, King Xiao visited again but was denied permission to stay. After returning, he became depressed. While hunting, a cow with feet on its back disgusted him. He fell ill with fever in mid-June and died six days later, posthumously titled King Xiao. He was filial, often wanting to serve the Queen Mother when she was ill. Her grief was immense, but Emperor Jing divided Liang into five kingdoms, granting all five sons and five daughters titles, which pleased her. King Xiao's eldest son, Liu Mai, inherited as King Gong; others became kings of Jichuan, Jidong, Shanyang, and Jiyin. His wealth was immense, with over 400,000 kilograms of gold left after death.

King Gong died after seven years, succeeded by his son Liu Xiang as King Ping. A dispute over a valuable bottle led to conflict between King Ping, his grandmother Queen Mother Li, and Queen Ren. Queen Ren took the bottle despite Queen Mother Li's objections. Queen Mother Li later died, with Queen Ren neglecting her in illness and death. A man named Lei Anfan, seeking revenge, revealed palace secrets to the court. Investigations confirmed Queen Mother Li's adultery and King Ping's mismanagement. The emperor reduced Liang's fiefdom by eight cities and executed Queen Ren. Liu Xiang died after 39 years, succeeded by his son Liu Wushang. Other sons of King Xiao faced fates: Liu Ming was deposed for shooting a lieutenant; Liu Pengli was deposed for robbery and murder; Liu Ding died without an heir; Liu Bushi died after one year without an heir. Their fiefdoms became counties.

Tai Shigong noted that King Xiao's wealth and splendor, though due to his position and the prosperous times, constituted transgression. Chu Shaosun added that the trouble stemmed from the court's failure to properly address succession issues, with ministers like Yuan Ang and Dou Ying providing wise counsel. He emphasized the importance of adhering to ritual and law, as exemplified by the Han system of limited court visits. The Queen Mother's desire to make King Xiao heir was ultimately thwarted by ministers citing historical precedents, such as the chaos in Song caused by Duke Xuan. After the assassination of Yuan An, the case was handled by officials who burned evidence, preserving King Xiao's reputation and calming the Queen Mother.
