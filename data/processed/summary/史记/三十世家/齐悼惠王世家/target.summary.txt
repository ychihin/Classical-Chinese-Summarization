Liu Fei, King Hui of Qi, was Emperor Gaozu's eldest son, granted the title of King of Qi with seventy cities. In the second year of Emperor Xiaohui's reign, he visited the capital and dined with the emperor as equals, angering Empress Dowager Lu, who sought to kill him. Following his internal historian Xun's advice, he donated Chengyang County to Princess Lu Yuan, appeasing the empress dowager and allowing his safe return to Qi. He died thirteen years into his reign, succeeded by his son Liu Xiang, King Ai.

During King Ai's reign, Empress Dowager Lu took control, appointing Lu family members as kings and dividing Qi's territories. Liu Zhang, Marquis of Zhuxu and King Ai's younger brother, boldly challenged the Lu clan's power. At a banquet, he used a farming song to criticize their rule and later executed a drunken Lu clan member under military law, instilling fear in the Lu family and reviving Liu clan influence. After Empress Dowager Lu's death, Lu Lu and Lu Chan plotted rebellion. Liu Zhang, aware of their plans, urged his brother King Qi to raise troops westward while he and Dongmou Hou served as internal allies. King Qi mobilized forces, detaining King Langxie to command his troops, and sent letters to princes condemning the Lu clan's usurpation. However, General Guan Ying, sent to intercept Qi, halted at Xingyang and allied with Qi instead.

Liu Zhang, along with Taiwei Zhou Bo and Prime Minister Chen Ping, killed Lu Chan and Lu Lu in Guanzhong, ending the Lu clan's threat. The ministers debated succession; King Langxie and others opposed King Qi due to his cruel maternal uncle, Sijun, and instead chose Dai Wang, Emperor Gaozu's son with the virtuous Bo family, as Emperor Xiaowen. King Qi withdrew his troops. Wei Bo, who had instigated Qi's rebellion, was spared but dismissed after Guan Ying deemed him incompetent. Wei Bo's background included serving Cao Shen, who recommended him to King Huihui.

In Emperor Xiaowen's first year, Qi regained Chengyang, Langye, and Jinan counties. Liu Zhang was made King of Chengyang, and Liu Xingju King of Jibei. King Ai of Qi died, succeeded by his son Liu Ze, King Wen. King Wen died without heirs, and his territory reverted to the court. Emperor Xiaowen then divided Qi among King Huihui's sons: Liu Zhi as King of Jibei, Liu Biguang as King of Jinan, Liu Xian as King of Sichuan, Liu Xian as King of Jiaoxi, Liu Xiongqu as King of Jiaodong, alongside the King of Chengyang and King of Qi, totaling seven kings.

In King Xiao of Qi's eleventh year, the Rebellion of the Seven Kingdoms erupted, with Wu and Chu leading the revolt. Kings of Jiaoxi, Jiaodong, Sichuan, and Jinan joined, besieging Qi. King Xiao hesitated but defended the city. Dr. Lu Zhong, sent to the emperor, returned with orders to hold out, but was captured by the rebels. He falsely reported the Han court's defeat to the city but secretly urged resistance, leading to his execution. Qi held until Han forces under Luan Bu and Cao Qi defeated the rebels and lifted the siege. Suspected of collusion, King Xiao committed suicide, but Emperor Jing later cleared his name, making his son Liu Shou King Yi of Qi. The rebel kings were executed, and their territories annexed.

King Yi reigned for twenty-two years, succeeded by his son Liu Ci, King Li. King Li's mother, Queen Mother Ji, arranged his marriage to her niece, but he disliked her. She then had her daughter Wengzhu oversee the harem, leading to an incestuous relationship. Eunuch Xu Jia, sent by Empress Dowager to propose a marriage for her granddaughter E, was rebuffed by Queen Mother Ji. Xu Jia later slandered Qi, comparing it to the fallen King of Yan. Master Father Yan, favored by the emperor, accused Qi of rebellion and incest, becoming prime minister to investigate. Under pressure, King Li committed suicide, ending his line. Emperor Jing executed Master Father Yan after King Zhao's complaint.

King Hui of Qi's descendants also ruled Chengyang and Suichuan. Liu Zhang, King of Chengyang Jing, was rewarded for killing Lu Chan. His lineage continued through several generations. Liu Xingju, King of Jibei, rebelled after feeling slighted by Emperor Wen, who reduced his promised territory. He was defeated and committed suicide. Later, Liu Zhi, King of Jibei, remained loyal during the Seven Kingdoms Rebellion and was renamed King of Sichuan. Other sons of King Huihui—Liu Biguang, Liu Xian, Liu Xian, and Liu Xiongqu—were made kings but rebelled and were killed, their territories becoming counties.

Sima Qian noted that King Hui of Qi's domain was the largest among princes, initially granted to stabilize the realm after the Qin dynasty's failure to support clan members, though it was later divided.
