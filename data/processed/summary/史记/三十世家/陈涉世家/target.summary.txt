Chen Sheng, a native of Yangcheng, and Wu Guang, from Yangxia, were conscripted in July of the first year of Qin II to defend Yuyang, along with 900 others stationed in Daze Township. Heavy rain blocked the roads, causing them to miss the deadline, which meant execution by law. Chen Sheng and Wu Guang discussed that fleeing or rising up both led to death, but dying for a national cause was preferable. Chen Sheng noted the world’s suffering under Qin rule, suggesting using the names of Prince Fusu and Chu general Xiang Yan to rally support, as both were respected. Wu Guang agreed. They consulted a fortune teller, who hinted at establishing prestige through supernatural means. Chen Sheng wrote “King Chen Sheng” on silk, hid it in a fish, and had Wu Guang mimic a fox’s cry at night, shouting “Great Chu will rise, King Chen Sheng.” The garrison soldiers were startled and discussed it the next day.

Wu Guang, known for his care, provoked a drunken county captain, who whipped him and drew his sword. Wu Guang killed the captain, and Chen Sheng helped kill two others. Chen Sheng addressed the troops, stating that missing the deadline meant beheading, and even if spared, many would die guarding borders. He declared that princes and generals were not born noble. The troops pledged obedience. They rebelled in the names of Fusu and Xiang Yan, baring their right arms under the banner “Great Chu.” They built an altar, sacrificed the captain’s head, and Chen Sheng declared himself general, Wu Guang as captain. They attacked Daze Township and Qi County, then sent Ge Ying to capture eastern areas, taking Qi, Feng, Kuzhe, and Qiao. As they marched, their forces grew to six to seven hundred chariots, over a thousand cavalry, and tens of thousands of infantry by the time they reached Chen County. The county magistrate was killed, and they occupied the city.

Chen Sheng summoned local elders and heroes, who praised him for attacking the tyrannical Qin and re-establishing Chu, urging him to become king. He proclaimed himself king of Zhang Chu. People in various counties responded by killing Qin officials. Wu Guang was appointed acting king to attack Xingyang westward. Chen Sheng ordered Wu Chen, Zhang Er, and Chen Yu to capture Zhao, and Deng Zong to take Jiujiang. Ge Ying killed Xiang Qiang, whom he had made king of Chu, after hearing of Chen Sheng’s kingship, but Chen Sheng executed Ge Ying. Zhou Shi was sent north to capture Wei lands. Wu Guang besieged Xingyang but failed to take it. Chen Sheng appointed Cai Ci as Shangzhu Kingdom and Zhou Wen as general to lead troops west. Zhou Wen gathered forces, reaching Hangu Pass with thousands of chariots and hundreds of thousands of soldiers, but was defeated by Qin’s Zhang Han, who recruited criminals and slaves. Zhou Wen fled and committed suicide after repeated defeats.

Wu Chen proclaimed himself King of Zhao in Handan. Chen Sheng, angered, imprisoned Wu Chen’s family but released them on Cai Ci’s advice, sending congratulations to Zhao. Zhao’s generals advised against marching west, instead expanding north into Yan. Han Guang became King of Yan. Zhou Shi was defeated by Tian Dan, who declared himself King of Qi. Zhou Shi made Ningling Jun Jiu King of Wei. Tian Zang killed Wu Guang, claiming Chen Sheng’s order, and led elite troops west but died in battle. Zhang Han defeated other Chu forces. Chen Sheng killed Deng Yue and Wu Xu after their defeats. Qin Jia killed Wu Pingpan and made Jing Ju king of Chu. Zhang Han attacked Chen County, killing Cai Ci. Chen Sheng was defeated and killed by his charioteer Zhuang Jia in December. He was buried in Dang County with the posthumous title King Yin. Lu Chen killed Zhuang Jia and made Chen County Chu’s capital. Song Liu surrendered to Qin and was executed. Chen Sheng reigned for six months. His former friend visited but was killed for speaking of past events, causing others to leave. Chen Sheng appointed Zhu Fang and Hu Wu, who harshly punished officials, leading to his failure. Despite his death, his uprising led to Qin’s downfall. Emperor Gaozu of Han later honored his tomb.
