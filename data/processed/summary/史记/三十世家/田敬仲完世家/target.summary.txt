Chen Wan, son of Chen Ligong, was born with a prophecy from a Zhou diviner: his descendants would prosper in a state ruled by the Jiang clan, likely after Chen’s decline. Chen Wan’s father, Ligong, became ruler after a series of violent successions involving the murder of Duke Huan and his son by Cai conspirators. Ligong was later killed for his promiscuity, and Chen Wan, unable to claim the throne, served as an official. Fearing for his life after a prince’s murder, he fled to Qi, where Duke Huan appointed him as a minister overseeing craftsmen. A divination for his marriage foretold that his Gui descendants would thrive under the Jiang clan, rising to prominence over generations. Chen Wan changed his surname to Tian, and his lineage grew influential in Qi.

Tian Wenzi served Duke Zhuang of Qi, but his son Tian Huanzi gained favor. Tian Lizi, a doctor under Duke Jing, won popular support by using unequal measures to lend and collect grain, amassing power. After Duke Jing’s death, Tian Qi opposed the appointed heir Yan Ruzi, instead installing Yangsheng as Duke Mourning. Tian Qi became prime minister, and his son Tian Chang continued consolidating power. Tian Chang killed rivals like Jian Zhi and Duke Jian, then installed Duke Ping, returning lands to appease other states. He controlled Qi’s government, executed powerful clans, and expanded his fiefdom. His harem of over a hundred concubines bore him seventy sons. Tian Chang’s son Tian Pan succeeded him, and the Tian family dominated Qi, with descendants like Tian He eventually becoming marquises, recognized by the Zhou emperor.

Tian He’s line continued through Huan Gong and King Wei of Qi. King Wei initially neglected governance, facing invasions, but reformed after executing corrupt officials and rewarding the virtuous. He defeated Wei and Zhao, stabilizing Qi. His minister Zou Ji advised him on governance through music metaphors, and later became a marquis. King Wei’s successors, including King Xuan and King Min, expanded Qi’s influence, with King Min briefly claiming the title of Eastern Emperor. However, King Min’s arrogance led to a coalition attack by Yan, Qin, and others, resulting in his death. His son King Xiang, hidden as a servant, was restored with help from Tian Dan, who defeated Yan. King Xiang’s reign saw Qi’s recovery, but his son King Jian eventually surrendered to Qin without resistance, ending Qi’s independence. The people blamed King Jian’s reliance on corrupt advisors and Qin spies for the fall.

The historian concludes that the Tian family’s rise, from Chen Wan’s flight to Qi to their seizure of power, may have been guided by the divinations of the Zhou priest and Yizhong, fulfilling prophecies over generations.
