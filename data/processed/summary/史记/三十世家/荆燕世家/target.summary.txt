King Jing Liu Jia was a member of the Liu clan, though his specific branch is unknown. During the early Han Dynasty, he served as a general under the King of Han, pacifying territories and joining campaigns against Xiang Yu. In the fourth year of the Han Dynasty, after the King of Han’s defeat at Chenggao, Liu Jia crossed the Yellow River into Chu territory, burning grain supplies and disrupting enemy logistics. He held his position against Chu attacks, coordinating with Peng Yue. By the fifth year, Liu Jia crossed the Huai River, recruited Chu’s Grand Sima Zhou Yin, who betrayed Xiang Yu, and captured Jiujiang. He then joined King Yingbu at Gaixia to attack Xiang Yu. Later, Liu Jia led the Jiujiang army with Taiwei Lu Wan against Linjiang King Gongwei, who died, leading to the renaming of Linjiang to Nanjun.

In the sixth year, Emperor Gaozu deposed the Chu King Han Xin and divided his territory. Seeking to install family members as kings, Gaozu appointed Liu Jia as King of Jing, commanding fifty-two cities east of the Huai, while his brother Liu Jiao became King of Chu. Liu Jia was killed in battle against the rebellious King Yingbu in the eleventh year. His territory later passed to Liu Bi, King of Wu.

King Liu Ze of Yan, a distant Liu relative, began as a doctor and later became Marquis of Yingling for capturing an enemy general. During Empress Lu’s reign, Tian Sheng, a Qi native, advised Liu Ze to gain favor by supporting the Lu clan’s rise. Through Zhang Qing, a favored emissary, Tian Sheng persuaded the court to make Lu Chan king, then proposed making Liu Ze a king to secure Lu power. Liu Ze was thus made King of Langxie. After Empress Lu’s death, Liu Ze allied with Qi King Liu Xiang to oppose the Lu clan, eventually supporting King Dai as emperor. He was then made King of Yan, dying in his second year. His descendants included his son Liu Jia and grandson Liu Dingguo, who committed heinous crimes—adultery with relatives and murder—leading to his suicide and the abolition of his state.

Tai Shigong noted that Liu Jia was made king for his military exploits during the Han’s early instability, while Liu Ze gained his title through political maneuvering, enabling his line to rule for three generations.
