Emperor Xiaojing had thirteen sons by five mothers, all named kings. Li Ji bore Liu Rong, Liu De, and Liu Yanyu; Cheng Ji bore Liu Yu, Liu Fei, and Liu Duan; Jia Fu bore Liu Pengzu and Liu Sheng; Tang Ji bore Liu Fa; and Mrs. Wang bore Liu Yue, Liu Ji, Liu Cheng, and Liu Shun.

Liu De, King of Hejian, was a Confucian scholar who attracted many followers. He reigned 26 years, succeeded by his son and grandson. Liu Rong, once crown prince, was deposed and later died by suicide after being convicted of expanding his palace onto temple grounds. His kingdom was abolished. Liu Yu, King of Lu, built palaces and loved music but stuttered. Liu Fei, King of Jiangdu, was a brave military leader who defeated Wu in the Rebellion of the Seven Kingdoms. His son Liu Jian committed suicide after plotting rebellion, adultery, and witchcraft. Liu Duan, King of Jiaoxi, was cruel and impotent, often killing officials who followed Han laws. He died without heirs, and his kingdom was abolished.

Liu Pengzu, King of Zhao, was deceitful and manipulative, causing many officials to be dismissed or executed. He amassed wealth through monopolies but spent it on concubines. Liu Sheng, King of Zhongshan, indulged in wine and women, fathering over 120 children. Liu Fa, King of Changsha, was born to a maid and ruled a small, poor kingdom. Liu Yue, King of Guangchuan, died after 12 years. Liu Ji, King of Jiaodong, prepared for a rebellion but died of illness. His son Liu Xian succeeded him. Liu Cheng, King of Qinghe, died heirless, and his kingdom was abolished. Liu Shun, King of Changshan, was favored but arrogant. His son Liu Bo was deposed after misconduct, and the kingdom was divided.

Sima Qian noted that after the Rebellion of the Seven Kingdoms, the power of princes was reduced; they only collected taxes, and the court appointed officials. Some princes became so poor they rode oxcarts.
