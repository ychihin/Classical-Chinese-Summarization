# Summary of the Historical Records of the State of Lu

Duke Dan of Zhou, younger brother of King Wu of Zhou, was known for his filial piety and virtue. He assisted King Wu in military campaigns, including the conquest of Yin Zhou at Muye. After Yin Zhou's death, Duke Zhou participated in ceremonies announcing King Zhou's guilt, released the imprisoned Jizi, and helped grant territories to various nobles. Though granted the title of Duke of Lu in Qufu, he remained in the capital to assist King Wu.

When King Wu fell ill, Duke Zhou offered himself as a sacrifice to the ancestral spirits, praying to die in King Wu's place. He performed divination, received auspicious signs, and King Wu recovered. After King Wu's death, with King Cheng still an infant, Duke Zhou assumed regency to prevent rebellion. His brothers Guan Shu and Cai Shu spread rumors that he would harm the young king. Duke Zhou explained his actions were to preserve the Zhou legacy, and eventually suppressed a rebellion led by Guan Shu, Cai Shu, and Wu Geng, executing Guan Shu and exiling Cai Shu.

Duke Zhou governed for seven years, then returned power to King Cheng when the king came of age. He wrote admonitions including "Wu Yi" and "Toushi" to warn King Cheng against indulgence, citing examples of Yin Dynasty rulers who prospered through diligence or fell through decadence. After Duke Zhou's death, King Cheng buried him beside King Wen. When a storm destroyed crops, King Cheng opened the golden-bound cabinet containing Duke Zhou's prayer to die for King Wu, realized his virtue, and performed ceremonies that restored the harvest.

The state of Lu continued through successive dukes. Duke Boqin, Duke Zhou's son, governed Lu and suppressed rebellions. The state experienced periods of stability and turmoil, with conflicts over succession and the growing power of the Three Huan families (Mengsun, Shusun, Jisun). Duke Zhao was forced into exile after attacking the Ji family, dying in Qianhou. The state declined as the Three Huan families grew increasingly powerful, reducing subsequent rulers to figureheads.

The state of Lu lasted for thirty-four generations from Duke Zhou to Duke Qing, when King Kaolie of Chu conquered Lu. The historian Sima Qian noted that despite Lu's reputation for ritual propriety, its actual history was marked by chaos, including the murder of legitimate heirs and ministers attacking their rulers, revealing a contradiction between their ceremonial observance and political reality.
