# Summary of the Chu State Historical Records

The ancestors of the Chu State originated from Gaoyang, Emperor Zhuanxu, who was the grandson of Huangdi. The lineage passed through several generations to Chongli, who served as Huozheng under Emperor Ku and was granted the title Zhurong. After Chongli failed to completely suppress the Gonggong rebellion, Emperor Ku executed him and appointed his younger brother Wu Hui as his successor. Wu Hui fathered Luzhong, who had six sons by caesarean section. The youngest son, Jilian, whose surname was Mi, became the ancestor of the Chu royal family. Jilian's descendants included Yuxiong, who served King Wen of Zhou like a son but died young.

During King Cheng of Zhou's reign, Yuxiong's descendant Xiong Yi was granted the title of baron in the Chu Man region with Danyang as his residence. Xiong Yi served the Zhou king alongside other noble descendants. The lineage continued through several generations until Xiong Qu, who gained support from people along the Yangtze and Han Rivers. He conquered territories including Yong and Yangyue, and declared his three sons kings of various regions in the Chu Man area. When King Li of Zhou ascended, Xiong Qu removed his kingly title out of fear.

After several successions and internal conflicts, Xiong Tong killed his nephew and ascended the throne as King Wu of Chu. He declared himself king after the Zhou royal family refused to grant him a higher title, stating that his ancestor had served King Wen and that the Zhou had only given his family a baron's title in the Chu region. King Wu attacked Sui and died of illness during the campaign. His son King Wen Xiong Yu moved the capital to Ying. Under King Wen, Chu attacked Shen, Cai, and Deng, becoming powerful and intimidating smaller states in the region.

King Cheng Xiong Yun extended kindness to the people and restored relations with other states. He expanded Chu's territory to a thousand miles in radius. When Duke Huan of Qi invaded Chu, King Cheng made an alliance with him. King Cheng later captured and humiliated Duke Xiang of Song, attacked Song, and entertained the Jin prince Chong'er. After Chong'er returned to Jin, Chu's general Ziyu was defeated at Chengpu, and King Cheng executed him. King Cheng initially established Shang Chen as crown prince despite warnings about his cruel nature. When King Cheng planned to replace him, Shang Chen surrounded the palace, and King Cheng hanged himself.

King Mu Shang Chen ascended and destroyed several states. His son King Zhuang Lu initially indulged in pleasure but reformed after remonstrance from Wu Ju and Su Cong. King Zhuang attacked Lu Hunrong, paraded troops near the Zhou capital, and asked about the Nine Tripods. Wang Sunman replied that ruling depends on virtue, not tripods, and that the Zhou dynasty's mandate was difficult to change. King Zhuang withdrew. He later attacked Chen, captured it, but restored it after Shen Shu's advice. He besieged Zheng, accepted its surrender, and defeated Jin at the Yellow River. He besieged Song for five months until its people resorted to cannibalism, then withdrew.

King Gong Shen was defeated by Jin at Yanling and shot in the eye. After several successions, King Ling Xiong Wei gathered the princes, built Zhanghua Terrace, and asked about obtaining the Zhou tripod. He became arrogant but faced rebellion when his brother Qiji conspired with Guan Cong. King Ling's soldiers abandoned him, and he died of starvation at Shen Hai's house. Qiji ascended as King Ping, who deceived and killed two kings to gain the throne. He extended favors to the people, returned territories, and improved governance.

King Ping listened to Fei Wuji's slander against Prince Jian, imprisoned Wu She, and forced Prince Jian to flee. Wu She's son Wu Zixu escaped to Wu. King Ping married the woman intended for Prince Jian and fathered Xiong Zhen, who became King Zhao. Fei Wuji's slander caused Prince Jian's exile and Wu She's execution. King Zhao fled when Wu invaded, and Wu Zixu desecrated King Ping's tomb. King Zhao eventually returned with Qin's help.

King Hui was kidnapped by Bai Gongsheng but rescued by Ye Gong. After several more successions, King Huai was deceived by Zhang Yi into breaking relations with Qi, then attacked Qin and was defeated. He was later tricked into meeting King Zhao of Qin, who detained him. King Huai died in Qin, causing Chu to break relations with Qin. King Qingxiang moved the capital eastward as Qin captured Chu territories. The Chu state continued to weaken, eventually moving its capital to Shouchun. Qin generals Wang Jian and Meng Wu captured the Chu king, destroyed the state, and established three commanderies in Chu territory.
