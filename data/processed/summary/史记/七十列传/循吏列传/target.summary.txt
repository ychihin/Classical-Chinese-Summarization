Tai Shigong observed that laws guide people toward good, while punishments deter evil. Even when legal codes were incomplete, virtuous people exercised self-restraint because officials upheld rules. He argued that if officials fulfill their duties with integrity, severe punishments are unnecessary.

Sun Shuao, a hermit from Chu, became prime minister after three months. His lenient yet effective governance fostered harmony, pure customs, and no deceit among officials or theft among people. He encouraged seasonal woodcutting and river transport, improving livelihoods. When King Zhuang replaced small coins with large ones, causing market chaos, Sun Shuao advised restoring the old currency, which stabilized the market within three days. To address low carriages, he suggested raising thresholds instead of issuing decrees; within half a year, people voluntarily raised their carriages. Sun Shuao remained humble in office and unregretful in dismissal, knowing his abilities and lack of fault.

Zichan, a senior official in Zheng, became prime minister under Zheng Zhaojun after prior mismanagement. In one year, frivolity ceased, elders and children were relieved of burdens. In two years, market transactions became fair. In three years, people left doors unlocked and returned lost items. In four years, farmers left tools in fields. In five years, military service was unnecessary, and funerals were conducted properly. After 26 years, Zichan died; young and old mourned, lamenting the loss of their reliance.

Gongyixiu, prime minister of Lu, upheld laws without alteration, ensuring officials behaved correctly. He forbade officials from competing with people for profit. When offered fish, he refused, fearing dismissal would end future gifts. He discarded tasty vegetables and burned his wife’s loom to avoid harming farmers and weavers.

Shi She, prime minister of Chu, pursued a murderer who was his father. He released his father and imprisoned himself, stating that punishing his father was unfilial and condoning crime disloyal. Despite King Zhao’s pardon, he committed suicide to uphold duty.

Li Li, a judge of Jin, discovered a wrongful execution. He imprisoned himself and sentenced himself to death. Duke Wen offered pardon, but Li Li insisted on accountability, refusing to shift blame. He committed suicide to uphold legal integrity.

Tai Shigong concluded that Sun Shuao restored market order, Zichan’s death moved Zheng, Gongyixiu drove out his wife, Shi She’s suicide honored King Zhao, and Li Li’s death rectified Jin’s laws.
