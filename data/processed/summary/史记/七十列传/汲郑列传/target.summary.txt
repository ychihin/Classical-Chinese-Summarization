Ji An, courtesy name Changru, was born in Puyang County, with ancestors serving as ministers for seven generations. During Emperor Xiao Jing's reign, he became a prince Xima through his father's recommendation and was respected for his seriousness. After Emperor Jing's death, the new emperor appointed him as an official of the audience. Sent to inspect conflicts between the Minyue and Ouyue peoples, he turned back at Wuxian, deeming the local aggression unworthy of imperial attention. Similarly, after a fire in Hanoi County burned over a thousand households, he reported it as an accident due to dense housing. While passing through Henan County, he saw severe flood and drought suffering, with over ten thousand families affected and reports of cannibalism. Using his talisman, he distributed grain from official stores for relief, then returned the talisman and accepted responsibility for falsely preaching the imperial edict. The emperor, considering him virtuous, exempted him from guilt and transferred him to Xingyang County Magistrate. Ji An, ashamed of the role, resigned claiming illness. The emperor then summoned him as a senior official in the imperial court, but due to his frequent outspoken opinions, he was released as the prefect of Donghai County.

Ji An admired Taoism, managed affairs cleanly, and delegated to capable subordinates, focusing on general principles rather than details. Despite frail health, he often stayed indoors, and within a year, Donghai County became peaceful and praised. The emperor recalled him as Lord Commander, treating him similarly to Jiu Qing. He governed by doing nothing, promoting big ideas without strict legal adherence. Arrogant and blunt, he contradicted people openly, befriending only those congenial to him and shunning others, causing scholars to avoid him. Yet he was eager to learn, chivalrous, and principled, often speaking out in court and offending the emperor, admiring figures like Fu Bai and Yuan An. He associated with Guan Fu, Zheng Shi, Zong Zheng, and Liu Qi, who also faced demotions for remonstrance.

When Wu'anhou Tianfu became prime minister, high-ranking officials knelt to him, but Ji An only bowed. The emperor, recruiting literary scholars, was rebuked by Ji An for having many desires while feigning benevolence. The emperor grew angry, and ministers feared for Ji An. Later, the emperor remarked on Ji An's stupidity, but Ji An defended his integrity, stating he would not harm court affairs. Ill for three months, Ji An's condition persisted. Zhuang Zhu, requesting leave on his behalf, told the emperor that Ji An could assist a young monarch steadfastly, unswayed by inducements or threats. The emperor agreed, comparing Ji An to ancient loyal ministers.

General Wei Qing entered the palace with the emperor seated by his bed, while Prime Minister Gongsun Hong sometimes saw the emperor without a hat. For Ji An, the emperor always wore a hat, once hiding in a tent when unprepared. Ji An was highly respected. When Zhang Tang became a court official, Ji An accused him of failing to uphold the late emperor's achievements and changing rules set by Emperor Gaozu, warning of lost descendants. They often argued, with Ji An scolding Zhang Tang for causing fear. The Han Dynasty was then conquering the Xiongnu, and Ji An advised peace, but the emperor favored Confucianism and Gongsun Hong. Ji An criticized Gongsun Hong and Zhang Tang as treacherous, leading to their deep hatred. Gongsun Hong suggested Ji An as Right Internal History to manage dignitaries, a role Ji An handled well for years.

General Wei Qing's status rose, but Ji An treated him as an equal. When advised to kneel, Ji An replied that bowing did not diminish respect. Wei Qing valued Ji An's virtue, seeking his advice. Liu An, the king of Huainan, feared Ji An's integrity, noting it was hard to tempt him. As Ji An enjoyed Jiuqing treatment, Gongsun Hong and Zhang Tang rose to equal ranks, then surpassed him. Ji An complained to the emperor about later officials being piled on top, but the emperor dismissed his stupidity. When King Hunxie of the Xiongnu surrendered, the court borrowed horses from the people, causing unrest. Ji An defended the magistrate of Chang'an, suggesting gradual preparations. Later, over 500 merchants were sentenced to death for trading with the Huns. Ji An argued against harsh laws, but the emperor disagreed. Months later, Ji An was convicted of a minor law, dismissed, and retired.

Years later, the emperor appointed Ji An as prefect of Huaiyang County due to private coin minting. Ji An declined but was forced to accept. He managed the county well, and after Zhang Tang's ruin, the emperor ordered Ji An to enjoy princes' salary while governing Huaiyang. Ji An died seven years later. His brother Ji Ren and son Ji Yan were promoted, and his aunt's son Sima An reached Jiuqing four times. Duan Hong from Puyang also rose to Jiuqing, but officials respected Ji An more.

Zheng Zhuang, courtesy name Zhuang, was born in Chen County. His ancestor Zheng Jun served under Xiang Ji and refused to address him by his first name, as ordered by Emperor Gaozu, leading to his dismissal. Zheng Zhuang was chivalrous, rescuing Zhang Yu, and gained fame in Liang and Chu. During Emperor Xiao Jing's reign, he served as Prince Sheren, often visiting friends and hosting guests. He loved Taoism, admired elders, and was humble. Under Emperor Wu, he rose to Right Internal History of Jiuqing but was demoted for improper opinions. As Right Internal History, he treated visitors equally, was upright, and had no private property, using his salary to support friends. He recommended scholars, praising them as better than himself, and was praised by elders east of Miao Mountain. Sent to inspect the Yellow River breach, he asked for five days to prepare, but the emperor noted his reputation for traveling without food. In later years, due to debts from recommended guests, he was demoted to civilian, then served as governor of Runan County until death. Zheng Zhuang and Ji An were both honest, dismissed, and poor, with no property after death. Six or seven of Zheng Zhuang's descendants reached two thousand shi.

Tai Shigong noted that Ji An and Zheng Zhuang's virtue attracted many guests when powerful, but few when powerless. Zhai Gong of Xiajun County wrote on his gate: "One death and one life show friendship; one poor and one rich show acquaintance; one noble and one humble show friendship." Ji An and Zheng Zhuang suffered this misfortune, which was sad.
