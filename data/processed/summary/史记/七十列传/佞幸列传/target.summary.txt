The text discusses how favoritism and flattery, rather than talent, often led to power and wealth in ancient China, particularly during the Han Dynasty. It begins by noting that both women and men, including scholars and eunuchs, used beauty and charm to gain imperial favor. During Emperor Gaozu's reign, flattery was common, and under Emperor Xiaohui, two favored individuals, Hongru and Jiru, gained such influence that ministers imitated their dress and behavior. They later moved to Anling.

Under Emperor Xiaowen, favored figures included Deng Tong, a boatman who became a high-ranking official after the emperor dreamed of a yellow-headed man pushing him to heaven, which matched Deng Tong's appearance. Deng Tong was rewarded generously, given a copper mountain to mint his own money, and even sucked pus from the emperor's carbuncle. However, after Emperor Jing ascended, Deng Tong was accused of minting coins abroad, his wealth was confiscated, and he died in poverty.

During Emperor Xiaojing's reign, only Zhou Ren was favored, but not deeply. Under Emperor Wu, Han Yan, a calligraphy classmate, gained favor through flattery and military skills, often sleeping with the emperor. He was later accused of adultery with concubines and forced to commit suicide by the empress dowager. Li Yannian, a singer and dancer, rose to favor after his sister became Emperor Wu's concubine, but after her death, his arrogance led to his execution.

The text concludes that most favored courtiers were relatives of the emperor, though Wei Qing and Huo Qubing achieved merit through talent. Tai Shigong reflects on the unpredictability of imperial favor, citing Mi Zixia as an example of ingratitude.
