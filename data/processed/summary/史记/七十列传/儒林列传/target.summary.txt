Tai Shigong reflects on the decline of the Zhou royal family, which led to the emergence of satirical poems like "Guan Ju." Confucius, troubled by the decay of the royal way and the rise of evil, compiled the "Poems" and "Books," organized ritual music, and became so captivated by the "Shao" music in Qi that he forgot the taste of meat for months. After returning to Lu from Wei, he corrected the music, restoring order to the "Ya" and "Song." Despite traveling to over seventy states seeking office, no one employed him. When a unicorn was hunted in Lu, Confucius lamented his unrealized ideals and wrote the "Spring and Autumn Annals" based on Lu's historical records to express imperial laws.

After Confucius's death, his seventy-plus disciples dispersed, some becoming teachers to princes, others teaching scholars, and some living in seclusion. Zilu served in Wei, Zizhang in Chen, Ziyu in Chu, Zixia taught in Xihe, and Zigong died in Qi. Figures like Tian Zifang and Wu Qi were trained by Zixia's generation. Confucianism declined during the Warring States, squeezed out by warring powers, but persisted in Qi and Lu. Under Kings Wei and Xuan of Qi, Mencius and Xunzi advanced Confucius's work. The Qin dynasty burned books and killed scholars, leaving the Six Arts incomplete. Chen She's rebellion saw Lu Confucians carrying Confucius's ritual vessels to him, and Kong Jia became his doctor, dying with him. Despite Chen She's brief rule, scholars followed him due to resentment over Qin's book burning. Even when Han Gaozu besieged Lu, Confucians continued reciting scriptures and practicing rituals.

After the Han dynasty's establishment, Confucian scholars revived their studies. Shusun Tong formulated Han rituals, becoming Taichang, and his disciples became court officials, sparking interest in Confucianism. However, wars delayed school establishment. Under Emperors Xiaohui, Empress Lu, Xiaowen, and Xiaojing, Confucianism was sidelined, with only a few doctorates retained. When the current emperor ascended, Zhao Wan and Wang Zang promoted Confucianism, leading to the recommendation of virtuous scholars. Teachers of the "Shi" included Shen Peigong in Lu, Yuan Yinsheng in Qi, and Han Taifu in Yan. "Shangshu" was taught by Fusheng in Jinan, "Li" by Gaotangsheng in Lu, and "Spring and Autumn" by Hu Wusheng in Qi and Dong Zhongshu in Zhao. After Empress Dowager Dou's death, Tian Fu became prime minister, favoring Confucianism, and Gongsun Hong rose through his expertise in "Spring and Autumn," inspiring widespread study.

Gongsun Hong, as prime minister, petitioned the emperor to promote education, citing ancient practices of village schools. He proposed assigning fifty disciples to doctors, exempting them from taxes, and selecting students from commoners and local officials. After a year of study, those mastering one classic could fill official vacancies, with exceptional students reported directly. Those failing were dismissed, and their recommenders punished. The emperor approved, leading to many educated Confucian scholars among officials.

Shen Peigong of Lu taught the "Shi" without elaborating on meanings, leaving doubts unforced. His disciples included Wang Zang and Zhao Wan, who recommended him to the emperor. Shen Gong, over eighty, advised the emperor to focus on actions over words, but the emperor, fond of rhetoric, was displeased. After Empress Dowager Dou's interference led to Zhao Wan and Wang Zang's deaths, Shen Gong retired and died. His disciples became doctors and officials, praised for their integrity.

Yuan Gusheng of Qi debated Huang Sheng before Emperor Jing about whether Tang and Wu usurped the throne. Yuan argued they followed heaven's mandate, while Huang insisted on hierarchical propriety. Emperor Jing ended the debate. When Empress Dowager Dou questioned Yuan about "Laozi," he dismissed it as common talk, angering her. She ordered him to kill a wild boar, which he did with the emperor's help. Later, he became tutor to King Qinghe but retired due to illness. Recruited by the current emperor, he was slandered and sent home at over ninety, advising Gongsun Hong to uphold uprightness.

Han Sheng of Yan, a doctor under Emperor Xiaowen, wrote "Nei Zhuan" and "Wai Zhuan" on the "Shi," with terms differing from Qi and Lu but similar purposes. His grandson Han Shang became a doctor.

Fusheng of Jinan, a former Qin doctor, hid "Shangshu" during the book burning. After the Han, he recovered twenty-nine chapters and taught them. His students included Zhang Sheng and Ouyang Sheng, who taught Erkuan. Erkuan, despite poverty, studied diligently, became an official, and served under Zhang Tang, rising to a high position but failing to correct the emperor's mistakes. Fusheng's grandson also studied "Shangshu" but couldn't clarify it. Kong Anguo re-copied ancient "Shangshu" in modern script, adding lost chapters.

Gaotangsheng of Lu best interpreted the "Book of Rites," which was incomplete after Qin's burning. Xu Sheng of Lu practiced rituals, passing them to his sons Xu Yan and Xu Xiang. Xu Xiang excelled in practice, while Xu Yan knew the text. Their disciples became ceremonial officials. Tian He of Qi taught the "Book of Changes," passed down from Confucius through six generations. His teachings influenced Yang He, who became a senior official.

Dong Zhongshu of Guangchuan, a doctor under Emperor Xiaojing, taught "Spring and Autumn" with such dedication that he didn't visit his garden for three years. He served as Prime Minister of Jiangdu, using yin-yang theory for rain prayers. After a fire at Gaodi Temple, his book on disasters was seen as accusatory, and he was nearly executed but pardoned. He resigned as Prime Minister of Jiaoxi due to fear of trouble, dying at home. He was the most proficient in "Spring and Autumn" during the Han, teaching Gongyang studies. His disciples included Chu Da, Yin Zhong, and Lu Bushu, who judged cases fairly.
