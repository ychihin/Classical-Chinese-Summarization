Zhao Sheng, Lord of Pingyuan, was a virtuous and talented young master of the State of Zhao, known for hosting thousands of scholars. He served as prime minister under Kings Huiwen and Xiaocheng, leaving and returning to the position three times, with his fief in Dongwu City. A crippled man fetching water near his home was laughed at by one of Lord Pingyuan’s concubines. The cripple demanded her head, but Lord Pingyuan initially refused, causing many scholars to leave. He later killed the concubine and apologized to the cripple, regaining his guests.

When Qin besieged Handan, Lord Pingyuan sought help from Chu to form an alliance. He needed twenty followers but could only find nineteen. Mao Sui, a retainer for three years, volunteered. Lord Pingyuan doubted his abilities, but Mao Sui argued that if placed in a pocket, the entire awl would show, not just its tip. Mao Sui joined and impressed the others. In Chu, negotiations stalled until Mao Sui confronted the King of Chu, threatening him and highlighting Chu’s past defeats by Qin. The king agreed to the alliance, and Mao Sui sealed it with a blood oath. Lord Pingyuan later praised Mao Sui as more powerful than an army.

After returning, Chu and Wei sent reinforcements, but Handan remained in danger. Li Tong advised Lord Pingyuan to mobilize his household and distribute resources to the people, gaining 3,000 volunteers. Li Tong died in battle, pushing Qin back thirty miles, and Handan was saved when reinforcements arrived. Yu Qing sought additional fiefs for Lord Pingyuan, but Gongsun Long argued against it, noting Lord Pingyuan’s position and fief were due to kinship, not merit, and that Yu Qing’s proposal was self-serving. Lord Pingyuan rejected the request.

Lord Pingyuan died in the fifteenth year of King Xiaocheng’s reign, and his descendants held the title until Zhao’s fall. He treated Gongsun Long well but dismissed him after Zou Yan refuted his debates. Yu Qing, a skilled lobbyist, advised King Zhao against ceding land to Qin after the Changping defeat, proposing instead to ally with Qi. King Zhao initially agreed but later followed Lou Huan’s advice to cede land, leading to further conflict. Yu Qing eventually fled to Daliang, where he wrote “Yu’s Spring and Autumn Annals,” criticizing political failures.

Tai Shigong noted that Lord Pingyuan, though talented, failed to grasp the bigger picture, leading to Zhao’s defeat at Changping and the loss of 400,000 soldiers. Yu Qing’s plans were thoughtful, but his inability to avoid trouble in Daliang highlighted his flaws, though his writings ensured his legacy.
