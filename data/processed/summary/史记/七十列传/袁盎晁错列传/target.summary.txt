Yuan Ang, courtesy name Si, was from Chu and later moved to Anling. He served as a retainer under Lü Lu during Empress Lü’s reign and was recommended as a lieutenant by his brother after Emperor Wen ascended. Yuan Ang remonstrated with Emperor Wen about his excessive respect for Prime Minister Zhou Bo, arguing that Zhou Bo was a “hero” rather than a true “important minister” for failing to prevent the Lü clan’s usurpation. The emperor heeded his advice, and Zhou Bo later resented Yuan Ang but reconciled after Yuan Ang defended him when Zhou Bo was imprisoned for treason.

Yuan Ang advised against indulging Liu Chang, the King of Huainan, whose arrogance led to rebellion and exile. When the king died en route, Yuan Ang consoled the grieving emperor by citing his three virtuous acts: filial piety to his mother, bravery during the Lü crisis, and humility in refusing the throne. The emperor later made the king’s three sons rulers. Yuan Ang’s outspokenness caused friction with eunuch Zhao Tong, whom he publicly humiliated to prevent him from riding in the emperor’s carriage. He also dissuaded the emperor from reckless chariot racing and corrected seating arrangements that favored a concubine over the queen.

Transferred to Longxi, Yuan Ang won soldiers’ loyalty through kindness. As prime minister of Wu, he followed his nephew’s advice to avoid conflict by drinking daily and persuading the king not to rebel. He later confronted Prime Minister Shen Tujia for arrogance, warning that closing off advice would lead to disaster. Shen Tujia apologized and treated him as an honored guest.

Yuan Ang clashed with Chao Cuo, who investigated him for accepting bribes from the King of Wu, leading to his demotion. When Wu rebelled, Yuan Ang blamed Chao Cuo and urged his execution to appease the rebels. After Chao Cuo’s death, Yuan Ang was sent as an envoy to Wu but was besieged. A former subordinate, whom Yuan Ang had forgiven for an affair with his maid, helped him escape. Yuan Ang later served as prime minister of Chu but retired due to illness, living humbly and befriending gambler Ju Meng despite criticism. He opposed making the King of Liang the emperor’s heir, prompting assassination attempts. Warned by an assassin who spared him, Yuan Ang was eventually killed by Liang’s agents outside Anling.

Chao Cuo, from Yingchuan, studied Legalist doctrines and Shangshu under Master Fu. He gained favor as a prince’s tutor during Emperor Wen’s reign, advocating for reducing princely power. Emperor Jing promoted him, and he revised laws, angering nobles. His father warned him of danger but Chao Cuo insisted on centralizing authority. The Wu-Chu rebellion used his execution as a pretext. After his death, Deng Gong argued that Chao Cuo’s policies were for the empire’s benefit, and Emperor Jing regretted the execution. Deng Gong later became a high official but retired.

Tai Shigong commented that Yuan Ang, though unlearned, was benevolent and outspoken, but his fame-seeking led to downfall. Chao Cuo, despite his talents, failed to adapt and died due to his rigidity, as the saying goes: altering ancient laws brings ruin.
