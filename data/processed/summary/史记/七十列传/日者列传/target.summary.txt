The text discusses the role of divination in ancient China, particularly during the Zhou and Han dynasties. It begins by noting that kings historically received destiny without relying on divination, but by the Han dynasty, diviners had emerged. Lord Sima Ji, a diviner from Chu, practiced in Chang'an's east city. Two officials, Song Zhong and Jia Yi, visited him after discussing governance and saints. They questioned why Sima Ji, despite his wisdom, held a lowly position. Sima Ji defended divination, contrasting it with corrupt officials who exploit power for personal gain. He argued that diviners follow moral principles, aiding people through predictions and advice, unlike officials who engage in deceit and greed. He cited historical figures like Fuxi and King Wen of Zhou to highlight divination's value in governance. Sima Ji criticized the hypocrisy of those who judge diviners while ignoring their own ethical failings. He emphasized that diviners serve society by promoting virtue and preventing harm, often without recognition. The officials were humbled by his words. Later, Song Zhong and Jia Yi faced personal misfortunes—Song was convicted for a failed mission, and Jia Yi died after a student's accident. The text concludes with reflections on diviners' wisdom and their role in society, noting that many skilled individuals, like Sima Ji, remain unrecognized. It advises that elders should nurture children's talents and that diverse skills, including divination, are valuable. The narrative underscores the tension between worldly status and moral integrity, advocating for humility and the pursuit of virtuous living.
