Sun Tzu, from Qi, demonstrated his military expertise to King Helu of Wu by training 180 palace women. After repeated orders failed due to laughter, he executed the two favorite concubines serving as captains, despite the king's plea. The drill then succeeded perfectly. Helu later appointed Sun Tzu as general, leading Wu to defeat Chu, capture Ying, and intimidate Qi and Jin.

Over a century later, Sun Bin, a descendant, studied warfare with Pang Juan. Jealous of Sun Bin's talent, Pang Juan had his feet cut off and face tattooed. Sun Bin secretly met Qi envoys, who smuggled him to Qi. Advising General Tian Ji, Sun Bin used clever horse-racing tactics to win bets, impressing King Wei of Qi, who made him a teacher. When Wei attacked Zhao, Sun Bin proposed attacking Daliang to force Wei's retreat, leading to victory at Guiling. Years later, when Wei and Zhao attacked Han, Sun Bin feigned weakness by reducing camp stoves daily. Pang Juan, believing Qi soldiers deserted, pursued with light troops to Maling, where Sun Bin ambushed him. Pang Juan, seeing a carved tree, lit a torch and was killed by arrows, saying, "This boy has made his name!" Sun Bin's fame spread.

Wu Qi, from Wei, served Lu after killing his Qi wife to gain trust, defeating Qi. Slandered for cruelty, he fled to Wei, where Marquis Wen appointed him general. He shared soldiers' hardships, even sucking pus from a soldier's sore, causing the soldier's mother to weep, recalling her husband's similar fate. Wu Qi governed Xihe, resisting Qin and Han. He advised Marquis Wu that state security relies on benevolence, not terrain. After Tian Wen became prime minister, Wu Qi felt slighted but accepted Tian Wen's reasoning about political stability. Later, a plot by the new prime minister made Marquis Wu suspect Wu Qi, who fled to Chu. King Mourning made him prime minister; he reformed laws, cut redundancies, and strengthened the military, pacifying Baiyue and defeating Qin and Han. After the king's death, nobles attacked Wu Qi, who hid on the king's corpse, causing arrows to strike both. The new prince executed over seventy families for this. Sima Qian notes Sun Tzu and Wu Qi's writings are famous, but Sun Bin couldn't avoid mutilation, and Wu Qi's harshness led to his death.
