Zhang Yi, a native of Wei, studied lobbying under Guiguzi alongside Su Qin, who acknowledged his own inferiority to Zhang Yi. After completing his studies, Zhang Yi faced humiliation when he was falsely accused of stealing a jade during a dinner with the Prime Minister of Chu, resulting in torture. Despite his wife's lament, Zhang Yi remained undeterred, noting that his tongue—his tool for persuasion—was still intact.

Su Qin, having secured a vertical alliance against Qin, secretly aided Zhang Yi by provoking him through public humiliation, which spurred Zhang Yi to seek fortune in Qin. Su Qin provided Zhang Yi with resources, enabling him to gain favor with King Hui of Qin and become a guest minister. Zhang Yi later realized Su Qin's strategy and refrained from attacking Zhao, honoring their unspoken pact.

As Qin's prime minister, Zhang Yi advised King Hui on military strategy, initially advocating an attack on Han to seize the Nine Tripods and control the Zhou emperor, while Sima Cuo argued for conquering Shu to expand territory and enrich the state. King Hui chose Sima Cuo's plan, capturing Shu and strengthening Qin. Zhang Yi later persuaded Wei to ally with Qin, leading to Wei ceding territory and Zhang Yi becoming Qin's prime minister.

Zhang Yi's diplomatic efforts extended to Chu, where he promised 600 li of land in exchange for Chu severing ties with Qi. King Huai of Chu agreed, but Zhang Yi reneged, offering only six li. This led to war, with Qin and Qi defeating Chu, seizing Danyang and Hanzhong. Zhang Yi later returned to Chu as an envoy, narrowly escaping execution through the intercession of Zheng Xiu, and convinced King Huai to ally with Qin.

Zhang Yi also lobbied Han, Qi, Zhao, and Yan to submit to Qin, emphasizing Qin's military superiority and the futility of resistance. Each state eventually agreed to serve Qin, but after King Hui's death, King Wu of Qin distrusted Zhang Yi, leading to his departure to Wei, where he died as prime minister.

Chen Zhen, a rival strategist, defended his loyalty to Qin against Zhang Yi's slander and later advised King Hui on the Han-Wei conflict, using the parable of Bian Zhuangzi killing two tigers to advocate non-intervention until both sides were weakened. This strategy succeeded, allowing Qin to attack and defeat both states.

Xishou, another strategist, undermined Zhang Yi's influence in Wei by aligning with Han, eventually becoming Wei's prime minister and forcing Zhang Yi's departure. After Zhang Yi's death, Xishou served as Qin's prime minister and led a five-state alliance.

Sima Qian concluded that Zhang Yi's actions were inferior to Su Qin's, but both were cunning figures whose strategies shaped the era.
