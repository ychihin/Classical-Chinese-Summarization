Tai Shigong observed that since ancient times, sage kings establishing states and accepting heaven's mandate have always respected divination to aid good deeds. Evidence from Xia, Shang, and Zhou dynasties shows their rises were based on auspicious divination omens: Dayu's marriage to the Tushan daughter was auspicious, leading to Xia Qi's dynasty; Jian Di swallowing a swallow's egg brought favorable predictions for Yin's rise; Houji's agricultural skill brought auspiciousness for Zhou kingship. Kings consulted turtle divination for difficult decisions as an unchanging tradition. Even barbarian tribes without Chinese hierarchy had divination customs using various materials like gold, stone, plants, and wood, all guiding warfare and predicting events.

During Xia and Yin, yarrow and turtle shells were discarded after divination, but Zhou collectors preserved them. Different dynasties valued divination differently, but all used it to predict the future. Some believed holy kings never lacked insights but established divination to guide future generations who might decline in wisdom. Others thought turtles' judgment surpassed human predictions. After Emperor Gaozu succeeded, he followed Qin's system to establish diviners, but the country's unification and ongoing wars prevented deep study. Emperors Xiaohui, Empress Lu, Xiaowen, and Xiaojing merely followed old systems, causing subtle divination methods to be lost.

When the current emperor ascended, he opened paths for talented people, recruiting scholars proficient in various skills. The Taibu office gathered many talents. The emperor planned attacks on Xiongnu, Dawan, and Baiyue, using divination to predict changes and suggest advantages. Generals' battlefield successes included divination's contributions. The emperor rewarded divination officials heavily, with some like Qiu Ziming gaining enormous wealth and favor, overwhelming court ministers. Divination even predicted witch and Gu behaviors accurately. Those who offended diviners faced revenge and family destruction, causing officials to flatter turtle and yarrow theories. Eventually, false accusations were exposed, and the diviners' three clans were wiped out.

Duke Zhou divined three times for King Wu's recovery. King Zhou's tyranny brought no good omens even from large turtles. Duke Wen of Jin's divination for restoring King Zhou Xiang showed a good omen about the Yellow Emperor at Banquan, leading to success. Duke Xian of Jin's divination for attacking Li Rong was unlucky, causing disaster extending to five Jin kings. King Ling of Chu's divination for betraying Zhou was unlucky, leading to defeat. Turtle omens indicate inner trends visible externally. Gentlemen believe despising divination shows ignorance, while abandoning human plans for auspicious omens displeases ghosts and gods. The "Shangshu·Hongfan" proposes five objects for solving difficulties, with divination accounting for two.

Investigating south of the Yangtze River, the author learned turtles live a thousand years, walking on lotus leaves, while yarrow has hundreds of branches with one root. Where turtles grow, no tigers, wolves, or poisonous weeds exist. Riverside residents raise turtles, drinking blood and eating meat to unblock tendons and resist aging. Mr. Chu, proficient in Confucian classics and a Langguan, studied "Spring and Autumn" and served in the palace for over ten years. He liked "Tai Shi Gong Zhuan," which said Xia, Shang, and Zhou had different divination methods, and four ethnic groups had varying customs, all judging good and bad luck. Searching Chang'an City for "Biography of Guice" failed, so he visited Taidivination officials and elderly experts to compile divination records.

Ancient divination books describe Fuling growing under rabbit threads, with yarrow above and turtles below. Fuling, resembling a bird, grows under rabbit silk. After spring rain in calm weather, one can cut rabbit silk at night, use a lantern to illuminate, and mark the spot where the lantern extinguishes. Digging four to seven feet deep reveals Fuling, the root of thousand-year-old pine trees, granting immortality when eaten. Yarrow with a hundred branches has a divine turtle guarding below, shrouded by blue clouds. In peaceful times, yarrow grows one foot long with a hundred stems per clump. Today, finding such yarrow is difficult; those with over eighty stems and eight feet long are rare. People can use yarrow with over sixty stems and six feet long.

Famous turtles include eight types: Big Dipper, Nanchen, Five Star, Eight Winds, Twenty-Eight Stars, Sun and Moon, Kyushu, and Jade turtles. Ancient books have words on turtle undersides indicating their type. Finding such turtles doesn't require one foot two inches; folks treasure seven or eight inch turtles. Jewels and jade shine out their gods, similar to famous turtles bringing wealth. Jade in mountains moistens trees; pearls in deep pools prevent shore vegetation from withering. Bright moon pearls from rivers and seas, hidden in clams with dragons, can protect the world when obtained by kings. Those obtaining a hundred-stem yarrow with a divine turtle underneath can ask a hundred questions and determine good or bad luck.

Divine turtles come from the Yangtze River. Lujiang County delivers twenty live turtles one foot two inches long to Taifu officials annually, who pick off turtle underbellies on auspicious days. Turtles reach one foot two inches after a thousand years. Kings drilling tortoise shells on temples determine good or bad luck when dispatching troops. Gaozu Temple has a turtle room collecting tortoise shells as sacred treasures. Ancient books say wearing a turtle's forefoot arm bone and hanging a turtle in the northwest corner prevents losing one's way in deep mountains. The "Wan Bi Shi Zhu Fang" mentions a magical turtle in Jia Lin south of the Yangtze River, where no tigers, wolves, owls, poisonous weeds, or insects exist, and wild fire cannot burn it.

Turtles in Jia Lin build nests on fragrant lotus. Text under the turtle's left side says obtaining it can make one a ruler or emperor. Those seeking turtles in white snake forests fast respectfully, offer wine to gods, let hair hang down, and beg for three days and nights. An old man in the south used a turtle to prop his bed for over twenty years; after his death, the turtle was still alive when someone moved the bed. Turtles can move qi. When diviners get live turtles, they kill them to pick off plastrons. Someone along the Yangtze River raised a famous turtle, making his family rich. Discussing releasing it, someone advised killing it instead. The turtle begged in a dream not to be killed, but the family killed it, causing the owner's death and family misfortune.

Commoners and monarchs have different principles; commoners obtaining famous turtles shouldn't kill them. Ancient Ming Kings and Holy Lords always killed turtles for use. King Song and Yuan obtained a turtle, killed it, and used it. In the second year of King Song and Yuan's reign, the Yangtze River God sent a divine turtle to the Yellow River. At Quanyang, a fisherman named Yuqie caught it in a net and imprisoned it. At midnight, the turtle entrusted a dream to King Yuan, saying it was on a mission for the River God but was blocked by a fishnet. King Yuan woke in surprise and summoned Dr. Wei Ping, who used divination tools to determine the turtle's identity. Wei Ping calculated the positions of sun, moon, and stars, determining the turtle was imprisoned. King Yuan sent someone to Quanyang, found Yuqie, and obtained the turtle.

The turtle was brought to King Yuan, stretching its neck and crawling forward before retreating. Wei Ping explained the turtle expressed gratitude and wished to leave quickly. King Yuan marveled at the turtle's spirit and wanted to release it, but Wei Ping argued against it, saying the turtle was the world's most precious thing, capable of making its owner emperor and winning ten battles. Wei Ping described the turtle's divine nature: born in the abyss, knowing heaven's way, understanding ancient affairs, living three thousand years without leaving its area, changing color with seasons, and clearly distinguishing yin and yang. King Yuan hesitated, fearing cruelty and tyranny, comparing himself to Xia Jie and Shang Zhou. Wei Ping countered, citing examples of hard work and treasure acquisition, urging the king to keep the turtle.

King Yuan eventually accepted the turtle, choosing auspicious days to fast and sacrifice. He killed a white pheasant and black sheep, filled the turtle with blood, and dissected it without damaging the shell. After burning thorn branches for omens, the omen pattern appeared clearly. The fortune teller's predictions were appropriate, and the country gained valuable treasure. King Yuan made Wei Ping prime minister, and Song became the world's most powerful state due to the turtle's magical power. Confucius later commented that divine turtles know good and bad but have hollow skeletons, comparing them to the sun bullied by a three-legged crow and the moon bitten by a toad. He noted everything has imperfections, and heaven itself is not perfect.

Mr. Chu concluded that the fisherman caught the turtle, which entrusted a dream to King Yuan. Wei Ping used formulas to calculate and urged keeping the turtle as a national treasure. This was good, as ancient divination always praised turtles for their efficacy. The text then lists taboos and methods for divination, including specific days for killing turtles, washing procedures, and prayers for various inquiries like illness, imprisonment, property, travel, warfare, and weather. Different omens like Hengji'an, signs, head raised and feet raised, and others indicate specific outcomes for each type of question, with detailed interpretations for conditions, prisoners, travel, and other matters.
