Liu Chang, King Li of Huainan, was the youngest son of Emperor Gaozu of Han. His mother, a concubine of the former King of Zhao, was favored by Emperor Gaozu and became pregnant. After the King of Zhao was implicated in a plot against the emperor, she was imprisoned and, despite being pregnant, her pleas for help were ignored due to the emperor's anger and Empress Lü's jealousy. After giving birth to Liu Chang, she committed suicide. The emperor regretted this and had Empress Lü raise the child. Liu Chang was later made King of Huainan after suppressing a rebellion.

Growing up under Empress Lü's protection, Liu Chang escaped political disasters but harbored resentment against Marquis Piyang for not helping his mother. After Emperor Xiaowen ascended, Liu Chang became arrogant, calling the emperor "big brother" and violating laws. He eventually killed Marquis Piyang in revenge, claiming three crimes against him. The emperor pardoned him out of brotherly love, but Liu Chang grew more unruly, imitating imperial decrees and plotting rebellion.

In the sixth year of Emperor Xiaowen's reign, Liu Chang conspired with seventy men to rebel, contacting Minyue and Xiongnu. The plot was discovered, and he was summoned to Chang'an. Officials reported his numerous crimes, including forging imperial chariots, appointing unauthorized officials, harboring fugitives, and killing innocents. The emperor, unable to bear punishing him, deposed him and exiled him to Shu County. Liu Chang went on a hunger strike and died en route. The emperor later posthumously honored him as King Li and enfeoffed his sons.

Liu Chang's son, Liu An, became King of Huainan. He was cultured and sought to win over the people, but he resented his father's death and plotted rebellion. He befriended guests, accumulated weapons, and bribed officials. His daughter, Liu Ling, spied in Chang'an. The king's advisor, Wu Bei, warned against rebellion, citing the failures of Wu and Chu and the stability of the Han dynasty. Despite this, Liu An continued preparations, forging seals and planning to assassinate officials.

The plot was exposed when Liu An's grandson, Liu Jian, informed on the prince. The court investigated, and Liu An, fearing exposure, hesitated to act. His advisor Wu Bei again urged caution, but Liu An persisted. Eventually, the court arrested the conspirators, and Liu An committed suicide before his arrest. His queen and prince were executed, and Huainan was reduced to a commandery.

Liu An's brother, Liu Ci, King of Hengshan, also plotted rebellion. He feuded with his brother and prepared for war, forging documents and building weapons. His internal conflicts, including disputes over succession and adultery, led to his downfall. After his son Liu Xiao surrendered and revealed the plot, Liu Ci committed suicide. His kingdom was also abolished.

Sima Qian comments that despite their noble status, the kings of Huainan and Hengshan failed in their duties and plotted rebellion, leading to their ruin. Their faults were compounded by the influence of unruly subordinates and local customs.
