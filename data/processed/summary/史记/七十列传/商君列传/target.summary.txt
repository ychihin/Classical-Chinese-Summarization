Lord Shang, originally named Gongsun Yang, was the son of a concubine of the Kingdom of Wei. As a youth, he studied criminal law and magic, serving under his uncle, the Prime Minister of Wei. When his uncle fell ill, King Wei Hui visited and asked about a successor. The uncle recommended Gongsun Yang, but the king remained silent. The uncle then privately advised the king to kill Gongsun Yang if he was not appointed, to prevent him from leaving the country. However, Gongsun Yang refused to flee, reasoning that the king would not heed advice to kill him if he ignored advice to appoint him. After his uncle's death, Gongsun Yang heard that Duke Xiao of Qin sought talented individuals to restore Qin's former glory. He entered Qin through the recommendation of Duke Xiao's favored minister, Jing Jian.

Initially, Duke Xiao was unimpressed, falling asleep during discussions of ancient imperial methods. After several attempts, Gongsun Yang shifted to strategies for enriching the state and strengthening the army, which pleased Duke Xiao. Despite concerns about public opinion, Gongsun Yang convinced Duke Xiao to enact legal reforms, arguing that extraordinary actions often face criticism but are necessary for success. He was appointed as Zuo Shu Chang and implemented a new legal code, organizing households into mutual surveillance groups, rewarding military merit, punishing private disputes, and promoting agriculture. To build trust, he erected a log at the market and rewarded a man who moved it, demonstrating his commitment to the law.

The new law faced opposition, including from the crown prince. Gongsun Yang punished the prince's tutors, Qian and Gongsun Jia, with facial tattooing, after which the law was widely accepted. Over ten years, Qin prospered: lost items were not stolen, families became wealthy, and the people were courageous in public duty but avoided private conflicts. Gongsun Yang was promoted to Da Liang Zao and led campaigns against Wei, capturing Anyi. He later oversaw the construction of Xianyang, reorganized territories into counties, abolished well-fields, and standardized weights and measures.

After further victories, Gongsun Yang persuaded Duke Xiao to attack Wei, exploiting Wei's recent defeat by Qi. He tricked Wei's general, Gongzi Yan, into a meeting, captured him, and defeated the Wei army. King Wei Hui ceded land and regretted not heeding the uncle's advice. Gongsun Yang was granted fifteen cities and titled Lord Shang. As prime minister for ten years, he faced resentment from nobles. Zhao Liang warned him of his dangerous position, criticizing his reliance on harsh methods and lack of virtue, but Lord Shang ignored the advice.

Five months after Duke Xiao's death, the new prince ascended. Gongzi Qian accused Lord Shang of rebellion. Lord Shang fled but was turned away by innkeepers due to his own laws requiring identification. He escaped to Wei, but the Wei people, angry over his deception, returned him to Qin. He attempted to raise troops in his fiefdom but was captured and executed by dismemberment. His entire clan was killed. Sima Qian concluded that Lord Shang's cruel and ungrateful nature, reliance on deceit, and refusal to heed wise counsel led to his downfall.
