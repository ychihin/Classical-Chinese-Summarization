Wei Bao, son of the Wei Kingdom during the Six Kingdoms period, had his elder brother Wei Jiu named Lord Ningling. After Qin destroyed Wei, Wei Bao became a commoner. When Chen Sheng rebelled, Wei Jiu followed him. Chen Sheng sent Zhou Shi to seize Wei land; after capturing it, others wanted Zhou Shi as king, but he insisted on supporting Wei descendants. Qi and Zhao sent chariots to back Zhou Shi, who refused and fetched Wei Jiu from Chen County. Chen Sheng agreed, making Wei Jiu King of Wei. Zhang Han defeated Chen Sheng, attacked Wei, and killed Zhou Shi. Wei Jiu surrendered to save his people, then set himself on fire. Wei Bao fled to Chu, received troops from King Huai of Chu, and captured over 20 cities. Xiang Yu made Wei Bao King of Wei, then moved him to Hedong as King of Western Wei. Wei Bao surrendered to the King of Han, followed him to attack Chu, but later betrayed him by cutting off the Yellow River crossing. The King of Han sent Li Sheng to persuade him, but Wei Bao refused, citing the King of Han's arrogance. Han Xin captured Wei Bao, who was later killed by Zhou Ke during a Chu siege.

Peng Yue, from Changyi, fished and robbed in Juye Lake. After Chen Sheng and Xiang Liang rose, youths urged him to lead, but he delayed. Over a year later, over a hundred youths insisted, and he agreed, executing a latecomer to enforce discipline. He gathered followers and assisted Peigong (Liu Bang) in attacking Changyi. After Xiang Ji divided the lords, Peng Yue's army grew to over 10,000. In the first year of Han, Qi's King Tian Rong gave him a seal to attack Chu. Peng Yue defeated Chu's Xiao Gongjiao. In the second year, he joined the King of Han with 30,000 troops and was made Prime Minister of Wei. After the King of Han's defeat, Peng Yue stationed along the Yellow River, harassing Chu's supply lines. In the fourth year, he captured 17 cities, but Xiang Ji recovered them. In the fifth year, he seized grain for the King of Han. Following Zhang Liang's advice, the King of Han promised Peng Yue land to become king, leading him to join the Han army at Gaixia, defeating Chu. Peng Yue was made King of Liang. In the tenth year, Chen Xi rebelled; Peng Yue feigned illness, angering Emperor Gao. Accused of conspiracy, he was arrested, pardoned to commoner status, and exiled. Empress Lu, fearing his influence, had him executed and his family exterminated.

Tai Shigong comments: Though Wei Bao and Peng Yue rose from poverty to become kings, their failure to die for honor and willingness to be prisoners led to their deaths. Their endurance of humiliation stemmed from superior strategy and fear for their lives, hoping to seize power as circumstances changed.
