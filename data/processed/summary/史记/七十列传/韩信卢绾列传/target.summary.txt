King Han Xin of Han, a concubine grandson of the original King Xiang of Han, was a tall man. Initially, after Xiang Liang's defeat, Han Cheng was made King of Han to appease the region. Han Xin later served under Pei Gong (future Emperor Gaozu), persuading him to march eastward. He was eventually made King of Han after capturing Korean cities and defeating rival King Zheng Chang. However, fearing execution due to suspicions of betrayal, Han Xin allied with the Xiongnu, rebelled, and attacked Taiyuan. Emperor Gaozu personally defeated him, and Han Xin fled to the Xiongnu. He continued to lead Xiongnu forces against Han until General Chai killed him in battle. His descendants later surrendered to Han and were granted marquisates, though some titles were eventually revoked.

Lu Wan, a close childhood friend and neighbor of Emperor Gaozu, was exceptionally favored, serving as a general and Taiwei. After the empire was pacified, he was made King of Yan, the most favored among non-Liu princes. During Chen Xi's rebellion, Lu Wan sent Zhang Sheng to the Xiongnu, who secretly aided Chen Xi. Lu Wan later realized this and continued to prolong the war. When Emperor Gaozu discovered Lu Wan's collusion with Chen Xi and the Xiongnu, he sent Fan Kuai to attack Yan. Lu Wan fled to the Xiongnu, becoming King of Donghulu, but always wished to return. After Gaozu's death, Lu Wan died among the Xiongnu. His family later returned to Han, but his wife died before meeting Empress Gao. His grandson eventually surrendered and was made a marquis.

Chen Xi, a marquis and prime minister of Zhao, commanded border troops. Suspected by Zhou Chang, he feared investigation and rebelled with Wang Huang, proclaiming himself King of Dai. Emperor Gaozu personally campaigned, pardoning many officials and rewarding Zhao generals. The Han army defeated Chen Xi's forces, killing his generals and eventually Chen Xi himself in Lingqiu.

The Grand Historian comments that Han Xin and Lu Wan, lacking accumulated virtue, succeeded through deception and violence during the Han's founding. Suspected internally and relying on foreigners, they were alienated and forced to surrender to the Xiongnu—a tragic fate. Chen Xi, admiring Lord Xinling, gathered guests and exceeded his reputation. Zhou Chang's suspicions, fear of disaster, and treacherous whispers led to his rebellion. Thus, the success or failure of plans profoundly impacts a person's destiny.
