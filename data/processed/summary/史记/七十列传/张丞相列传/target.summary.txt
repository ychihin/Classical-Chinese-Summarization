The text details the lives and careers of several prominent officials in the early Han Dynasty, focusing on their roles, achievements, and interactions with emperors.

Prime Minister Zhang Cang, from Yangwu, was a scholar of books, music, and calendars. After serving as a Qin censor, he fled home due to a crime. He later joined Pei Gong (Emperor Gaozu) and was saved from execution by Wang Ling, who was impressed by his appearance. Zhang Cang served in various posts, including governor and prime minister of several states, and was granted the title of Marquis of Beiping. He became chief accountant and later imperial censor. As prime minister, he revised music and calendars, adhering to the Qin calendar and the virtue of water. He lived to over a hundred years old, relying on wet nurses in his old age.

Zhou Chang, from Peixian, served as a soldier under Pei Gong and became a lieutenant. After his cousin Zhou Ke was killed by Xiang Yu for refusing to surrender, Zhou Chang became imperial censor. He was known for his bluntness, once comparing Emperor Gaozu to tyrants. He opposed the emperor's plan to depose the crown prince, earning gratitude from Empress Lu. Later, he was appointed prime minister of Zhao to protect the young King Ruyi, despite his reluctance. After Gaozu's death, Empress Lu summoned King Zhao to court, leading to his death. Zhou Chang then feigned illness and died three years later.

Zhao Yao, a young censor, advised Gaozu to appoint Zhou Chang as Zhao's prime minister. He later became imperial censor and was named Marquis of Jiangyi. After Gaozu's death, Empress Lu stripped him of his title for his role in protecting King Zhao. Ren Ao, a former jailer who aided Empress Lu, succeeded him as censor and was named Marquis of Guang'a. He was later dismissed, and Cao Cheng briefly served as censor before Zhang Cang was reappointed.

Shen Tujia, from Liang, was a warrior who followed Emperor Gaozu and became prime minister under Emperor Wen. He was known for his honesty and strict adherence to court etiquette. He once ordered the execution of the favored courtier Deng Tong for disrespect, but Emperor Wen intervened. As prime minister, he clashed with Chao Cuo, who changed laws and cut through a temple wall. Shen Tujia sought to execute Chao Cuo but was thwarted by Emperor Jing. He died of anger after being outmaneuvered.

The text also lists later prime ministers, including Che Qianqiu, Wei Xian, Wei Xiang, Bing Ji, Huang Ba, Yu Dingguo, Wei Xuancheng, and Kuang Heng. Many were scholars who rose through official ranks, with some losing their titles for crimes like riding into temples. The historian notes that while some achieved high office through fate, others schemed in vain, and many talented individuals remained unrecognized.
