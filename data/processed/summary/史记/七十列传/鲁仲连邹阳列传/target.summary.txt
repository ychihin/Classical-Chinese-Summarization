Lu Zhonglian, a native of Qi, was skilled in grand strategies but refused to serve as an official, valuing integrity. During the siege of Handan by Qin, Zhao sought aid. Wei sent General Jin Bi, who hesitated, and envoy Xinyuan Yan proposed Zhao honor King Zhao of Qin as emperor to secure peace. Pingyuan Jun hesitated. Lu Zhonglian confronted Xinyuan Yan, arguing that submitting to Qin would bring disaster. He cited historical examples, such as King Wei of Qi’s humiliation by Zhou and the fates of Jiuhou and Ehou under Yin Zhou, to illustrate the dangers of imperial ambition. Lu declared he would rather die than serve Qin. Xinyuan Yan was convinced and withdrew his proposal. The Qin army retreated after Wei’s Prince Wuji rescued Zhao. Pingyuan Jun offered Lu a reward, but he refused, stating that a worthy person resolves conflicts without seeking payment, and left forever.

Over twenty years later, a Yan general held Qi’s Liaocheng. Lu Zhonglian wrote him a letter, urging him to choose wisely: return to Yan or surrender to Qi. He argued that clinging to a besieged city was unwise, citing Guan Zhong and Cao Mo as examples of those who overcame disgrace to achieve greatness. The general, torn between fear of execution and shame, committed suicide, leading to Qi’s capture of Liaocheng. Qi’s king offered Lu a title, but he fled to the seaside, preferring poverty to serving others.

Zou Yang, also from Qi, served King Xiao of Liang but was slandered by rivals and imprisoned. From prison, he wrote a letter defending his loyalty, citing historical figures like Bian He, Li Si, and Bigan, who suffered unjustly. He argued that true understanding between ruler and subject transcends slander, as seen with Su Qin and Yan, or Baili Xi and Duke Mu of Qin. He warned that listening to flattery leads to ruin, as with Lu and Song. King Xiao released him, and Zou became his honored guest.

Sima Qian comments that while Lu Zhonglian’s arguments may not be entirely righteous, his independence and ability to sway powerful figures are admirable. Zou Yang’s words, though immodest, are sincere and compelling, earning him a place in this biography.
