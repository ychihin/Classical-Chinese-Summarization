Zhang Shizhi, a Tingwei from Duyang, served Emperor Wen of Han but remained unknown for a decade. Yuan Ang recommended him as a visitor. Zhang advised the emperor on practical governance, discussing Qin's fall and Han's rise. Emperor Wen appointed him as an emissary. During a visit to a tiger enclosure, a keeper answered questions fluently while the Shanglin Lieutenant faltered. The emperor wanted to promote the keeper, but Zhang warned against valuing eloquence over substance, citing Qin's decline due to such practices. The emperor agreed and canceled the promotion.

Zhang later stopped the prince and King Liang from entering the palace without dismounting, reporting them for disrespect. The empress dowager pardoned them, and Emperor Wen promoted Zhang to Zhonglang General. At Ba Ling, the emperor lamented about his coffin, but Zhang argued that greed, not the coffin, determines security. The emperor praised him.

As Tingwei, Zhang judged a man who startled the emperor's horse. The emperor wanted severe punishment, but Zhang insisted on the legal fine, stating the law must be trusted. The emperor relented. When someone stole from Gaozu Temple, the emperor demanded execution of the entire family, but Zhang argued the law prescribed only death for the thief. The emperor agreed after consulting the empress dowager.

After Emperor Wen's death, Zhang feared Emperor Jing and sought Wang Sheng's advice. Wang humiliated Zhang in court by asking him to tie his garters, which elevated Zhang's reputation. Zhang later served Emperor Jing but was demoted and died.

Feng Tang, known for filial piety, served Emperor Wen. He compared Zhao generals Li Qi, Lian Po, and Li Mu, stating the emperor could not use them due to strict laws. He cited Wei Shang, a governor punished for minor errors despite his success against the Xiongnu. Emperor Wen pardoned Wei Shang and appointed Feng Tang as chariot and cavalry captain. Under Emperor Jing, Feng became prime minister of Chu but was dismissed. Under Emperor Wu, he was recommended but too old to serve; his son Feng Sui became an official.

The historian comments that Zhang Shizhi's integrity and Feng Tang's insights on generalship are admirable, aligning with the principle of impartial governance.
