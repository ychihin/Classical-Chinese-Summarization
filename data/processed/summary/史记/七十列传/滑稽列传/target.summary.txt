Confucius stated that the Six Classics serve the same purpose in governing a country: "Li" regulates conduct, "Music" promotes harmony, "Book" records past deeds, "Poetry" expresses emotions, "Yi" explores cosmic changes, and "Spring and Autumn" discerns subtle meanings and right from wrong. Tai Shigong remarked on the vastness of truth, noting that concise words can resolve many troubles.

Chunyu Kun, a short, witty son-in-law of Qi, often served as an envoy without disgrace. During King Wei of Qi's reign, the king neglected state affairs, indulging in feasting and drinking, leading to invasions and national peril. No minister dared to advise. Chunyu Kun used an allegory of a great bird that neither flew nor cried for three years. King Wei understood, summoned all county chiefs, rewarded one, executed another, and repelled invaders, restoring Qi's prestige for 36 years. Later, when Chu invaded, Chunyu Kun was sent to Zhao for reinforcements with modest gifts. He laughed at a farmer praying for abundant harvests with meager offerings, prompting King Wei to increase the gifts. Zhao then sent 100,000 elite troops, causing Chu to withdraw. King Wei asked Chunyu Kun about his drinking capacity. Chunyu Kun explained that his drunkenness varied by context: fear made him drunk after one measure, while joy allowed him to drink eight measures. He warned against excess, as it leads to decline. King Wei ceased nightly drinking and appointed Chunyu Kun as a guest official.

Over a century later, You Meng, a tall Chu performer, used humor to advise King Zhuang. When the king wanted to bury his beloved horse with grand rites, You Meng suggested an even grander burial to expose the king's folly. King Zhuang relented and had the horse cooked. Later, You Meng impersonated the deceased prime minister Sun Shuao to advise King Zhuang. He highlighted Sun Shuao's poverty despite his integrity, leading the king to grant Sun's son land and households for ancestral worship.

Two centuries later, You Zhan, a short Qin performer, used wit to aid others. During a rainstorm, he joked about guards being wet while he was dry, leading Qin Shihuang to halve their duty. He also dissuaded the emperor from expanding hunting grounds and Qin Ershi from painting city walls, using humor to highlight impracticality.

Tai Shigong praised these three for their clever advice. Mr. Chu Shaosun added six more tales, including that of Guo Sheren, who helped a wet nurse avoid exile by prompting her to show reluctance, moving Emperor Wu to pity. Dongfang Shuo, a learned scholar, used humor to gain favor with Emperor Wu, often receiving rewards but spending them on marriages. He defended his behavior as "court reclusion" and advised against slander on his deathbed. Mr. Dong Guo advised General Wei Qing to gift gold to Empress Wang's parents, earning himself an official post. Other tales include a clever envoy who lost a swan but gained rewards, a prefect coached by a drunkard to credit the emperor for peace, and Ximen Bao, who stopped the practice of sacrificing girls to the River God by throwing the corrupt priests into the river, then dug irrigation canals that benefited the people for generations.
