Lord Xinling, named Wei Wuji, was the youngest son of King Wei Zhao and half-brother of King Wei Anli. After King Zhao's death, King Anli enfeoffed him as Lord Xinling. Fan Sui, who fled to Qin and became prime minister, resented Wei's mistreatment and led Qin forces to besiege Daliang, defeating Wei troops at Huayang. The king and lord were deeply troubled.

The lord was generous, humble, and virtuous, attracting three thousand retainers from across the realm. For over a decade, no feudal lords dared invade Wei due to his reputation. Once, while playing chess with the king, a false alarm about a Zhao invasion arose. The lord correctly identified it as a hunting expedition, revealing he had a spy in Zhao's court. This frightened the king, who thereafter feared the lord's abilities and refused him state affairs.

The lord befriended Hou Ying, a seventy-year-old gatekeeper, by personally driving his carriage to fetch him for a banquet. Hou Ying tested the lord's humility by sitting in the honored seat and visiting a friend, Zhu Hai, in the market. The lord remained respectful, winning Hou Ying's loyalty. Hou Ying later recommended Zhu Hai as a capable man.

In the twentieth year of King Anli's reign, Qin besieged Handan, Zhao's capital. The lord's sister, married to Zhao's Lord Pingyuan, begged Wei for aid. King Wei sent General Jin Bi with 100,000 troops but halted at Ye, fearing Qin's threats. Despite the lord's pleas, the king refused to advance. Determined to die with Zhao, the lord gathered retainers and chariots to attack Qin.

Passing through the east gate, the lord informed Hou Ying, who offered no farewell. Suspicious, the lord returned. Hou Ying explained the futility of a direct assault and proposed stealing Jin Bi's military tally from the king's favored concubine, Ruji, whom the lord had previously helped. The lord obtained the tally through Ruji. Hou Ying also advised taking Zhu Hai to kill Jin Bi if he resisted. The lord wept, foreseeing Jin Bi's death.

At Ye, Jin Bi verified the tally but refused to surrender command. Zhu Hai killed him with a hidden iron mallet. The lord reorganized the army, sending home fathers, elder brothers, and only sons, then led 80,000 elite troops to break the Qin siege, saving Handan. Hou Ying fulfilled his promise by cutting his own throat.

Fearing the king's wrath, the lord remained in Zhao. King Zhao offered five cities, but the lord, humbled by a retainer's rebuke, refused credit. He later befriended recluses Mao Gong and Xue Gong, angering Lord Pingyuan, who deemed them unworthy. The lord defended his actions, causing many of Pingyuan's retainers to defect to him.

After ten years, Qin attacked Wei. The king begged the lord's return. Mao and Xue convinced him to go back. The lord became general, rallied five allied states, and defeated Qin at the Yellow River, pursuing them to Hangu Pass. His fame spread, and he compiled "Wei Gongzi's Art of War."

Qin bribed slanderers to claim the lord aimed for kingship. The king believed them and replaced him. The lord feigned illness, indulged in wine and women, and died four years later. King Anli died the same year. Qin then conquered twenty Wei cities and eventually destroyed Wei.

Emperor Gaozu of Han, who admired the lord, ordered five families to guard his tomb and offer seasonal sacrifices. Sima Qian noted the lord's unique ability to befriend humble folk, ensuring his lasting renown.
