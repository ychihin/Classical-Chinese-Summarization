Liu Jing, a native of Qi, advised Emperor Gao of Han against establishing the capital in Luoyang, arguing that the Han Dynasty's rise differed from the Zhou Dynasty's gradual accumulation of virtue over generations. He noted that the Zhou built Luoyang as a central capital during their peak, but their decline showed that a weak situation, not just virtue, led to loss of control. In contrast, the Han emerged from warfare, with countless battles and suffering, making it unsuitable to emulate the Zhou. Liu Jing advocated for capitalizing in Guanzhong, protected by mountains and the Yellow River, with fertile land and a strategic position to "grab the world by its throat." Despite opposition from ministers favoring Luoyang, Zhang Liang's support led Emperor Gao to establish the capital in Guanzhong, rewarding Liu Jing with a surname change to Liu, the title of doctor, and the name Fengchun Jun.

Later, in the seventh year of Emperor Gao, Liu Jing was sent to scout the Xiongnu before a campaign against the rebellious King Xin of Han. He warned that the Xiongnu's display of weak soldiers and livestock was a ruse to ambush the Han army, advising against attack. Emperor Gao ignored this, imprisoned Liu Jing, and marched to Pingcheng, where he was besieged for seven days. After escaping, he released Liu Jing, rewarded him with 2,000 households, and granted him the title of Marquis of Guannei. To address Xiongnu threats, Liu Jing proposed a peace marriage alliance, suggesting Emperor Gao marry his eldest princess to Maodun Chanyu to ensure future submission through kinship. Emperor Gao agreed but, due to Empress Lu's protests, sent a palace maid instead, with Liu Jing negotiating the treaty.

Upon returning, Liu Jing warned that Xiongnu tribes near Chang'an posed a threat and recommended strengthening Guanzhong's population by moving powerful clans from Qi, Chu, and other states, as well as wealthy families, to bolster defenses against both the Xiongnu and potential rebellions. Emperor Gao approved, and Liu Jing relocated over 100,000 people.

Shusun Tong, a scholar from Xue County, served under Qin II, where he flattered the emperor by dismissing a rebellion as mere thievery, earning rewards but fleeing after Qin II punished those who spoke truthfully. He later served Xiang Liang, King Huai of Chu, and Xiang Yu before surrendering to Liu Bang, the King of Han. Disliking Confucian robes, Shusun Tong adopted Chu-style short coats, pleasing the king. He recommended warriors over his own disciples, explaining that the king needed fighters, not scholars, during wartime. After the Han unification, Emperor Gao abolished strict Qin rituals, leading to chaotic court behavior. Shusun Tong proposed creating a simplified court etiquette, blending ancient and Qin practices. He recruited 30 Confucian scholars, despite two refusing, and practiced for a month. In the seventh year, the Changle Palace ceremony impressed Emperor Gao, who remarked, "Only today do I know the dignity of being an emperor." Shusun Tong was made Taichang, rewarded with gold, and his disciples received official posts.

As crown prince tutor in the ninth year, Shusun Tong opposed Emperor Gao's plan to replace Crown Prince Liu Ying with Zhao Wang Liu Ruyi, citing historical disasters from such actions. He emphasized the prince's virtue and Empress Lu's shared hardships, threatening death if the change proceeded. Emperor Gao relented, especially after seeing four scholars supporting the prince. Under Emperor Xiaohui, Shusun Tong served as Taichang, formulating ancestral temple rituals. He advised against a pavilion road built over the Gaodi Temple passage, suggesting a new temple instead, and introduced cherry offerings to the temple. Sima Qian concluded that Liu Jing's strategic advice and Shusun Tong's adaptable ritual-making were crucial to the Han's stability, highlighting their unique contributions.
