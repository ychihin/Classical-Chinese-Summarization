Li Shiqi, a learned man from Gaoyang, Chenliu, lived in poverty and worked as a lowly clerk. Despite his humble status, local elites dared not employ him casually, calling him "Kangsheng." During the rebellions against Qin, he avoided serving narrow-minded generals until he heard of Pei Gong (Liu Bang). Through a cavalryman, he arranged a meeting. When they met, Pei Gong was washing his feet with two women, but Li Shiqi criticized his disrespect, prompting Pei Gong to apologize. Li Shiqi advised capturing Chenliu for its strategic location and grain stores, offering to persuade the magistrate to surrender. Pei Gong agreed, and with Li Shiqi's help, Chenliu was taken. Li Shiqi was titled Lord of Guangye and later recommended his brother Li Shang to serve Pei Gong.

During the Chu-Han conflict, Li Shiqi advised the King of Han to hold Aocang's grain and key fortresses instead of retreating. He then volunteered to persuade the King of Qi to submit to Han. The King of Han agreed. Li Shiqi convinced King Tian Guang of Qi that the world's hearts turned to Han due to Han's generosity and Xiang Yu's failures. Tian Guang surrendered and removed defenses. However, Han Xin, jealous of Li Shiqi's success, attacked Qi by night. Tian Guang, believing Li Shiqi betrayed him, threatened to boil him alive. Li Shiqi refused to lobby Han Xin and was executed. Later, Emperor Gaozu enfeoffed Li Shiqi's son as Marquis of Gaoliang, a title passed for three generations until it was revoked.

Lu Jia, a Chu native and eloquent lobbyist, served Emperor Gaozu as an envoy. He was sent to appoint Wei Tuo as King of South Vietnam. Lu Jia criticized Wei Tuo's barbarian customs and warned that Han could destroy him. Wei Tuo apologized, acknowledged Han's superiority, and submitted. Lu Jia returned, was appointed Taizhong doctor, and wrote "New Speech" at the emperor's request, discussing statecraft. During Empress Dowager Lü's reign, he retired, distributed wealth to his sons, and traveled. He later advised Chancellor Chen Ping to ally with General Zhou Bo against the Lü clan, using gifts to secure their cooperation. After the Lü clan's fall, Lu Jia was sent to South Vietnam again, succeeding in enforcing Han rituals. He died of old age.

Pingyuanjun (Zhu Jian), a Chu native, served Yingbu but opposed his rebellion. After Yingbu's defeat, he was spared. He was upright, refusing to associate with the corrupt Piyanghou. When Pingyuanjun's mother died, Lu Jia advised Piyanghou to give generous funeral gifts, winning his favor. Later, when Piyanghou was imprisoned, Pingyuanjun persuaded the emperor's favorite Hong Jiru to intercede, securing his release. After Empress Dowager Lü's death, Pingyuanjun helped save Piyanghou again. Under Emperor Wen, Pingyuanjun was implicated in Piyanghou's crimes and committed suicide to protect his family. His son was appointed Taizhong doctor but died on a mission to the Xiongnu.

An alternate account describes Li Shiqi's earlier meeting with Pei Gong, where he insisted on being called a "drunkard" rather than a scholar, criticized Pei Gong's disrespect, and again advised capturing Chenliu. He killed the magistrate at night, enabling Pei Gong to take the city and raise troops. Sima Qian notes that later accounts misplace Li Shiqi's role, but he confirms the details through his friendship with Pingyuanjun's son.
