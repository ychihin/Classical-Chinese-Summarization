The text recounts the deeds of five assassins from ancient China, spanning over four centuries. It begins with Cao Mo of Lu, a general who, after military defeats against Qi, threatened Duke Huan of Qi with a dagger during a peace alliance. Cao Mo forced the duke to promise the return of all occupied Lu lands. Though the duke initially wanted to break the promise, his advisor Guan Zhong convinced him that keeping his word was crucial for maintaining credibility among the feudal lords, and the lands were returned.

One hundred and sixty-seven years later, Zhuan Zhu of Wu was recommended to Prince Guang by Wu Zixu. Prince Guang believed he was the rightful heir to the Wu throne, which had passed through brothers instead of to him. He plotted to kill King Liao of Wu. During a banquet, Zhuan Zhu hid a dagger inside a grilled fish and assassinated the king. Prince Guang's warriors then killed the king's guards, and Prince Guang became King Helu of Wu, rewarding Zhuan Zhu's son.

Seventy years later, Yu Rang of Jin sought to avenge his master Zhibo, who was killed by Zhao Xiangzi. Yu Rang first attempted assassination while disguised as a prisoner repairing a toilet, but was discovered and released. He then disfigured himself by painting his skin and swallowing charcoal to change his appearance. He hid under a bridge, but Zhao Xiangzi's horse was startled, revealing him. Zhao Xiangzi confronted him, and Yu Rang explained that Zhibo treated him as a national scholar, so he must repay him accordingly. He asked to stab Zhao Xiangzi's robe to fulfill his revenge, then committed suicide.

More than forty years later, Nie Zheng of Zhi was sought out by Yan Zhongzi, who had a grudge against the Korean prime minister Xia Lei. Nie Zheng initially refused because his mother was alive, but after her death, he agreed. He went alone to the Korean court, killed Xia Lei, and then disfigured his own face before dying to protect his family. His sister Nie Rong identified his body, proclaiming his honor, and died of grief beside him.

Two hundred and twenty years later, Jing Ke of Wei was recruited by Prince Dan of Yan to assassinate the King of Qin. Jing Ke gained the king's trust by presenting the head of a fugitive general and a map of Yan territory. During the audience, he attempted to stab the king but failed, being wounded and killed. The king then attacked Yan, eventually destroying it. Jing Ke's friend Gao Jianli later attempted to assassinate the First Emperor with a weighted lute but also failed and was executed. The text concludes with the historian's commentary, affirming that despite varying outcomes, these assassins' intentions were clear and their reputations endure.
