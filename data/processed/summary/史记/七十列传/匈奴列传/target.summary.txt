The Xiongnu, whose ancestor was Chunwei, a descendant of the Xia dynasty, were a nomadic people living in the northern wilds. They moved with their livestock—horses, cattle, sheep, camels, and donkeys—in search of water and pasture, without permanent cities or agriculture. They had no written language, relying on oral agreements. From childhood, they learned archery and riding, and all men were armored cavalry. Their customs prioritized warfare and plunder, favoring attack when advantageous and retreating without shame. They respected the strong and disdained the weak, practiced levirate marriage, and had no taboo against using personal names. Their leaders were called Chanyu, and they held seasonal gatherings for sacrifices and censuses. Laws were harsh, with punishments for murder and theft, and burial practices included human sacrifice for the Chanyu.

Historically, the Xiongnu and their predecessors, such as the Shanrong and Xunzhu, interacted with Chinese dynasties. During the Zhou dynasty, King Mu of Zhou campaigned against the Dog Rong, and later, King You of Zhou was killed by the Quanrong, leading to the Zhou capital's eastward move. The Qin dynasty, under Qin Shihuang, sent Meng Tian to drive the Xiongnu from the Ordos region and built the Great Wall. After Qin's fall, the Xiongnu regained strength under Chanyu Touman, who was killed by his son Modun. Modun, after training his troops with a signaling arrow, usurped power and expanded Xiongnu territory, defeating the Donghu, Yuezhi, and other tribes, and amassing over 300,000 cavalry. He established a hierarchical structure with left and right kings, generals, and officials, and his rule marked the Xiongnu's peak, making them a major threat to the Han dynasty.

The Han dynasty, after its founding, faced constant Xiongnu raids. Emperor Gaozu was besieged at Pingcheng by Modun but escaped through bribery and a peace treaty involving marriage and tribute. Subsequent rulers, including Empress Dowager Lu and Emperor Wen, maintained this policy despite Xiongnu provocations. The Xiongnu, under Chanyu Laoshang and later Junchen, continued to raid border regions, while Han envoys like Zhonghang Yue, who defected to the Xiongnu, advised them to resist Han cultural influence. The Han dynasty alternated between appeasement and military campaigns, with Emperor Wu launching large-scale offensives led by generals Wei Qing and Huo Qubing, who defeated the Xiongnu in the Ordos and Gobi, capturing key territories and forcing them northward.

Despite these victories, the Xiongnu remained resilient. After Huo Qubing's death, the Han halted major campaigns, and the Xiongnu, under Chanyu Uwei and his successors, continued to raid and negotiate. The Han attempted to sow discord among Xiongnu leaders and used diplomacy with western states like Wusun to isolate them. However, internal strife and harsh winters weakened the Xiongnu. Later Chanyu, such as Er Chanyu and Huyan, faced rebellions and military defeats, including the capture of Han general Li Ling and the defection of Li Guangli. The Han built fortifications and expanded into the Hexi Corridor, but the Xiongnu persisted in their raids, leading to a prolonged stalemate.

The historian Sima Qian notes that the Xiongnu's nomadic lifestyle and martial culture made them formidable adversaries. He criticizes Han officials for short-sighted policies and emphasizes the importance of selecting capable generals, as even wise rulers like Yao needed talented ministers to achieve success. The Xiongnu's lineage, from Chunwei to Modun, spanned over a thousand years, with their power fluctuating until Modun unified them. Their social structure, with hereditary titles and a focus on warfare, allowed them to dominate the northern steppes and challenge Chinese authority for centuries.
