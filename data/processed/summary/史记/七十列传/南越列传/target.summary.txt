### Summary of the Historical Text on Nanyue (Nam Viet)

The king of Nanyue, Wei Tuo (Zhao Tuo), was from Zhending. During the Qin dynasty, after conquering the six states, the Qin annexed Yang Yue, establishing Guilin, Nanhai, and Xiangjun, and resettled criminals there to live with the Yue people for thirteen years. Zhao Tuo served as the magistrate of Longchuan County in Nanhai. In the second year of the Qin dynasty, Ren Xiao, the captain of Nanhai, fell ill. He summoned Zhao Tuo and explained that with Chen Sheng’s rebellion and the Qin’s cruel policies, the Central Plains were in chaos. He noted that Nanhai was remote and could be defended by mountains and sea, and suggested Zhao Tuo could establish a state. Ren Xiao appointed Zhao Tuo as acting captain. After Ren Xiao’s death, Zhao Tuo cut off roads, gathered troops, killed Qin-appointed officials, and replaced them with his cronies. Following the Qin’s fall, he annexed Guilin and Xiangjun and declared himself King Wu of Nanyue.

Emperor Gaozu of Han, due to the people’s exhaustion, spared Zhao Tuo. In the eleventh year of Gaozu, he sent Lu Jia to confirm Zhao Tuo as King of Nanyue, with an agreement for envoys and harmony with Baiyue. During Empress Lü’s reign, officials banned ironware trade with Nanyue. Zhao Tuo suspected King Changsha’s influence, declared himself Emperor Wu of Nanyue, and attacked Changsha’s border cities. Empress Lü sent General Zhou Zao to attack, but heat and illness halted the army. After her death, the Han stopped. Zhao Tuo expanded his territory to over 10,000 miles, bribing Minyue, Xiou, and Luoyue, and acted as an emperor.

In the first year of Emperor Wen of Han, the emperor honored Zhao Tuo’s parents’ tomb and his cousins. He sent Lu Jia to rebuke Zhao Tuo for claiming the emperor title. Zhao Tuo apologized, citing fear of King Changsha and the queen’s actions, and claimed he used the title for self-comfort among barbarians. He pledged vassalage to Han, removed imperial trappings, and continued as a vassal. During Emperor Jing’s era, Zhao Tuo outwardly submitted but secretly kept the emperor title. He died in the fourth year of Jianyuan, succeeded by his grandson Zhao Hu.

Zhao Hu faced an attack from Minyue’s King Ying. He requested Han intervention, and Emperor Wu sent troops, leading to Ying’s death. Zhao Hu thanked the emperor but, advised by ministers, avoided visiting the capital, fearing captivity. He died as King Wen. His son Yingqi, who had married a woman from Handan and fathered Zhao Xing, became king. Yingqi hid his grandfather’s emperor seal, refused to visit the capital due to fear of Han laws, and died as King Ming. Zhao Xing succeeded, with his mother as queen mother.

The queen mother, who had previously committed adultery with Anguo Shaoji, sought to submit to Han. Emperor Wu sent Anguo Shaoji to persuade them. The young king and queen mother agreed to become vassals, but Prime Minister Lu Jia, who had served three kings and held great influence, opposed. He feared losing power and plotted rebellion. At a banquet, the queen mother tried to provoke the Han envoy to kill Lu Jia, but the envoy hesitated. Lu Jia left, gathered troops, and refused to meet the king. The queen mother’s efforts failed.

The emperor sent Han Qianqiu with 2,000 troops to South Vietnam. Lu Jia rebelled, killed the king, queen mother, and Han envoys, and installed Zhao Jiande as king. Han Qianqiu’s army was ambushed and destroyed. The emperor then launched a large-scale attack in the fifth year of Yuanding, with generals Lu Bode, Yang Pu, and others leading forces from multiple directions. In the sixth year, Yang Pu’s troops captured Panyu, and Lu Bode’s forces accepted surrenders. Lu Jia and Zhao Jiande fled by sea but were captured. South Vietnam was pacified, and the Han established nine counties. The kingdom lasted five generations for ninety-three years. The historian notes that Zhao Tuo’s rise was due to Ren Xiao, and the kingdom’s fall stemmed from internal conflicts and Lu Jia’s actions.
