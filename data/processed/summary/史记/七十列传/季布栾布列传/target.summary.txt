Ji Bu, a man from Chu known for his ambition and chivalry, served under Xiang Yu and frequently embarrassed Liu Bang, the King of Han. After Xiang Yu's death, Emperor Gaozu of Han offered a thousand gold for Ji Bu's capture and punished anyone who harbored him. Ji Bu hid with the Zhou family in Puyang, who shaved his head, bound him with an iron hoop, and sold him as a slave to the Zhu family of Lu. The Zhu family, recognizing Ji Bu, treated him well and arranged for him to work in the fields. Zhu Jia then visited Ruyin Marquis Teng Gong in Luoyang, persuading him that pursuing Ji Bu out of personal resentment would harm the emperor's reputation and potentially drive Ji Bu to flee to the Huns or Yue. Teng Gong relayed this to the emperor, who pardoned Ji Bu. Ji Bu was later appointed as a doctor and served as Zhonglang General under Emperor Hui. When the Xiongnu insulted Empress Lu, General Fan Kuai proposed a military campaign, but Ji Bu condemned him, citing past failures and the risk of unrest, leading Empress Lu to abandon the plan. Ji Bu became Governor of Hedong County. During Emperor Wen's reign, he was summoned for a potential promotion but was sent back due to slander. Ji Bu criticized the emperor for being swayed by praise and slander, embarrassing him. A Chu man named Cao Qiu, known for rhetoric, sought Ji Bu's favor despite initial rejection. Ji Bu eventually welcomed him after Cao Qiu praised his reputation, and Cao Qiu helped spread Ji Bu's fame. Ji Bu's brother Ji Xin was also renowned for bravery and chivalry. Ji Bu's uncle Ding Gong, a former Chu general, was executed by Emperor Gaozu for disloyalty. Luan Bu, a friend of Liang King Peng Yue, was captured after Peng Yue's execution for treason. Luan Bu mourned Peng Yue publicly, was arrested, and faced execution. He defended Peng Yue's loyalty, arguing that his death was unjust, and was pardoned by the emperor, later becoming a general and prime minister. Luan Bu died in the fifth year of Emperor Jing's reign. Sima Qian commented that Ji Bu's endurance of humiliation to achieve greatness made him a famous Han general, while Luan Bu's willingness to die for righteousness surpassed even ancient heroes.
