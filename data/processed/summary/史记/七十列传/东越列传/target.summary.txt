The text recounts the history of the Minyue and Dongou kingdoms, descendants of King Goujian of Yue, with the surname Zou. After the Qin Dynasty annexed their lands, they were demoted to rulers and their territory became Minzhong County. During the rebellion against Qin, Wu Zhu and Yao led the Yue people to join Wu Rui and helped destroy Qin, but Xiang Ji refused to make them kings, so they later supported the King of Han against Xiang Ji. In the fifth year of the Han Dynasty, Wu Zhu was reinstated as King of Minyue, with his capital at Dongye. In the third year of Emperor Hui's reign, Yao was made King of Donghai, known as King of Dongou, for his contributions.

During Emperor Jing's reign, King Wu Liu Bi of Wu rebelled. Minyue refused to join, but Dongou followed. After Wu's defeat, Dongou killed him in Dantu under Han bribery and returned home. Liu Bi's son, Ziju, fled to Minyue and urged attacks on Dongou. In the third year of Emperor Wu's reign, Minyue besieged Dongou. Dongou sought Han aid; after debate, Zhuang Zhu was sent to mobilize troops from Kuaiji. Before the army arrived, Minyue withdrew. Dongou then relocated its people to the Jianghuai region.

In the sixth year of Jianyuan, Minyue attacked South Vietnam. The Han emperor sent generals Wang Hui and Han Anguo to intervene. King Ying of Minyue resisted, but his brother Yu Shan killed him and surrendered, ending the conflict. Yu Shan later proclaimed himself king, and the emperor appointed him King of Dongyue alongside King Yao. In the fifth year of Yuanding, Yu Shan feigned support for a Han campaign against South Vietnam but secretly aided the enemy. After the Han victory, General Yang Pu sought to attack Dongyue, but the emperor initially declined. In the sixth year of Yuanding, Yu Shan rebelled, attacked Han forces, and proclaimed himself emperor. The Han sent multiple generals to suppress him. Yu Shan was killed by his own people, including King Yao Jugu, who surrendered. The Han emperor then relocated the Dongyue population to the Jianghuai region, leaving the area empty. Tai Shigong reflects on the Yue kingdom's longevity, attributing it to ancestral merits and the legacy of Dayu, despite Yu Shan's treachery leading to its downfall.
