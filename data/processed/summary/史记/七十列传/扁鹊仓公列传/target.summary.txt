Bian Que, a physician from Bohai County, was originally named Qin Yue. As a young innkeeper, he met a mysterious guest named Chang Sang, who recognized his potential and gave him a secret medical prescription. After taking the medicine for thirty days, Bian Que gained the ability to see through walls and diagnose illnesses internally. He practiced medicine in Qi and Zhao, where he became known as Bian Que.

During the reign of Duke Zhao of Jin, Zhao Jianzi fell unconscious for five days. Bian Que diagnosed him, noting his blood was normal, and predicted he would recover in three days, similar to a past episode involving Duke Mu of Qin. Zhao Jianzi indeed woke up after two and a half days, recounting a vision of the Emperor of Heaven. He rewarded Bian Que with 40,000 acres of land.

In Guo State, Bian Que encountered a prince who had seemingly died. He explained the prince suffered from "corpse disease," caused by an imbalance of yin and yang, and was not truly dead. Using acupuncture and medicinal applications, Bian Que revived the prince, though he insisted he could not resurrect the dead, only help those who could recover.

When visiting Qi, Bian Que warned Marquis Huan of a minor illness between his skin and muscles. The marquis ignored him, and over successive visits, the disease progressed to his blood, then intestines and stomach, and finally to his bone marrow, where it became incurable. Marquis Huan died five days after Bian Que fled, illustrating the importance of early treatment.

Bian Que's fame spread, and he adapted his practice to local customs—treating women in Handan, the elderly in Luo, and children in Xianyang. However, Li Xi, the Qin imperial physician, jealous of his skills, had him assassinated. Despite this, Bian Que's methods in pulse diagnosis endured.

The text then shifts to Chunyu Yi, a Taicang official from Qi, who studied medicine under Gongcheng Yangqing. Yangqing taught him the pulse books of Huangdi and Bian Que, along with diagnostic methods. After three years, Chunyu Yi became skilled but faced legal trouble in the fourth year of Emperor Wen's reign. His daughter Tiying petitioned the emperor, offering to become a slave to atone for his crimes. Moved by her plea, the emperor pardoned Chunyu Yi and abolished corporal punishment.

Chunyu Yi later detailed his medical cases to the emperor. He diagnosed a censor with gangrene from excessive drinking and sexual activity, predicting death in eight days, which occurred. He treated the King of Qi's son for a gas diaphragm disease caused by melancholy, curing him with a qi decoction. Another patient, Xun, had a hernia from sexual intercourse, treated successfully with fire medicine. A palace official named Xin had a fever from bathing in cold water, cured with a liquid decoction. The Queen Mother of Qi suffered from wind-heat affecting the bladder, treated with gunpowder decoction. Cao Shantao had a fatal lung disease from anger and sexual activity, dying as predicted. Pan Manru died from a mast disease caused by drinking and sex, as diagnosed. Zhao Zhang had wind disease from alcoholism, dying later than predicted due to his porridge diet. The King of Jibei had wind syndrome from sweating on the ground, cured with medicinal wine. Sikong Chu's wife had a hernia from suppressed urination and sex, treated with moxibustion and decoction. A wet nurse had fever from excessive drinking, cured with acupuncture. A maid named Shu died from spleen damage due to sweating, as predicted. A doctor had dental caries from poor hygiene, treated with moxibustion and rinses. A pregnant woman with dystocia was given hyoscyamus powder to deliver. A servant with spleen damage died in spring, as foreseen. King Shichuan had a fever from washing hair, cured with cold water and acupuncture. Song Jian had kidney paralysis from lifting heavy objects, treated with a soft decoction. A maid named Han had internal cold from unfulfilled desire, cured with medicine and moxibustion. A woman with pinworm disease was treated with a purgative. Sima Chunyu had Dongfeng disease from overeating and running, cured with fire agent soup. A man named Po Shi died from lung damage after a fall, as predicted. A doctor named Sui developed gangrene from improper use of Five Stone Powder, dying as warned. The King of Qi, as Marquis of Yangxu, had paralysis from sexual intercourse, cured with gunpowder porridge. Cheng Kaifang developed tracheitis from drinking and wind, becoming paralyzed and mute. Xiang Chu died from male hernia after strenuous activity, as predicted.

Chunyu Yi explained that disease names often overlap, requiring careful pulse diagnosis to distinguish them. He noted that patients' deaths sometimes deviate from predictions due to diet or improper treatment. He avoided serving as a court physician, traveling to learn from multiple teachers. His teacher Yangqing, from a wealthy family, taught him secretly. Chunyu Yi also taught several students, including Songyi, Gao Ming, Wang Yu, Feng Xin, Du Xin, and Tang An, though not all mastered his skills. He admitted that errors in pulse diagnosis could lead to mistakes in predicting outcomes.

The historian concluded that Bian Que suffered due to his medical skills, while Chunyu Yi faced punishment but was saved by his daughter Tiying's petition, reflecting Laozi's saying that beautiful things are unlucky.
