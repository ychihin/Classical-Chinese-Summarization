Mengchangjun, whose surname was Tian and given name Wen, was the son of Jingguo Jun Tian Ying. Tian Ying, the youngest son of King Qi Wei, served as a general and later as prime minister of Qi, leading campaigns against Wei and forming alliances with Han and Wei. He had over forty sons, but Tian Wen was born on May 5th, a date considered inauspicious. Tian Ying ordered the child abandoned, but his mother secretly raised him. When Tian Wen grew up, he confronted his father, arguing that fate was determined by heaven, not by the doorframe, and criticized Tian Ying for accumulating wealth while neglecting the state's welfare. Impressed, Tian Ying made Tian Wen his heir.

After Tian Ying's death, Tian Wen inherited the title of Lord Mengchang in Xue Yi. He generously hosted thousands of guests, treating them equally regardless of status. He recorded their conversations and sent gifts to their relatives. Once, a guest suspected unequal treatment, but Lord Mengchang compared his own meal to prove fairness, leading the guest to commit suicide in shame. Lord Mengchang accepted all guests without discrimination, earning their loyalty.

King Zhao of Qin, hearing of Lord Mengchang's virtue, invited him to Qin and appointed him prime minister. However, officials warned that Lord Mengchang, being a Qi noble, would prioritize Qi's interests, so King Zhao imprisoned him. Lord Mengchang's concubine demanded his white fox fur as a bribe for her intercession. A guest skilled in thievery disguised himself as a dog, stole the fur from the palace, and gave it to the concubine, who secured Lord Mengchang's release. As he fled, another guest imitated a rooster's crow to open the Hangu Pass gates before pursuers arrived. Though initially ashamed of these lowly guests, Lord Mengchang's reliance on them earned admiration for his inclusive recruitment.

Later, Lord Mengchang became Qi's prime minister and planned to attack Qin with Han and Wei. Su Dai advised him to avoid strengthening Han and Wei, instead using the Western Zhou to negotiate with Qin for Chu's eastern territory and the release of King Huai of Chu. Lord Mengchang agreed, averting war. When dismissed as prime minister due to slander, he retired to Xue Yi. After Qin's general Lu Li became Qi's prime minister, Lord Mengchang, following Su Dai's advice, marched north to secure alliances, causing Lu Li to flee. When King Qi Min grew proud and sought to eliminate him, Lord Mengchang fled to Wei, became prime minister, and allied with Qin and Zhao to attack Qi, leading to King Min's death. King Xiang of Qi later reconciled with Lord Mengchang, who remained neutral among the states. After his death, his sons fought over the title, and Qi and Wei destroyed Xue Yi, ending his lineage.

Feng Chen, a poor scholar, joined Lord Mengchang's household. Initially placed in low-class quarters, he sang about lacking fish, a carriage, and means to support his family, prompting Lord Mengchang to upgrade his status. When Lord Mengchang needed to collect debts in Xue Yi, Feng Chen was sent. Instead of collecting, he burned the deeds of the poor, arguing that this would earn the people's trust and enhance Lord Mengchang's reputation. Though initially angry, Lord Mengchang later thanked him.

When Lord Mengchang was dismissed as prime minister, his guests abandoned him. Feng Chen went to Qin, convincing King Zhao to send gifts to recruit Lord Mengchang. He then urged King Qi to restore Lord Mengchang's position before the Qin envoy arrived, which King Qi did. When Lord Mengchang expressed bitterness toward his former guests, Feng Chen explained that people naturally flock to the wealthy and abandon the poor, advising him to forgive them. Lord Mengchang accepted this counsel.

Sima Qian noted that Xue's unique customs stemmed from Lord Mengchang's hospitality, which attracted over sixty thousand lawbreakers, affirming his well-deserved reputation for entertaining guests.
