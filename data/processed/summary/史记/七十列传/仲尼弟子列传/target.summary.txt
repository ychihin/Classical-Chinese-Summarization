Confucius identified seventy-seven disciples proficient in the six arts, categorizing them by virtue (Yan Yuan, Min Ziqian, Ran Boniu, Zhong Gong), political skill (Ran You, Ji Lu), eloquence (Zaiwo, Zigong), and erudition (Ziyou, Zixia). He noted individual traits: Zhuan Sun was extreme, Zeng Shen slow, Gao Chai stupid, Zhong You rude, and Yan Hui poor. Zigong, rejecting fate, became a successful merchant. Confucius respected figures like Laozi, Zang Boyu, Yan Zhongping, Laolaizi, Zichan, and Meng Gongchuo, and praised others from earlier eras.

Yan Hui (Yan Yuan), thirty years younger than Confucius, was praised for virtue, living frugally without changing his joy. Confucius said he restrained himself to conform to etiquette, was not stupid, and shared a philosophy of serving when employed and hiding when not. Yan Hui died prematurely at twenty-nine, causing Confucius great grief. Duke Ai of Lu was told Yan Hui was the best learner, never transferring anger or repeating mistakes.

Min Ziqian, fifteen years younger, was deemed exceptionally filial, avoiding official service. Ran Boniu suffered an incurable disease; Confucius lamented his fate. Zhong Gong, asked about governance, was advised to be courteous and cautious; Confucius deemed him fit for high office despite his humble father. Ran Qiu, twenty-nine years younger, managed military affairs for the Ji family; Confucius encouraged his timidity. Zilu (Zhong You), nine years younger, was bold and straightforward, initially bullying Confucius but later becoming a disciple. Confucius taught him to prioritize righteousness over bravery, and he died in Wei during a rebellion, tying his hat before death.

Zaiwo, eloquent, questioned the three-year mourning period, suggesting one year sufficed; Confucius rebuked him for lacking benevolence. Zaiwo later died in a rebellion in Qi. Zigong, thirty-one years younger, was articulate and wealthy. Confucius compared him to a vessel and praised his humility. Zigong’s diplomatic mission saved Lu, disrupted Qi, destroyed Wu, strengthened Jin, and enabled Yue’s dominance, altering the balance of power among states.

Ziyou, forty-five years younger, governed Wucheng with music and ritual; Confucius joked about using a “knife to kill a chicken” but later praised him. Zixia, forty-four years younger, discussed the Book of Songs with Confucius, who noted his slight deficiency. After Confucius’s death, Zixia taught in Hexi. Zizhang, forty-eight years younger, learned about gaining office through caution and about achieving success through loyalty and respect. Zeng Shen, forty-six years younger, understood filial piety and wrote the Book of Filial Piety.

Other disciples included Tantai Mieming, initially judged poorly by appearance but later renowned; Mi Zijian, praised as a gentleman; Yuan Xian, who valued poverty over shame; Gongye Chang, married to Confucius’s daughter; Nangong Kuo, praised for morality; and Gao Chai, deemed stupid. Youruo, resembling Confucius, was briefly treated as a teacher but failed to answer questions. The text lists thirty-five disciples with recorded ages and deeds, and forty-two without, concluding that later accounts vary, with the author relying on The Analects for accuracy.
