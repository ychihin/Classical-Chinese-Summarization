Prime Minister Gongsun Hong, from Xue County in Qi Prefecture, began his career as a prison officer but was dismissed due to crimes. His family was poor, and he herded pigs until his forties, when he studied the Spring and Autumn Annals. He raised his stepmother with filial piety. At age sixty, during Emperor Wu's reign, he was recruited as a virtuous person and became a doctor. After a mission to the Xiongnu displeased the emperor, he was dismissed and returned home. In the fifth year of Yuanguang, he was recommended again, despite initial reluctance, and his countermeasure essay was promoted to first place by Emperor Wu, who made him a doctor. He inspected the southwestern Yi region but advised against further development, which the emperor ignored.

Gongsun Hong was known for his frugality, covering himself with a quilt and eating simple meals. He mourned his stepmother for three years. In court, he avoided direct confrontation, letting the emperor decide. He was promoted to Zuo Neishi within two years. He often agreed with the emperor's will, even breaking prior agreements with ministers, leading to accusations of deceit from Ji An. Gongsun Hong apologized, claiming loyalty was misunderstood. Despite slander, the emperor favored him. In the third year of Yuanshuo, he became imperial censor. He advised against expanding into the southwestern barbarians and Canghai County, focusing on Shuofang County instead. When questioned by Zhu Maichen, he couldn't answer and apologized. Ji An accused him of fraud for his frugality, but Gongsun Hong defended himself, citing historical examples. The emperor made him prime minister and Marquis of Pingjin.

Gongsun Hong was outwardly magnanimous but inwardly vengeful, causing harm to enemies like Dong Zhongshu. He supported old friends with his salary, leaving no savings. During the Huainan and Hengshan rebellions, he fell ill and wrote to the emperor, citing his inadequacy and offering to resign. The emperor refused, granting him leave and gifts. He recovered but eventually died as prime minister. His son Gongsun Du inherited the title but lost it for breaking the law.

Zhu Fuyan, from Linyi, studied political theories and later the Zhouyi and Spring and Autumn Annals. Poor and rejected by scholars, he traveled to various states without success. In Yuanguang's first year, he sought an audience with General Wei Qing, who recommended him, but the emperor ignored him. After spending his money, he wrote a memorial, which led to an immediate summons. His advice included warnings against war with the Xiongnu, citing Qin and Han failures. He advocated for frugality and caution, referencing historical precedents. The emperor appointed him and others as doctors, and Zhu Fuyan was promoted four times in a year.

Zhu Fuyan advised dividing the territories of princes to weaken their power, which the emperor adopted. He also suggested moving powerful families to Maoling. He exposed secrets of King Yan and King Qi, leading to their deaths. Accused of bribery, he was executed after Gongsun Hong argued his guilt. His guests abandoned him, but Kong Che buried him. Tai Shigong noted Gongsun Hong's success due to timing and Zhu Fuyan's tragic end. Empress Dowager Wang Zhengjun praised Gongsun Hong's frugality and virtue, citing his example as a model for officials. His descendants were granted titles and lands.
