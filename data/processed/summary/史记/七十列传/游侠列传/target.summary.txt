The text presents Han Feizi's critique of Confucian scholars and chivalrous men for undermining law and order, contrasting them with figures like Ji Ci and Yuan Xian, who lived in poverty but were remembered for their virtues. It praises rangers for their integrity, willingness to sacrifice for others, and modesty, despite their actions not always aligning with moral and legal standards. The text cites historical figures like Yu Shun, Yi Yin, and Confucius who faced hardships, emphasizing that even virtuous individuals encounter disasters. It critiques societal hypocrisy, where minor thieves are punished while those who seize power are honored, and notes that true chivalry involves keeping promises and aiding others without boasting.

The narrative then focuses on civilian knights from the Han Dynasty, such as Zhu Jia, Tian Zhong, Ju Meng, and Guo Xie. Zhu Jia, a knight from Lu, hid and rescued hundreds without boasting, living frugally. Tian Zhong served Zhu Jia like a father. Ju Meng, from Luoyang, was sought after by Zhou Yafu during the Wu-Chu rebellion, and his mother's funeral drew thousands. Guo Xie, initially cruel, later reformed, repaying hatred with kindness and saving lives without boasting. He mediated disputes, avoided taking credit, and maintained humility, earning widespread respect. However, his past crimes and a murder by his disciples led to his execution under Emperor Wu, with Gongsun Hong arguing his influence made him more dangerous than if he had committed the murders himself. The text concludes by noting that later knights were often arrogant, except for a few like Fan Zhongzi, and laments that Guo Xie's reputation outshone his unremarkable appearance and speech.
