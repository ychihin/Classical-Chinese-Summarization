Bai Qi, a skilled military commander from Yidi, served King Zhao of Qin. His early campaigns included victories against Han, Wei, and Zhao, capturing cities and killing tens of thousands of enemies. He was promoted through ranks, eventually becoming Lord Wu'an after conquering Chu's capital Ying and burning its royal tombs. His most notable battle was against Zhao at Changping in the 47th year of King Zhao's reign. After initial setbacks, Zhao replaced General Lian Po with Zhao Kuo. Bai Qi used deception, cutting off Zhao's supply lines and surrounding their army. After 46 days without food, Zhao Kuo was killed, and 400,000 Zhao soldiers surrendered. Bai Qi, fearing rebellion, ordered them buried alive, shocking Zhao.

Following this, Qin planned to besiege Handan, but Su Dai, sent by Han and Zhao, persuaded Prime Minister Yinghou to halt, arguing that Bai Qi's growing fame threatened Yinghou's position. King Zhao agreed to a truce, but Bai Qi's resentment toward Yinghou grew. When Qin later attacked Handan under Wang Ling, Bai Qi refused to lead, citing the army's exhaustion and the risk of allied reinforcements. Despite King Zhao's orders, Bai Qi feigned illness. After Qin's defeat, Bai Qi's remarks angered the king, who demoted him and ordered his exile. Accused of discontent, Bai Qi was forced to commit suicide in the 50th year of King Zhao's reign.

Wang Jian, from Pinyang, served Qin Shihuang. He conquered Zhao, Yan, and other states, often requesting 600,000 troops for campaigns. After Li Xin's failed attack on Chu with 200,000 men, Wang Jian was recalled. He led 600,000 troops, avoided direct engagement, and waited for Chu's army to weaken before striking, defeating them and capturing King Chu. Wang Jian's son Wang Ben and Li Xin later conquered Yan and Qi, unifying China. Wang Jian's grandson Wang Li, a third-generation general, was captured by Xiang Yu during Chen Sheng's rebellion, fulfilling a saying that general families often fail by the third generation due to accumulated killings.

Sima Qian noted Bai Qi's strategic brilliance but inability to avoid Yinghou's schemes, and Wang Jian's military success but failure to promote moral governance, leading to his grandson's capture.
