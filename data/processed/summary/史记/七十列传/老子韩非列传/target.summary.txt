The text provides a biographical account of Laozi, a native of Chu State who served as a Zhou dynasty librarian. When Confucius sought his advice on etiquette, Laozi dismissed the topic, noting that its proponents had long since perished, and advised Confucius to abandon arrogance and excessive desires. Confucius later likened Laozi to a dragon, elusive and transcendent. Laozi, observing Zhou's decline, wrote a book of over 5,000 words on morality at the request of a gatekeeper before disappearing. Some claim he lived over 160 years, while others identify him with the historian Taishi Dan, who prophesied about Qin and Zhou's future. Laozi's son, Li Zong, became a Wei general, and his descendants served in Han times. The text contrasts Laozi's philosophy of non-action with Confucianism, noting mutual criticism between followers.

Zhuangzi, from Mengyi, served as a minor official and wrote extensively in allegorical style, critiquing Confucianism and Mohism. He rejected a lucrative offer from King Wei of Chu, preferring freedom over officialdom. Shen Buhai, a Zheng native, studied legalist philosophy and served as Han prime minister for 15 years, strengthening the state. Han Fei, a Korean prince and student of Xun Qing, wrote prolifically on legalism, including works like "Shuo Nan" on the difficulties of persuasion. Despite his insights, Han Fei was executed in Qin after being slandered by Li Si and Yao Jia. The text details the perils of lobbying, using historical anecdotes to illustrate how understanding a ruler's psychology is crucial. It concludes with Sima Qian's reflections, praising Laozi's profound teachings, Zhuangzi's freedom, Shenzi's diligence, and Hanzi's legal rigor, while lamenting Han Fei's tragic fate.
