Yingbu, a commoner from Liuxian County with the surname Ying, was predicted in his youth to become a king after being tortured. After being sentenced to tattooing for breaking the law, he laughed at the prophecy's fulfillment. Sent to hard labor at Lishan, he later rebelled against the Qin Dynasty with Wu Rui, the magistrate of Fan County, gathering thousands of troops. Yingbu joined Xiang Liang, proving himself brave in battle, and was titled Dangyang Lord. After Xiang Liang's death, he served under Xiang Ji (Xiang Yu), leading attacks that defeated the Qin army and forced Zhang Han's surrender. Yingbu buried over 200,000 Qin soldiers alive and helped Xiang Yu enter Xianyang. He was named King of Jiujiang and later killed the Righteous Emperor on Xiang Yu's orders.

During the Chu-Han conflict, Yingbu feigned illness to avoid aiding Xiang Yu, who grew resentful. The Han king, valuing Yingbu's talents, sent Sui He to persuade him to defect. Sui He argued that Chu was weak and unjust, while Han was strong, promising Yingbu land and titles. Yingbu agreed, killed the Chu envoy, and attacked Chu. After being defeated by Long Qie, he fled to Han with Sui He. Initially angered by the Han king's casual reception, he was pleased by the luxurious treatment. The Han king granted him the title King of Huainan, and Yingbu helped defeat Xiang Yu at Gaixia.

After peace, Yingbu grew fearful upon seeing the execution of Han Xin and Peng Yue. His favored concubine's praise of a doctor's neighbor, Ben He, led to suspicion of adultery. Ben He fled to Chang'an and reported Yingbu's rebellion. Despite initial disbelief, Yingbu killed Ben He's family and rebelled. The emperor consulted Xue Gong, who predicted Yingbu would choose a poor strategy—attacking Wu and Chu but fleeing to Changsha. Yingbu indeed attacked Jing and Chu, defeating them, but was routed by the emperor's army at Huizhen. He fled south with a hundred men, was tricked by King Ai of Changsha, and killed by Fanyang people. The emperor made Prince Liu Chang King of Huainan. The historian notes Yingbu's rise from tattooing to kingship, his role in massacres, and his downfall due to jealousy over a concubine.
