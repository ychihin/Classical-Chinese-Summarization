Sima Xiangru, courtesy name Changqing, was born in Chengdu, Shu County. As a youth, he studied literature and swordsmanship, and later admired Lin Xiangru, changing his name accordingly. He initially served as a Langguan under Emperor Jing of Han due to family wealth, but disliked his post. When Emperor Jing showed little interest in rhapsodies (Ci Fu), Xiangru befriended scholars like Zou Yang and Mei Cheng during a visit from King Xiao of Liang. He resigned on pretence of illness and lived in Liang, where he wrote "Zixu Fu." After King Xiao's death, Xiangru returned to Chengdu impoverished.

Xiangru's friend Wang Ji, magistrate of Linqiong, invited him to stay. Wang Ji feigned deference, but Xiangru eventually refused visits, feigning illness. Wealthy locals, including Zhuo Wangsun and Cheng Zheng, hosted a banquet for the magistrate. Xiangru initially declined but attended after the magistrate personally invited him. During the feast, Xiangru played the zither, captivating Zhuo Wangsun's newly widowed daughter, Wenjun, who secretly admired him. After the banquet, Xiangru bribed her attendants to convey his feelings, and Wenjun eloped with him to Chengdu, where they lived in poverty with only four walls.

Zhuo Wangsun was furious and refused to support them. Wenjun suggested they return to Linqiong, where they sold their possessions and opened a tavern. Wenjun managed the shop while Xiangru worked publicly in menial tasks. Ashamed, Zhuo Wangsun, persuaded by relatives, eventually gave them 100 slaves, money, and belongings, enabling them to become wealthy.

Later, Emperor Wu of Han read "Zixu Fu" and admired it. Yang Deyi informed him that Xiangru was the author. Summoned, Xiangru offered to write a grander rhapsody about the emperor's hunting grounds. He composed "Shanglin Fu," using fictitious characters—Zixu (Chu), Mr. Wuyou (Qi), and Wu Shigong (the emperor's perspective)—to contrast princely extravagance with imperial virtue. The rhapsody described vast landscapes, hunting scenes, and concluded with a call for frugality and benevolence. The emperor was delighted and appointed Xiangru as Langguan.

Xiangru served as an envoy to blame Tang Meng for harsh policies in Ba and Shu, issuing an imperial proclamation reassuring the people. He later advocated for reopening southwestern territories, becoming Zhonglang General to pacify the region. Despite opposition, he succeeded, and the emperor was pleased. However, accusations of bribery led to his dismissal, though he was later reinstated as Langguan.

Xiangru suffered from diabetes and avoided court debates. He advised the emperor against dangerous hunting, writing a remonstrance. He also composed "Ode to the Immortals" and "Adult Fu," depicting celestial journeys. His final work, a treatise on the Feng and Shan sacrifices, urged the emperor to perform these rites. The emperor later conducted them after Xiangru's death.

Sima Qian noted that Xiangru's works, though ornate, promoted frugality, akin to the "Book of Songs." Yang Xiong criticized their imbalance between luxury and admonition, but Sima Qian defended their value.
