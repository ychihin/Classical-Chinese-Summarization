# Summary of Historical Text

In ancient times, when Zhuanxu ruled the world, he appointed Nan Zhengzhong to oversee astronomy and Bei Zhengli to oversee geography. During the Tang and Yu dynasties, the descendants of Chong and Li continued these duties through the Xia and Shang dynasties, with the Chongli family holding these positions for generations. During the Zhou Dynasty, Cheng Boxiu Fu was their descendant. Under King Xuan of Zhou, the Chongli family became the Sima family after losing their official position. The Sima family then managed Zhou history for generations. During the reigns of Kings Hui and Xiang of Zhou, the Sima family left the Zhou capital for the Kingdom of Jin. Later, when the Jin army marshal fled to Qin, the Sima family moved to Shaoliang. From there, clan members scattered to Wei, Zhao, and Qin states.

In Wei, a Sima became Prime Minister of Zhongshan. In Zhao, they were known for teaching swordsmanship. In Qin, Sima Cuo disputed with Zhang Yi, leading King Hui of Qin to send him to attack Shu, where he became governor. Sima Jin, Sima Cuo's grandson, served Wu Xinjun Bai Qi and participated in killing the Changping Army of Zhao. Both were later killed and buried in Huachi. Sima Chang, Sima Jin's grandson, served as an iron-smelting official during Qin Shihuang's era. Sima Xian, a descendant of Kuai Chi, was a general under Wu Xinjun who captured Chaoge. When princes vied for kingship, Sima An became king in Yin. When Liu Bang, King of Han, attacked Xiang Yu, Sima An surrendered, and the Han made Yin into Hanoi County.

Sima Chang fathered Sima Wuze, who served as mayor of Han. Wuze fathered Sima Xi, who was awarded the title of five great officials. Sima Xi fathered Sima Tan, who became Grand Historian. Tai Shigong studied astronomy from Tang Du, the Book of Changes from Yang He, and Taoist theory from Huang Zi. He served from Jianyuan to Yuanfeng and worried that scholars could not understand each school's essence, so he discussed the six schools: Yin-Yang, Confucianism, Mohism, Ming, Legalism, and Taoism. He noted that while all sought peace and order, their doctrines differed. Yin-Yang focused on good and bad omens with many taboos, making people fearful, but its principles on the four seasons could not be discarded. Confucianism was broad but rarely grasped essentials, taking much effort with little effect, yet its etiquette on君臣父子 relationships could not be changed. Mohism was frugal and difficult to follow, but its emphasis on utility could not be discarded. Legalism advocated harsh punishments without kindness, but its correction of status relationships could not be changed. Famous scholars made people constrained and lost authenticity, but the relationship between names and reality required examination. Taoism made minds focused and actions consistent with the invisible Tao, absorbing strengths from other schools and adapting to times and changes.

Tai Shigong had a son named Qian. Sima Qian was born in Longmen, farming and herding north of the Yellow River. At ten, he learned ancient prose. At twenty, he traveled south to Jiang and Huai, climbed Kuaiji, explored Yu Cave, visited Jiuyi, rafted on Yuan and Xiang rivers, crossed north to Wenshui and Sishui, studied in Qi and Shandong, investigated Confucius's legacy, performed shooting rituals in Zou and Yi, was trapped in Po, Xue, and Pengcheng, and returned through Liang and Chu. He then served as a doctor, went west to conquer south of Bashu, traveled south through Qiong, Zuo, and Kunming, and returned to report. That year, the emperor began the Han Zen ceremony, but Tai Shigong was stranded in Zhounan and could not participate, becoming ill and near death.

When Sima Qian visited his father between the Yellow River and Luoshui, Tai Shigong held his hand and wept, saying their ancestor was Taishi of Zhou, responsible for astronomical affairs since Yu and Xia. He urged Sima Qian to continue as Taishi and not forget the writings he wanted to complete. He emphasized that filial piety begins with serving parents, then the emperor, and finally establishing one's reputation. He cited the Duke of Zhou for expounding King Wen and King Wu's merits, and Confucius for studying old classics, writing the Spring and Autumn Annals. He lamented that for over four hundred years since Confucius, history had been discarded, and with the Han rise, he feared cutting off the historical tradition. Sima Qian wept and promised to detail the historical news compiled by their ancestors without omission.

Three years after Sima Tan's death, Sima Qian became Taishi Ling and began collecting historical books. In the first year of Taichu, the Han calendar began using Xia Zheng with the first month as the new year. Tai Shigong reflected on Confucius appearing five hundred years after the Duke of Zhou, and wondered if someone would now inherit the Qingming, correct the Yi Zhuan, and continue the Spring and Autumn. He discussed with Doctor Hu Sui why Confucius wrote the Spring and Autumn Annals, explaining that when Zhou kingship declined, Confucius praised and criticized 242 years of history as a standard for judging right and wrong, belittling unrighteous emperors, reprimanding wrong princes, and denouncing chaotic officials. The Spring and Autumn Annals explained the governance of three kings, distinguished personnel rules, and allowed ruined countries to exist and severed lineages to continue.

Sima Qian then began compiling documents. In the seventh year, he suffered disaster from Li Ling and was imprisoned. He sighed that this was his sin, but reflected that King Wen deduced the Book of Changes while imprisoned, Confucius wrote the Spring and Autumn Annals when distressed, Qu Yuan wrote Li Sao when exiled, and others wrote when frustrated. He finally resolved to record history from the Tao and Tang dynasties to Emperor Wu's Huolin year, starting from the Yellow Emperor. He organized his work into twelve basic annals, ten tables, eight books, thirty hereditary houses, and seventy biographies, totaling 130 chapters and 526,500 words, called "Tai Shi Gong Shu." He stored it in a famous mountain and left a copy in Kyoto for future sages and gentlemen to see.
