Han Xin, the Marquis of Huaiyin, was a native of Huaiyin. In his youth, he was poor, lacked good character, and could not secure an official position or make a living through business. He often relied on others for meals, which made him disliked. He frequently ate at a pavilion in Nanchang, but the pavilion chief's wife, annoyed by his presence, began eating breakfast early in the inner room. When Han Xin arrived, no food was prepared for him. Angered, he left and never returned. While fishing under the city, an old woman rinsing silk cotton pitied his hunger and fed him for dozens of days. Han Xin promised to repay her heavily, but she rebuked him, saying she did not expect repayment. A young butcher insulted Han Xin, calling him a coward and challenging him to either stab him or crawl under his crotch. Han Xin chose to crawl, and the crowd mocked him for his timidity.

When Xiang Liang led his army across the Huai River, Han Xin followed but gained no reputation. After Xiang Liang's defeat, he served under Xiang Yu as a doctor, offering suggestions that were ignored. He then joined Liu Bang, the King of Han, but was only made a minor official. Sentenced to death for breaking the law, he caught the attention of Teng Gong, who spared him after a bold speech. Teng Gong recommended him to Liu Bang, who appointed him as a commander, though without recognizing his talents. Han Xin frequently conversed with Xiao He, who considered him a genius. When Han Xin fled due to lack of recognition, Xiao He chased him personally. Liu Bang initially suspected Xiao He of desertion but learned he was pursuing Han Xin. Xiao He argued that Han Xin was unparalleled and essential for Liu Bang's ambitions to conquer the world. Liu Bang agreed to make Han Xin a general, and after a formal ceremony, Han Xin was appointed.

Han Xin analyzed Xiang Yu's weaknesses, such as his inability to delegate, his reluctance to reward subordinates, and his harsh rule, which alienated the people. He advised Liu Bang to adopt the opposite approach, emphasizing the loyalty of the Qin people due to Liu Bang's merciful policies. Liu Bang followed Han Xin's plan, and in August, they advanced eastward through Chencang, pacifying the Three Qin regions. In the second year of the Han Dynasty, they subdued the kings of Wei and Henan. However, in April, the Han army was defeated at Pengcheng. Han Xin regrouped and met Liu Bang at Xingyang, defeating the Chu army. Later, King Bao of Wei rebelled, and Han Xin captured him, pacifying Wei. He then attacked Zhao with Zhang Er, defeating a 200,000-strong Zhao army at Jingxing by using a river-backed formation and a surprise cavalry attack. Han Xin spared Li Zuoche, who advised him to subdue Yan and Qi through diplomacy rather than force. Han Xin followed this advice, and Yan surrendered.

Han Xin requested that Zhang Er be made King of Zhao, which Liu Bang approved. The Chu army besieged Liu Bang at Xingyang, but he escaped and seized Han Xin's troops. Han Xin was ordered to attack Qi, and despite Liu Bang's envoy Li Shiqi persuading Qi to surrender, Han Xin, urged by Kuai Tong, attacked and conquered Qi. Chu sent Long Qie to aid Qi, but Han Xin defeated him by damming the Wei River and then releasing the water, killing Long Qie. Han Xin then requested to be made acting King of Qi. Liu Bang, initially furious, was persuaded by Zhang Liang and Chen Ping to grant the title to secure Han Xin's loyalty. Kuai Tong later advised Han Xin to rebel and divide the world into three parts, but Han Xin refused, citing Liu Bang's generosity. Despite Kuai Tong's warnings about the dangers of his position, Han Xin hesitated and ultimately remained loyal.

After Xiang Yu's defeat, Liu Bang demoted Han Xin to King of Chu and later, fearing his influence, arrested him during a visit to Yunmeng. Han Xin was pardoned but reduced to Marquis of Huaiyin. He grew resentful and avoided court. When Chen Xi rebelled, Han Xin conspired with him but was betrayed by a retainer. Empress Lu and Xiao He tricked Han Xin into the palace, where he was executed. Before his death, he regretted not following Kuai Tong's advice. Kuai Tong was captured but spared after arguing that he only served his master. The historian comments that Han Xin's early ambition was evident, but his failure to remain humble and his eventual rebellion led to his downfall.
