Su Qin, a native of Luoyang in the Eastern Zhou Dynasty, studied under Mr. Guiguzi in Qi State. After years of travel left him impoverished, he returned home in embarrassment, where his family mocked him for abandoning practical trades to pursue rhetoric. Ashamed, he secluded himself to study, discovering the "Yin Fu" text. After a year of intense effort, he sought to persuade rulers. He first approached King Xian of Zhou, but was dismissed due to the ministers' disdain. He then went west to Qin, where King Hui declined his advice, citing the state's recent execution of Shang Yang and distrust of lobbyists.

Su Qin next traveled to Zhao, but Lord Fengyang disliked him, so he proceeded to Yan. After over a year, he met Marquis Wen of Yan, arguing that Yan's safety depended on Zhao as a southern barrier. He noted that Qin would struggle to attack Yan from afar, while Zhao could strike within days. He urged an alliance with Zhao to secure Yan. Marquis Wen agreed, funding Su Qin's mission to Zhao. There, Lord Fengyang had died, so Su Qin lobbied Zhao Suhou, emphasizing the importance of diplomatic choices for stability. He proposed a united front of six states against Qin, detailing how each state would support the others if attacked. King Zhao accepted, providing resources for Su Qin to persuade other states.

Su Qin then approached King Xuan of Han, highlighting Korea's military strength and warning that serving Qin would lead to endless land demands. King Han angrily refused to serve Qin. He similarly persuaded King Wei of Wei, stressing Wei's power and the folly of ceding land to Qin. King Wei agreed to follow Su Qin's plan. In Qi, he advised King Xuan that Qin could not easily attack Qi due to geographical barriers, and that serving Qin was a strategic error. King Qi consented. Finally, he lobbied King Wei of Chu, arguing that Chu and Qin could not coexist and that alliance would bring glory. King Wei, fearing Qin's ambition, entrusted his state to Su Qin.

The six states united, with Su Qin as their leader and prime minister. On his return to Luoyang, his family, once scornful, now bowed in awe. He distributed wealth to relatives and repaid debts. The alliance kept Qin from invading for fifteen years. However, Qin later deceived Qi and Wei into attacking Zhao, breaking the pact. Su Qin fled to Yan, where King Yi sought his help to recover ten cities lost to Qi. Su Qin persuaded King Qi to return them by warning of Qin's enmity. Despite slander, Su Qin regained King Yan's trust, though he had an affair with the king's mother. Fearing death, he feigned offense and fled to Qi, where he served as a guest minister. He secretly plotted to weaken Qi for Yan, advising King Min to waste resources on grand projects. Eventually, rivals assassinated him. Before dying, he told King Qi to display his body publicly, promising the assassin would be caught. The ruse worked, and the assassin was executed.

Su Qin's brothers, Su Dai and Su Li, later pursued similar careers. After Su Qin's death, Su Dai served Yan, advising King Yan on strategy against Qi. He noted Qi's exhaustion from wars and suggested using bribes to manipulate Qi into attacking Song, weakening itself. King Yan agreed. Su Li also served Qi, but faced suspicion due to Su Qin's past. The brothers eventually fled to Qi after Yan's internal chaos. Su Dai later returned to Yan, helping King Zhao plan and execute a successful attack on Qi, forcing King Min to flee. Su Dai later advised King Yan against visiting Qin, warning of Qin's violent tactics. King Yan heeded this advice. Both Su Dai and Su Li lived to old age, renowned for their diplomatic skills.

The historian concludes that the Su brothers were famous for their lobbying, but Su Qin's death from conspiracy led to widespread ridicule. However, his ability to unite six states from humble origins shows extraordinary intelligence. The historian compiled his deeds in correct order to preserve his legacy.
