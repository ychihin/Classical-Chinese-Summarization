The text recounts the lives of several officials from the Han Dynasty, focusing on their conduct and family traditions. Wan Shijun, originally from Zhao, served under Emperor Gaozu and rose through ranks due to his respectful and cautious nature. He became a tutor to the prince and later a high-ranking official, with his four sons also holding significant posts. His family was renowned for filial piety and prudence, influencing even Confucian scholars. Wan Shijun’s strict household rules included requiring descendants to show respect, and he would refuse to eat if they erred. His son Shi Jian was notably filial, washing his father’s clothes secretly and being meticulous in his duties. Another son, Shi Qing, became prime minister but struggled during turbulent times, eventually dying in office. The family’s tradition declined after his death.

Wei Wan, Marquis of Jianling, served Emperors Wen and Jing with loyalty and prudence. He refused a banquet invitation from the future Emperor Jing, claiming illness, and kept six swords given by Emperor Wen unused. He took blame for subordinates’ mistakes and deflected credit. During the Seven Kingdoms Rebellion, he led troops and was made marquis. He later became prime minister but was dismissed for failing to address injustices during Emperor Jing’s illness.

Zhi Buyi, Marquis of Sai, was known for honesty, once apologizing for a mistaken accusation of theft. He served as imperial censor and was dismissed during Emperor Wu’s reign. He followed Laozi’s teachings and governed without seeking fame.

Zhou Wen, a doctor, served Emperor Jing with reserve, wearing shabby clothes to avoid concubines. He never revealed others’ words and declined gifts. He retired with a salary under Emperor Wu.

Zhang Ou, imperial censor, studied Legalism but was loyal and merciful, often weeping over cases he could not retry. He retired due to illness.

The historian concludes that Wan Shijun, Wei Wan, and Zhang Ou exemplified Confucian ideals of cautious speech and action, while Zhi Buyi and Zhou Wen, though flawed, were still honest gentlemen.
