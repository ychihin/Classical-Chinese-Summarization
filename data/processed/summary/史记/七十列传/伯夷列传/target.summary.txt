The text examines the authenticity of historical records, emphasizing the need to rely on trustworthy sources like the Six Classics, despite the incomplete state of the "Book of Songs" and "Shangshu." It recounts the abdication of Tang Yao to Yu Shun and Yu Shun to Xia Yu, highlighting the solemn and prudent transfer of power, as the world and emperor are precious. However, it contrasts this with accounts from Zhuzi's Miscellaneous Notes, where Xu You refused Tang Yao's abdication out of shame, and later Bian Sui and Wuguang similarly rejected Shang Tang's offer. The historian climbs Jishan Mountain, noting Xu You's possible tomb, and reflects on Confucius's discussions of ancient sages like Wu Taibo and Boyi, yet finds no scriptural record of Xu You and Wu Guang's virtues.

The text then focuses on Boyi and Shuqi, sons of Lord Guzhu, who fled to avoid a succession dispute. They sought Xibochang but found him dead; his son King Wu marched against Yin Zhou. Boyi and Shuqi remonstrated against this, citing filial piety and righteousness, but were spared by Taigong Lu Shang. After the Zhou conquest, they refused to eat Zhou's food, starved on Shouyang Mountain, and composed a song expressing despair. The historian questions whether they felt resentment, given their fate.

It further questions divine justice: Boyi and Shuqi, though virtuous, starved, while Yan Yuan, a studious disciple, died poor. In contrast, the cruel Robber Zhi lived long. Modern times show lawbreakers thriving while the righteous suffer. The historian is perplexed by this apparent lack of heavenly reward. Confucius's sayings on differing paths and the value of virtue in chaotic times are cited. The text concludes that reputation depends on association with the virtuous, as Boyi and Shuqi were glorified by Confucius, and scholars in obscurity need such recognition to be remembered.
