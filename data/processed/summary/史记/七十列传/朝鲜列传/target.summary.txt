King Weiman of North Korea, originally from the state of Yan, settled in the empty region of Shangxiazhang after Yan's fall and Lu Wan's rebellion. During the reigns of Emperor Hui and Queen Gao of Han, the Liaodong governor appointed Weiman as a foreign minister to protect border barbarians, allowing him to use military power to subdue nearby states like Zhenfan and Lintun, expanding his territory. Weiman's rule passed to his son and grandson Youqu, who lured Han refugees and blocked smaller states from contacting the Han emperor.

In the second year of Yuanfeng, the Han sent Shehe to reprimand Youqu, but Youqu rejected the edict. Shehe assassinated a Korean general on his return, earning a promotion but provoking a Korean attack that killed him. The Han emperor then recruited pardoned prisoners to attack Korea. In autumn, General Yang Pu led 50,000 troops by sea, while General Xun She led forces from Liaodong. Youqu defended strategic points. Yang Pu's initial assault on Wangxian City failed, and his army scattered. Xun She also failed to break through Korean defenses.

The emperor sent Weishan to negotiate, and Youqu agreed to surrender, sending his prince with tribute. However, suspicions arose between the Han envoys and the Korean prince, leading to a breakdown. Weishan was executed for mishandling the situation. Xun She then defeated the Korean army at Haishui River and besieged Wangxian City, joined by Yang Pu. The two generals disagreed on strategy: Xun She favored aggressive attack, while Yang Pu sought peaceful surrender. This discord prolonged the siege.

The emperor sent Gongsun Sui to mediate, but Xun She convinced him to arrest Yang Pu on suspicion of treason. Gongsun Sui complied, but the emperor later executed him. Xun She merged the armies and intensified the attack. Korean officials, fearing defeat, killed King Youqu and surrendered. The Han established four counties, granting titles to defectors. Xun She was executed for rivalry and violating battle plans, while Yang Pu was reduced to civilian status after paying a fine. The historian notes that Korea's reliance on terrain led to its fall, and the generals' conflicts caused their downfall.
