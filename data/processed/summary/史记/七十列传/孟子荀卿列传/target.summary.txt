The historian Tai Shigong reflects on Mencius, noting that King Hui of Liang’s focus on profit troubled him, as Confucius rarely discussed profit to prevent resentment. Mencius, a student of Zisi’s disciples, failed to gain favor with King Xuan of Qi or King Hui of Liang, who found his ideals impractical. During an era of reforms—where states like Qin, Chu, Wei, and Qi thrived through figures like Shang Yang, Wu Qi, and Sun Bin—Mencius advocated ancient virtues, which did not align with the prevailing strategies of alliances and warfare. He returned home to compile the *Mencius* with Wan Zhang.

After Mencius, Zou Yan emerged in Qi, observing princes’ decadence and developing theories on yin-yang and the five virtues (metal, wood, water, fire, earth). His writings, such as *The End and the Beginning*, were vast and speculative, tracing history from the present to before heaven and earth formed. He divided the world into nine continents like Chixian Shenzhou, surrounded by seas, and linked dynastic changes to cosmic cycles. Despite their grandiosity, his ideas emphasized benevolence and frugality, impressing rulers like King Zhao of Yan, who treated him with high respect—unlike Confucius and Mencius, who faced hardship.

Other scholars included Chunyu Kun of Qi, known for his wit and ability to read minds; he remained silent before King Hui of Liang until the king focused on governance, later conversing for three days but declining a prime minister post. Shen Dao, Tian Pian, and Jiezi wrote on Huang-Lao doctrines, while Zou Shi expanded on Zou Yan’s work. The King of Qi honored these scholars with high positions and grand residences to attract talent.

Xunqing of Zhao, at age fifty, lectured in Qi, criticizing Zou Yan’s empty theories and Chunyu Kun’s cleverness. He became the eldest Jixia scholar, serving as Lanling magistrate in Chu after slander in Qi. His student Li Si became Qin’s prime minister. Xunqing condemned chaotic times and was buried in Lanling. Other figures like Gongsun Long, Hui Shi, Li Kui, and Mo Zhai—who advocated defense and cost-saving—also contributed to the era’s diverse philosophies.
