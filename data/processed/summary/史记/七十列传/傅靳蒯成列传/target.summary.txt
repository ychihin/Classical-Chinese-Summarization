The text summarizes the military careers and titles of three Han Dynasty figures: Fu Kuan, Jin She, and Zhou Xie (Kuai Chenghou), as recorded in historical records.

Fu Kuan, Marquis of Yangling, began as a cavalry officer under Pei Gong (Liu Bang). He participated in numerous battles against Qin and Chu forces, including attacks on Anyang, Gangli, and Kaifeng, and defeating generals like Zhao Bi and Yang Xiong. After Liu Bang became King of Han, Fu Kuan was promoted to right cavalry general and granted Diaoyin County. He fought against Xiang Yu, capturing an enemy cavalry general at Aocang Mountain. Serving under Han Xin and later Cao Shen, he helped pacify Qi, earning a hereditary title as Marquis of Yangling with 2,600 households. He served as right prime minister of Qi for five years, guarded against Tian Heng’s rebellion, and attacked Chen Xi under Zhou Bo. Fu Kuan died in the fifth year of Emperor Hui’s reign, posthumously named Jinghou. His lineage continued until his descendant Fu Yan was executed for treason with Liu An, King of Huainan.

Jin She, Marquis of Xinwu, served as an attendant under Pei Gong, raising troops in Wanqu. He defeated Qin forces under Li You and others, capturing numerous enemies and receiving the title Linping Lord. After Liu Bang became King of Han, Jin She was made Marquis of Jianwu and Cavalry Commander. He pacified the Three Qin Dynasties, defeated Zhang Ping in Longxi, and fought in the Chu-Han conflict, capturing Xing Shuo’s officers and surrendering 4,180 troops. He conquered Zhao forces, captured Pingyang, and forced Ye to surrender. He defeated Xiang Yu’s army at Chenggao and Lucheng, pacified Nanjun, and arrested Han Xin. He was titled Xinwuhou with 4,600 households, later increased to 5,300. Jin She died in the fifth year of his reign, posthumously named Suhou. His son Jin Ting inherited but lost the title in the third year of Emperor Wen’s reign.

Zhou Xie, Marquis of Kuai Cheng, from Peixian, served as Gaozu’s bodyguard and retainer. He accompanied Gaozu to Bashang, entered Shu and Han, and pacified the Three Qin Dynasties, receiving Chiyang as a fief. He led troops eastward to cut enemy supply lines and joined Han Xin at Xiangguo. Gaozu granted him the title of Marquis of Xinwu with 3,300 households, later changed to Kuai Chenghou. He once tearfully dissuaded Gaozu from personally attacking Chen Xi, earning special privileges. Zhou Xie died in the fifth year of Emperor Wen’s reign, posthumously named Zhenhou. His son Zhou Chang inherited but lost the title due to crime. Zhou Ju, his grandson, was later granted the title but lost it in the third year of Emperor Wu’s reign.

Sima Qian comments that Fu Kuan and Jin She received high titles, raised troops from Shandong, defeated dozens of enemy armies, and surrendered many cities without setbacks, attributing this to divine favor. Zhou Xie, loyal and unwavering, never doubted by Gaozu, wept during expeditions, showing a deeply loyal and kind heart.
