King Wu Liu Bi was the son of Liu Zhong, brother of Emperor Gaozu of Han. After Gaozu pacified the realm, he named Liu Zhong acting king, but Liu Zhong abandoned his post during a Xiongnu attack and was demoted to marquis. In the eleventh year of Gaozu, Yingbu rebelled, and Liu Bi, then twenty, served as a cavalry commander, helping defeat Yingbu. Gaozu, concerned about the unruly people of Wu and Kuaiji and lacking mature sons, appointed Liu Bi King of Wu over three counties. However, Gaozu noted Liu Bi's rebellious appearance and warned him against rebellion, which Liu Bi denied.

During Emperor Xiaohui and Queen Gao's reigns, Wu prospered by minting coins from copper mines and making salt from seawater, avoiding taxes and attracting desperadoes. Under Emperor Xiaowen, a chess dispute led to the Crown Prince of Wu's death at the hands of the Han crown prince. The King of Wu, angered, buried his son in Chang'an and began feigning illness to avoid court, gradually disobeying etiquette. The court detained Wu envoys, prompting the King of Wu to plan rebellion. Emperor Xiaowen later pardoned Wu's envoys and granted the king leniency, easing tensions. However, Wu continued to harbor fugitives and control its people for over forty years.

Chao Cuo, favored by Emperor Jing, repeatedly urged reducing Wu's territory, arguing that rebellion was inevitable. In the third year of Emperor Jing's reign, Chao Cuo reduced the lands of Chu, Zhao, Jiaoxi, and Wu. King Wu Liu Bi, fearing further losses, plotted rebellion. He sent Ying Gao to recruit King Jiaoxi, citing Chao Cuo's tyranny and promising joint action. King Jiaoxi agreed, and alliances formed with Qi, Sichuan, Jiaodong, Jinan, and Jibei. The Seven Kingdoms rebelled, with Wu conscripting over 200,000 men and gaining support from Dongyue. King Wu issued a letter to the princes, denouncing Chao Cuo and calling for a punitive campaign, offering rewards for capturing Han officials.

Emperor Jing responded by sending Zhou Yafu to attack Wu and Chu, and other generals against Qi and Zhao. Yuan An advised killing Chao Cuo to appease the rebels, and Chao Cuo was executed. Yuan An then attempted to negotiate with Wu, but King Wu refused, declaring himself the Eastern Emperor. Zhou Yafu, following a strategy to avoid direct confrontation, cut off Wu's food supplies. Wu's army, led by King Wu, failed to adopt effective strategies, such as a surprise attack on Wuguan or a swift march to Luoyang. After a prolonged siege of Liang, Wu's forces starved and scattered. King Wu fled to Dongyue but was assassinated by Dongyue forces bribed by Han. Other rebel kings, including those of Jiaoxi, Jiaodong, Sichuan, Jinan, and Zhao, were defeated or committed suicide, and their states were abolished.

The rebellion lasted from January to March, with Zhao falling later. Emperor Jing reinstated some royal lines and granted Wu's territory to others. The historian concludes that Liu Bi's rebellion stemmed from his son's death and greed, while Chao Cuo's plans led to his own death, and Yuan Ang's strategies brought initial favor but eventual disgrace.
