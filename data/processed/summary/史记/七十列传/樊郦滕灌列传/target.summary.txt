Fan Kuai, a native of Peixian County, initially made a living by killing dogs and selling meat. He joined Liu Bang (later Emperor Gaozu of Han) in raising troops, capturing Peixian, and serving as a servant. Throughout numerous battles, Fan Kuai consistently demonstrated valor, often leading assaults on cities and beheading enemy soldiers. His military exploits included defeating Qin forces at Puyang, Chengyang, and Kaifeng, among others, earning him successive titles and promotions from Pei Gong (Liu Bang). A pivotal moment occurred at the Hongmen Banquet, where Xiang Yu plotted to kill Pei Gong. Fan Kuai, stationed outside, burst into the camp with an iron shield, confronted Xiang Yu, and condemned his actions, ultimately saving Pei Gong's life. Following Xiang Yu's defeat, Fan Kuai continued to serve Gaozu, suppressing rebellions by Zang Tu, Han Xin, Chen Xi, and Lu Wan, and was enfeoffed as Marquis of Wuyang with 5,400 households. He married Empress Lü's sister, Lü Xu, and remained close to Gaozu. During Gaozu's illness, Fan Kuai boldly entered the palace to urge him to attend to state affairs. Later, slandered for conspiring with the Lü clan, Gaozu ordered his execution, but Chen Ping delayed, and after Gaozu's death, Empress Lü released him. Fan Kuai died in the sixth year of Emperor Hui's reign, posthumously titled Marquis Wu. His descendants held the title until it was revoked in Emperor Jing's time due to legal transgressions.

Li Shang, from Gaoyang, initially gathered followers during Chen Sheng's rebellion and later joined Pei Gong with over 4,000 soldiers. He distinguished himself in battles, capturing cities like Changshe and Wan, and was appointed Lord of Xincheng and later Captain of Longxi. Li Shang fought against Xiang Yu, defeated forces of Yong King Zhanghan, and pacified regions including Beidi and Shangjun. He was enfeoffed as Marquis of Zhuoxian with 5,000 households, later serving as Right Prime Minister and leading campaigns against Chen Xi and Yingbu. His fief was changed to Quzhou with 5,100 households. Li Shang served under Emperor Xiaohui but was unable to govern due to illness. His son Li Ji succeeded but was executed for treason during the Rebellion of the Seven States. Another son, Li Jian, continued the lineage until it was eventually revoked for legal violations.

Xiahou Ying, from Peixian, began as a stable hand and befriended Gaozu, even taking a false imprisonment for him. He joined Gaozu's uprising, serving as a chariot commander and fighting in key battles such as Dong'a and Kaifeng. After Xiang Yu's defeat, Xiahou Ying rescued Gaozu's children during a retreat and was enfeoffed as Marquis of Ruyin with 6,900 households. He served as a servant under Gaozu, Xiaohui, and Empress Lü, and played a role in welcoming Emperor Wen to the throne. He died posthumously titled Wenhou. His descendants held the title until it was revoked for adultery.

Guan Ying, a silk merchant from Suiyang, joined Pei Gong and fought in numerous campaigns, including against Qin forces and Xiang Yu. He distinguished himself as a cavalry commander, defeating Chu forces and capturing key figures like Zhou Lan. After Xiang Yu's death, Guan Ying pursued and killed him, leading to his enfeoffment as Marquis of Yingyin with 5,000 households. He later suppressed rebellions by Zang Tu, Han Xin, Chen Xi, and Yingbu. During the Lü clan crisis, he feigned support but secretly allied with Zhou Bo to establish Emperor Wen, becoming Taiwei and later Prime Minister. He died in office, posthumously titled Yihou. His descendants held the title until it was revoked for bribery.

Sima Qian notes that these figures, originally humble commoners, rose to prominence and established lasting legacies through their service to the Han dynasty.
