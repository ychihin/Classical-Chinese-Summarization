Sima Ranju, a descendant of Tian Wan, was recommended by Yan Ying to Qi Jinggong during a crisis when Jin and Yan attacked Qi. Despite his humble status, Ranju’s literary and military talents impressed the king, who appointed him general. Ranju requested a trusted minister, Zhuang Jia, to supervise the army. They agreed to meet at the camp gate at noon. Ranju arrived early and set up timing devices, but Zhuang Jia, delayed by farewell feasts, arrived at dusk. Ranju condemned his lateness, citing that a general must forsake family, friendships, and personal safety in war. He executed Zhuang Jia under military law, shocking the army.

When Qi Jinggong’s envoy arrived with a pardon, racing into camp, Ranju declared that generals in the field need not accept all royal orders. He executed the envoy’s servants and horses, parading them before the troops. Ranju then personally cared for soldiers, sharing provisions and tending to the sick. After three days, the army was revitalized. The Jin and Yan forces withdrew upon learning of this discipline, and Qi reclaimed lost territory.

Returning in triumph, Ranju was appointed Grand Sima by Qi Jinggong, elevating the Tian family’s status. However, officials Bao, Gao, and Guo slandered him, leading to his dismissal and death from illness. The Tian family later retaliated, killing the Gao and Guo clans. Tian He, a descendant, became King Qi Wei, who imitated Ranju’s methods and incorporated his strategies into the “Sima’s Art of War.” Sima Qian notes that while Ranju’s achievements were for a small state, the “Sima’s Art of War” is profound, and he refrains from further commentary, presenting only this biography.
