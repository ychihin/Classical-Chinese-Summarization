Qu Yuan, named Ping, shared the same surname as the Chu royal family and served as a left disciple under King Huai of Chu. He was knowledgeable, had a strong memory, understood statecraft, and was skilled in rhetoric. King Huai trusted him deeply, but the official Shangguan, jealous of Qu Yuan's talents, slandered him by claiming Qu Yuan boasted about his own decrees. King Huai believed this and alienated Qu Yuan. Grief-stricken by the king's inability to discern truth from falsehood, Qu Yuan wrote "Li Sao," a work expressing his sorrow and indignation. The text praises "Li Sao" for combining the virtues of "The Book of Songs: Guofeng" and "Xiaoya," using metaphors like vanilla to symbolize noble character, and comparing Qu Yuan's purity to the sun and moon.

After Qu Yuan's demotion, Qin, fearing the Qi-Chu alliance, sent Zhang Yi to deceive Chu. King Huai, swayed by greed, broke ties with Qi and was tricked into believing Qin would cede six hundred miles of land, only to receive six. Enraged, King Huai attacked Qin but suffered a devastating defeat, losing 80,000 soldiers and the Hanzhong region. Wei also attacked Chu, and Qi refused aid. Later, Qin proposed peace, but King Huai demanded Zhang Yi. Zhang Yi bribed Chu officials and King Huai's concubine, Zheng Xiu, to secure his release. Qu Yuan, then an envoy to Qi, urged King Huai to kill Zhang Yi, but it was too late. Subsequently, vassal states defeated Chu, killing General Tang Wei. King Zhao of Qin invited King Huai for a meeting; Qu Yuan warned against it, but King Huai's son Zilan persuaded him to go. King Huai was captured, refused to cede land, fled to Zhao (which rejected him), and died in Qin. His body was returned to Chu.

King Qingxiang succeeded King Huai and appointed Zilan as Ling Yin. Qu Yuan, exiled but still loyal, blamed Zilan for King Huai's death and hoped for reform. However, Zilan and Shangguan slandered Qu Yuan to King Qingxiang, leading to his banishment. Qu Yuan wandered by the river, disheveled and gaunt. A fisherman asked why he was exiled; Qu Yuan replied that he alone was pure in a corrupt world. The fisherman advised adapting to the times, but Qu Yuan refused to taint his integrity, preferring to die in the river. He wrote "Huaisha," expressing his resolve to maintain his principles despite injustice. He then drowned himself in the Miluo River.

After Qu Yuan's death, Chu scholars like Song Yu imitated his style but lacked his boldness. Chu weakened and was conquered by Qin decades later. Over a century later, Jia Yi of the Han Dynasty, while serving as Grand Tutor of Changsha, wrote a poem to honor Qu Yuan. Jia Yi, a talented young scholar from Luoyang, was favored by Emperor Wen of Han but slandered by officials like Zhou Bo, leading to his demotion. He wrote "Ode to the Birds" to console himself, reflecting on the cyclical nature of fortune and the futility of worldly concerns. Later, he served as tutor to King Liang Huai, who died in a riding accident. Jia Yi, blaming himself, died of grief at age 33. The historian Sima Qian, after reading Qu Yuan's works and Jia Yi's poems, initially criticized Qu Yuan for not seeking refuge elsewhere but later, after reading "Ode to the Birds," understood his equanimity toward life and death.
