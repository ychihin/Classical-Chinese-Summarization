Li Si was a native of Shangcai in the State of Chu. As a young county official, he observed rats in the toilet fleeing from people and dogs, contrasting them with granary rats that ate stored grain undisturbed. This led him to conclude that a person's competence depends on their environment. He studied under Xunzi to learn the art of governing an empire. Afterward, deeming the King of Chu unworthy and the six kingdoms too weak for him to achieve merit, he decided to go west to Qin. He told Xunzi that opportunity must be seized, and that the King of Qin's ambition to unify the world was a perfect chance for a commoner to gain fame and wealth. He considered humble status the greatest shame and poverty the greatest sorrow, rejecting the notion of a scholar who claims to be above worldly pursuits.

Upon arriving in Qin, Li Si became a retainer of Prime Minister Lü Buwei, who appointed him as a Lang official. He then advised the King of Qin that while Duke Mu of Qin had dominated the world, he could not annex the six kingdoms because the Zhou dynasty's prestige had not yet declined. Since Duke Xiao of Qin, the Zhou had weakened, and the six kingdoms had formed. Now, with Qin's power and the king's wisdom, the time was ripe to unify the world. He warned that hesitation would allow the princes to strengthen and form alliances, making conquest impossible. The King of Qin appointed Li Si as Chief Historian, following his plan to bribe or assassinate key figures in other states to alienate them from their rulers, followed by military attacks. Li Si was later made a Guest Minister.

When the Korean spy Zheng Guo was discovered, Qin's nobles urged the king to expel all foreign guests, including Li Si. Li Si wrote a letter arguing against this. He cited Duke Mu of Qin, who used five non-Qin officials to dominate Xirong; Duke Xiao, who used Shang Yang to make Qin rich and powerful; King Hui, who used Zhang Yi to expand territory and break up alliances; and King Zhao, who used Fan Sui to strengthen the royal house. He argued that these four rulers relied on guest ministers. He then compared the king's love for foreign treasures, music, and women to his rejection of foreign talent, stating this was not the way to unify the world. He concluded that expelling guests would weaken Qin and aid its enemies. The king abolished the expulsion order, restored Li Si's position, and later promoted him to Tingwei. Over twenty years, Qin unified the world, and Li Si became Prime Minister.

In the 34th year of Qin Shi Huang's reign, Dr. Zhou Qingchen praised the emperor, but Chun Yuyue advised enfeoffing clan members, warning that without strong assistants, the dynasty could not last. The First Emperor asked Li Si, who dismissed the argument. Li Si wrote that in ancient times, scholars used the past to criticize the present, undermining the emperor's authority. He requested that all "Poems," "Books," and works of the hundred schools be banned, with punishment for those who disobeyed, except for books on medicine, divination, and planting. The emperor approved, confiscating these texts to make the people ignorant. Li Si also contributed to unifying the writing system and building imperial villas. His sons married Qin princesses, and his daughters married Qin royalty. At a banquet, Li Si lamented that his wealth and status had reached their peak, and he feared the decline that must follow.

In the 37th year, the First Emperor died at Shaqiu. Li Si, Zhao Gao, and Hu Hai kept his death secret. Zhao Gao withheld the emperor's edict to Fusu and persuaded Hu Hai to usurp the throne, arguing that power was in their hands and that great deeds required decisive action. Hu Hai agreed, and Zhao Gao convinced Li Si by warning that Fusu would replace him with Meng Tian, leading to his death. Li Si wept but complied. They forged an edict making Hu Hai crown prince and another ordering Fusu and Meng Tian to commit suicide. Fusu obeyed, but Meng Tian was imprisoned. Hu Hai became the Second Emperor, and Zhao Gao gained great power.

The Second Emperor wanted to indulge his desires, but Zhao Gao warned that the princes and former ministers were suspicious and might rebel. He advised implementing harsh laws, killing all opponents, and replacing them with loyalists. The Second Emperor agreed, revising the law. Many princes and ministers were executed, including ten princes beheaded in Xianyang and twelve princesses dismembered. Prince Gao requested to die with the First Emperor, which the Second Emperor approved. Laws grew crueler, taxes heavier, and corvée endless, leading to rebellions like that of Chen Sheng and Wu Guang. Li Si tried to remonstrate but was blamed by the Second Emperor, who argued that a ruler should use the world to satisfy his own desires, not labor like Yao or Yu.

Li Si, fearing for his position, wrote a letter flattering the Second Emperor, advocating strict supervision and severe punishments to ensure the ruler's absolute power and pleasure. He cited Shen Buhai, Han Fei, and Shang Yang, arguing that harsh penalties for minor crimes prevent greater offenses. The Second Emperor was pleased and intensified supervision and taxation, leading to half the population being prisoners and corpses piled in the markets. Zhao Gao, fearing exposure, persuaded the Second Emperor to stay in the palace and not meet ministers, allowing Zhao Gao to control all affairs. Zhao Gao then tricked Li Si into seeking an audience during the emperor's leisure, angering the Second Emperor. Zhao Gao accused Li Si of involvement in the Shaqiu conspiracy and of colluding with rebels through his son Li You. The Second Emperor ordered an investigation.

Li Si wrote to expose Zhao Gao's ambition, comparing him to usurpers like Tian Chang. The Second Emperor defended Zhao Gao, but Li Si warned of his greed. The Second Emperor told Zhao Gao, who accused Li Si of plotting rebellion. Li Si was arrested and tortured into confessing. In prison, he lamented that the Second Emperor was worse than Jie and Zhou, killing brothers and loyal ministers, building the Epang Palace, and overtaxing the people. He wrote a letter listing his seven crimes, which were actually achievements, but Zhao Gao suppressed it. When the Second Emperor sent investigators, Li Si, fearing torture, did not change his confession. He was sentenced to five punishments and executed in the Xianyang market, along with his entire clan.

After Li Si's death, Zhao Gao became Prime Minister. He tested his power by presenting a deer and calling it a horse, which all officials affirmed. The Second Emperor, confused, went to fast in Shanglin Garden, where he killed a pedestrian. Zhao Gao advised him to leave the palace to pray, then forced him to commit suicide. Zhao Gao tried to take the throne but failed, so he installed Ziying, a younger brother of the First Emperor. Ziying, fearing Zhao Gao, feigned illness and had him assassinated, annihilating his clan. Three months later, Liu Bang's army entered Xianyang, and Ziying surrendered. Xiang Yu later killed them, and Qin fell. The Grand Historian commented that Li Si, though a commoner, achieved great success but failed to correct the emperor's faults, instead flattering him and implementing harsh laws. He listened to Zhao Gao, deposed the rightful heir, and when rebellions arose, his belated advice was foolish. His loyalty was questionable, and his achievements, while great, were not comparable to those of the Duke of Zhou.
