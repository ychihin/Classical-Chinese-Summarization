The text begins by contrasting an idealized ancient prosperity, where people were self-sufficient and content within their own communities, with the modern reality where such isolation is impractical. It argues that human desires for pleasure, wealth, and power are deeply ingrained and cannot be changed by persuasion. The best governance, therefore, is to let nature take its course, followed by guiding, educating, and correcting, with conflict as the last resort.

The text then describes the natural distribution of resources across different regions of China, from the west of Mount Wei to the east of Mount Tai, the south of the Yangtze River, and the north of Longmen. It explains that people rely on farmers, miners, fishermen, craftsmen, and merchants to meet their needs, and that this economic activity occurs naturally without government decree, driven by self-interest and the pursuit of profit. The "Book of Zhou" is cited to emphasize that agriculture, industry, commerce, and resource extraction are the four pillars of a nation's wealth.

Historical examples illustrate the principles of wealth creation. Jiang Taigong turned the barren, sparsely populated state of Qi into a prosperous center of trade and manufacturing by encouraging textile production and commerce. Later, Guan Zhong continued this work, enabling Duke Huan of Qi to become a hegemon. The text emphasizes that a full granary leads to knowledge of etiquette, and that wealth enables virtuous behavior. It also details the strategies of Ji Ran, who advised King Gou Jian of Yue on managing resources and prices based on cyclical patterns of good and bad years, leading to Yue's wealth and eventual victory over Wu. Fan Li, who implemented these strategies, later applied them to personal business, becoming immensely wealthy as Tao Zhugong.

The text profiles several other successful individuals, including Bai Gui, who mastered market timing and price speculation; Yi Dun, who made a fortune from salt; and Guo Zong, who profited from iron smelting. It also notes the rise of Wu Shiluo, a livestock trader who gained favor with King Rong and was later honored by Qin Shi Huang, and Widow Qing of Bajun, who maintained her family's cinnabar monopoly and was also respected by the emperor. These examples demonstrate that wealth can elevate even humble individuals to a status comparable to feudal lords.

The text then surveys the economic geography and customs of various regions under the Han Dynasty, from the fertile Guanzhong area to the remote Bashu region, and from the central plains of Hedong, Hanoi, and Henan to the northern frontiers of Yan and Dai. It describes how local products, trade routes, and historical legacies shape the economic activities and social character of each area. For instance, the people of Qi are resourceful and love to talk, while those of Zou and Lu are cautious and favor Confucian etiquette. The text concludes that all people, whether officials, merchants, farmers, or outlaws, are ultimately driven by the pursuit of wealth.

The final section provides a detailed account of various profitable enterprises and the individuals who excelled at them. It lists the annual output of goods—such as wine, vinegar, livestock, grain, and timber—that can generate wealth comparable to a noble household. It then profiles specific successful families, like the Zhuo family of Shu, who prospered through iron smelting after being relocated, and the Ren family of Xuanqu, who stored grain during a famine and became wealthy. The text emphasizes that wealth can be achieved through diverse means, from farming and commerce to seemingly humble trades like selling water or sharpening knives, and that success depends on skill and adaptability rather than a fixed profession. Ultimately, it asserts that a wealthy person can enjoy a status akin to a king, a condition it calls "Su Feng."
