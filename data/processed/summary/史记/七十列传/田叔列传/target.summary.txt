Uncle Tian, a native of Xingcheng in Zhao State and a descendant of the Tian family of Qi, was a strict and honest man who studied Huang-Lao theories and swordsmanship. Recommended by Zhao Wu, he served as a doctor under King Zhao Zhang Ao, known for his uprightness. During Emperor Gaozu of Han’s campaign against Chen Xi’s rebellion, the emperor insulted King Zhao, angering Zhao Wu and others who plotted rebellion. King Zhao refused, but Guan Gao secretly conspired to kill the emperor. When discovered, Zhao Wu committed suicide, and Guan Gao was imprisoned. An edict threatened the three-clan punishment for anyone following King Zhao to Chang’an. Meng Shu, Tian Shu, and over a dozen others shaved their heads, wore prison clothes, and pretended to be King Zhao’s slaves to accompany him. After Guan Gao’s rebellion was resolved, King Zhao was deposed as Marquis of Xuan Ping and recommended these men. The emperor appointed them all as county governors or ministers. Uncle Tian served as governor of Hanzhong for over ten years. After Empress Gao’s death, Emperor Wen of Han ascended and asked Uncle Tian about loyal and virtuous men. Uncle Tian praised Meng Shu, who had shaved his head and followed King Zhao to die for him, and who, as governor of Yunzhong, spared his tired soldiers from fighting the Xiongnu, leading to his dismissal. Emperor Wen reinstated Meng Shu. Later, Uncle Tian lost his position due to a crime. Emperor Jing of Han sent him to investigate King Xiao of Liang’s assassination of Yuan Ang. Uncle Tian advised against pursuing the case to avoid upsetting the Queen Mother, and was made prime minister of Lu. There, he punished those accusing King Lu of theft, and King Lu returned the property. Uncle Tian refused to rest in pavilions while King Lu hunted, curbing the king’s excess. He died as prime minister of Lu. His son Tian Ren refused gold offerings, preserving his father’s reputation. Tian Ren became General Wei Qing’s retainer, later a doctor, and served as prime minister with a two-thousand-stone salary. He lost his position, then became Jingfu Duwei and Si Zhi. Implicated in the prince’s rebellion, he let the prince escape the city gate and was executed. Ren An, from Xingyang, rose from poverty to become a retainer of General Wei Qing alongside Tian Ren. Both were recommended by Zhao Yu for their talents. Emperor Wu of Han appointed Ren An to supervise the Northern Army and Tian Ren to oversee border farming. Ren An became governor of Yizhou. Tian Ren, as prime minister, inspected Sanhe, executing corrupt officials, and became Prime Minister Sizhi. During the prince’s rebellion, Tian Ren let the prince escape and was executed. Ren An, as Northern Army protector, refused the prince’s summons but was later accused of duplicity and executed. The text warns against failing to retreat from wealth and power, citing Fan Li as a model.
