General Li Guang, a native of Chengji County, Longxi, descended from Li Xin, a Qin general. Skilled in archery from a young age, he joined the army during Emperor Wen's reign, distinguishing himself against the Xiongnu and becoming a Zhonglang. Emperor Wen lamented his unlucky timing, suggesting he would have been a marquis in Emperor Gaozu's era. Under Emperor Jing, Li Guang served as a cavalry general, capturing enemy flags during the Rebellion of the Seven States but receiving no reward due to a private seal from the King of Liang. He later governed several border commanderies, earning fame for his fierce fighting.

During a Xiongnu invasion of Shangjun, Li Guang, with only a hundred cavalry, encountered thousands of Xiongnu. He cleverly dismounted and unsaddled his men, feigning a lure, which deterred the enemy from attacking. At night, the Xiongnu retreated, suspecting an ambush. Under Emperor Wu, Li Guang became a palace guard chief alongside Cheng Bushi. While Cheng's army was strict and disciplined, Li Guang's was simple and flexible, earning soldiers' loyalty. The Xiongnu feared Li Guang's strategy, though his lax approach sometimes led to danger.

Li Guang was captured by the Xiongnu but escaped by feigning death, stealing a horse, and shooting pursuers. He was sentenced to death for losses but redeemed himself with money, becoming a commoner. Later, as Right Beiping governor, he killed a disrespectful Ba Ling guard and was called the "Flying General of Han," causing the Xiongnu to avoid him for years. He once mistook a stone for a tiger and shot it, but could not repeat the feat. Li Guang was generous, sharing rewards and food with soldiers, and lived simply despite decades of high office.

In the sixth year of Yuanshuo, Li Guang joined General Wei Qing's campaign but had no military merits. Two years later, he was surrounded by 40,000 Xiongnu with 4,000 cavalry. His son Li Gan's bravery calmed the troops, and Li Guang's personal archery killed several enemy officers, forcing a retreat. Despite heavy losses, Li Guang's calmness earned admiration. His cousin Li Cai, though less talented, became a marquis and prime minister, while Li Guang remained unenfeoffed. Astrologer Wang Shuo attributed this to Li Guang's past massacre of 800 surrendered Qiang rebels.

In the fourth year of Yuanshou, Li Guang, now a former general, joined Wei Qing's campaign against the Xiongnu. Wei Qing, warned by the emperor about Li Guang's age and bad luck, ordered him to take a circuitous eastern route. Li Guang protested but was overruled, leading to his army losing its way. After the campaign, Wei Qing demanded an inquiry. Li Guang, refusing to face humiliation, declared, "I have fought over seventy battles. Now, at over sixty, I cannot bear the insults of clerks," and committed suicide. His death caused widespread mourning.

Li Guang's sons—Danghu, Jiao, and Gan—served as officials. Danghu died young, and Jiao became a prefect. Li Gan, a marquis, wounded Wei Qing in revenge but was later killed by Huo Qubing, who claimed it was a deer accident. Li Guang's grandson Li Ling, a skilled archer, led 5,000 infantry against 80,000 Xiongnu, killing over 10,000 before surrendering due to lack of supplies. The Han executed his family, ending the Li family's reputation. The historian concluded that Li Guang's honest character, like peach and plum trees, naturally drew people to him, despite his lack of eloquence.
