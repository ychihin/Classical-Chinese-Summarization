Tian Dan, a distant descendant of the Qi royal family, initially served as a minor official in Linyi but was not favored by King Qi Min. When Yan's general Le Yi attacked Qi, Tian Dan fled to Anping, where he advised his people to reinforce their chariot axles with iron sheets. This precaution allowed him and his clan to escape when Anping fell, while others were captured due to broken axles. They retreated to Jimo, one of only two Qi cities not conquered by Yan.

After King Qi Min was killed in Ju, Yan besieged Jimo. The city's defenders were defeated, and the soldiers elected Tian Dan as their leader, citing his tactical foresight. When King Yan Zhao died and King Yan Hui succeeded him, a rift with Le Yi emerged. Tian Dan exploited this by sending agents to spread rumors that Le Yi planned to become king of Qi, leading Yan Hui to replace him with Qi Jie. Le Yi fled to Zhao, angering Yan troops.

Tian Dan then employed psychological tactics. He had soldiers offer sacrifices in courtyards, attracting birds that Yan soldiers mistook for divine signs. He declared a god had descended and appointed a soldier as a "spiritual teacher," attributing all orders to him. He also provoked Yan into mutilating Qi captives and desecrating ancestral graves, which enraged Jimo's defenders. When morale peaked, Tian Dan joined his troops in labor, shared food, and hid elite soldiers while displaying weak defenders. He feigned surrender and bribed Yan's general to spare families, lulling Yan into complacency.

At night, Tian Dan unleashed a thousand oxen with painted dragon patterns, sharp knives on horns, and burning reeds on tails through holes in the city wall. The fiery oxen charged Yan's camp, followed by 5,000 silent warriors and townspeople making noise. Panicked, Yan fled, and Qi killed Qi Jie. Tian Dan pursued, recovering over 70 lost cities. He then welcomed King Xiang of Qi back to Linzi, who granted Tian Dan the title Lord Anping.

The text concludes with Tai Shigong praising Tian Dan's use of surprise tactics, and recounts two subplots: the discovery of King Xiang (Fazhang) and his marriage to Tai Shi's daughter, and the martyrdom of Wang Yu, a recluse who refused Yan's offer to become a general, inspiring Qi officials to rally behind King Xiang.
