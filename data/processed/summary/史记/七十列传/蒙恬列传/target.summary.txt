Meng Tian, a Qin general, hailed from Qi through his grandfather Meng Ao, who served Qin under King Zhao and later as a general under King Zhuangxiang and the First Emperor, conquering territories in Han, Zhao, and Wei before his death in 7 BCE. Meng Tian’s father, Meng Wu, also a general, defeated Chu alongside Wang Jian. Meng Tian began as a legal clerk, became a general in 26 BCE, and led 300,000 troops to expel northern nomads, reclaim land south of the Yellow River, and construct the Great Wall from Lintao to Liaodong. He garrisoned Shangjun for over a decade, intimidating the Xiongnu. His brother Meng Yi served as a trusted advisor to the First Emperor.

Zhao Gao, a eunuch of Zhao royal descent, rose to favor due to legal expertise, teaching Prince Hu Hai. When Zhao Gao committed a serious crime, Meng Yi sentenced him to death, but the First Emperor pardoned him. During the First Emperor’s fatal illness in 37 BCE, Meng Yi was sent to pray; after the emperor’s death, Zhao Gao conspired with Li Si and Hu Hai to install Hu Hai as crown prince, fabricating charges against Fusu and Meng Tian. Fusu committed suicide; Meng Tian was imprisoned. Zhao Gao, fearing the Meng family’s resurgence, falsely accused Meng Yi of opposing Hu Hai’s appointment, leading to his imprisonment in Dai. After Hu Hai became the Second Emperor, Zhao Gao slandered the Mengs daily. Ziying advised against killing them, warning of past disasters from killing loyal ministers, but Hu Hai ignored him.

Meng Yi was ordered to commit suicide; he defended his loyalty and criticized unjust executions, citing historical examples, but was killed. Meng Tian, ordered to die, declared his innocence and loyalty, recounting the Duke of Zhou’s story to urge the emperor to reconsider. The envoy refused to relay his words. Meng Tian lamented his sin of cutting earth’s veins while building the Great Wall and poisoned himself. Sima Qian criticized Meng Tian for failing to counsel the First Emperor to aid the people after conquests, instead building the Great Wall, which led to his and his brother’s deaths.
