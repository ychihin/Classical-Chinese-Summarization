Guan Zhong, also known as Yiwu, was from Yingshang. In his youth, he frequently interacted with Bao Shuya, who recognized his wisdom and talent. Despite Guan Zhong's poverty and tendency to take advantage of him, Bao Shuya remained kind and without complaint. Bao Shuya later served Prince Xiaobai of Qi, while Guan Zhong served Prince Jiu. After Xiaobai became Duke Huan of Qi, he had Prince Jiu killed and imprisoned Guan Zhong. Bao Shuya then recommended Guan Zhong to Duke Huan. Appointed to power, Guan Zhong helped Duke Huan become an overlord, uniting the princes and bringing order to the realm through his wisdom.

Guan Zhong reflected that Bao Shuya never judged him harshly: when Guan Zhong took more profit in their business, Bao Shuya understood his poverty; when his plans failed, Bao Shuya knew fortune's ups and downs; when he was dismissed from office, Bao Shuya saw missed opportunities; when he fled battles, Bao Shuya recognized his duty to his mother; when he endured imprisonment, Bao Shuya knew he valued fame over minor shame. Guan Zhong declared, "My parents gave me life, but Bao Shuya truly understands me." After recommending Guan Zhong, Bao Shuya served under him. Their descendants received fiefs for generations, producing many famous officials. The world praised Bao Shuya's ability to recognize talent over Guan Zhong's abilities.

As Prime Minister, Guan Zhong enriched Qi by circulating goods and accumulating wealth, aligning with the people's desires. He wrote that ample food leads to understanding etiquette, and abundant clothing allows distinction between honor and disgrace. He emphasized that government decrees should flow like water, easily implemented when consistent with circumstances. He turned disaster into fortune and failure into success, such as using Duke Huan's desire to attack Cai as an opportunity to blame Chu for not paying tribute to Zhou, or persuading Duke Huan to honor a treaty at Ke, winning the princes' submission. Guan Zhong's wealth rivaled a monarch's, but the people did not consider him extravagant. After his death, Qi followed his policies and remained strong.

More than a century later, Yan Ying, also known as Yanzi, emerged from Qi. He served Dukes Ling, Zhuang, and Jing, respected for his frugality and hard work. As prime minister, he ate no more than two meats and his family wore no silk. He acted honestly in court and followed orders when the ruler was righteous. Yanzi once ransomed Yue Shi's father from imprisonment. When Yue Shi's father criticized Yanzi for not treating him politely after understanding him, Yanzi apologized and honored him as a guest. Yanzi's coachman, initially proud, became humble after his wife threatened to leave him for his arrogance compared to Yanzi's modesty. Yanzi recommended the coachman to become a doctor.

Tai Shigong noted that he read Guan Zhong's and Yanzi's works but focused on their unknown deeds. He questioned whether Confucius despised Guan Zhong for not encouraging Duke Huan to practice the kingly way, instead assisting him as an overlord. He praised Yanzi for crying over Duke Zhuang's body and daring to offend the monarch, expressing a wish to serve Yanzi if he were alive.
