The text begins with Confucius and Laozi discussing governance: Confucius argues that laws and punishments prevent crime but do not instill shame, while morality and etiquette lead to genuine reform. Laozi adds that strict laws increase theft. Tai Shigong affirms these views, noting that harsh legal nets in the past led to deceit and ruin. He observes that after the Han Dynasty simplified Qin laws, officials became honest and people peaceful, concluding that good governance relies on the ruler’s generosity, not legal severity.

The narrative then details cruel officials from the Han era. During Empress Gao’s reign, Hou Feng oppressed the royal family and was executed after the Lu clan’s fall. Under Emperor Jing, Chao Cuo’s harshness sparked the Rebellion of the Seven Kingdoms, leading to his death. Zhi Du, a lieutenant, was known for his bravery and integrity. He refused to save a concubine from a boar, earning the Empress Dowager’s favor. As governor of Jinan, he executed a powerful clan, ending crime. He was fair, refused bribes, and prioritized duty over family. He later arrested King Linjiang, who committed suicide, leading to Zhi Du’s dismissal and eventual execution by Empress Dowager Dou. Ning Cheng, a captain, was cunning and cruel, bullying superiors and subordinates alike. He imitated Zhi Du but lacked his integrity, though he intimidated the royal family. Under Emperor Wu, he was punished but later became wealthy through land rental, wielding influence over officials.

Zhou Yangyou, a relative of the Huainan king, was tyrannical, bending laws to favor friends and kill enemies. He clashed with the governor Shentu Gong, leading to Shentu’s suicide and Zhou’s execution. Zhao Yu, a censor, co-created strict laws with Zhang Tang, increasing legal severity. Zhang Tang, from Du County, showed early talent in legal cases. He befriended powerful figures and rose to censor, crafting laws to control officials. He was cunning, pretending to admire Confucianism while using harsh methods. He handled major rebellions, minted coins, and monopolized salt and iron, draining state resources. His arrogance led to his downfall: after conflicts with officials and accusations of corruption, he committed suicide, leaving minimal wealth. His mother refused a lavish burial, and the emperor later executed his accusers.

Yi Zong, a former robber, became a harsh official, executing hundreds in Dingxiang and terrorizing Nanyang. Wang Wenshu, a tomb robber, rose to governor, using informants and brutal tactics to eliminate thieves, boasting he needed more winter days to kill. He later became lieutenant, flattering the powerful while crushing the weak. He committed suicide after corruption charges. Yin Qi, a rigid official, killed without mercy, dying in poverty. The text concludes with a surge in banditry, leading to the “Senior Law” punishing officials for failing to catch thieves, which ironically increased crime. Jiaxuan and Du Zhou continued harsh practices, with Du Zhou justifying decisions based on the emperor’s will rather than law. Tai Shigong reflects that while some cruel officials like Zhi Du were principled, others like Du Zhou were sycophantic, leading to legal decay and corruption.
