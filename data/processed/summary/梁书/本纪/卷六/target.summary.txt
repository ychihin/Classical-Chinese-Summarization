The emperor, Fang Zhi (courtesy name Huixiang, baby name Fazhen), was the ninth son of Shizu. He was first made Marquis of Xingliang, then King of Jin'an with a 2,000-household fief. He served as General Pingnan and Governor of Jiangzhou. In the third year of Chengsheng, after Jiangling fell, Wang Sengbian and Chen Baxian agreed to welcome him as emperor. He entered the capital in the fourth year, and Wang Sengbian was appointed to high military and civil posts. Qi sent Xiao Yuanming to claim the Liang throne, but after initial defeat, Wang Sengbian accepted Xiao Yuanming as crown prince. Chen Baxian then killed Wang Sengbian, deposed Xiao Yuanming, and Emperor Jing ascended the throne.

In the first year of Shaotai, an edict lamented the dynasty's misfortunes and declared a change of reign title, with amnesties and promotions. Chen Baxian was appointed to key military and civil roles. Rebellions by Du Gong, Wei Zai, Ren Yue, and Xu Sihui followed. Chen Baxian suppressed Wei Zai and defeated Ren Yue and Xu Sihui, who fled to Jiangxi. Further campaigns involved Zhou Wenyu and Hou Andu against Zhang Biao, who was killed. Qi forces under Xiao Gui were defeated by Chen Baxian. The year saw various appointments, deaths, and military actions, including the defeat of Qi forces and the capture of rebel leaders.

In the second year of Taiping, edicts were issued to restore Confucian rituals, establish local officials, and reorganize administrative divisions. Rebellions by Xiao Bo and others were suppressed by Zhou Wenyu and Hou Andu. Currency reforms were enacted, and Qi sought peace. Chen Baxian was progressively elevated to Prime Minister, Duke of Chen, and finally King of Chen, with grand ceremonial privileges. An edict formally abdicated the throne to Chen, citing the dynasty's decline and Chen's merits. Emperor Jing was made King of Jiangyin and died at sixteen, posthumously named Emperor Jing.

The historian Wei Zheng commented that the Liang dynasty collapsed due to chaos and the emperor's abdication. He praised Emperor Gaozu's talents but criticized his neglect of agriculture, love of luxury, and later decline into tyranny and poor judgment, leading to disaster. He noted Emperor Taizong's intelligence but faulted his ornate writing style and lack of substance. Emperor Yuan was criticized for jealousy, cruelty, and prioritizing personal grudges over national defense, leading to his downfall. Emperor Jing, facing family ruin and a difficult fate, had no choice but to concede power.
