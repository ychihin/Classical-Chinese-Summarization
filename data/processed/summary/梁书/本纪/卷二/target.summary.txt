In the summer of the first year of Tianjian, Emperor Gaozu ascended the throne in the southern suburbs, offering a sacrifice to heaven to announce the mandate. The text declares that the Qi dynasty had exhausted its fortune, and prosperity was imminent, with heaven's inspiration bestowed upon Xiao Yan. It justifies the dynastic change by citing historical precedents like Yao, Shun, Han, Wei, and Jin, emphasizing that rulers are chosen based on worthiness, not hereditary right. The Qi monarch's cruelty and corruption led to chaos, prompting Xiao Yan to raise an army, defeat the tyrant, and establish a new ruler. Auspicious signs from the sun, stars, and mountains confirmed the shift in destiny, and officials urged him to accept the throne. Despite his humility, he yielded to heaven's will and the people's wishes, performing the Zen ritual and ascending to the Tai Chi Palace.

An edict was issued granting amnesty, changing the era name from Zhongxing to Tianjian, and rewarding officials and the needy. The Qi emperor was demoted to King of Baling, with reduced privileges, while the Southern Qi's rituals and calendar were retained. Posthumous honors were given to Xiao Yan's parents and siblings, and various princes and dukes were appointed. The edict also condemned the Song dynasty's extravagance, ordering the release of palace servants and providing for the elderly. Foreign kings, including those of Goguryeo, Baekje, and Japan, were promoted in rank.

Further edicts addressed governance, urging officials to observe customs, seek talented individuals, and reform legal practices. The use of money to atone for crimes was revived, and officials were instructed to report injustices. The text records the establishment of new offices, the promotion of generals, and the suppression of rebellions, such as Chen Bozhi's revolt in Jiangzhou. Natural disasters, including a severe drought and plague, led to high rice prices and widespread starvation.

In subsequent years, the emperor focused on legal reforms, establishing the "Liang Lv" code and abolishing certain punishments. He promoted education, building schools and testing students, while also addressing economic issues like tax relief for flood victims. Military campaigns against the Northern Wei saw victories and surrenders, though some cities were lost. The emperor personally performed rituals, including sacrifices to heaven and the Mingtang, and issued amnesties.

The text details the construction of palaces, temples, and embankments, as well as the appointment and dismissal of officials. Notable events include the death of key figures like Shen Yue and Wang Ying, the coronation of the crown prince, and the establishment of new states. The emperor's efforts to curb corruption and promote agriculture are evident, with edicts encouraging the return of migrants and the fair distribution of land. Foreign envoys from Funan, Khotan, and other states regularly presented tribute.

Throughout the reign, the emperor emphasized moral governance, often citing ancient precedents. He ordered the burial of war dead, the release of elderly slaves, and the reduction of taxes for the poor. The text concludes with the emperor's continued focus on legal and administrative reforms, aiming to stabilize the realm and ensure the well-being of his subjects.
