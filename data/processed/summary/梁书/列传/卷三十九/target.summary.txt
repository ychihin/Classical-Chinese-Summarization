The text details the lives of several individuals from the Northern Wei Dynasty who later served the Liang Dynasty, focusing on their careers, military actions, and fates.

Yuan Faseng, a relative of the Northern Wei imperial family, served as an official and later declared himself emperor during a period of chaos. He submitted to the Liang Dynasty and was granted high titles. He died at age 83, and his sons, Jinglong and Jingzhong, also served the Liang. Jingzhong later rebelled with Hou Jing but was defeated and hanged himself.

Yuanshu, another Northern Wei relative, served as an official and later defected to the Liang. He was appointed to military and civil posts, captured a city, but was eventually captured and died in the Northern Wei at age 48.

Yuan Yuanda, a branch of the Wei family, served in the Northern Wei and later submitted to the Liang, receiving titles and positions. He died at age 57.

Wang Shennian, from Qi, was a Confucian scholar who served as a prefect in the Northern Wei and later defected to the Liang. He was known for his uprightness, destroying a fraudulent temple. He died at age 75, and his son also served as an official.

Yang Hua, from Qiuchi, was a brave general forced into adultery by Empress Dowager Hu. He defected to the Liang, served in expeditions, and later surrendered to Hou Jing, dying in the rebel territory.

Yang Kan, from Tai Shan, was a tall and learned man who followed his father in military service. He shot a rebel leader, was promoted, and later defected to the Liang with his family. He was besieged by Wei forces but broke out, arriving in the Liang capital. He served in various military posts, advised against attacking Hou Jing prematurely, and defended the capital during Hou Jing's rebellion. He refused bribes, used tactics to counter siege engines, and remained loyal despite his son being captured. He died of illness at age 54, known for his bravery, strength, and luxurious lifestyle.

Yang Yaren, also from Tai Shan, was brave and served as an official. He defected to the Liang, was granted a marquisate, and held military commands. He was sent to pick up Hou Jing but later feared punishment. During Hou Jing's rebellion, he aided the court but was defeated. After the capital fell, he served Hou Jing but planned to flee, eventually being killed.

The text concludes with a commentary praising the loyalty of Yang Kan and Yang Yaren during the Taiqing period, comparing their steadfastness to ancient martyrs.
