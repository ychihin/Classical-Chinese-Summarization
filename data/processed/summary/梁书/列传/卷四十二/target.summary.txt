Zang Dun, courtesy name Xuanqing, was from Ju, Dongguan. His lineage included notable ancestors: great-great-grandfather Zang Tao served as doctor Zuo Guanglu during the Song Dynasty; grandfather Zang Tanzhi was Minister of Zuo Min; and father Zang Weizhen, a talented scholar versed in literature and history, held various official posts under Emperor Wu of Qi and later under Emperor Gaozu of Liang, including Taiwei Jijiu, Shangshu Zhugulang, and prefect of Xin'an. Zang Weizhen mourned his mother for three years in a thatched hut by her grave and died as Grand Administrator of Jiangxia.

Zang Dun studied the "Five Classics" under Zhuge Gu, excelling in textual analysis. Zhuge admired him as a future great leader. Zang Dun began his career as Fu Junxing to join the army, later becoming Shangshu Binglang. He was known for his handsome appearance and elegant manners, which pleased Emperor Gaozu. He served as Zhongshu General Secretary Shiren and An Youlushi. His filial piety was evident when he felt a sudden pain in his finger the night his mother died, leading him to mourn for five years in a thatched hut, becoming emaciated. Emperor Gaozu praised and comforted him.

After mourning, Zang Dun held positions including Yin Cheng of Danyang, Zhongshu Lang, Shangshu Zuocheng, and governor of Kuaiji County. He became Yushi Zhongcheng, known for his strong and fair character. In 536, during a Buddhist lecture at Tongtai Temple, a tame elephant ran wild, but Zang Dun remained calm, earning the emperor's admiration. He was appointed Sanqi Changshi and later leader of the army, handling military affairs with intelligence and courage. He died in 543 at age 66, posthumously honored as Attendant.

Zang Dun's brother, Zang Jue, also talented, served as prefect of Jin'an, where he quelled crime through enlightenment but was known for harsh punishments, earning the nickname "Zang Hu." He died in office at 48. Emperor Gaozu valued his judgment, often entrusting him with difficult cases.

Fu Qi, courtesy name Jingping, from Lingzhou, had ancestors like Fu Hongren (Taichang) and father Fu Hui (Shanyin magistrate). Fu Qi started as a student, served as Zuo Changshi, and later as Jinbulang. He resigned to mourn his mother. He supervised the northern suburbs altar construction and became Shixin county magistrate, where he released a criminal on the winter solstice, who returned as promised, earning the prefect's praise. He served as Tingweizheng, Zhongshu General Secretary, and Jiankang Order. During the Datong period, he received Wei envoys. In 547, he opposed peace with Wei, warning it would alienate Hou Jing and cause rebellion, but Emperor Gaozu followed Zhu Yi's advice. Hou Jing rebelled in 548, and Fu Qi's predictions proved correct. He refused a marquis title and died at home after Miyagi fell. Yao Cha praised Fu Qi's foresight, noting that if his advice had been heeded, the Taiqing chaos might have been avoided.
