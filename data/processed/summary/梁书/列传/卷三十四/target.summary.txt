Zhang Mian, also known as Yuan Chang, was the son of General Hong Ce. As a child, his maternal grandfather Liu Zhongde recognized his exceptional nature, declaring him a noble person of the Zhang family. During the Qi Dynasty's Yongyuan period, when his father followed Gaozu to war, ten-year-old Zhang Mian was left in Xiangyang, where his emotions mirrored the army's victories and defeats. After his father was killed by demon thieves in the first year of Tianjian, Zhang Mian mourned excessively, beyond normal rituals. Upon completing mourning, he inherited the title of Marquis of Taoyang County. At eighteen, he was appointed governor of Huainan. Concerned about his youth, his superior sent a master to evaluate him but was impressed by his appropriate decisions. Returning to the capital, he served as Prince Sheren and later as a foreign soldier joining the army. Zhang Mian studied diligently, mastered required readings, and wrote extensively, clearly critiquing various academic schools. He answered guests' questions without omission. When a palace position became vacant, the chief minister recommended Zhang Mian for his literary talent. He later served as prefect of Wuling and Prince Xima. His mother Liu Ya, due to his father's poor burial, never lived as the main wife or in official residences. Zhang Mian did not use his salary for personal gain, giving it all to his mother, leaving his household as frugal as a poor family. He was promoted to Beizhonglang and served as internal history of Yuzhang, governing with favor and without suspicion, inspiring officials and people not to commit fraud. Elders noted no such governance in decades. In the first year of Datong, he was summoned as Situ Zuo Changshi but declined due to illness, later becoming the prince's concubine and Yulin Supervisor. He was moved to Yushi Zhongcheng but demoted for arresting people and fighting with foreign envoys. As imperial censor, he was fearless and upright, prompting Gaozu to have his portrait painted to encourage officials. In the third year of Zhongdatong, he was moved to serve as a minister but died at forty-two. Gaozu wept bitterly, and Prince Zhaoming mourned, praising Zhang Mian's knowledge and official skills in a letter to his brother. Zhang Mian loved classical books, collecting over 10,000 volumes, and compiled "Hou Han Ji" and "Jin Chao" along with "Jiangzuo Collection," though unfinished. His son Fu inherited his title.

Zhang Zan, courtesy name Boxu, was Zhang Mian's third brother, adopted by his uncle Hong Ji. At eleven, he married Princess Fuyang, Emperor Gaozu's fourth daughter, becoming Prince Consort Duwei. At seventeen, he was recruited as a secretary. Standing seven feet four inches tall with handsome features, Gaozu found him remarkable. Zhang Zan was eager to learn, reading his brother's vast collection day and night. As one of four Secretary Langs, he firmly requested not to move positions to read all books in the pavilion. He was later moved to Prince Sheren and then Xima and Zhongsheren, managing records. He was as famous as Wang Xi. In the early Tongren years, Northern Wei's Liu Shanming admired him greatly. Zhang Zan was promoted to Taiwei and Minister of Libu Lang, becoming commander and minister at a young age. Pei Ziye praised him as a mouthpiece but regretted his late appointment. In the first year of Datong, he served as official historian of Ningyuan Huarong and managed Langye and Pengcheng counties. He later became Huarong Gongbeizhonglang Changshi and Mu Lanling prefect. In the third year, he entered the court as minister of the branch but resigned when his mother died. After mourning, he became prefect of Wuxing, simplifying administration for the people's convenience. In the second year of Datong, he was recruited as Minister of the Ministry of Personnel, promoting upright people without changing for the powerful. In the fifth year, Gaozu praised him as the leader of the imperial court. Zhang Zan had conflicts with He Jingrong, refusing to treat his guests. In office, he proposed suitable ancient and modern reforms for court uniforms. In the ninth year, he was transferred to General Xuanhui and Danyang Yin but was appointed as envoy Chijie and governor Yu Guasai. On his way, he wrote "Ode to the Southern Expedition," reflecting on his journey, governance, and historical reflections. He managed Xiangzhou effectively, increasing household registration by over 100,000 and bringing peace. In the second year of Taiqing, he was recruited as leader and later as governor of multiple regions. He had conflicts with Xiao Shu and Xiao Yu, leading to his arrest and forced shaving as a Taoist priest. He was killed at fifty-one. Emperor Yuan posthumously honored him as Jian Xian Gong. Zhang Zan wrote one hundred volumes of "Hongbao" and twenty volumes of collected works. His son Zhang Wang married Princess Haiyan.

Zhang Wan, courtesy name Xiaoqing, was Zhang Zan's fourth brother. Excelling in shooting as a first-year student, he was recruited as chief and secretary, later moving to Prince Sheren and other roles. He was promoted to Zhongshu Lang and Doctor of Guozi, serving as Lang Changshi of Beizhong and Grand Administrator of Lanling. During the Han Dynasty, he managed Danyang Yin's affairs due to Xiao Yuanzao's illness. He moved to Zhongcheng, where Gaozu praised his legal uprightness. In the fourth year of Datong, he and his brother Zhang Zan served as servants, an unprecedented situation praised by contemporaries. He became internal history of Yuzhang, lecturing on "The Right Statement of the Book of Rites" to hundreds. In the eighth year, when Liu Jinggong held Ao Dao hostage, Zhang Wan ignored advice to flee, built defenses, recruited over 10,000 brave men, and pacified the thieves with Wang Sengbian's help. Ten years later, he again served as censor Zhongcheng, unstoppable and feared by the powerful. He opened a scholar's hall in the city. In the second year of Taiqing, he was promoted to Zuowei General, guarding Shuye Gate during Hou Jing's invasion. Three years later, he became Minister of the Ministry of Personnel. After the palace fell, he fled to Jiangling, where King Xiangshu granted him titles. He served as Chijie and Internal History of Eastern Hunan. In the second year of Chengsheng, he was recruited as Minister Youpushe and later Shizhong. When Jiangling fell, he survived due to illness and died at sixty-three. His son Zhang Jiao married Princess Anyang. Yao Cha commented that during the Taiqing period, Zhang Zan's personal grudges led to discord among kings, contributing to the Liang Dynasty's chaos.
