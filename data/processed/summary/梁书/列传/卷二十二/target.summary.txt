# Summary of Historical Records of Emperor Taizu's Sons

Emperor Taizu had ten sons. Queen Zhang raised Xiao Yi, Xiao Fu, and Xiao Chang. Concubine Li gave birth to Xiao Rong. Xiao Zhu and Xiao Rong were killed during the Yongyuan period of Qi; Xiao Fu and Xiao Chang died during the Jianwu period and were posthumously named county kings after Gaozu ascended the throne. Concubine Chen bore Xiao Hong and Xiao Wei. Concubine Wu gave birth to Xiao Xiu and Xiao Qi. Fei Taifei gave birth to Xiao Hui.

Xiao Hong, Prince Jinghui of Linchuan, was Taizu's sixth son. He was eight feet tall with impressive manners. During the Qi Dynasty, he served in various military positions. When Changsha King Xiao Yi was besieged by Wei forces, Xiao Hong led elite troops to rescue him, though the Wei army retreated before his arrival. After Gaozu's uprising, Xiao Hong was appointed as auxiliary general. He was granted the title of Prince of Linchuan County in the first year of Tianjian. Over subsequent years, he held numerous high-ranking positions including General of the Central Army, commander-in-chief of northern expeditions, General of Hussars, and Situ. He was known for his filial piety, particularly when his mother Concubine Chen fell ill. He died at age fifty-four, and the emperor issued an edict praising his character and granting him posthumous honors. Xiao Hong had seven sons.

Xiao Xiu, Prince Kang of Ancheng, was Taizu's seventh son. Orphaned at twelve, he was raised by Concubine Chen. During the Yongyuan period, when disaster struck the imperial family, Xiao Xiu escaped with his brothers. After Gaozu's uprising, he was appointed auxiliary general and later granted the title of Prince of Ancheng County. He governed various regions, focusing on recruiting people, providing relief during famines, and building schools. He recruited hermits and scholars, and when floods destroyed crops, he distributed grain for relief. He died at age forty-four, and people from four states mourned him. He was known for his kind and forgiving nature, never showing anger. He collected scriptures and employed scholars to compile works.

Xiao Wei, Prince Yuanxiang of Nanping, was Taizu's eighth son. He loved learning from youth. During the uprising, he helped stabilize Yongzhou by defeating rebel forces. He was granted the title of Prince of Anjun and later changed to Nanping County Prince. He held various positions including General of the Guard and Grand Sima. He died at age fifty-eight. Xiao Wei was known for his generosity toward scholars and the poor, often providing relief to those in need. He believed in Buddhist teachings and wrote philosophical treatises. His residence was the most luxurious among Liang princes.

Xiao Hui, Prince Zhonglie of Poyang, was Taizu's ninth son. Intelligent from childhood, he could understand classic texts at age seven. He served in various military and governmental positions. As governor, he ordered proper burials for plague victims and purchased horses to relieve the burden on citizens. He died at age fifty-one. Known for filial piety, he once dreamed of his mother's illness and later learned she had recovered. He was tolerant and charitable, distributing his salary to others.

Xiao Qi, Prince Zhongwu of Shixing, was Taizu's eleventh son. After the uprising, he was appointed to govern Jingzhou, where he worked diligently to cultivate farmland and provide for the poor. During floods, he personally led efforts to build embankments and rescued hundreds of families. He established schools and encouraged learning. He died at age forty-five and was posthumously honored.
