Xie Bei, courtesy name Jingchong, was a native of Yangxia, Chen County, born into a distinguished family with his grandfather and father both holding high offices in the Song Dynasty. A child prodigy, he could write essays at age ten and was highly praised by his father Xie Zhuang and Wang Jingwen of Langye. Emperor Xiaowu also recognized his talent. Xie Bei began his official career as Fujun Fa Cao Xing and joined the army, later becoming Prince Sheren but resigning due to his father's death. After mourning, he held various positions including Zhongshulang and Huangmen侍郎. He served as internal history of Linchuan but was impeached for bribery, though the case was shelved by Yuan Can.

During Qi Emperor Gao's reign, Xie Bei was among the "four friends" of the emperor, serving as chief historian and later Shizhong. When Emperor Gao sought to usurp the throne, Xie Bei expressed reluctance, citing historical precedents, which displeased the emperor. He was eventually dismissed and stayed at home. In the Yongming era, he was recalled to serve as Tongzhi Sanqi Changshi and later as champion general and prefect of Yixing, where he neglected official duties but amassed wealth. He refused several appointments, including Shizhong and Zhongshuling, preferring to avoid political turmoil.

Under Emperor Ming, Xie Bei was summoned but declined, building a house in the county. He was later forced to serve under Emperor Gaozu, accepting positions like Shizhong and Situ, though he often feigned illness. He died at sixty-six, receiving posthumous honors. His son Xie Mo was dismissed for killing cattle, and his nephew Xie Lan, known for his literary talent and demeanor, served in various roles, including prefect of Wuxing, where he suppressed bandits and corruption. Xie Lan died in office at thirty-seven. Yao Cha commented that Xie Bei was loyal to the Song Dynasty, while Xie Lan excelled in governance.
