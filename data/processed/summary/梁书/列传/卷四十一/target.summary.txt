Wang Gui, courtesy name Weiming, was from Linyi, Langxie. His grandfather Wang Jian was a Taiwei during the Qi Dynasty, and his father Wang Qian served as a doctor. At age eight, Wang Gui lost his biological mother and showed extreme filial piety during mourning, moving Taiwei Xu Xiaosi to tears. His uncle Wang Chen praised him as "my family's horse." By twelve, he understood the Five Classics, and as he grew, he became eloquent and learned. He was promoted as a scholar, served as Secretary Lang, and held various posts including Prince Sheren and Prince Xima. In the twelfth year of Tianjian, he presented the "New Hall Fu" after the Taiji Hall was rebuilt. He served as Secretary Cheng, Prince's Zhongsheren, and other roles. Selected by Xiao Gang, king of Jin'an, as Yunhui to consult and join the army, he later became prefect of Xin'an but resigned due to his father's death. After mourning, he was granted the title of Marquis of Nanchang County and appointed as Minister of Huangmen of Zhongshu. He served the prince alongside others, and Prince Zhaoming treated them with courtesy. At a banquet, Wang Gui noted that making drinking orders had not occurred since the Jin Dynasty moved to Jiangzuo, impressing officials. In the early years of ordinary times, he warned against Chen Qingzhi's Northern Expedition, predicting disaster, which soon came true. In the sixth year, Emperor Gaozu admired his poetic talent at a banquet and appointed him minister. He held various posts, including Minister of the Fifth Army and infantry captain. In the second year of Zhongdatong, he became prefect of Wu County, where he distanced himself from the powerful Rui Zhenzong, leading to a transfer. He resigned due to illness and built a house in Songxi Temple. He died at age forty-five, and the emperor posthumously awarded him titles and gifts. The crown prince mourned him, praising his charm and talent. Wang Gui collected different versions of the "Book of the Later Han" and annotated 200 volumes of the "Sustained Book of Han" and 20 volumes of collected works.

Wang Gui's son Wang Bao, courtesy name Ziyuan, could write at age seven. Grandfather Sikong Yuan Ang praised him as a future distinguished nephew. At twenty, he was promoted as a scholar and served as Secretary Lang, but left due to his father's death. After mourning, he was granted the title of Marquis of Nanchang and held various posts. During the Taiqing period, Hou Jing captured the capital, and Wang Bao stood firm in Ancheng against rebels. In the second year of Dashi, Emperor Shizu summoned him to Jiangling, appointing him General Zhongwu and Internal History of Nanping. He was promoted to Minister of the Ministry of Personnel and Shizhong, then to right servant of Shangshu and Zuopushe. In the third year of Chengsheng, Jiangling was captured, and he surrendered to the Zhou Dynasty. Wang Bao wrote "Young Training" to warn his sons, emphasizing the importance of studying diligently and adhering to Confucian principles while also respecting Taoism and Buddhism. He noted that since the Jin Dynasty moved to Jiangzuo, the three schools had not been annihilated.

Liu Yu, courtesy name Zhongshi, was a seventh-generation descendant of Yin Liu Zhenchang. He was well-behaved and talented, serving as Ning Hailing and later as Prince of Xiangdong to join the army. During the Zuochang period, he helped suppress a rebellion and was favored by Shizu. He served as Minister Zuocheng and Yushi Zhongcheng, and in the second year of Cheng Sheng, he became Minister of the Ministry of Personnel and Guozi Jijiu. Zong Mao, courtesy name Yuan, was from a family that moved to Jiangling during the Yongjia Rebellion. He was studious and called "Boy Bachelor." He served as secretary and later as Shangshu Lang, granted the title of Marquis of Xin'an County. In the third year of Chengsheng, Jiangling was captured, and he belonged to the Zhou Dynasty. Wang Cheng, courtesy name Anqi, was proficient in the "Book of Changes" at seven and served as Secretary Lang. He loved Confucian classics and taught students. He served as Guozi Jijiu, and three generations of his family held this position, considered a great honor. He died in Shuyang County at forty-one. He despised powerful people and, along with his brother Wang Zhi, avoided visiting the influential Zhu Yi, earning praise. Chu Xiang, courtesy name Shiju, lost his parents young and became knowledgeable. He served as Secretary Lang and various posts, known for his uprightness and handsome appearance. He died in office at age forty-four, and his cousin Xie Gao wrote an epitaph praising his character. Chu Xiang was filial and once saw a strange light while praying for his mother's illness, which was cured. Xiao Jie, courtesy name Mao Jing, was knowledgeable and good at writing. He served as Prince Sheren and Shangshu Jinbulang, with outstanding political achievements. He was appointed as prefect of Shixing and later as Shaofu Qing. He advised against accepting Hou Jing, warning of his treacherous nature, but Emperor Gaozu did not adopt his advice. Xiao Jie died at seventy-three. Xiao Qia, courtesy name Hongcheng, was smart and loved learning. He served as prefect of Southern Xuzhou, known for his honesty. He died at fifty-five, leaving twenty volumes of collected works. Chu Qiu, courtesy name Zhongbao, was poor but studious. He served as magistrate of Liyang and later as censor Zhongcheng, known for his fairness. He died at seventy. Liu Ru, courtesy name Xiaozhi, could write at seven and was filial. He served as Tai Mo Ling and was admired by Emperor Gaozu. He died at fifty-nine, leaving twenty volumes of collected works. His brothers Liu Lan and Liu Zun were also notable. Liu Qian, courtesy name Xiaoyi, was a generous person who served his widowed sister-in-law respectfully. He died at sixty-seven, leaving twenty volumes of collected works. His brothers Liu Xiaosheng and Liu Xiaowei were also talented. Yin Yun, courtesy name Guanshu, was free-spirited and studious, dying at fifty-nine. Xiao Ji, courtesy name Dexuan, could write at ten and was gentle and self-reliant. He died in office as prefect of Xin'an. The historian comments that these individuals were all famous and showed their talents when opportunities arose.
