**Summary of Historical Records: Kong Xiuyuan and Jiang Ge**

Kong Xiuyuan, courtesy name Qingxu, was from Shanyin, Kuaiji, and a descendant of Jin Dynasty official Kong Chong. Orphaned at eleven, he strictly observed mourning rites and was deeply moved by his father's handwriting. He studied Confucian classics under Xuan Diezao in Wuxing. In the fourth year of Jianwu, his policy essays impressed Taiwei Tu Cunqi, who compared him favorably to Dong Zhongshu and Hua Lingsi. Wang Rong of Longxie recommended him as a bachelor of Xili to King Situ Jingling. Upon arriving in the capital, he stayed with Minister Kong Deng, where Attendant Fan Yun praised him highly. Later, when Renzhao prepared a banquet expecting many guests, he visited only Xiuyuan, causing Kong Deng shame. Shangshu Ling Shen Yue, a powerful court figure, received Xiuyuan humbly and seated him at his right to discuss literature.

Xiuyuan was appointed Prince of Linchuan's army adjutant. During Emperor Gaozu's reign, Xu Mian recommended him as Minister Cao Lang for his knowledge of Jin and Song court rituals. He served as Doctor Cao and Minister of Book Yi, answering historical questions from memory without hesitation. Renfang, an official, called him "Kong Dusong." He moved to Kangyuanzheng, where he judged cases fairly. Later, as Zhongshu Sheren and Zuocheng, he rectified the Shangshu Province. He compiled "The Book of Rites" with Prince Zhan Shizhou She, including his own memorials. As Shilang of Huangmen and Censor Zhongcheng, he enforced laws strictly, intimidating officials. He served as Shaofu Qing and Danyang Yin, then as chief historian of Xuanhui Jin'an Palace and Prefect of Nanjun, overseeing Jingzhou. Emperor Gaozu entrusted his young son to him, comparing him to Zhou Chang. Xiuyuan served with distinction, rejecting favor-seeking. He later held posts including Standing Attendant, Yulin Supervisor, and Secretary Supervisor. King Jin'an reserved a special seat for him. In the seventh year of the ordinary year, he became Governor of Yangzhou after Wang Hong's death, praised for his talent and knowledge. In the second year of Zhongdatong, he was awarded Doctor Jin Ziguanglu. He died in May at sixty-four, mourned by Emperor Gaozu, who granted posthumous honors and a frugal burial. His collection included over 7,000 volumes and fifteen volumes of impeachment proceedings.

Jiang Ge, courtesy name Xiuying, was from Kaocheng, Jiyang. His father died when he was nine, and his mother when he was sixteen, after which he and his brother Jiang Guan studied at Taixue, excelling. Wang Rong and Xie Chui respected them. Once, Ji Ji saw Jiang Ge studying in ragged clothes during a snowstorm and gave him half a felt. King Situ Jingling made him a bachelor. At twenty, he was elected a scholar in Xuzhou. He served as an official, with Pu She Jiang Zhe introducing him. He kept his role in handling imperial edicts secret, avoiding implication in Liao Tuo's death. In the first year of Zhongxing, Emperor Gaozu appreciated his impromptu letter to Yuan Ang. He served under King Jian'an in Yongzhou, but his brother Jiang Guan died en route. He held various posts, including Zhongshu Sheren and Yushi Zhongcheng, impeaching the powerful. During a Wei invasion, he was captured but refused to write inscriptions for the enemy, even under threat of beating. He was given three liters of millet daily. Emperor Gaozu later praised his integrity and appointed him to high offices. As governor of Dongzhou, he refused gifts, handled cases efficiently, and punished corruption. He left office with only a small boat, using stones for ballast. In Wu County, he quelled thieves through kindness. He served as Minister of Ren Duzhi, supporting commoners and juniors. He died in February of the first year of Datong, leaving a collection of twenty volumes. He served eight palaces and four kings without acquiring concubines, living frugally. His sons Xingmin and Cong Jian died early, the latter killed by Hou Jing's rebels. The historian noted that Emperor Gaozu valued Kong Xiuyuan's insights, while Jiang Ge was known for his intelligence and straightforwardness.
