Wei Can, grandson of General Wei Rui and son of Governor Wei Gan, was a tall and learned man who served in various official positions under Prince Jin'an. He became close friends with Yu Zhongrong and Zhang Shu. As the prince rose to crown prince, Wei Can held several palace guard roles, though his arrogant nature caused resentment among peers. General Zhu Yi once rebuked him for his haughty demeanor. In 545, Wei Can became governor of Hengzhou, but resigned shortly after. Recalled in 546, he learned of Hou Jing's rebellion while traveling and rushed to the capital with 5,000 elite troops. After consulting with officials Liu Xiaoyi and Xiao Daxin, he coordinated with his cousin Liu Zhongli and General Pei Zhigao, eventually securing command for Liu. During battle at Qingtang, Wei Can ignored advice to hold position, ordered an attack, and was overwhelmed by Hou Jing's forces. He and his sons, brothers, and cousin were killed at age 54. The emperor mourned him deeply and posthumously promoted him.

Jiangzi, a seventh-generation descendant of Jiang Tong, was a studious and principled man from a poor family. He served as a county magistrate with distinction before becoming a court official. His brother Jiang Zisi served as Youcheng and once boldly defended colleagues Shen Jiong and Gu Qi during a court dispute, angering Emperor Gaozu but eventually being pardoned. During Hou Jing's rebellion, Jiangzi led naval forces to intercept the rebels but was betrayed by his deputy. He retreated to Nanzhou and then to the capital, where he proposed a breakout. He and his brothers Jiang Zisi and Jiang Ziwu charged into enemy lines alone and were killed. The emperor posthumously honored them with high titles. Jiangzi also authored works like "Huang Tu" and "Guang Jiupin."

Zhang Sheng, son of General Zhang Ji, was a virtuous and scholarly man who became a vegetarian after his father's murder. He held various official posts, including governor of Wuxing. During Hou Jing's siege of the capital, he sent troops to help. After the city fell, he organized resistance in Wuxing, defeating rebel Liu Shenmao. When Hou Jing sent reinforcements, Zhang Sheng's forces were overwhelmed. He refused to surrender, was captured, and executed at age 62. He was posthumously honored as Zhongzhenzi.

Shen Jun, a learned official from Wukang, served as magistrate of several counties and later as censor. During Hou Jing's siege, he negotiated with the rebel leader, boldly condemning his treachery. Hou Jing admired his loyalty but later killed him after breaking through Zhang Sheng's camp.

Liu Jingli, grandson of Liu Qingyuan, was known for bravery. He defended Qingxidai during Hou Jing's invasion, leading charges. After the palace fell, he plotted with his brother Liu Zhongli to assassinate Hou Jing at a banquet, but the plan failed due to heavy guards. Later, he conspired with Prince Xiao Huili to attack Hou Jing's stronghold but was betrayed and killed.

The historian concludes that these men exemplified the principle of sacrificing life for righteousness, proving that the Liang Dynasty had loyal ministers.
