Xiao Jing, courtesy name Zizhao, was a cousin of Emperor Gaozu. His father Xiao Chongzhi was a son of Xiao Daomo, who had three sons: Xiao Shangzhi, Emperor Taizu Wen, and Xiao Chongzhi. Xiao Shangzhi was known for his virtuous conduct and served as an infantry captain, dying in office. Posthumously titled Wenxuanhou in the early Tianjian era, his son Xiao Lingjun served as a county magistrate and was later granted the title of Marquis of Dongchang County. Xiao Chongzhi, a champion general and Dongyang governor, was killed during a rebellion in the Yongming era and posthumously titled Marquis Zhongjian.

Xiao Jing became famous at age eight for his grief during mourning. He loved learning, was decisive, and served as Chengkong County Magistrate, where his political performance was exemplary. After resigning due to illness, he returned to court and was appointed Infantry Colonel. Following Gaozu's uprising, he became General Ningshuo and pacified the Chu people on the Yangtze's north bank. After Gaozu ascended, Xiao Jing was granted the title of Marquis of Wuping County and governed several states. He was known for his honesty, strictness, and efficiency, with no backlog of documents. During a famine, he provided relief and coffins. In the fourth year of Tianjian, he led an army in the Northern Expedition. After his mother's death, he resigned but was later reappointed to various positions, including General Zuo Xiaoqi. He was strict with officials, causing resentment among favored supervisors, leading to his transfer to Yongzhou as governor.

In the eighth year, when Wei's governor Wu Jin invaded, Xiao Jing accepted surrendered barbarians, defeating the enemy and burying the dead. He built barracks, strengthened defenses, and encouraged farming, resulting in a peaceful and theft-free territory. He was later recalled to court and served in various roles, including General Youwei. In the thirteenth year, he was summoned as leading general, receiving a salary increase. Xiao Jing was personable and well-spoken, and Gaozu treated him with great courtesy, consulting him on military and state affairs. He declined a promotion to Yangzhou governor but was eventually appointed. His strictness was legendary; an old woman noted his talismans were like fire. He died at age forty-seven, posthumously titled Shizhong.

Xiao Chang, Xiao Jing's second brother, served in various roles, including governor of Hengzhou, but was dismissed for crimes. He was understanding but prone to drinking and making mistakes, often killing people while drunk without remorse. He died at age thirty-nine.

Xiao Ang, the third brother, served as governor of Southern Yanzhou and Guangzhou, among other roles. He died at age fifty-three, posthumously titled Gong.

Xiao Yu, the fourth brother, served as Secretary Lang and other positions but frequently refused appointments. He wrote a memorial expressing shame and fear, requesting dismissal. Emperor Gaozu replied, criticizing his refusals and dismissing him. Later, Xiao Yu was exiled for minting coins but was recalled. He became a model of propriety and was appointed Prefect of Jinling County, where he implemented beneficial policies. He died after a severe illness, mourned by the people, who built temples and steles for him. He was posthumously appointed governor of Xiangzhou, titled Gong.

The historian comments that Xiao Jing's talent and judgment benefited the government, making him a person of grace and dignity in the You clan.
