This historical text records the lineage and fates of the sons of Emperor Taizong and Shizu during a period of rebellion. Queen Taizong bore Prince Xiao Daqi and Xiao Dalian; other consorts and ladies bore numerous other princes, including Xiao Daxin, Xiao Dalin, Xiao Dachun, Xiao Daya, Xiao Dazhuang, Xiao Dajun, Xiao Dawei, Xiao Daqiu, Xiao Daxin, and Xiao Dazhi. The deeds of some sons are not recorded.

Xiao Daxin, King of Xunyang, was intelligent and a skilled writer. Ennobled as a duke at age thirteen, he was appointed to military and gubernatorial posts. Despite his youth, his opinions were wise. During Hou Jing's rebellion, he raised a 10,000-man army to rescue the capital. After the capital fell, he was made King of Xunyang. He relied on his former subordinate Zhuang Tie, who later rebelled. A conflict arose with King Poyang, who aided Zhuang Tie, causing a rift. After a military defeat, Xiao Daxin hesitated to flee, persuaded by his mother to stay. He was killed at age twenty-five.

Xiao Dalin, King of Nanhai, was also intelligent and known for filial piety after his mother's death. He held various official posts. During Hou Jing's rebellion, he was stationed at Xinting and later oversaw military affairs. He collected cattle to reward soldiers. He was made King of Nanhai. He refused to join a rebel army, deeming it unwise. He was killed at age twenty-five.

Xiao Dalian, King of Nanjun, was handsome, a skilled writer and painter. He and his brother Xiao Dalin impressed Emperor Gaozu with their riding skills. He held ministerial posts and led 40,000 troops to rescue the capital during the rebellion. After the capital fell, he defended Yangzhou from bandits. He was made King of Nanjun. Captured by rebels after a subordinate's betrayal, he was appointed to posts but plotted escape. He was killed at age twenty-five.

Xiao Dachun, King of Anlu, was well-read, filial, and physically strong. He held posts and was captured by rebels while fleeing. He was made King of Anlu and later killed at age twenty-two. Xiao Daya, Duke of Liuyang, was beloved by Gaozu. During the capital's fall, he fought rebels until overwhelmed and died from illness at age seventeen. Xiao Dazhuang, King of Xinxing, was killed at age eighteen. Xiao Dajun, King of Xiyang, was honest and recited poetry for Gaozu. He was killed at age thirteen. Xiao Dawei, King of Wuning, was handsome and killed at age thirteen. Xiao Daqiu, King of Jianping, was precocious and, at age seven, vowed to bear others' suffering. He was killed at age eleven. Xiao Daxin, King of Yi'an, mourned his mother deeply as a child. He was killed at age eleven before assuming his post. Xiao Dazhi, King of Suijian, was brave and vowed to destroy rebels. He was killed at age ten.

The text also records Shizu's sons. Xiao Fang, the Zhongzhuang prince, was intelligent and loved nature. He wrote a treatise expressing his desire for a carefree life. After his mother lost favor, he felt endangered. He led troops to rescue the capital and later to conquer the King of Hedong, but was defeated and drowned at age twenty-two. Shizu was not saddened by his death but later posthumously honored him. Xiao Fangzhu, the Zhenhui prince, was erudite and favored. After his brother's death, he was appointed to guard Jiangxia but neglected duties, enjoying leisure. He was captured by a surprise rebel attack and killed. The historian laments that these princes, though ennobled, were imprisoned by bandits and died untimely deaths during the era's chaos.
