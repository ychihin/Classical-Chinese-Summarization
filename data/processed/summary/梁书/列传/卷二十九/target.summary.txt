Emperor Gaozu had eight sons by various consorts, including Prince Zhaoming, Emperor Taizong Jianwen, and others. Among them, Xiao Ji, the fourth son, was titled King of Nankang Jian. At age seven, he demonstrated intelligence by catching a subordinate altering documents. He held various military and civil posts, including governor of South Xuzhou and Jiangzhou, and was praised for his frugality and personal handling of affairs. After his mother's death, he mourned strictly and insisted on resigning his post. He died in office at twenty-five, leaving behind substantial unnamed funds in the treasury. His son Xiao Hui succeeded him, showing early intelligence and literary talent. During the Hou Jing rebellion, Hui initially resisted but eventually surrendered to Hou Jing after the capital fell. He secretly plotted to restore the Liang dynasty but was betrayed and killed, along with his brother Xiao Congli, who was captured and executed at twenty-one.

Xiao Xu, the fifth son, titled King of Luling, was known for his bravery and physical strength, excelling in archery. He held numerous military governorships, including in Yongzhou and Jiangzhou, and was praised for his martial prowess. He died at forty-four, posthumously honored as Sikong.

Xiao Lun, the sixth son, titled King of Shaoling, was intelligent and erudite, skilled in writing. He held various posts but was dismissed for embezzling civilian property and ordering an assassination. He later regained titles and led troops against Hou Jing, suffering defeats. After the capital fell, he fled and eventually established a base in Yingzhou. He wrote a letter to Emperor Yuan urging reconciliation among the Liang princes to unite against Hou Jing, warning that internal strife would benefit the enemy. Despite his efforts, he was defeated by Wang Sengbian and fled, eventually captured and killed by Western Wei forces at thirty-three. His son Xiao Jian was killed during Hou Jing's siege, and another son Xiao Que, known for his bravery, was executed by Hou Jing after the city fell.

The historian comments that while some princes like Xiao Ji and Xiao Xu were commendable for their filial piety and courage, Xiao Lun, despite his talents, was sinister and impetuous, though his loyalty during the Taiqing period was noteworthy.
