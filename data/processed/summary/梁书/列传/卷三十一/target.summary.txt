Yuan Ang, courtesy name Qianli, was from Yangxia, Chen County. His grandfather and father were military officials in the Song Dynasty; his father was killed after supporting Prince Xun of Jin'an. At age five, Yuan Ang was hidden in Lushan by his wet nurse, returning home at fifteen after a pardon. He mourned his father deeply, vomiting blood and building a hut by the grave. Raised by his brother Yuan Yuzeng, he later served in various official posts under the Qi Dynasty, including Secretary Cheng and Huangmen Shilang. Emperor Wu of Qi renamed him Yuan Ang, using his courtesy name Qianli.

During the Liang Dynasty, Yuan Ang held numerous high-ranking positions, such as Censor Zhongcheng, Prefect of Wuxing, and Minister of the Ministry of Personnel. He was known for his integrity, once impeaching a corrupt official despite the latter's powerful connections. When his mother died, he tied himself to her coffin during a storm, vowing to sink with it; the storm ceased, and his boat alone survived.

At the end of the Yongyuan period, when the righteous army arrived, Yuan Ang initially resisted but later surrendered after Emperor Gaozu wrote him a letter. He was pardoned and continued to serve, eventually becoming Sikong, Shizhong, and Shangshuling. He died at age eighty, posthumously titled Muzheng Gong. In his final words, he refused posthumous honors, citing his humble origins and gratitude for imperial grace. His son, Zijun, also served in official posts but died of illness after Hou Jing's rebellion.

The historical commentary praises Yuan Ang for his unwavering loyalty and integrity, comparing him to the virtuous Boyi and Shuqi, and noting his role in supporting the Liang dynasty.
