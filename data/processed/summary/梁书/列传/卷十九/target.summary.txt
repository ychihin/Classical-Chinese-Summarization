Zongyang, also known as Mingzha, was from Nieyang County, Nanyang, and had lived in Jiangling for generations. His grandfather Zong Bing was a renowned scholar who declined an official appointment. His father Zongfan served as Xizhonglang. Zongyang was diligent and talented, passing the Yingzhou scholar exam at twenty. He served as a regular attendant and knight for King Lin, and was among scholars painted in the West Residence by King Jingling of Southern Qi. During the Yongming era, he received Wei envoys with Ren Fang. He managed secretarial duties for Prince Nanjun, known for his calligraphy and integrity. After Prince Wenhui's death, he served the emperor's grandson but later distanced himself due to the ruler's failings, becoming magistrate of Moling and Shangshu Duguanlang. During the Longchang era, he survived disasters due to his integrity. Under Emperor Ming, he governed Yingzhou competently but resigned to care for his aging father. He later served Liang Gaozu, providing strategies and supplies, and held various posts including Censor Zhongcheng and Champion General. He died at forty-nine, succeeded by his son Zong Yaoqing. His cousin Zongyue was also renowned.

Liu Tan, courtesy name Dedu, was from Anzhong County, Nanyang, a descendant of General Liu Qiao. He served as official servant to the Nanjun King and magistrate of Xiaoling, known for his administrative skills. Under King Nankang, he joined the army and managed Changliu. During Liang Gaozu's uprising, he became a consultant. He advised sending a veteran to govern Xiangzhou, was appointed Assistant Chief Historian and Governor of Changsha, where he mobilized over 300,000 hu of rice for troops. He suppressed rebellions by Wang Sencan and others, using strategy to kill the traitor Zhong Baoti and pacifying the region. For his merits, he was made Lord of Yiqiu Shu and later served as Sima Pingxi and prefect of Xinxing. He died at sixty-two, succeeded by his son Liu Quan.

Bimai, courtesy name Lanwei, was a descendant of Bi Tong from Chenmo County, living in Jiangling. As a child, he impressed his uncle Zong Maggot by choosing books over other items. He served King Yi of Yuzhang as chief administrator and later joined the army. After his father's death, he resigned but was recalled. He impressed Xiao Yi with his knowledge of local geography and customs. He restored Jingzhou's official buildings after a rebellion, completing the work quickly without conscripting laborers. He served in various roles under Liang, including Minister of Huangmen and Yushi Zhongcheng. He was known for his fairness and knowledge, once correctly attributing a missing silk net to natural decay. He later became governor of Guangzhou, where he killed the rebellious Xu Yuanyu. He died in office. His sister, married to Liu Qiu, was praised for her virtue. His son Le Nazuo, courtesy name Genbei, was also renowned, serving in various posts including Qibu Lang and Taizhou Qing, known for his integrity and refusal of unearned salary. He died at sixty-three.

Yao Cha commented that these three men were key figures in Chu, contributing to Liang's establishment: Liu Tan in military affairs, Le Ai in official duties, both achieving high positions as expected.
