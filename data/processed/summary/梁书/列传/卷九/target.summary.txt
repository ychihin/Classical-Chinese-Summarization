# Summary of Historical Records: Wang Mao, Cao Jingzong, and Liu Qingyuan

Wang Mao, courtesy name Xiuyuan, was born in Qi, Taiyuan. His grandfather Wang Shen served as Beizhonglang Sima, and his father served as a general in the late Song Dynasty, defeating Situ Yuancan at Shitou and becoming prefect of Brazil and Zitong counties. As a child, Wang Mao was valued by his grandfather, who predicted he would establish the family. He grew to eight feet tall with a white and beautiful face, calm and reserved by nature, and fond of military books.

During the late Song and Ming Dynasties, Wang Mao served in various military positions. When Wei general Li Wunu invaded, Wang Mao marched westward. After the Wei army retreated, he served as Sima of Zhennan and later as Yue Cavalry School captain. When Wei invaded Yanzhou, he served as General Shi of Ningshuo. In the early Jianwu years, he rescued Sizhou from Wei siege and followed Gaozu to defeat Wei generals. After the Wei retreat, he became chief minister of the auxiliary state and governor of Xiangyang.

When Emperor Gaozu raised a righteous army, Wang Mao consulted with Zhang Hongce about welcoming Emperor He, but Gaozu disagreed. Wang Mao served as vanguard, pacifying Jiahu and defeating Guangzijin and Wu Ziyang, beheading tens of thousands. After Jiankang City was pacified, he became general guarding the army, then Shizhong and leading general. He was granted the title of Duke of Wangcai County with 2,300 households. In the first year of the Han Dynasty, he conquered the rebellious Chen Bozhi. He later served as right servant of Shangshu, general of chariots and cavalry, and Kaifu Yitong Sansi. He died at sixty, receiving posthumous honors and the title Zhonglie.

Cao Jingzong, courtesy name Zizhen, was a new savage. His father Cao Xinzhi was a military general in the Song Dynasty. Young Cao Jingzong excelled at horseback archery and hunting. At twenty, while traveling alone with few followers, he was surrounded by hundreds of barbarians but fought them off with arrows, gaining fame for courage. He loved reading history books, particularly admiring Rang Ju and Le Yi.

During the Southern Qi Dynasty, Cao Jingzong served as prefect of Tianshui and fought against barbarian uprisings. He showed loyalty by retrieving the body of his friend Zhang Daomen after Zhang's father was killed. In the Jianwu years, he fought against Northern Wei invasions, defeating Tuoba Ying's 40,000 reinforcements with 2,000 ambush soldiers. He became close friends with Xiao Yan (Emperor Wu of Liang) when Xiao served as governor of Yongzhou.

When Emperor Wu raised troops, Cao Jingzong gathered his forces and served as vanguard, besieging Yingcheng for months until surrender. He advanced toward Jiankang, defeating Li Jushi at Jiangning despite his troops' worn equipment. After the city fell, he was made Marquis of Xiangxi County with 1,600 households. In the Tianjian years, he was promoted to General Pingxi and Marquis of Jingling County. During the Northern Wei invasion of Sizhou, he was impeached for not sending timely rescue but was protected by Emperor Wu.

In the fifth year, when Tuoba Ying invaded Zhongli, Cao Jingzong led rescue forces. Despite imperial orders to wait, he attempted to advance alone but was turned back by storms. He eventually joined Wei Rui at Shaoyangzhou, building fortresses near Wei positions. In the sixth year, during spring floods, they launched a coordinated attack, burning Wei camps and causing massive casualties. Over 50,000 Wei soldiers were captured alive, with countless supplies seized. Cao Jingzong was promoted to Duke with 2,000 households.

Cao Jingzong was self-reliant and proud, showing respect only to Wei Rui. He was a womanizer with hundreds of concubines, impatient and restless, fond of drinking and hunting. He died at fifty-two while traveling to assume the governorship of Jiangzhou.

Liu Qingyuan, named Wenhe, was born in Hedong Jie. He served as chief administrator of Yingzhou and prefect of Wei Xing, where he handled a flood wisely. Emperor Gaozu recruited him as a historian, and Qingyuan became a loyal strategist during Gaozu's rebellion. After Jiankang was pacified, he served as minister and was deeply trusted by Gaozu, who entrusted him with palace keys during a fire.

After Gaozu ascended the throne, Qingyuan became Marquis of Chong'an with 1,000 households. He served as governor of Yongzhou, where Emperor Gaozu praised him for eliminating western concerns. He later returned to Kyoto and served in various high positions. He died at fifty-seven while serving as governor of Yongzhou again, receiving posthumous honors and the title Zhonghui.

Yao Cha commented that Wang Mao, Cao Jingzong, and Liu Qingyuan, though military leaders, achieved great success during the Liang Dynasty's rise, with their accomplishments recorded on bells and tripods. Unlike Emperor Guangwu of Han who limited his officials, these men were able to serve as local governors and reach high military positions, demonstrating superior monarch-minister relations.
