The text provides biographical accounts of several scholars and officials from the Liang Dynasty, focusing on their lineage, careers, and contributions to ritual studies and governance.

Sima Xian, from Wen, Hanoi, inherited a family tradition of scholarship in the "Three Rites." He served in various official posts, including as a doctor of the Shangshu Temple, where he contributed to the establishment of state rituals and music. He was known for his expertise in predicting fortunes and was consulted by renowned scholars. He held positions such as Infantry School Captain and Minister Youcheng, and later served as a chief historian and governor. He died in the seventeenth year of the Tianjian era, and his works were compiled posthumously.

Maoguan, from Pengcheng, was orphaned young but gained recognition for his talent. He served in several official roles, including as a chief historian and governor. Known for his frugality and integrity, he was favored by Emperor Gaozu, who often played chess with him. He was demoted due to implication in a case but later reinstated. He was a devout Buddhist, donating his salary to a temple and living a simple life. He died at seventy-two, leaving a collection of twenty volumes.

Liu Xian, from Pei State, was a child prodigy known for his vast knowledge. He served in various capacities, including as a legal officer and minister. He was praised for his memory and ability to decipher ancient texts. He held positions such as Infantry School Captain and Doctor Guozi. He died at sixty-three, and his friend Liu Zhilin petitioned the crown prince for an epitaph, which was granted.

Liu Zhilin, from Nanyang, was a prolific writer and scholar. He served in multiple roles, including as a minister and prefect. He was known for his extensive collection of ancient artifacts and his expertise in textual criticism. He wrote works on the "Spring and Autumn Annals" and other classics. He died at seventy-two during the Hou Jing rebellion, leaving fifty volumes of collected works.

Xu Mao, from Gaoyang, was a dedicated scholar of the "Mao Shi." He served as a lecturer and official, advising against the performance of the Feng and Shan sacrifices based on historical and textual analysis. He corrected many ritual errors and held positions such as prefect and regular attendant. He died at sixty-nine, leaving several volumes of works.

The text concludes with Yao Cha's commentary, praising these scholars for their learning and diligence, noting that their official success depended on seizing opportunities.
