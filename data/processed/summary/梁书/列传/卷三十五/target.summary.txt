Xiao Zike, courtesy name Jingchong, was the second son of Xiao Yi, King Wen of Yuzhang in the Qi Dynasty. During the Yongming era, he was named Marquis of Nankang County. At age twelve, he co-wrote "Gaosong Fu" with his cousin, King Jingling of Situ, earning admiration from the guard Gongjian. He began his career as General Ningshuo and Grand Administrator of Jinduo, later moving to General Fuguo and Grand Administrator of Wujun during the Jiangan period. When Grand Sima Wang Jingze rebelled in Kuaiji, Zike fled to the capital, avoiding harm from Emperor Ming, who appointed him as the crown prince's concubine. Under Donghunhou, he became Secretary Supervisor, Right Army General, and Minister. In the second year of Zhongxing, he served as Auxiliary to Consult the Country. In the first year of Tianjian, he was demoted to Viscount, appointed as Regular Attendant of the Cavalry and Infantry Captain, but declined due to illness. He later held posts including Guanglu Doctor and Chief Historian of Situ Zuo.

Emperor Gaozu, in a conversation with Zike's disciples, emphasized that fame and fortune cannot be forced, citing historical examples like Xiang Ji's failure. He contrasted the jealous nature of Emperor Xiaowu of Song, who poisoned talented brothers, with the survival of Liu Ziye. Gaozu noted that those with destiny cannot be harmed, referencing his own rise. He advised against killing during dynastic changes, warning it damages harmony, and stressed the close clan relationship between Qi and Liang, urging loyalty. He also instructed Zhao Shuzu to reassure the princes of Beidi of his intentions.

Zike later served as Prefect of Yongjia, Guanglu Qing, Secretary Supervisor, General Mingwei, Prefect of Lingling, Regular Attendant of Sanqi, General of the Auxiliary Country, Zong Zhengqing, Du Guan Shangshu, Minister of Personnel, and Prince Zhan Shi. In the second year of Datong, he became General Ningyuan and Prefect of Yuxing, dying at age fifty-two in 536. He was posthumously given the titles Shi Zhong and Zhong Shu Ling, with the posthumous name Gong. His sixteen brothers all served in Liang, with five talented ones: Zike, Zizhi, Zixian, Ziyun, and Zihui. Wang Deng, another brother, praised their literary skills but abandoned his own writing.

Zifan, Zike's sixth younger brother, was named Marquis of Qiyang County in the tenth year of Yongming. In Tianjian, he was demoted to Viscount and held various posts, including Prince Xi Ma and Master Situ. Known for filial piety, he mourned excessively for his mother. He later served as Chief Secretary of Situ, Yan Yin Cheng, Prefect of Jian'an, and Grand Sima of the Imperial Household. He loved literary talents, favoring Wang Yan, and compiled the "Thousand-Character Essay." After Wang's death, Zifan moved to Xuanhui to consult and join the army. He felt undervalued compared to his brothers, often comparing himself to Du Xu's middle brother. He later became Sima, Zhongsan Doctor, Guanglu, Tingweiqing, General Rong Zhao, and Direct Internal History of Zhou. He returned to the capital as Taizhong Doctor and Secretary Supervisor. Under Taisai, he was appointed Guanglu Doctor but declined due to thieves. He died at sixty-four, posthumously given Jinzi Guanglu and the posthumous name Wen. His collected works totaled thirty volumes. His sons, Xiao Pang and Xiao Que, were early writers, with Xiao Pang serving as a lieutenant and Xiao Que as an official in Taiqing.

Xiao Ti, Zike's eighth younger brother, was intelligent and valued by his father. At seven, he was named Kongdu Tibao. In Tianjian, his title was reduced to Viscount. He held posts including Foreign Soldier in Anxi, Wei Jishi, and Taiwei Recorder. Tall and handsome, he wrote "Hongxu Fu," praised by Shen Yue, and compiled "Hou Mo" and "History of Qi." He later served as Prince Zhongsheren, Jianchenling, Shaoling Wangyou, Danyan Yincheng, Zhongshulang, and Zong Zhengqing. In the second year of Zhongdatong, he became Chief and Minister. Emperor Gaozu admired his talent and manner, once asking him about compiling "General History." In three years, he became a Doctor of Guozi, later moving to Guozi Jijiu and Minister of Civil Affairs. He was proud and solemn, often ignoring guests. He died at forty-nine in the third year of Datong, posthumously given Shizhong and Zhongshu Ling, with the posthumous name Mi. His works include "Houmo Shu," "Qi Shu," "Ordinary Northern Expedition," "Guijian Biography," and collected works. His sons, Xiao Xu and Xiao Kai, were early achievers, with Xiao Kai praised by Taizong for his poetry.

Ziyun, Zike's ninth younger brother, was named Marquis of Xinpu County at twelve in the fourth year of Jianwu. In Tianjian, his title was reduced to Viscount. Diligent in study, he began writing at twenty. Quiet and reluctant to serve, he was recruited as a secretary at thirty. He held posts including Prince Sheren, Beizhong Lang, and Danyang Yin Cheng. King Shu of Xiang appreciated him. He later served as Huang Menlang, General Qingche, and Minister of Civil Affairs. In the first year of Zhongdatong, he became Taifuqing, then General Zhenwei and Internal History of Linchuan. He returned as Regular Attendant of Sanqi and Minister of State. He proposed revising sacrificial music lyrics, replacing Shen Yue's works with classical references. Skilled in cursive calligraphy, he imitated Zhong Yao and Wang Xizhi, earning Emperor Gaozu's praise. In the seventh year, he served as General Renwei and Prefect of Shuyang. In the first year of Taiqing, he became Shizhong and Guozi Jijiu. In the second year, Hou Jing invaded, and Ziyun fled, dying of starvation at sixty-three in 549. His works include "Book of Jin" and "New Records of the East Palace." His son Xiao Te, skilled in calligraphy, died at twenty-five.

Zihui, Ziyun's younger brother, was talented in literature. He started as a member of the imperial family and served as Sanqi Minister and Lin'an Order. Quiet and reserved, he wrote "Lectures on Fu" after attending a lecture. He later served as Consultant to King Wuling and Chief Historian of Hussar, dying in office. Chen Libu Shangshu Yao Chari noted that the Liang Dynasty, unlike previous dynasties, did not suppress former clans, allowing Zike's brothers to hold positions, reflecting Emperor Gaozu's magnanimity.
