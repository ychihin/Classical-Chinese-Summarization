Zhu Yi, courtesy name Yanhe, was from Qiantang, Wu County. His father, Zhu Xun, served as an official in the Qi Dynasty. As a child, Zhu Yi was fond of gambling and was disliked by his community. In his youth, he reformed his habits, studied the Five Classics—especially the "Li" and "Yi"—and became skilled in literature, history, chess, calligraphy, and arithmetic. At age twenty, he went to the capital, where Shen Yue interviewed him and remarked on his diverse talents. Despite the rule that officials must be at least twenty-five, Zhu Yi was appointed at twenty-one as Yangzhou Councilor due to an imperial exception. Doctor Mingshan of the Five Classics recommended him, praising his moral character and intellect. Emperor Gaozu summoned Zhu Yi to explain the "Book of Filial Piety" and the "Book of Changes," was pleased, and appointed him to the Western Province and later as a doctor of Imperial College. He rose through various posts, including Hongluqing and prince's right guard.

In the fifth year of the Ordinary Period, during a northern expedition, Yuan Faseng of the Northern Wei sought to surrender. Zhu Yi confirmed the sincerity of the surrender, and Emperor Gaozu placed him in command. After Zhou She's death, Zhu Yi managed court affairs, military appointments, and imperial edicts. He was known for his efficiency in handling documents. In the sixth year of Datong, he lectured on the "Laozi Yi" to over a thousand listeners. He later served as a servant and general. When Hou Jing surrendered, Zhu Yi advised Emperor Gaozu to accept him, citing a dream of unification. Despite warnings from officials like Xie Li, the emperor followed Zhu Yi's advice. Hou Jing later rebelled, blaming Zhu Yi, who raised troops to defend the capital. Zhu Yi had suppressed earlier reports of Hou Jing's conspiracy, leading to the city's siege. The crown prince's poem criticized Zhu Yi, who fell ill and died at sixty-seven. Emperor Gaozu mourned him deeply and granted posthumous honors. Zhu Yi held power for over thirty years, was skilled at flattering the emperor, and amassed wealth through gifts. He was stingy, never sharing with others, and wrote over 100 works, many lost in war.

He Chen, courtesy name Guobao, was from Shanyin. His uncle He Chang taught him Confucian classics, and he excelled in the "Three Rites." After He Chang's death, He Chen sold millet to support himself. He later taught economics and was summoned by the governor of Linchuan. Emperor Gaozu appointed him as a minister and doctor of Taixue. He Chen compiled the "New Mi Law" and debated ritual matters, such as whether one could marry during mourning. He argued that rituals should not interfere with mourning, and his views were accepted. He served as a regular attendant and censor, but was dismissed for buying the princess's house. He later held various posts, including Huangmen minister and Guozi doctor. He Chen was known for his elegant demeanor and was called "He Ya" in the palace. He submitted a sealed memorial criticizing current affairs, including the decline in household registrations due to corrupt officials, extravagant feasts and entertainments, and the need for frugality. He also criticized the emperor's reliance on flattering officials and the waste of resources. Emperor Gaozu responded angrily, defending his own frugality and questioning He Chen's claims. He Chen apologized and did not criticize again. He later served as Taifuqing and general. During Hou Jing's rebellion, He Chen was captured and forced to persuade officials to surrender. He fled home after Taicheng fell but was recaptured and appointed as Doctor Jin Ziguanglu. He died at sixty-nine, leaving over 100 chapters of works.

Yao Cha commented that Zhu Yi and He Chen rose from humble backgrounds through their knowledge of Confucian classics. However, Zhu Yi's flattery and failure to assist the emperor properly led to national disaster. Despite his guilt, he received special favors after death, which Yao Cha criticized as unjust governance.
