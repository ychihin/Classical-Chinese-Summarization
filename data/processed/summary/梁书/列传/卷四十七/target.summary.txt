The text describes filial piety as the foundation of virtue, emphasizing its importance for both the people and rulers. Emperor Gaozu promoted filial piety through edicts, transforming customs and encouraging virtuous behavior. The text then recounts the deeds of several individuals known for their exceptional filial piety.

Teng Tanggong, from Nanchang, demonstrated filial piety at age five by seeking cold melons for his ill mother, which a monk miraculously provided. After his parents' deaths, he mourned excessively, refusing food and warm clothing. Divine signs, including a vision of Buddha, appeared at his home. He refused official positions and was praised as "Teng Zengzi."

Xu Puji, from Linxiang, protected his mother's coffin from a fire, suffering severe burns. A girl from Wanling fought a tiger to save her mother, carrying her home before she died. Shen Chongsu, from Wuxing, mourned his mother with extreme grief, lying on her grave in all weather. Despite an imperial edict promoting him, he refused the position and died from grief at age thirty-nine.

Xunjiang, from Yingyin, mourned his father and brother with such intensity that he lost his hair and eyesight. He was promoted by imperial decree but died at twenty-one from grief. Yu Qianlou, from Xinye, tasted his father's excrement to check his health and prayed to die in his father's place. He served as an honest official, refusing treasures, and died at forty-six.

Ji Feo, from Xiangyang, begged to die for his father, who was falsely accused. At fifteen, he beat the Dengwen drum and faced torture without flinching. His father was pardoned, but Ji Feo later died from heart palpitations. Zhen Tian, from Jiangling, mourned his father from childhood, with birds responding to his cries. He was commended by the emperor and appointed to a military post.

Han Huaiming, from Jingzhou, prayed for his mother's recovery and heard a divine voice. He mourned his father excessively and served his mother with devotion. White doves nested at his mourning hut. He refused official posts and died at home. Liu Tanjing, from Pengcheng, mourned his father with vomiting blood and later served his mother with care, dying within a year of her death.

He Jiong, from Lujiang, served his ill father without rest, changing appearance drastically. He died from grief after his father's death. Yu Shami, from Yingyin, refused fine clothes due to family tragedy. He served his aunt with devotion, and pine trees grew at her tomb. He was commended for filial piety and died in office.

Jiang Yu, from Kaocheng, served his blind father, dreaming of "Wisdom Eye Water." He built a temple where an old well appeared, curing his father's eyes. He mourned his father excessively and died. Liu Ji, from Pingyuan, mourned his father with vomiting blood and served his mother, reciting sutras. White cranes appeared at his mourning hut. He died before mourning ended at fifty-two.

Chu Xiu, from Qiantang, mourned his parents with extreme grief, fasting for twenty-three days and dying from weakness. Xie Lin, from Yangxia, refused food before his parents ate. He mourned his father excessively and later his mother, dying from grief at thirty-eight. The text concludes by comparing these individuals to ancient filial exemplars like Zeng Shen, noting that some, like Liu Tanjing, He Jiong, Jiang Yu, and Xie Lin, died from grief, similar to Ruqiu and Wu.
