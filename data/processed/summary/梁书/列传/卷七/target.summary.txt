The text begins by referencing the "Yi" to establish the natural order of creation: heaven and earth, then all things, then men and women, and finally couples, emphasizing the supreme morality between husband and wife. It describes the Zhou etiquette system, where the emperor maintained a hierarchy of concubines—three wives, nine concubines, twenty-seven wives, and eighty-one royal wives—to govern women's education. The "Huanyi" compares the emperor and empress to the sun and moon, complementing each other like yin and yang. During the early Yi Dynasty, Qin titles were adopted, with the emperor's mother called empress dowager and his wife called empress, while other women received titles like beauty, beloved, eighth son, and seventh son. By the time of the ridge stem, there were fourteen titles including Jieyu. From the Wei and Zhi dynasties onward, queen mother titles followed Mo Dynasty rules, while wife titles varied by generation.

Emperor Gaozu, learning from past extravagance that led to ruin, practiced frugality, ate simple food, and wore plain clothes. His first wife died early, leaving the queen position vacant, and concubine numbers remained stable. Taizong and Shidan, heirs from outside the country, had concubines who died before them, so no new boudoirs were built. The text then details the lineage of a queen, whose grandfather was a salt magistrate. Her mother, Mrs. Xiao, was Saitong's cousin. The queen became Saitong's concubine during Sai Chengtai's reign, bearing King Lu Deng of Xuanwu in Changsha, Lu Ti of King Zhao of Yongyang, and Zhidan. Early in her household, she saw Changpu flowers glowing in the hall, swallowed them, and conceived Gaozu. Before giving birth, she saw many people in the hall. She later gave birth to Xiao Chang, King Xuan of Hengyang, and a daughter. She died in the seventh year of Taishi in the Song Dynasty and was buried in Dongcheng Mountain. In the first year of Tianjian, she was posthumously honored as queen with the title Mi.

Her father, Mu Zhi, was a sixth-generation grandson of Zhang Hua, a Jin Dynasty Sikong. After Zhang Hua's punishment, Mu Zhi's great-grandfather was transferred but recalled. Mu Zhi served as a member of the Communist Party of China during the Song, Yuan, and Jia years, befriending Jiang Zhan and Yuan Shu. Foreseeing disaster, he sought an outside post, becoming General Ningyuan and Governor of Jiaosha, where he governed well. After the governor's death, he calmed the territory. Emperor Severn praised him but he died before being appointed governor of Manye. His son Hongji died in office. Gaozu posthumously gave Mu Zhi the title of Guanglu Doctor and a gold medal, later adding Ting Wei Qing. Hongzhan had no sons, so his nephew Hongce's son Zhang Lan became his heir.

Empress Xiao Yan, Emperor Wu of Liang, was from Jinxiang, Gaoping. Her grandfather Xi Shao was a teacher to King Shu Hai, and her father Xi Ye was a crown prince who died early. Her mother, Princess Xunyang, dreamed of having a son before her birth. At birth, a red light shone, alarming the family. A wizard performed an exorcism. The queen was intelligent, skilled in calligraphy, and loved history. She refused marriage proposals from the Song deposed emperor and Anlu King Xiao Mian. At the end of Jianyuan, Gaozu became engaged to her. They had three daughters. In the first year of Chengtai, she served as governor of Zong Maggot, then died in Xiangyang in the first year of Yongyuan at age thirty-two. She was buried in Dongchengshan. In the second year of Zhongxing, she was granted the title of Liang Gong Concubine, and later queen. Her posthumous title was Empress De, and her tomb was Xiuling. Her father Xi Ye was posthumously named Doctor Jin Ziguanglu.

Taizong's Queen Jian, named Wang, was from Linyi, Langxie. Her grandfather Wang Jian was a Taiwei and Duke Wenxian. She was gentle and wise, becoming Princess Jin'an in the eleventh year of Tianjian. She bore Prince Ai, Prince Zuolian of Nanjun, and Princess Chengshan. She became crown princess in the third year of Zhongdatong and died in the third year of Li Zha at age forty-five. Taizong posthumously titled her empress with Mi Jian, and she was buried in Zhuangling. An imperial decree mandated frugality for her funeral, citing historical precedents. Her father Wang Qian, originally named Si Ji, changed his name to avoid conflict with Emperor Qi Gao. He served as a foreign minister, Prince Xima, and prefect of Yixing, later becoming a hussar councilor and Huangmenlang. He was calm and did not seek advancement. At the end of Yongyuan, he refused promotion to Shizhong. Under Gaozu, he served as Grand Sima consultant and later Shizhong. After the abdication, he was granted the title of Marquis of Nanchang County. He died in the third year of his life at age forty-nine, posthumously granted Shizhong and Jin Ziguanglu Doctor.

Emperor Gaozu's concubine Ding, named Lingguang, was from Qiao State, living in Xiangyang. Born in Fancheng with a divine light and purple smoke, she was named "Light." A fortune teller predicted her wealth. At fourteen, Gaozu married her. She had a red mole on her left arm that disappeared when she was sent away. She served Empress De carefully and once saw divine figures. During Gaozu's rebellion, Crown Prince Zhaoming was born. In the first year of Tianjian, she was made a noble concubine, living in Xianyang Hall. A minister argued that her status should be respected due to her son, citing historical precedents. She was kind, frugal, and avoided luxury. She promoted Buddhism, refused meat, and ate only vegetables. On her ordination day, sweet dew fell. She was proficient in the Pure Name Sutra. She died in the seventh year of her life at age forty-two. A condolence poem praised her virtues. She was posthumously titled Mu. After Taizong ascended, she was honored as Empress Dowager Mu. Her father was a governor.

Emperor Gaozu's doctor, Zhao Yao, originally surnamed Hu, was from Hejikung Yao. King Zha Shi'an took her as a concubine. After Dong Guang's defeat, she was brought into the palace by Donghun Hou. After Jianchenyuan was pacified, Zhidan accepted her as a palace maid. In the seventh year of Yue, she gave birth to Shidan and was granted the title of Xiurong. She died in the sixth year of Zuo Hui at age sixty-seven and was buried in Tongwang Mountain. After Shidan became emperor, she was honored as Empress Dowager Wenxuan. Her father was posthumously granted titles, and her mother was made a marquis's wife.

Shizu's concubine Xu, named Zhaopei, was from Tan in the East China Sea. Her grandfather was a Taiwei and Duke Wenzhong, and her father was a general. In the 16th year of Tianjian, she was named Princess Xiangshu. She bore Fang Deng and Princess Hanzhen. In the third year of Taiqing, she was ordered to commit suicide and buried in Waguan Temple. The text concludes that the empress supported royal customs, while Mu Guifei's glory was displayed through her son, but the ancestor's concubine Xu lacked virtue and brought about her own destruction.
