The Han Dynasty, learning from the Qin Dynasty's book burning, vigorously promoted Confucianism, with the Imperial Academy often hosting over ten thousand students and schools flourishing even in remote areas. However, at the end of the Han Dynasty, political turmoil led to Confucianism's decline. During the Wei Dynasty's Zhengshi period, Taoist philosophy gained prominence, reducing interest in Confucianism. Figures like Xun Pin and Zhi Yu attempted reforms but failed to change customs. After the Jin Dynasty's founding, the Central Plains fell into chaos, and the court moved south, leaving no time for education. During the Song and Qi Dynasties, schools were sporadically opened but quickly abandoned, with no sustained encouragement. Local schools vanished, ministers rarely mastered the classics, and young people had no means to study. The three virtues and six arts were neglected for a long time.

Emperor Gaozu of Liang, deeply concerned, issued edicts to seek scholars, rectify rituals, revise laws, and correct standards. In the fourth year of Tianjian, he decreed the establishment of Five Classics doctors, one per classic, and the widespread opening of schools. Scholars like Pingyuan Mingshanbin, Wu Jun Lu Lian, Wu Xing Shen Jun, Jianping Yan Zhizhi, and Kuaiji He Chang were appointed as doctors, each presiding over a school. Hundreds of students received salaries from the court, and those who excelled were appointed as officials. Over a decade, many proficient in Confucian classics gathered in the capital. Students were also sent to study with He Yin in Kuaiji. In the seventh year of Tianjian, Emperor Gaozu further decreed the promotion of education, encouraging the children of nobles to study the ten ethics and three virtues. The crown prince, princes, and nobles began attending schools, and the emperor personally visited to honor sages and encourage students. The school became solemn and prosperous, with scholars like Fu Manrong, He Tongzhi, and Fan Zhen gaining fame.

Fu Manrong, from Anqiu, lost his father early and lived with his mother and brother in the South China Sea. He was studious, proficient in Laozi and the Book of Changes, and bold in speech. He gathered students to teach and held various official positions, including Piaoqi Canjun and Nanhai Prefect. During the Qi Dynasty, he served as Tongzhi Sanqi Changshi and later as Zhongshu Lang. He was known for lecturing on scriptures at home, attracting dozens or hundreds of students. In the Liang Dynasty, he was appointed as Sima and Linhai Prefect, dying at eighty-two. He authored annotations on the Zhouyi, Mao's Poems, and other works.

He Tongzhi, from Lujiang, was deeply interested in the Three Rites from childhood, studying independently and mastering three hundred chapters of the Li. Respected by Taiwei Wang Jian, he held various posts, including Shangshu Temple Minister and Infantry Colonel. During the Yongyuan period, despite war, he continued lecturing. After Emperor Gaozu's ascension, he became Zuocheng, aiding in system creation. He died at fifty-five, posthumously honored as Huangmen Shilang. He wrote about one hundred articles on Li Yi.

Fan Zhen, from Nanxiang, lost his father young and served his mother filially. He studied under Liu Shan, excelling in the Three Rites. He served as an envoy to the Wei and was known for his straightforward nature. He debated Buddhism with Xiao Ziliang, arguing against the existence of gods. His "Theory on the Destruction of Gods" sparked controversy. He held various posts, including Yidu Prefect and Jin'an Prefect, dying as Zhongshu Lang. His son Fan Xu inherited his knowledge.

Yan Zhizhi, from Jianping, was proficient in Zhuangzi, Laozi, and the Three Rites. He served as Luling Kingdom Minister and Guanghan Kingdom Right Changshi, known for his righteousness in handling the Guanghan King's funeral. He was appointed as a Five Classics doctor, lecturing to hundreds of students. He died at fifty-two, leaving his family in poverty. He authored 479 volumes of Annotations on Evil Etiquette.

He Chang, from Kuaiji, inherited family education in the Three Rites. Recommended by Liu Xian, he served as Taichang Cheng and Five Classics doctor, lecturing on the Li. He authored works on etiquette and the Five Classics, dying at fifty-nine. His son He Ge also became a renowned scholar, lecturing on the Three Rites.

Sima Yun, from Hanoi, lost his father young but loved learning, excelling in the Three Rites. He served as Shangshu Temple Minister and debated mourning rites for loving mothers. He died as Shixing Neishi. His son Sima Shou inherited his studies.

Bian Hua, from Jiyin, lost his father young but studied the Five Classics, especially the Book of Changes. He served as a Five Classics doctor, known for his eloquence and musical expertise. He died as Wuling Prefect.

Cui Lingen, from Qinghe, studied the Five Classics, especially the Three Rites and Three Commentaries. He served as Taichang Doctor in the Northern Dynasties before returning to Liang, where he lectured to hundreds. He authored numerous works on the classics.

Kong Qian, from Kuaiji, studied under He Yin, excelling in the Five Classics. He served as a Five Classics doctor but was not skilled in governance. He died during the Liqian turmoil.

Lu Guang, from Fanyang, was proficient in scriptures and served as a Five Classics doctor, known for his clear pronunciation. He died as Xunyang Prefect.

Shen Jun, from Wuxing, studied the Five Classics, especially the Three Rites. Recommended as a Five Classics doctor, he lectured to hundreds. He died as Wukang Ling.

Taishi Shuming, from Wuxing, was proficient in Zhuangzi, Laozi, and the Book of Changes, lecturing to over five hundred. He died at seventy-three.

Confucius Qu, from Kuaiji, lost his father young but became proficient in the Guwen Shangshu. He served as a lecturer and helped compile the Liang Guan. He died at fifty-one, authoring works on the Shangshu.

Huang Kan, from Wu County, studied under He Chang, excelling in the Three Rites. He lectured to hundreds and authored the Book of Rites. He died at fifty-eight.

Yao Cha noted that these scholars, despite varying fortunes, contributed to Confucian learning during the Liang Dynasty.
