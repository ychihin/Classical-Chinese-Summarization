# Summary of Historical Records on Liang Dynasty Governance and Officials

Emperor Xuan of the Han Dynasty believed that fair administration and proper handling of lawsuits defined a good governor. Historical records note that local governors, considered "officials close to the people," were responsible for promoting virtue, refining customs, and changing social practices through etiquette. During the chaotic end of the Qi Dynasty, power fell into villainous hands, with harsh taxes and unrestrained corvée labor. Emperor Gaozu, having lived among the people and understood their suffering, frequently issued lenient orders after establishing his rule, exempting heavy taxes and allowing the populace to recuperate.

After ascending the throne, Emperor Gaozu personally attended to state affairs, working through the night and understanding the people's hardships. He sent envoys to inspect local customs, set up complaint stones outside the imperial court for the poor to voice grievances, and showed sympathy for the suffering. In the first year of Tianjian, taxes on minors were removed, with only adults taxed. The emperor wore washed old clothes, kept no luxuries in the treasury, and court officials wore only silk without pearls or brocades. Eunuchs followed his example, eating only vegetables and drinking no more than three cups, setting a frugal example for the nation.

When selecting local governors, Emperor Gaozu chose honest and fair individuals, personally summoning them to encourage benevolent governance. He promoted capable officials from small counties to larger ones, and from larger counties to governorships. Notable appointees included Dao Gai as internal history of Jian'an and Liu as prefect of Jin'an, both known for integrity. Qiu Zhongfu, magistrate of Shanyin County, was promoted to internal history of Changsha for outstanding achievements, while He Yuan of Wukang became prefect of Xuancheng for honesty. These governors became admired models, with some using Confucian classics to embellish government orders and being remembered fondly by the people after leaving office.

The text provides detailed biographies of several officials. Yu Mi, from a prominent Western Chu family, served in various positions including as envoy to Qiaoli. Known for honesty, he twice held the position of Biejia in Jingzhou without seeking personal gain, living in poverty with his family suffering hunger and cold. Emperor Ming commended him, and after his death in Tianjian's first year, Emperor Gaozu provided silk and rice for his burial.

Shen Zhen, from Wukang, gained fame for pleading for his uncle Shen Chang's pardon. He served under Prince Jingling, punishing bullies according to law and earning respect. He built roads efficiently, completed construction projects under budget, and as Jiande magistrate, ordered planting of mulberry, persimmon, pear, and chestnut trees. He later persuaded Chen Bozhi to surrender to Gaozu during the siege of Yingcheng. As Yuyao magistrate, he curbed powerful families who sought favors, punishing those who came to the yamen without proper cause. However, he used his position to take revenge on wealthy families who had humiliated him when he was poor selling pottery, causing fear and complaints among local gentry. He maintained personal honesty throughout. He was killed by robbers at age fifty-nine, with many believing Xiao Yingda responsible.

Fan Shuzeng, from Qiantang, studied the Five Classics under Lu Daohui, who predicted he would become the emperor's teacher. He served as mentor to Prince Wenhui and King Wenxuan of Jingling. As governor of Chengsei, he governed with integrity without harsh methods. In Yongjia County, he refused gifts and the customary 200,000 yuan given to outgoing prefects. When leaving, his luggage was so simple no cart was needed, and the people wept for miles. Emperor Gaozu appointed him Taizhong doctor with silk rewards, but Fan donated all his salary and died in poverty at age seventy-nine.

Qiu Zhongfu, from Wucheng, was called a "thousand-mile horse" by his grandfather. Poor and unable to support his family, he advised thieves in Sanwu, successfully avoiding exposure. He later served as Huling magistrate with a reputation for capability, resisting insults from the favored minister Lu Wenxian. During Wang Jingze's rebellion, he drained canal water to block rebel ships, contributing to their defeat. As Shanyin magistrate, he was so effective that people sang "Two Fu Shen Liu are not as good as one Qiu," surpassing previous magistrates. Though involved in corruption during Qi's chaos, he escaped punishment through a pardon. Under Emperor Gaozu, he served as Shanzhao magistrate again, known for handling complex affairs wisely. He wrote twenty volumes of "Imperial Code" and other works.

Sun Qian, from Dongguan, was known for ability from age seventeen. He farmed to support his siblings after his father's death. As Jurong magistrate, he was honest with a strong memory. As governor of Badong and Jianping counties in the Three Gorges, he refused soldiers, instead extending benevolence to barbarians who surrendered and offered treasures, which he refused. He exempted officials and people from bearing official salaries. After three years, the counties were peaceful. He served in multiple positions, always maintaining integrity, often borrowing government carports to live in. As Lingling prefect, tigers disappeared during his tenure but returned after he left. He encouraged farming and sericulture, with his people earning more than neighboring counties. At over ninety, he was as strong as a fifty-year-old, arriving at court before others. He cared for his sick brother and took in the homeless Liu Rong, burying him properly. He died at ninety-two in Tianjian's fifteenth year.

He Yuan, from Donghai, participated in Xiao Baoxuan's failed rebellion, fleeing to various protectors before surrendering to Wei. He eventually reached Gaozu's rebel army, who praised him as an outstanding man. As Wuchang prefect, he changed his conduct, refusing gifts and buying cold water from wells in summer. His carriages and clothes were shabby, and he ate only dried fish despite cheap aquatic products. However, he whipped many for minor offenses, leading to accusations and interrogation. He withstood twenty-one days of station test punishment without confessing, but was expelled for hiding the emperor's ceremonial guard. As Wukang magistrate, he eliminated unethical sacrifices and served only dry food and tea to inspecting prefect Wang Bin, presenting a bucket of wine and a goose as farewell. He was promoted to Xuancheng prefect, an unusual promotion from county magistrate. He built roads and organized city warehouses, using his salary to pay taxes for the poorest. Wherever he served, people built shrines for him. He was dismissed from Shuyang after being slandered, but never discussed personal honor or disgrace. He died at fifty-two in the second year of Putong.

Yao Cha noted that previous dynasties included biographies of good officials because the times demanded them. During Emperor Wu of Han, heavy corvée and widespread treachery necessitated cruel officials, but excessive punishment caused resentment. With Liang's rise, harsh laws were eliminated, simplicity replaced glitz, people were educated in filial piety and brotherhood, and farming encouraged. Consequently, even fierce people became virtuous, and honest customs formed naturally. No biographies of cruel officials were included in Liang's records.
