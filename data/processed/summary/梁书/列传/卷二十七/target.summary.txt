# Summary of Historical Biographies

Lu Chui, styled Zuogong, was from Wu County and a sixth-generation descendant of Jin Dynasty Taiwei Lu Wan. He studied diligently from youth, building a two-story thatched house where he isolated himself for years of study. He possessed remarkable memory, once silently reconstructing four lost volumes of the "Han Shu" from memory without error. At seventeen, he became a scholar of the state. Throughout his career, he held various military and civil positions including Yi Cao参军, Luling King Fa Cao参军, and chief secretary. He was highly regarded by contemporaries, with Ren Fang praising him as "a generation of great men." He authored twenty volumes of collected works and died at fifty-seven.

Dao Qia, styled Maoyi, was from Pengcheng. At eighteen, he began his official career. Known for his talent and moral character, he impressed Xie Tiao, who praised his civil and military abilities. When offered a position by Xie Peng, he declined, foreseeing chaos in the realm, and built a secluded mountain dwelling. Emperor Gaozu recognized his talents, and he held numerous positions including prince's sacrificer, chief of staff, and censor Zhongcheng. He was known for his integrity, refusing bribes and correcting wrongs without fear of nobles. He died at fifty-one while serving as Xunyang Prefect.

Mingshan Bin, styled Xiaoruo, was from Pingyuan. His father lived in seclusion. From age seven, Shan Bin could discuss right and wrong, and by thirteen mastered classical texts. He held various academic and official positions, including the first Doctor of the Five Classics. As governor, he once released grain to aid famine victims without proper documentation, losing his house when audited, yet he did not appeal. Prince Zhaoming praised his virtue and provided assistance. He authored 224 volumes of "Notes on Ji Li" and other works, dying at eighty-five.

Yin Jun, styled Jihe, was from Changping. His father was killed alongside his father-in-law Wang Huan. Orphaned at nine, Yin Jun was known for filial piety. He married Princess Yongxing and held various positions including Secretary Lang and Prince Sheren. He compiled the four books of Mi Ge and catalogued calligraphy monuments. As Linchuan Internal History, he governed with virtue, causing thieves to flee. He once captured a robber leader but released him after a gentle scolding, and the man reformed. He died at forty-nine.

Lu Xiang, styled Shiqing, was from Wu County. His father and brother were killed during a rebellion, and he mourned beyond ritual requirements. He held positions including Yongning magistrate and served Prince Zhaoming. When his mother fell ill requiring corn syrup in winter, an old man appeared selling exactly the needed amount, then vanished—considered divine intervention. As Poyang Internal History, he suppressed a rebellion led by Xian Yu Chen, capturing him alive while protecting innocent people from false accusations. He reconciled feuding families through gentle persuasion. During Hou Jing's rebellion, the city fell and Lu Xiang fled, later dying of grief at seventy. He lived simply, avoiding music and discussions of killing for fifty years.

The historian Yao Cha commented that these men, despite different positions and salaries, were all listed among famous ministers for their learning, loyalty, integrity, and filial piety.
