Wang Sengru, courtesy name Sengru, was a native of Tan, Donghai, and the eighth-generation grandson of Wang Su, a general of the Wei Dynasty. His great-grandfather Wang Ya served as Dr. Zuo Guanglu and Yi Tong in the Jin Dynasty, while his grandfather Wang Zhun served as Situ Zuo Changshi in the Song Dynasty. At age five, Sengru read the "Book of Filial Piety" and asked his teacher about its contents. Told it discussed loyalty and filial piety, he expressed a desire to read it often. By six, he could write articles and grew up eager to learn. His family was poor, so he often copied books for others to support his mother, memorizing them afterward. He served as an official in the Qi Dynasty, recruited as the Kingdom's Zuo Changshi and Doctor of Taixue. Wang Yan, the minister of public affairs, admired him and appointed him to compile "New Records of the East Palace." He later held various posts, including Grand Sima Yuzhang and Wang Xing joined the army, and Doctor of Imperial College. Prince Wenhui summoned him to Shu Palace, but his planned appointment as a vassal of the crown prince was not implemented due to the prince's death. In the early years of the founding, an edict recommended talents, and Wang Luoguang praised Sengru's intelligence and familiarity with sages' words and deeds. He was awarded titles such as Cao Lang, Minister Yi, and later Chief Secretary, Censor, and Ling of Qian and Tang Dynasties.

Sengru was highly regarded by his friends, including Ren Zuo, who wrote a poem praising his conduct and speech. In the early years of Tianjian, he served as governor of Nanhai, where he refused to exploit foreign merchants for profit, unlike previous officials. After one month, he was ordered to return to the capital, but six hundred people requested his retention, which the court denied. He then became Zhongshulang, compiled "Zhongbiaobu" and "Daily Notes," and later served as Minister Zuocheng and guerrilla general. When his mother, who had sold gauze for a living, died, he grieved deeply. He was demoted to General Yunqi due to official duties but soon returned to full-time work. Emperor Gaozu praised his poem "Poetry on Spring Scenery and Mingzhi" as the most exquisite among those composed by officials. He later served as Shaofu Qing and Shangshu Libulang, participating in official selections. As chief historian of King Renwei of Nankang, he suppressed the domineering Wang Dian, who slandered him, leading to his arrest and imprisonment. In a farewell letter to the king, Sengru expressed regret for his imprisonment and lamented his inability to repay the king's kindness. He was dismissed from office and not promoted for a long time.

Sengru wrote to his friend He Jiong, expressing his sorrow and describing his suffering. He lamented his lack of talent and the misfortunes that had befallen him, including illness and humiliation. He reflected on his low status and the envy of others, noting that his family was poor and he had no gifts to offer friends. He felt isolated and slandered, leading to his deposition. He described the sadness of autumn and his loneliness, living among birds and beasts. He hoped to meet intellectual friends again but feared he would die in obscurity. After a long time, he was appointed by King Cheng of Anxi to join the army and later served in various posts, including Wang Zhongji of Youduo County. He died three years later at age fifty-eight. Sengru loved ancient classics and collected over 10,000 volumes of books, many rare. His articles were elegant and recorded new things. His works included "Eighteen Prefecture Genealogy" (710 volumes), "Collection of Hundred Family Genealogy" (15 volumes), "Collection of Southeastern Genealogy" (10 volumes), and "Collection of Essays" (30 volumes), among others.

Zhang Shu, courtesy name Shi Jian, was from Wu County. His grandfather Zhang Yong served as Doctor Youguanglu in the Song Dynasty, and his father Zhang Quan held high status in the Qi Dynasty. At twelve, Zhang Shu could write articles, limiting himself to one poem daily. By sixteen, he had written over two thousand poems. In the Qi Dynasty, he was summoned as chief secretary by Xiao Yaoguang but did not take the post. He started as Zuo Lang and later became Prince Sheren. He was friendly with Lu Chui, and they visited Shen Yue together, who praised them as outstanding talents. Zhang Shu was moved to various posts, including Zhonglang of Shangshu Palace and Xizhonglang. He served as prince to wash horses and later as chief minister of the state. In the early years of Tianjian, he was appointed as a plastic orbit friend. He was moved to Situ Xie Naotun and ordered to copy books and write articles on women's affairs. He wrote "Wai Shao Fu," praised by Emperor Gaozu, who compared him to Sima Xiangru and Mei Gao. He was moved to Secretary Cheng and treated with great honor. In March of the fourth year, a banquet was held in Huaguang Hall, where he wrote a poem about a dancing horse. He later served in various posts, including Zhongquan Jian'an Wang Zhongji's Office and Yunhui Zhongji Office. He stayed in the palace for ten years, receiving great favor. After returning to the capital, he served as a servant of the prince and later as General Zhaoyuan. He was moved to Huangmen Shilang and later became prefect of Xin'an. He died before the mourning period for his mother was over at age fifty-three. Prince Zhaoming sent an envoy to present funeral property. Zhang Shu liked drinking, was tolerant, and did not mind housework. He wrote fifteen volumes of "Wenheng" and thirty volumes of collected works.

Liu Xiaochuo, courtesy name Xiaochuo, was from Pengcheng. His grandfather Liu Meng was Duke Zhongzhao of Sikong in the Song Dynasty, and his father Ming Hui served as a lieutenant. Xiaochuo could write articles at seven and was admired by his uncle Wang Rong as a child prodigy. He drafted for his father during the Qi Dynasty. Shen Yue, Ren Fang, and Fan Yun visited him, with Ren Fang particularly appreciative. In the early years of Tianjian, he started as Zuo Lang and wrote "Poem of Returning to Mu." He was moved to Prince Sheren and later served as an official and minister of the Ministry of Water. Emperor Gaozu praised his articles and appointed him to various posts, including Secretary Cheng. He served as a consultant to King Ansheng and later as Minister of the Taifu and Prince's Servant. Prince Zhaoming treated him as a distinguished guest and had his portrait painted in Lexian Hall. Xiaochuo compiled the prince's collection and wrote the preface. He was moved to Sanqi Changshi and later served as Tingweiqing. He was dismissed after being impeached for bringing his concubine into the government while his mother lived elsewhere. He wrote letters criticizing Dao Qia, which were burned by Prince Zhaoming. He later served as Xizhong Lang to consult with the king of Xiangdong. He thanked the emperor and crown prince for their favor. He later served as a servant of the prince and died at age fifty-nine. Xiaochuo was famous but willful and scornful of others. His articles were highly valued and widely circulated. His three younger sisters were married to talented men, and his wife was particularly elegant.

Wang Jun, also named Yuanli and Derou, was from Linyi, Langxie. His grandfather Jianglu served as Duke of Kong Gelong, and his father Dan served as Taizhong doctor. Wang Jun could write articles at seven and wrote "Ode to Peony Root" at sixteen. He was as famous as his cousin Wang Tai. He started his career joining the army as King Xing of Linchuan and later served as Prince Sheren. He was appointed Zhonglang of the Shangshu Palace, a rare position for the Wang family. Shen Yue praised his articles, saying he was unmatched. Wang Jun wrote poems with uncommon rhymes and was highly regarded by Prince Zhaoming. He served as Danyang Yin Cheng and later as Zhongshu Lang. He compiled "Zhongshu Biaozuo" and other works. He was appointed Shangshu Libulang and later served as Prince's concubine. After Prince Zhaoming's death, he wrote a mourning poem. He served as General Zhenwei and Prefect of Linhai but was sued. He later served as Yunzhang Wang Changshi and moved to secretary supervisor. He died at age sixty-nine after falling into a well during a thief attack. Wang Jun was generous and loyal, famous for his talents. He wrote extensively, copying many classics himself. His collection included one hundred volumes of articles.
