Zhou She, courtesy name Shengyi, was from Runan'an and a descendant of Jin official Zhou Chan. His father Zhou Yi served as Vice Minister of Zhongshu in the Qi Dynasty. Zhou She was intelligent from youth, and his father advised him to uphold morality rather than worry about wealth. He grew up erudite, eloquent, and skilled in recitation and debate. He began his official career as a Doctor of Taixue in the Qi Dynasty and later held various posts. During the Jianwu period, he impressed Confucian scholar Wu Bao with his knowledge, gaining a reputation for eloquence. Wang Liang appointed him chief administrator, entrusting him with political affairs. In the Liang Dynasty, Fan Yun recommended him to Emperor Gaozu, who appointed him Shangshu Temple Minister. Zhou She contributed to the establishment of state rituals and held numerous positions, including Zhongshu Shilang and Hongshanqing. He remained loyal to Wang Liang even after Wang Liang's disgrace, personally arranging his funeral. Despite frequent role changes, he rarely rested, overseeing national history, rituals, laws, and military strategy. He served the emperor in confidential matters for over twenty years, never leaking secrets. He lived frugally, with simple clothing and dusty homes. After his mother's death, he resigned but later returned to service. In the seventh year of the Putong era, he was implicated in a letter from Wuling Prefect Bai Guo offering him a bribe and was dismissed. He died that year at age fifty-six. The emperor mourned him, granting posthumous honors and praising his integrity, knowledge, and loyalty. Zhou She left two sons, Hongyi and Hongxin.

Xu Mian, courtesy name Xiuren, was from Tan, East China Sea. His father Xu Rong served as Prime Minister of Nanchang. Orphaned young, Xu Mian cultivated moral integrity early. At six, he wrote a prayer during a rainstorm, praised by elders. He grew up diligent in learning, starting as a son of the state. Wang Jian, the wine minister, praised his prime ministerial demeanor. He ranked first in exams and held various posts, including Doctor of Taixue and Shangshu Palace lieutenant. He was dismissed due to official business but later reinstated. Wang Yuanchang sought his acquaintance, but Xu Mian declined, foreseeing Wang's trouble, earning admiration for his insight. He befriended King Xuanwu of Changsha and met Emperor Gaozu in Xinlin, who valued him. After Gaozu's accession, Xu Mian served as Minister of Zhongshu and other roles, known for effective law enforcement and impeachments. In the second year of Tianjian, he became Minister of Huangmen and Libu, participating in talent selection. During a northern campaign, he worked tirelessly on military documents, often not returning home for days. In the sixth year, he became Minister of Shizhong and later Minister of the Ministry of Personnel, managing selections efficiently. He avoided discussing official matters at social gatherings, earning respect for his lack of selfishness. He served as Prince Zhan Shi and taught the "Book of Filial Piety" to Prince Zhaoming. Xu Mian reformed funeral customs, advocating for three-day burials per ancient rites, which the emperor approved. He rose to Shangshu Pushe and Zhongwei General, serving the emperor wholeheartedly. He participated in revising the Five Rites, submitting a detailed memorial in the sixth year of the Putong era, compiling 1,176 volumes of rituals. He left his descendants integrity rather than wealth, as expressed in a letter to his son Xu Song. He built a small garden for leisure, not profit. After his son Xu Qiu's death, he wrote "Reply to Guests" to express his grief. In the third year of Zhongdatong, he retired due to illness, receiving honors. He died in the first year of Datong at age seventy. The emperor mourned deeply, granting posthumous titles and gifts. Xu Mian was a prolific writer, compiling works like "Notes on Living Things" and "Huilin." His son Xu Qiu was also talented, serving as Prince Sheren and Jin'an internal history. Chen Libu Shangshu praised Xu Mian as a respected minister of the Liang Dynasty.
