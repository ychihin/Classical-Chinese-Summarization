Liu Jun, courtesy name Xiaobiao, was from Pingyuan. His father was Liu Ge. At age one, his mother took him home. During Song Taishi, Qingzhou fell to the Wei, and at eight, Liu Jun was kidnapped to Zhongshan. A wealthy man, Liu Shi, redeemed him and taught him. The Wei moved him to Sangqian. He was an eager learner, often reading by torchlight all night, sometimes burning his hair. During Qi Yongming, he returned to Qi. He sought out rare books, and Cui Weizu called him a "book slut." He sought a position under Xiao Ziliang but was devalued by Xu Xiaosi. He served under Xiao Yaoxin, who treated him generously. After Xiao Yaoxin's death, Liu Jun was long without promotion. In early Tianjian, he served as a secretary. He was dismissed for transporting prohibited items. He was later given books to compile "Lei Yuan," but left due to illness. He wrote "Mountain Qi Zhi." Emperor Gaozu disliked him for not following others, so he was not used. Liu Jun then wrote "Discrimination of Fate" to express his feelings.

The article "Discrimination of Fate" argues that a scholar's situation is determined by fate. It laments that talented people like Guan Ren have low positions. It states that the difference between human nature and endowment, and a lifetime of setbacks, cannot be understood. It discusses the concepts of Tao and nature, stating that destiny is determined in the dark and never changes. It lists sages like Jin Wengong and Confucius who could not escape fate. It mentions modern examples like Liu Shan and Liu Fa, who were talented but died in obscurity. It argues that nobility, longevity, and wealth are produced naturally, not by talent. It lists five areas of confusion regarding fate, including the belief that honor and disgrace are determined by people, that success comes from martial power, that good and evil are always rewarded, and that a country's success depends on its ruler. It concludes that a gentleman follows the right path, obeys heaven, and secures his own destiny, understanding that fate is beyond human control.

After Liu Jun's "Distinction of Fate" was written, Liu Bo of Zhongshan wrote to refute him. Liu Jun replied, but Liu Bo died before seeing the reply. Liu Jun wrote a letter to the deceased Liu Bo, expressing his sorrow. Liu Jun's paper was not published due to its length. Liu Jun wrote an "Automatic Preface" comparing himself to Feng Jingtong, listing three similarities and four differences. He lived in Shuyang, where many studied under him. He died at sixty, and his disciples gave him the posthumous name Mr. Xuanjing.

Liu Bo, courtesy name Mingxin, was from Zhongshan. He was good at writing articles and knowledgeable. He held various official positions and died during a dispatch.

Xie Jiqing was from Yangxia. His great-grandfather was Xie Lingyun. He was a child prodigy. He once threw himself into a river when his father was demoted. He was studious and had literary talent. He held various official positions, including Censor. He was open-minded and not bound by court laws. He was once dismissed for misconduct. He later petitioned to accompany the Northern Expedition but was dismissed after the army was defeated. He lived in seclusion, often drinking with friends. He died of illness. He had a collection of essays.

Liu Xie, courtesy name Yanhe, was from Ju. He lost his father early, did not marry, and lived with a monk, becoming proficient in Buddhist scriptures. He became an official in early Tianjian. He suggested changing suburban sacrifices to fruits and vegetables, which was adopted. Prince Zhaoming admired him. He wrote "The Literary Mind and the Carving of Dragons" in fifty chapters. The preface explains his motivation, stating that a gentleman wants to establish merit and write books. He dreamed of following Confucius. He argues that articles are branches of classics. He criticizes previous literary critics like Cao Pi and Lu Ji for being incomplete or trivial. His book discusses rhymed and unrhymed passages, analyzes emotions and literary talents, and covers various aspects of writing. He sought evaluation from Shen Yue, who valued the book. Liu Xie later became a monk, changing his name to Huidi, and died less than a year later.

Wang Ji, courtesy name Wenhai, was from Linyi. He could write at seven. He was talented and once wrote a poem that Shen Yue appreciated. He held various official positions, including magistrate, but was dismissed for misconduct. He visited mountains in Kuaiji, sometimes staying for months. He wrote a famous poem with the line "The forest with noisy cicadas is more quiet." He died soon after. His son Wang Bi died before him.

He Sicheng, courtesy name Yuanjing, was from Tan. He was diligent and good at writing. He held various official positions. Shen Yue admired his poem and had it inscribed on his wall. He was recommended to compile "Qing Lue." He moved to the position of Censor. He was knowledgeable and admired by Xu Mian and Zhou She. He died at fifty-four. He had fifteen volumes of collected works. He once said that He Xun was the most talented among the three Hes, but meant the reputation should go to himself. He Zilang, also from the same clan, was talented and died at twenty-four.

Liu Yao, courtesy name Shishen, was from Pingyuan. He was studious and well-read. Shen Yue and Ren Fang often asked him about forgotten knowledge. He corrected Shen Yue's interpretation of ancient sacrificial bottles. He was knowledgeable about many things, including the origin of the "long-necked king." Shen Yue praised his writings. He held various official positions, including magistrate, and had a clean record. He was recommended to compile "Bianlue." He wrote "Lin Ting Fu," which Wang Sengru praised. He died at fifty. He was honest and frugal. He wrote several books, including "Ya Ya" and "Chu Ci Cao Mu Shu."

Xie Zheng, courtesy name Xuandu, was from Yangxia. He was smart and handsome, fond of learning. He held various official positions. He once finished a poem in one second at a banquet, which Emperor Gaozu admired. He was appointed to discuss the appointment of a new crown prince. He died at thirty-seven. His friend Wang Shuan compiled his manuscripts.

Zang Yan, courtesy name Yanwei, was from Ju. He was filial and studious. He wrote "Tuenyou Fu," which Ren Fang admired. He was upright and did not follow rules. He was knowledgeable, especially in "Han Shu." He served as a county governor in barbarian areas, where he quelled banditry. He died during his tenure. He has ten volumes of collected works.

Fu Ting, courtesy name Shibiao. He was smart and talented, good at five-character poems. Ren Fang admired him. He met Emperor Gaozu at eighteen and was promoted. He lectured on "The Analects." He later built a house in the eastern suburbs and stopped serving. He wrote a letter to Xu Mian, expressing his desire for seclusion. Xu Mian replied, praising him but encouraging him to serve. Fu Ting later became an official again but was accused of accepting bribes. He hid as a Taoist priest. He later served under King Shaoling. He died during Hou Jing's rebellion. He wrote ten volumes of "Yi Shuo" and twenty volumes of collected works. His son, Fu Zhiming, served Hou Jing and died in prison.

Yu Zhongrong, courtesy name Zhongrong, was from Geling. He lost his father young and was studious. He was appointed as a crown prince through his uncle's request. He was knowledgeable but impulsive and liked to drink. He was friends with Wang Ji and Xie Jiqing. He died at seventy-four. He copied many books and wrote several works.

Lu Yungong, courtesy name Zilong, was from Wujun. He could recite classics at a young age. He was studious and talented. He wrote the "Taibo Temple Stele," which Zhang Zuan praised. He was called to serve by Emperor Gaozu. He was good at Go. Emperor Gaozu favored him greatly. He died at thirty-seven. Emperor Gaozu mourned him. Zhang Zan wrote a letter expressing his sorrow.

Ren Xiaogong, courtesy name Xiaogong, was from Linhuai. He was filial and studious. He was summoned to compile history books. He wrote quickly and was praised by Emperor Gaozu. He was well-versed in Buddhism. He was arrogant and looked down on others. During Hou Jing's invasion, he led soldiers but was killed when the rebels attacked.

Yan Xie, courtesy name Zihe, was from Linyi. He lost his father young. He was magnanimous and good at calligraphy. He served in the Xiangdong Kingdom. He and Gu Xie were called "Er Xie." He mourned his uncle with proper etiquette. He did not pursue prominent positions. He died at forty-two. His works were burned in a fire. His sons, Yan Zhiyi and Yan Zitui, became famous early.
