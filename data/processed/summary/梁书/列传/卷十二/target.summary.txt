**Summary of Historical Records: Liu Xun, Liu Chen, Xi Chanwen, Wei Rui, and Wei Ai**

Liu Xun, courtesy name Wentong, was born in Hedong. He began his official career at seventeen under Emperor Wu of Qi, serving in various posts including Sangonglang and Prince Zhongsheren. He befriended Prince Xiang of Badong but foresaw disaster due to the prince’s association with villains, returning to the capital feigning illness. He later served as Minister of Zhongshu, Commander of the Zhonghu Army, and Prefect of Xin'an, though he was dismissed for lack of political achievements. During the Jianwu era, he became captain of Xirong and governor of Liang, Nan, and Qin states. When Emperor Gaozu rebelled, Liu Xun responded with Hanzhong troops. Under Emperor He, he served as a servant and general, and later as Prince Zhan Shi under Emperor Gaozu. Gaozu praised him in a poem and consulted him on pardoning Xu Yuanyu’s disciples, to which Liu Xun wisely replied, earning acclaim. He died in office in October of the sixth year at age forty-six, posthumously honored as Shizhong and General of the Fu Army, with the title Mu. He authored "The Biography of Renzheng" and various poems.

Liu Chen, courtesy name Wenruo, was Liu Xun’s fifth younger brother. Known for filial piety, he cared for his ill parents without undressing for years. He served as Situ Xing and Prince Zhongsheren. During Qi Donghunhou’s reign, he advised Xiao Yingzhou against attacking Emperor Gaozu, warning of mutual destruction and citing past examples. His counsel led Yingzhou to ally with Gaozu. Liu Chen later held posts such as Shangshu Libu Lang, General of the Assistant State, and Prefect of Nanping. He opposed moving the capital to Xia, arguing it would destabilize the people, and was proven correct when Ba Shu attacked. Under Emperor Gaozu, he became Minister of the Five Arms and was enfeoffed as Prefecture Lingbo. He died at home at age forty-one, posthumously honored as Zhongshu Ling with the title Mu.

Xi Chanwen, from Anding, lost his parents young and loved reading. He befriended Xiao Yingzhou and served as a doctor in Xizhong. He persuaded Yingzhou to support Gaozu’s rebellion, secretly sending a silver sword and receiving a gold Ruyi in return. Under Emperor He, he became minister of Huangmen and later Weiqing. After Yingzhou’s sudden death, he stabilized the region by welcoming Xiao Qi to govern Jingzhou. Under Emperor Gaozu, he served as official minister and was enfeoffed as Shanyang Bo. He died in office after two years as prefect of Shuyang, known for his integrity.

Wei Rui, courtesy name Huaiwen, was born in Duling. He was filial to his stepmother and praised for his integrity. He served under various officials, avoiding disaster by requesting a post in Yicheng County during Yuan Yun’s rebellion. During the Qi Dynasty turmoil, he predicted the failure of rebels Chen Haoda and Cui Huijing, and allied with Gaozu, bringing 2,000 men and 200 horses. Gaozu appointed him champion general and prefect of Jiangxia. During a plague in Ying City, he personally comforted survivors and ensured proper burials. After the Liang Dynasty’s establishment, he became Dali and later Tingwei. He repelled Northern Wei invasions and led successful campaigns, including the capture of Xiaoxian City and Hefei, using strategic weirs and naval attacks. At Shaoyang, he defeated a massive Wei army, causing over 100,000 casualties. He was promoted to Marquis and served in various high offices. Known for humility, he treated old friends kindly and avoided boasting. He died at seventy-nine, posthumously honored as Shizhong and Chariot and Cavalry General, with the title Yan. His sons included Wei Fang, Wei Zheng, Wei Ling, and Wei An.

Wei Ai, courtesy name Xiaoyou, was calm and thoughtful. He lost his father young, served his mother filially, and was dedicated to learning, often studying alone in a dusty room. He was erudite in Zhouyi and Chunqiu Zuoshi. During Gaozu’s rebellion, he pacified local rebels and later followed Xiao Qi to Jingzhou. He died of illness en route to attack Liu Jilian, leaving a legacy of integrity.
