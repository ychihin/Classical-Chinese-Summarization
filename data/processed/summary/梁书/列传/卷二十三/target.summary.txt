The text details the lives and careers of several members of the Xiao family, descendants of Gaozu, focusing on their official positions, military campaigns, and personal virtues.

Xiao Yi, son of Gaozu's eldest brother, held various military and civil posts, including governor of Liangzhou. He successfully repelled a Wei invasion, capturing enemy garrisons and securing the border. Promoted multiple times, he was later killed on the orders of Marquis Shuhun due to court intrigue. He was posthumously honored with titles and a grand funeral.

His son, Xiao Ye, was intelligent and served in the Qi and Liang dynasties, holding positions such as governor and general. Known for his honesty and devotion to Buddhism, he died at forty-eight. His works were widely circulated.

Xiao Zao, younger brother of King Yuan, was a capable governor who personally suppressed a rebellion in Yizhou, earning him respect. He held numerous high-ranking posts, including governor of Yongzhou and Yanzhou, and was praised for his humility and literary talent. He refused to flee during the Hou Jing rebellion, choosing to starve himself to death at sixty-seven.

Xiao Boyou, son of Xiao Fu, was known for his demeanor and was appointed to oversee remote border regions, dying young at twenty-three.

Xiao Yuanjian, son of Xiao Chang, served as governor of Guangzhou and Yingzhou, dying in office.

Xiao Xiang, son of King Xuanwu, was noted for his filial piety and effective governance. As governor of Xiangzhou, his virtue reportedly stopped tiger attacks. He held various high offices before dying in the second year of Datong.

The text concludes that the successor kings of Changsha were prosperous, with Xiao Xiang of Guiyang particularly praised for his filial piety and benevolent rule, which surpassed previous governance.
