Pei Ziye, courtesy name Jiyuan, was from Wenxi, Hedong, and a descendant of Jin Dynasty royalty. He was raised by his grandmother after his mother died at his birth, and showed deep filial piety when she passed when he was nine. Known for his studiousness and literary skill, he began his official career as Zuo Changshi of the Wuling Kingdom of Qi. His mourning period for his father was so exemplary that withered grass and a white rabbit appeared at his father's tomb. Fan Yun praised his conduct but died before reporting it to the emperor. Ren Fang, a famous scholar, resented Ziye for not visiting him, despite being a cousin. Ziye later served as a military advisor but was dismissed when a document was signed in his absence without his knowledge. He refused to litigate to clear his name, accepting the dismissal without resentment.

Fan Zhen later recommended Ziye, praising his moral integrity, filial piety, and scholarly achievements, including his twenty-volume "Song Lue." Despite some officials objecting to his qualifications, Ziye was appointed as Minister Bibu Lang and later as a county magistrate, where he resolved disputes through reasoning, eliminating lawsuits. He completed his great-grandfather's unfinished history, earning praise from Shen Yue and others. He was then appointed as a historian and editor, known for his broad knowledge, such as identifying the origins of envoys from the Baiti and Hua kingdoms. He compiled the "Picture of Envoys from Fang Kingdom" and was admired by contemporaries like Liu Xian and Liu Zhilin. In the seventh year of the ordinary year, he drafted an edict against Wei quickly, impressing Emperor Gaozu with his heroic writing. Ziye's style imitated the ancients, and he wrote quickly, saying he wrote with his heart. He served in the palace for over a decade, living frugally, giving his salary to poor relatives, and supporting his family on borrowed land. He was a devout Buddhist vegetarian in his later years. He died at sixty-two, and the emperor mourned him, granting posthumous honors and a burial. His works include "Collection of Mourning Clothes," "Continued Biography of the Pei Family," and others.

Gu Xie, courtesy name Zhengli, from Wu County, lost his father young and was raised by his grandfather. As a child, he expressed a desire for seclusion, impressing his maternal grandfather. He was studious and praised for his concentration. He began his career as a historian in Yangzhou and later as a Taixue doctor. Minister Shen Yue praised his policy essay as unmatched since the Eastern Jin. He served in various roles, including as a magistrate and military advisor. At sixty, he was recommended for his conduct and knowledge, though he remained indifferent to fame. He was appointed as a minister and died at seventy-three. The emperor mourned him, noting his honesty and lack of close relatives. Gu Xie was known for his integrity, refusing gifts and living frugally. He married his uncle's daughter in his sixties, having no children. He was well-read and compiled works like "Yi Xing Yuan" and "Suo Yu."

Xu Zhai, courtesy name Shixiu, from Donghai, was studious and known for his innovative writing. He served as a Taixue doctor and was recommended by Zhou She to accompany Prince Jin'an, despite his plain appearance. He held various posts, including as a magistrate and military advisor. His writing style, known as "gong style," was imitated in the palace, initially angering Emperor Gaozu but later earning his favor. Zhu Yi, feeling threatened, had Xu Zhai appointed as prefect of Xin'an, where he governed fairly and improved customs. He later served as a general and advisor on marriage rituals, advising the emperor on proper etiquette. During Hou Jing's rebellion, Xu Zhai stood firm, impressing Hou Jing with his fierceness. He died at seventy-eight after refusing a general title. His eldest son, Xu Ling, was famous.

Bao Quan, courtesy name Runyue, from Donghai, was well-read and skilled in writing. He served Emperor Yuan and was appointed governor of Xinzhou. He defeated Xiao Yu but was later accused of delaying the king's army and imprisoned by Wang Sengbian. He was forgiven and later served as chief historian in Yingzhou. The city fell due to neglect by Fang Zhu and Bao Quan, who were captured by Hou Jing. Bao Quan was killed in Jiangxia. He had prophetic dreams about his fate. He was an expert on "Ritual" and compiled forty volumes of "Xinyi."

Yao Cha noted that Ruan Xiaoxu observed that virtue and literature rarely coexist, but Pei Ziye exemplified both, combining the qualities of Ziyou, Zixia, Yan Hui, and Ziqian.
