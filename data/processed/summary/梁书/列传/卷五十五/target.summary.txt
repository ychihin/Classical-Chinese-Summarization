Xiao Zong, Prince of Yuzhang, was Emperor Gaozu's second son. He held various military and civil posts, including governor of South Xuzhou and Yingzhou. His mother, Wu Shuyuan, was a former concubine of the Marquis of Shuhun of Qi, and Xiao Zong was born seven months after Gaozu married her, leading to suspicions he was not Gaozu's biological son. Xiao Zong grew up talented but resentful, believing Gaozu did not understand him. He performed a blood-dripping test on the bones of the Marquis of Shuhun and his own son, confirming his paternity, which fueled his rebellious intentions. He eventually surrendered to Wei, changed his name to Xiao Zuan in memory of the deposed Emperor Shuhun, and was killed by Wei at age 49. His poems, "Listening to the Ringing of the Bell" and "Sad Poems on Falling Leaves," expressed his sorrow and frustration.

Xiao Ji, King of Wuling, was Gaozu's eighth son. He was diligent and talented in literature. He held several official positions, including governor of Jiangzhou and Yangzhou. During the Taiqing period, Hou Jing rebelled, and Xiao Ji did not send troops to reinforce the capital. After Gaozu's death, he proclaimed himself emperor in Shu with the reign name Tianzheng. He killed advisors who warned him, and his rule was marked by omens, such as flowers growing from palace pillars. He led an army downriver to attack Hou Jing but was defeated by Western Wei forces. He was captured and killed at age 46. His reign name Tianzheng was considered unlucky, and he was posthumously given the surname Taotie.

Xiao Zhengde, Prince of Linhe, was the third son of Prince Jinghui of Linchuan. He was adopted by Gaozu but became dissatisfied when he was not made crown prince. He fled to Wei but later returned. He conspired with Hou Jing, who supported him as emperor with the reign name Zhengping. After Hou Jing captured Taicheng, he demoted Xiao Zhengde and later killed him out of fear of rebellion.

Xiao Yu, Prince of Hedong, was the second son of Prince Zhaoming. He held various posts, including governor of Xiangzhou. When Hou Jing invaded the capital, Xiao Yu led reinforcements but arrived after Taicheng fell. He returned to Xiangzhou to defend it. Shizu, suspicious of Xiao Yu's intentions, sent forces to attack him. Xiao Yu was besieged in Changsha for a long time but eventually captured and beheaded. Before his fall, he experienced ominous visions, including his head disappearing in a mirror and a large white dog fleeing the city.

The historical commentary concludes that Xiao Zong and Xiao Zhengde's rebellions led to their own destruction, while Xiao Ji's failure to aid the capital during Hou Jing's rebellion and his subsequent uprising brought disaster upon himself.
