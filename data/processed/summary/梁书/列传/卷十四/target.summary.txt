Jiang Yan, courtesy name Wentong, was a native of Kaocheng, Jiyang. He was lonely and impoverished in his youth but diligent, studious, and reserved. He began his official career as an appointee in Southern Xuzhou and later served at the imperial court. He followed Prince Jingsu of Song Jianping to southern Yanzhou. When Guangling Magistrate Guo Yanwen was convicted, Jiang Yan was imprisoned. From prison, he wrote a letter to Jingsu, pleading his innocence and loyalty, citing historical examples of unjust suffering. He described his humble origins and sincere service, lamenting that slander had led to his imprisonment. He begged for mercy, and Jingsu, moved by the letter, released him that day.

Jiang Yan was soon promoted and served in various posts, including as a scholar in Southern Xuzhou and Zuo Changshi of Baling Kingdom. When Jingsu became governor of Jingzhou, Jiang Yan followed him. As Jingsu grew arrogant and plotted rebellion, Jiang Yan repeatedly advised him against it, warning of historical disasters. Jingsu ignored him. When Jingsu was stationed in Beijing, Jiang Yan served in the army and governed Nandonghai. He wrote fifteen poems to satirize Jingsu’s schemes. After a dispute over county governance, Jingsu had Jiang Yan demoted to Jian'an Wuxingling, where he served for three years.

When Qi Yi came to power, he recruited Jiang Yan as Shangshu Jiabulang. During Shen Youzhi’s rebellion, Emperor Gao consulted Jiang Yan, who analyzed the strengths of the emperor and the weaknesses of the rebels, predicting victory. Emperor Gao was pleased and entrusted Jiang Yan with drafting military documents. Jiang Yan held numerous high offices under Qi, including Hussar Yuzhang Wang Ji's Office, Vice Minister of Zhongshu, and General Xiaoqi. He served as governor of Luling and later returned to the capital as General of Cavalry and Minister Zuocheng. As Censor Zhongcheng, he impeached several officials for negligence and corruption, earning praise from Emperor Ming for his strictness.

Under Emperor Ming, Jiang Yan held positions such as Chief Historian of the Chariot and Linhai King, Shizhong, and Prefect of Xuancheng. During the Yongyuan period, when Cui Huijing besieged the capital, Jiang Yan feigned illness and avoided the rebels, earning admiration for his foresight. He later served as Secretary Supervisor and Guard, though he felt unqualified. He was enfeoffed as Founding Uncle of Linju County. In his later years, he expressed contentment and a desire to retire. He died at age sixty-two, and the emperor mourned him with gifts. Jiang Yan was known for his literary talent in youth but declined in later years. He authored over a hundred works and ten records of "History of Qi."

Ren Fang, courtesy name Yansheng, was a native of Bochang, Le'an. His father was Yao, a doctor of Zhongsan in Qi. Ren Fang was studious and became famous early. At sixteen, he served as chief bookkeeper for Song Danyang Yin Liu Bing. He later held various posts, including Dr. Taichang and chief secretary for Wang Jian. Ren Fang was known for his filial piety, mourning his parents with extreme devotion. He served as a defense officer and secretary of Guan Dong Palace. During Qi Mingdi’s reign, he drafted a memorial declining high honors, citing his inadequacy and the state’s troubles. His refusal angered the emperor, stalling his career.

Ren Fang excelled in writing essays, biographies, and memorials, often composing without revision. He was admired by contemporaries like Xuan. After the dynasty’s fall, he served under Liang, holding posts such as Huangmen Shilang and Minister of Personnel. As governor of Yixing, he lived frugally, eating only wheat. He later served as Censor Zhongcheng and Secretary Supervisor, personally proofreading library titles. As governor of Xin'an, he governed simply, deciding lawsuits on the road. He died in office at age forty-nine, and the people built a shrine for him. The emperor mourned deeply.

Ren Fang was generous with friends, recommending scholars and hosting many guests. He was called "Renjun" for his patronage. Despite poverty, he accumulated over ten thousand books. After his death, the emperor took rare books from his collection. Ren Fang’s writings totaled hundreds of thousands of words. However, after his death, his children were neglected. Liu Xiaobiao wrote an essay criticizing fair-weather friends, describing five types of snobbish acquaintances: those drawn to power, wealth, reputation, shared misfortune, and calculated gain. He lamented that Ren Fang’s former friends abandoned his family.

Yao Cha commented that Jiang Yan and Ren Fang were talented writers of their time. Jiang Yan’s calm and cautious nature allowed him to maintain his reputation, though he relied on prior connections. Ren Fang’s works included 247 volumes of "Miscellaneous Biography," 252 volumes of "Diji," and 33 volumes of articles. His son Seri resembled him and served as Shangshu Waibinglang.
