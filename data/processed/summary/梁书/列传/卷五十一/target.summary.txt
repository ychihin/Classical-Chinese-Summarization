**Summary of Historical Records on Hermits and Recluses**

The text begins by categorizing hermits into three tiers. The highest are those who value morality above life itself, despising wealth and power, and willing to face death without regret—rare figures who occasionally appear. The second tier includes "great hermits" who hide within cities or courts, living simply to fulfill their ambitions without shame despite low status. The third tier consists of those who feign madness, blind themselves, or abandon rituals to protect themselves and escape worldly entanglements. All these hermits, however, master the art of silence and seclusion, ultimately achieving good outcomes, unlike those who chase wealth and cater to the world. The text quotes Mencius and Huainanzi to emphasize that hermits alone can inspire righteousness and curb corruption.

Historically, no emperor has failed to respect hermits. Even Tang Yao could not compel Chaofu and Xu You to obey, nor could King Wu of Zhou force Boyi and Shuqi to serve. Emperors like Gaozu of Han and Guangwu of Han showed deference to hermits such as Qili Ji and Yan Guang. During the Liang Dynasty, hermits with exemplary morals were recorded in the "Chushi Pian."

The text then details the lives of several notable hermits. He Dian, from a wealthy family, refused marriage and office after his father’s execution, living simply among the people. He rejected multiple imperial summons, fled from nobles who sought him, and gave away stolen clothes to a thief. He was known for his discerning judgment of talent and his unconventional marriage, where he did not meet his wife. Emperor Gaozu, an old friend, repeatedly invited him to court, offering gifts and honors, but He Dian remained reclusive until his death at sixty-eight.

He Yin, He Dian’s younger brother, was a scholar who studied Confucian and Buddhist texts. He served in minor official posts but later resigned to live in seclusion, building a house in the mountains. He refused high-ranking positions, even when Emperor Gaozu personally wrote to him, citing his age and health. He taught students, forbade killing, and was known for miraculous events, such as a deer seeking refuge with him and a strange bird appearing in his lecture hall. He died at eighty-six, leaving extensive annotations on the "Book of Changes" and "Book of Rites."

Ruan Xiaoxu, from a wealthy family, refused inheritance and lived in poverty, avoiding even his prominent cousin Wang Yan. He was known for his filial piety, once finding ginseng for his mother by following a deer. He refused official summons, stating that seclusion depends on personal choice, not the state of the world. He wrote a "Biography of Gao Yin" and a treatise on the balance between action and inaction, arguing that saints act to remedy ills while wise men promote the foundation of Tao. He died at fifty-eight.

Tao Hongjing, a polymath, resigned from office at thirty-six to live in Maoshan, where he studied alchemy and the Talisman Sutra. He built a three-story house, cutting off contact with the world, and was known for his love of pine wind and his expertise in yin-yang, astrology, and medicine. He predicted the rise of the Liang dynasty through prophecies and maintained a close friendship with Emperor Gaozu, who frequently sent envoys. He died at eighty-one, leaving instructions for a modest burial.

Other hermits include Zhuge Gu, a teacher who refused office and lived in poverty; Shen Yi, who remained content despite extreme hardship; Liu Huifei, who copied Buddhist scriptures in Kuangshan; Fan Yuanyan, who hid his kindness to thieves; Liu Xu, who fled his own wedding; and Liu Xian, who wrote a treatise on death and burial, advocating simplicity. Yu Shen, a scholar of vast knowledge, lived frugally, forgave a thief, and refused official posts, dying at seventy-eight. Zhang Xiaoxiu and Yu Chengxian also lived in seclusion, the latter known for his lectures on Laozi.

The text concludes with a reflection by Yao Chaqiu, who defends hermits against accusations of stealing empty names, noting that figures like Zhuge Can and Ruan Xiaoxu could have easily sought office but chose seclusion by nature.
