The text discusses the historical tradition of compiling biographies of literary figures, beginning with Sima Qian and Ban Gu, who included Sima Xiangru's biography due to his literary fame, and Fan Ye's "Book of Houmo," which detailed the deeds of literary scholars. It emphasizes the importance of writing in governance, ritual, and recording history, noting that rulers and scholars have always valued literary works. Emperor Gaozu of Liang promoted literature by rewarding poets and scholars, leading to a flourishing of talent in the capital. Notable literary figures like Shen Yue, Jiang Yan, and Ren Fang were celebrated for their skills, while others like Pengcheng Daohang and Wu Xingqiuchi gained favor at court.

The text then provides biographies of several scholars. Daohang, a native of Pengcheng, was intelligent from childhood, excelling in writing and calligraphy. He served in various official roles under Emperor Gaozu, who admired his talent. Daohang was known for his humility and friendships with contemporaries like Ren Fang and Fan Yun. He died at thirty, leaving over a hundred poems. Qiu Chi, from Wuxing, began writing at eight and was recognized for his talent. He held several official positions, including prefect of Yongjia, and was known for his literary contributions, such as writing letters that persuaded Chen Bozhi to surrender. He died at forty-five, with his works widely circulated.

Liu Bao, from Pengcheng, lost his father early and showed filial piety by managing family burials alone at sixteen. He was learned and held various posts, including lecturer in Shouguang Palace. He was part of a group of young scholars favored by Emperor Gaozu for their literary talents. Liu Bao died at thirty, known for his upright character and frugal funeral requests. Yuan Jun, from Chen County, was orphaned young and studied diligently, copying books daily. He served in official roles and wrote "Guan Zhen," earning praise and silk from Emperor Gaozu. He also copied historical texts and composed "Xinque Ming" with Lu Jue.

Yu Yuling, from a scholarly family, was sharp-witted and served as a bookkeeper for Xiao Zilong. After Xiao Zilong's death, Yu Yuling alone handled his funeral, showing loyalty. He held various posts, including prefect of Guangling, and died at forty-eight, leaving ten volumes of works. His brother Yu Jianwu began writing at eight and served under the Prince of Jin'an, later becoming a scholar in Wende Province. He was part of a literary circle that included his son Yu Xin and Xu Ling. The text includes a letter from the prince criticizing contemporary literary trends for being weak and imitative, advocating a return to classical styles like those of Yang Xiong and Sima Xiangru.

Liu Zhao, from Pingyuan, was intelligent and studied "Laozi" and "Zhuangzi" early. He annotated Fan Ye's "Book of Houmo" and wrote 180 volumes of commentary, along with other works. He died in office. His sons Liu Sui and Liu Su were also learned, with Liu Su serving as a secretary. He Xun, from Donghai, began writing at eight and was praised by Fan Yun and Shen Yue for his poetry. He served under King Jian'an and died in Jiangzhou, with his poems collected by Wang Sengru. He Xun was compared to Shen Yue and Xie Tiao, and his works were valued alongside Liu Xiaochuo's.

Zhong Rong, from Yingchuan, was learned in the "Book of Changes" and served in various roles. He wrote a letter criticizing the misuse of official titles during the Yongyuan period, advocating for merit-based appointments. He later wrote "Poetry Criticism," a review of five-character poetry from ancient times to his era, discussing its evolution and key figures like Cao Zhi, Lu Ji, and Xie Lingyun. He died in office. His brothers Zhong Yan and Zhong Yu also held official posts and wrote works. Zhou Xingsi, from Chen County, was a prolific writer who served Emperor Gaozu, composing inscriptions and poems. He was highly valued by Gaozu and Ren Fang, and died at an unknown age, leaving over 100 volumes of works.

Wu Jun, from Wuxing, was a talented writer whose style was imitated as "Wu Jun style." He served under King Jian'an and wrote "Qi Chunqiu," which was criticized for inaccuracies and burned. He later worked on a "General History" but died before completing it, leaving numerous works. Other writers like Gao Shuang, Jiang Hong, and Yu Qian were also noted for their literary skills, with Gao Shuang's "Ode on Wok Fish" being particularly praised.
