The text summarizes the lives and military careers of several figures from the Liang Dynasty. Hu Sengyou, a brave champion from Nanyang, served Wei and later returned to Liang, where he was appointed to guard Xiangcheng. After its capture, he returned to Wei but eventually came back to Liang, serving as a prefect with good governance. Despite his poor poetry, he remained confident. He was imprisoned for remonstrating against killing barbarian chiefs but was later released to aid Wang Sengbian against Hou Jing. He defeated Ren Yue, captured him, and caused Hou Jing to flee. He died at 63 from a stray arrow while defending against Wei, leading to the city's fall.

Xu Wensheng, from Pengcheng, led his father's troops to surrender to Liang and became a governor of Ningzhou, where he reformed corrupt barbarian customs. During Hou Jing's rebellion, he was sent eastward but hesitated to fight, influenced by his wife's return from Hou Jing. His army was defeated after Yingzhou's capture, and he fled to Jingzhou. He was imprisoned for corruption and resentment, dying in prison after a taunt from Ren Yue.

Du Chan, from Duling, was the seventh son of Du Huaibao. He joined Shizu and followed Wang Sengbian against Hou Jing, defeating him at Baling and Shitou. He later fought Qi general Guo Yuanjian and suppressed rebellions in Changsha and against King Wuling. He died of illness. His brothers Du An and Du Youan also served, with Du An killed attacking Xiangyang and Du Youan surrendering to and killed by Hou Jing. Du Chong, Du Chan's nephew, was a brave general who defeated Hou Jing's forces, captured Song Zixian, and later resisted Chen Baxian after Wang Sengbian's death, ultimately surrendering and being killed.

Yin Zichun, from Guzang, served as a general and prefect, suppressing barbarian rebellions. He fought with Wang Sengbian and Xu Wensheng against Hou Jing, achieving many victories, but died in Jiangling after the army retreated. The text concludes with a commentary praising Hu Sengyou's loyalty, criticizing Xu Wensheng's failure, and lauding Du Chan's moral choices and achievements.
