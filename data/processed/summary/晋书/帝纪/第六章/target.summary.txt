Emperor Yuan, named Sima Rui, was born in Luoyang in 276 CE with auspicious signs. He succeeded as King of Langya at fifteen and maintained a cautious, thrifty demeanor during the political turmoil of Emperor Hui's reign, avoiding disaster. Initially unrecognized for his talents, only servant Ji Shao perceived his extraordinary nature. He held various military posts, including General of the Left and General of the Auxiliary State, and participated in campaigns against Sima Ying. After a failed battle, he narrowly escaped capture during a thunderstorm. Following Sima Yue's westward march, Emperor Yuan adopted Wang Dao's strategy and established his base in Jianye, winning the loyalty of Jiangdong's elite through respectful governance.

After Emperor Huai was captured, Emperor Yuan was recommended as alliance leader and later appointed Prime Minister Zuo. He pacified Jiangdong, defeating rebels like Sun Bi and Du Zeng. When Chang'an fell, he mobilized troops and received auspicious signs, including a jade seal reading "Longevity for Ten Thousand Years." In 318 CE, Emperor Min's edict authorized him to govern, and after mourning, he accepted the title of King of Jin. He ascended the throne on the ninth day of the third month, implementing a general amnesty and establishing a bureaucracy in Jiankang. He rejected auspicious omens, stating his focus on self-blame for state failures.

Emperor Yuan's reign faced challenges from Shi Le's invasions, repelled by General Zu Ti. He issued proclamations offering rewards for Shi Jilong's head. He mourned ministers Zhu Song and Gu Qiu despite protocol, emphasizing the need for special rites during national decline. In 319 CE, after Emperor Min's death, he assumed the imperial title, issuing edicts on governance, frugality, and moral conduct. He banned extravagant gifts and promoted merit-based evaluations. Natural disasters like droughts and earthquakes prompted personal prayers and relief efforts. He dealt with rebellions, including those by Zhou Fu and Xu Gong, and faced military defeats against Shi Le's forces.

In 322 CE, Wang Dun rebelled, capturing Stone City and defeating imperial forces. Emperor Yuan offered to abdicate, but Wang Dun assumed dictatorial powers, killing officials like Dai Ruosi and Zhou Qiye. Emperor Yuan died in the inner hall at age forty-seven, buried in Jianping Mausoleum with the temple name Zhongzong. He was known for simplicity and frugality, once pouring away wine after Wang Dao's admonition and using plain curtains instead of expensive ones. He refused costly gifts for his concubine and discouraged excessive construction. However, his reign was plagued by weak military control and internal strife, leading to his death from sorrow and anger.

Historical accounts note prophecies of imperial destiny in Jinling, fulfilled when Emperor Yuan crossed the Yangtze River 526 years after Qin Shihuang. A nursery rhyme about five horses crossing the river symbolized the southern migration of kings, with Emperor Yuan becoming the dragon. Rumors suggested his birth resulted from an affair, fulfilling the "ox succeeds the horse" prophecy. Despite initial successes, the state's reliance on powerful generals led to internal conflicts, and Emperor Yuan's efforts to restore order ultimately failed, with later historians attributing the dynasty's decline to fate and flawed succession.
