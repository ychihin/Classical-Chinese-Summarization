Emperor Xuan, named Sima Yi with the courtesy name Zhongda, was from Wen County, Henan. His ancestors traced back to Chongli, a descendant of Emperor Gaoyang, who served as Xia Guan Zhurong. During the Zhou Dynasty, a descendant named Cheng Baixiu's father was granted the surname Sima for pacifying Xu Fang. In the Chu-Han period, Sima Yan was a general. By the eighth generation, Sima Gou emerged, and Emperor Xuan was the second son of Si Yaduo.

From a young age, Emperor Xuan displayed extraordinary integrity, intelligence, and lofty ambitions, being erudite in Confucianism. During the late Han Dynasty's chaos, he lamented the state of the world. Nanyang Prefect Dangyu recognized his talent early on, and Cui Yanyu praised his wisdom and decisiveness. In the sixth year of the founding, he was recommended for office but initially declined due to illness, fearing the declining fortunes of the Han. When Emperor Wu of Wei (Cao Cao) summoned him as a literary advisor, he was threatened with imprisonment and reluctantly took the post. He served as Huangmen Shilang, Yilang, and Prime Minister Shu Cao's family, eventually becoming chief secretary.

Emperor Xuan accompanied Cao Cao on campaigns, advising against missing opportunities to attack Shu after capturing Hanzhong, but Cao Cao refused. He later followed Cao Cao to defeat Sun Quan. After Sun Quan's surrender, Emperor Xuan argued that the Han dynasty's fate was ending and Cao Cao should accept heaven's will. In the Wei Kingdom, he became crown prince and concubine, trusted by the prince, and was known as one of the four friends with Chen Qun, Wu Zhi, and Zhu Shuo. As Army Sima, he advocated for agricultural cultivation to support the military, which Cao Cao adopted, enriching the state. He also criticized corrupt officials Hu Xiu and Fu Fang, but Cao Cao ignored him; they later surrendered to Guan Yu, worsening Cao Ren's siege. Emperor Xuan advised against moving the capital, suggesting instead to ally with Sun Quan against Guan Yu, which led to Guan Yu's death. He also opposed relocating people from Jingzhou, allowing exiles to return home.

After Cao Cao's death, Emperor Xuan managed the funeral and escorted the coffin to Ye. Under Emperor Wen of Wei (Cao Pi), he was made Marquis of Hejin Ting and Prime Minister Chang Shi. When Sun Quan attacked, he advised against recalling Cao Ren from Xiangyang, but was ignored; Cao Ren burned the city, and Sun Quan did not invade, causing regret. After the Han abdication, Emperor Xuan served as Shangshu, then military governor and censor Zhongcheng, later becoming Shizhong and Shangshu Youpushe. He guarded Xuchang and was granted titles, though he refused honors. During campaigns against Wu, he stayed behind to manage affairs. On his deathbed, Emperor Wen entrusted him, Cao Zhen, and Chen Qun with assisting the government.

Under Emperor Ming, Emperor Xuan became Wuyang Marquis. He repelled Sun Quan's forces, killing Zhang Ba. He was promoted to General of Hussars and garrisoned in Wan. When Shu general Meng Da rebelled, Emperor Xuan suspected his cunning and, despite warnings, marched swiftly, capturing and executing Meng Da in sixteen days. He then dealt with Shen Yi, who had forged edicts, and exiled Meng Da's followers. He advised leniency for surrendered border counties and proposed attacking Wu via Xiakou and Shuguan. He was promoted to general and joined Cao Zhen against Shu, but heavy rain forced a retreat.

When Zhuge Liang invaded, Emperor Xuan was sent west to command forces. He marched to Yumi, forcing Zhuge Liang to flee. He defeated Shu forces at Kongshan, capturing thousands. He predicted Zhuge Liang would not attack for three years due to food shortages, so he moved farmers and dug canals to enrich the state. In the second year, Zhuge Liang led 100,000 troops to Wuzhangyuan. Emperor Xuan avoided battle, despite Zhuge Liang sending women's clothes to provoke him. He petitioned for a decisive battle but was stopped by Xinpi. Zhuge Liang died, and Emperor Xuan pursued, confirming his death. He later learned Zhuge Liang ate little and handled all affairs, predicting his early death. He defeated Shu general Ma Dai and accepted surrenders from Di kings.

When Gongsun Wenyi rebelled in Liaodong, Emperor Xuan predicted he would hold the Liao River and estimated a year for the campaign. He advised halting palace construction to relieve the people. In the second year of Jingchu, he led 40,000 troops, crossing the Liao River and feinting south while crossing north. He besieged Xiangping, ignoring calls to attack the enemy camp, and defeated Wenyi after three battles. Despite heavy rain, he forbade moving camp, beheading a violator. He rejected surrenders, executed Wenyi, and killed 7,000 men over fifteen, plus 2,000 officials. He released Gongsun Gong and honored loyalists. He refused to give short coats to soldiers, citing public property. He dismissed elderly soldiers and mourned the dead.

Returning, he was granted more fiefs. When Emperor Ming was dying, he entrusted Emperor Xuan with the young King Qi, alongside Cao Shuang. Emperor Xuan became Taifu with high honors. He refused titles for his sons. Under King Qi, he repelled Wu attacks, digging canals for farming. He advised against Cao Shuang's Shu campaign, which failed. When Cao Shuang moved the Empress Dowager and controlled the government, Emperor Xuan feigned illness. He tricked Li Sheng into thinking he was dying, so Cao Shuang relaxed. In the first year of Jiaping, he deposed Cao Shuang during an imperial visit, executing him and his party. He refused the prime minister position and Jiuxi honors.

Later, he suppressed rebellions by Ling Huyu and Wang Leng, executing them and Cao Biao. He confined Wei princes. He refused further honors. He died at seventy-three, buried simply as per his wishes. Posthumously honored as Emperor Xuan of Jin, his temple name was Gaozu. He was described as jealous but generous, suspicious and manipulative. Cao Cao warned about his ambition, but Cao Pi protected him. He massacred after victories and usurped Wei power. His cruelty was noted, and Emperor Ming of Jin lamented the dynasty's foundation. His strategies were praised but his loyalty questioned, as he betrayed trust and killed rivals, leading to later ridicule.
