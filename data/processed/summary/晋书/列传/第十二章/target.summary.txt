Wang Hun, courtesy name Xuanchong, was from Jinyang, Taiyuan, and inherited his father Wang Chang's title of Marquis of Jingling. He served under Cao Shuang but was dismissed after Shuang's execution. Later, he held various posts, including Governor of Xuzhou, where he opened granaries to relieve famine. During the Taishi era, he was transferred to guard Zhuchang and frequently submitted governance strategies that were often adopted. As General of Conquering Captives in Yuzhou, he attracted many surrenders. He defeated Wu generals Xue Ying and Lu Shu, earning his son Wang Shang the title of Marquis of Guannei. As General Andong in Yangzhou, he repelled Wu forces, burning their grain and ships. During the major campaign against Wu, Wang Hun led troops from Hengjiang, capturing Wu generals and receiving surrenders from Chen Dai and Zhu Ming. He defeated Wu Prime Minister Zhang Ti, killing 7,800, leading to Sun Hao's surrender via He Zhi and Sun Yan. However, Wang Jun later took Sun Hao's surrender, causing Wang Hun shame and jealousy. He reported Wang Jun's crimes, drawing public ridicule. The emperor praised Wang Hun's role in pacifying Moling, adding fiefs and promoting him to Duke. He was later transferred to General Zhengshu, where he comforted surrendered Wu people. As Shangshu Zuopushe, he advised against sending Prince Sima You to a vassal state, citing historical precedents and urging retention for state security, but the emperor did not accept. In the Taixi era, he became Situ. Under Emperor Hui, he was given military command but insisted on wearing black robes to avoid impropriety, earning praise. When Prince Sima Zhang of Chu sought to kill Sima Liang, Wang Hun feigned illness and resisted with soldiers, and Sima Zhang was later killed. The emperor consulted Wang Hun on customs inquiries; Wang Hun proposed detailed questioning of local planners, which was approved. He died at seventy-five, posthumously named Yuan.

Wang Ji, courtesy name Wuzi, was Wang Hun's son. He was exceptionally talented, skilled in archery and the "Yi," "Zhuang," and "Lao" texts, and married Princess Changshan. He served as Zhongshu Lang and later as a servant, known for his eloquence and favored by Emperor Wu. However, he was inwardly jealous and harsh with words. He opposed sending Prince Sima You away, crying with the princess, which angered the emperor and led to demotion. As Henan Yin, he was dismissed for whipping an official. He was extravagant, paving a riding track with money and betting a prized cow with Wang Kai, cutting it up after winning. He once served human-milk-steamed food to the emperor, who left displeased. He understood horses, once recognizing a horse's reluctance to cross mud. He died at forty-six, posthumously named General of Hussars. At his funeral, Sun Chu brayed like a donkey in tribute, making guests laugh. He married a blind, jealous princess and had two sons by a concubine.

Wang Jun, courtesy name Shizhi, was from Hongnong Lake. He was well-read and handsome, initially lacking reputation but later changed his ways. He built a house with a wide gate, saying it would accommodate halberds and flags. He married Xu Miao's daughter after she saw him at a gathering. Yang Hu recognized his talent and helped him. As Governor of Bajun, he eased corvée for newborns, saving thousands. As Governor of Guanghan, he was benevolent. He dreamed of knives, interpreted as a sign he would govern Yizhou, and became its governor, pacifying rebels. He built large ships for attacking Wu, with iron cones and locks countered by rafts and torches. He captured Wu territories, and Sun Hao surrendered. Wang Jun entered Shitou, accepting Sun Hao's surrender. He was promoted but conflicted with Wang Hun, who accused him of violating orders. Wang Jun defended himself, citing edicts and circumstances. He was made General of the Auxiliary State but felt under-rewarded, often complaining. He died at eighty, posthumously named Wu. His descendants later fell into poverty, prompting Huan Wen to request restoration of their title.

Tang Bin, courtesy name Confucian, was from Zou. He was skilled in riding and archery and studied the "Book of Changes." He served as a county official and was recommended for his talent. He investigated Deng Ai's execution, reporting no border concerns. As Governor of Yiyang, he stabilized the people. He was appointed General Guangwu to supervise Badong military affairs. During the Wu campaign, he served as vanguard, capturing Xiling and Lexiang. He feigned illness near Jianye to avoid credit disputes. He later guarded the northern border, training soldiers and expanding farmland, restoring the Great Wall and securing peace. He died at sixty, posthumously named Xiang. The historian's commentary notes that Wang Hun and Wang Jun both contributed to pacifying Wu but were marred by jealousy and conflict, while Tang Bin's humility preserved his integrity.
