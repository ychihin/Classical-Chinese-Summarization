# Summary of Historical Records: Clan Anping Xian Wang and Related Nobility

Sima Fu, courtesy name Shuda, was Emperor Xuan's second brother. He was one of the "Bada" (Eight Talents) along with his brothers, known for their fame. Sima Fu was gentle, honest, upright, and widely read in classics. During the late Han Dynasty's turmoil, he maintained simple living while studying tirelessly. He was open-minded, tolerant, and held no grudges. When Yin Wu of Chenliu, a nationally known figure who had been punished, was visited by Sima Fu, they ate and lived together, earning praise.

Sima Fu served as literary director under Cao Zhi, King of Chensi, offering sincere advice that Cao Zhi eventually accepted. After Emperor Wu of Wei died, Sima Fu advised the crown prince against excessive mourning, emphasizing his responsibilities to the ancestral temple and feudal states. When ministers gathered weeping without forming a procession, Sima Fu sternly organized them, completed funeral arrangements, and supported the crown prince's ascension as Emperor Wen. During official selections, Sima Fu opposed nepotism, advocating for recruiting talented individuals nationwide.

When Sun Quan claimed vassalage to Wei but delayed sending hostages and returning Yu Jin, Sima Fu advised leniency, suggesting military preparation while observing developments. Yu Jin eventually returned, detained due to illness, but hostages never arrived. Sima Fu later served as Diannong in Hanoi, Marquis of Guannei, and Prefect of Qinghe. Emperor Ming appointed him Duzhi Shangshu, remarking he had "two Sima Yis." Sima Fu proposed preparing 20,000 infantry and cavalry as reserves against Zhuge Liang's invasions and stationed 5,000 farmers in Shangying for agricultural-military training, ensuring Guanzhong's surplus and defense readiness.

During Cao Shuang's corrupt regime, Sima Fu maintained rectitude and avoided harm. After Emperor Xuan executed Cao Shuang, Sima Fu was granted the title of Marquis of Changshe County and appointed Minister. When Wu general Zhuge Ke besieged Xincheng, Sima Fu commanded 200,000 troops, deliberately delaying before advancing, causing the Wu army to flee. Regarding funeral flags for Wei Ming's mourning, Sima Fu argued against writing "Wei" or surnames, citing classical precedents, and his view prevailed.

Sima Fu served as Sikong, then Taiwei. When Shu general Jiang Wei invaded Longyou, Sima Fu guarded Guanzhong, and Jiang Wei withdrew. After Lord Gao Gui was killed, Sima Fu alone wept over his body, requesting investigation of the mastermind. He petitioned for royal burial rites, which were granted. Sima Fu was cautious, humble during Emperor Xuan's time, and uninvolved in monarch depositions. He was granted the title of Duke of Changle. When Emperor Wu accepted abdication and King Chenliu departed, Sima Fu bid farewell tearfully, vowing lifelong loyalty to Wei. He was made King of Anping with 40,000 households, later promoted to Dazai and Commander-in-Chief. Despite honors, Sima Fu remained unassuming and often sorrowful. Before death at age ninety-three, he ordered simple burial in ordinary clothes. The emperor mourned three days and provided lavish funeral gifts, though Sima Fu's family followed his wishes and used none.

Sima Fu had nine sons: Sima Yong, Sima Wang, Sima Fu, Sima Yi, Sima Huang, Sima Quan, Sima Gui, Sima Heng, and Sima Jing. Sima Yong died before his father. Sima Wang, adopted by his uncle Sima Lang, served as Prefect of Pingyang and Luoyang Diannong, followed Emperor Xuan against Wang Bo, and was named Yong'an Tinghou. He later served as General of Hussars and Situ. Emperor Wu made him King of Yiyang with 10,000 households. He was promoted to Taiwei, commanding troops against Wu invasions. He died at sixty-seven, known for frugality and money accumulation, leaving four sons.

Sima Hong, King of Hejian, was adopted by his uncle Sima Qian. He served as Diannong Zhonglang Jiang and Yuanwu Taishou, was made King of Hejian by Emperor Wu, and died after twelve years. Sima Wei, his son, succeeded but was later transferred to Zhangwu. Sima Wei flattered Sima Lun, king of Zhao, and served as Zhongshu Ling during Sima Lun's usurpation. After Sima Lun's failure, Emperor Hui ordered his execution. Sima Zheng died before his father Sima Wang and was posthumously named King of Sui County.

Sima Qiao, King of Jingling, was initially Marquis of Lelingting. Emperor Wu made him King Shuping. He was skilled at flattery, served Yang Jun, but avoided punishment after Yang Jun's execution due to connections. He later served as Dahonglu and Minister. During Sima Lun's usurpation, Sima Qiao was appointed General of the Guard. After Sima Lun's failure, he was dismissed. He later served as General of Chariots and Cavalry, but was defeated by Tian Hui and fled to his kingdom. Emperor Huai changed his title to King of Jingling. When Sima Yue became shepherd of Yuzhou, Sima Qiao attempted to attack him but failed. He escaped and was later killed by rebels when Luoyang fell.

Sima Fu became King of Taiyuan, then King of Bohai with 5,379 households. He served as Weiwei and Dongzhonglang General, later moved to King of Taiyuan, supervising Bingzhou's military affairs. He died in the fifth year of Taikang. His son Sima Hong succeeded him. Sima Huang, King of Xia Pi, was granted title by Wei, served as Huangmen Shilang and Prefect of Sechai. Emperor Wu made him King of Wu, then King of Xia Pi. He was filial, honest, and respected by the clan. He served as Changshui Captain and Nanzhonglang General, later as General of Zhendong. During Emperor Hui's reign, he served as General of Chariots and Cavalry. He died in the sixth year of Yuankang, posthumously awarded Taifu.

Sima Quan, King of Taiyuan, was Marquis of Changleting in Wei, later Marquis of Guishou Township. He served as General Zhenwei and Secretary Supervisor. Emperor Wu made him King of Taiyuan with 5,496 households. He was given Gun Mian attire and moved to General of Shu Zhonglang. He died after ten years, posthumously awarded General. His son Sima Ji succeeded him. Sima Gui, King of Gaoyang, was talented and famous young, served as Magistrate of Henan and Huangmen Shilang. Emperor Wu made him King of Gaoyang with 5,570 households. He served as General Beizhonglang, supervising Yecheng's military affairs. He died after ten years, posthumously awarded General of Chariots and Cavalry. Since he had no son, Sima Zhouwei, son of King Sima Fu of Taiyuan, inherited the title.

Sima Heng, King of Changshan, was Marquis of Deyang Township in Wei, later Ruyangzi and Prince-in-law. Emperor Wu made him King of Changshan with 3,790 households. He died without sons, succeeded by Sima Yin, fourth son of Sima Yong. Sima Jing, King of Pei, was Le'anting Marquis of Wei, served as Admonishment Official. Emperor Wu made him King of Pei with 3,400 households. He died after eleven years, succeeded by his son Sima Tao.

Sima Quan, King Mu of Pengcheng, was son of Sima Kui, younger brother of Emperor Xuan. He inherited the title and served as a servant. Emperor Wu made him King of Pengcheng with 2,900 households. He served as Beizhonglang General, died in the first year of Xianning, succeeded by his son Sima Zhi. Sima Zhi served as General, Minister of Wine, Imperial Servant, Minister, and General Anshu. He died of depression, posthumously awarded General of Chariots and Cavalry with 15,000 households. His son Sima Shi succeeded him, serving as General Nanzhonglang and General Pingnan. After his death, his son Sima Xiong succeeded but was killed for defecting to Su Jun. Sima Xian, son of Sima Shi, then succeeded.

Sima Xian, courtesy name Weide, was initially Duke of Tangyi County. Emperor Yuan succeeded him to Sima Ju, King of Gaomi. He served as Minister of Sanqi and Lieutenant of the Yi Army. After Sima Xiong's execution, Sima Xian returned to inherit his clan. He served as Prince of the Country, Wine Officer, and Sanqi Changshi. He suffered from madness, sometimes attacking military temples and officials. He was impeached but Emperor Cheng issued an edict allowing him to keep his position. He died in the eighth year of Xiankang, posthumously awarded Dr. Sanqi Changshi. He had two sons: Sima Xuan and Sima Jun. Sima Xuan succeeded him but hid five households, violating the Gengxu system. Huan Wen reported this, and Sima Xuan was arrested but later pardoned. He served as Minister of Zhongshu. After his death, his son Sima Hongzhi succeeded, then Sima Shao, then Sima Chongzhi, then Sima Ji. After Song accepted abdication, the feudal state was cancelled.

Sima Jun, Prince Gong, was adopted by King Sima Lue of Gaomi, served as Sanqi Changshi. After his death, his son Sima Chunzhi succeeded, serving as Neishi of Linchuan and Minister of Shaofu. After his death, his son Sima Hui succeeded. In the last years of Yixi, he concurrently served as Taiwei. After Song accepted abdication, the feudal state was cancelled.

Sima Tai, King of Gaomi, was younger brother of Sima Quan, King of Pengcheng. He was Marquis of Yangting in Wei, served as Prefect of Fufeng. Emperor Wu made him King of Longxi with 3,200 households, serving as Guerrilla General. He served as Governor of Yanzhou and General Yingyang, later as General Anxi and Governor of Yizhou, but claimed illness and did not go. He served as General Anbei, then General of Zhenxi. He died in the ninth year of Yuankang, posthumously awarded Taifu. Sima Tai was clean, quiet, not close to musical women, dressed and ate like ordinary people, filial and humble. Among the kings, only Sima Tai and Sima Huang, King of Xia Pi, were praised for self-control. He had four sons: Sima Yue, Sima Teng, Sima Lue, and Sima Mo.

Sima Lue, King Xiao, was filial and kind, treating wise men with caution and humility. He served as Minister of Sanqi Huangmen and Secretary Supervisor, later as General Annan and General Anbei. He threatened Governor Cheng Mu of Qingzhou and took charge himself. When Liu Gen raised troops, Sima Lue could not resist and defended Liaocheng. Emperor Huai moved him to Commander-in-Chief of Jingzhou's military affairs. He defeated Wang Di's rebellion. He died in the third year of Yongjia, posthumously awarded Shizhong and Taiwei. His son Sima Ju succeeded him but died without sons. Sima Xian, son of King Kang of Pengcheng, succeeded, then returned to his clan, and Sima Xian's son Sima Jun became heir.

Sima Teng, King of Xincai, served as Prefect of Nanyang and Wei, competent in office. He was recruited as Zongzheng, moved to Taichang, then served as General Ningbei and Governor of Bingzhou. When Emperor Hui attacked Sima Ying, Sima Teng and Wang Jun defeated Sima Ying's forces. Sima Ying fled with the emperor to Luoyang. Sima Teng was promoted to General Anbei, later General of Chariots and Cavalry. He was renamed King Xincai. When Ji Sang and others rebelled, Sima Teng dismissed them as insignificant but was killed by Li Feng. Sima Teng had four sons: Sima Yu, Sima Jiao, Sima Shao, and Sima Que. Sima Teng was frugal and stingy, not giving relief to people, leading to disaster. His bones were not recovered. Sima Que succeeded him, served as General Dongzhonglang, guarded Xuchang, and was killed by Shi Le. He had no sons, so Sima Tao, son of Sima Hun, was asked to worship Sima Que, later Sima Bi, son of Sima Ku, succeeded. Sima Bi died without sons, succeeded by his brother Sima Miao. After his death, his son Sima Huang succeeded, but Huan Wen deposed him. Emperor Xiaowu established Sima Chong as heir. Song accepted abdication and the feudal state was cancelled.

Sima Mo, King of Nanyang, was eager to learn young, famous with Emperor Yuan and Sima You. He was initially named Pingchang Gong. He served as Crown Prince's Concubine and Official Attendant. Sima Yue appointed him Beizhonglang General to guard Ye. He was promoted to King of Nanyang. During famine in Guanzhong, Sima Mo cast bronze vessels into cooking utensils for food, criticized by commentators. Sima Yue summoned him as Sikong, but Sima Mo refused, persuaded by Chun Yuding. He was promoted to Taiwei and Grand Governor. When Luo Jing fell, Sima Mo surrendered to Zhao Ran, who recited his crimes and sent him to Liu Can, who killed him. Sima Mo's son Sima Bao succeeded him.

Sima Bao, courtesy name Jingdu, was talented in literature. When Sima Mo was killed, Sima Bao was in Shangying. He controlled Qinzhou, appointed officials, and Di and Qiang people obeyed him. Emperor Min appointed him Right Prime Minister and Commander-in-Chief. After Emperor Min's humiliation, Sima Bao claimed to be King of Jin. Famine struck Shangbang, and Zhang Chun escorted Sima Bao to Nan'an. Sima Bao died of illness at twenty-seven. He was tall, fat, weighed 800 jin, slept well but was impotent. Having no sons, Zhang Chun established Sima Zhan as successor. Chen An attacked Zhang Chun, Sima Zhan surrendered to Chen An, who sent him to Liu Yao, who killed him. Chen An buried Sima Bao with emperor's rites, posthumous title Yuan.

Sima Sui, King of Fanyang, was younger brother of Sima Quan, King of Pengcheng. He was initially an Admonishment Official, granted title in the first year of Taishi, reigned fifteen years, died in the fifth year of Xianning, succeeded by his son Sima Gai. Sima Gai was famous for studiousness, studied classics, good at speaking. He served as Sanqi Changshi, Minister, General Annan, guarding Xuchang, promoted to General Conquering the South. Sima Yong proposed appointing Wang Siyan. Sima Gai, Sima Liu, and Zhou Fu reported to the emperor about Sima Ying's failures and recommended Zhang Fang. They urged the emperor to give Guan You to Dazai and send Zhang Fang back to the county. Sima Gai led everyone from Xu to Rongyang to garrison. When Emperor Hui moved west, Sima Gai formed an alliance with Sima Mo and elected Sima Yue as leader. Liu Qiao attacked Xu, Sima Gai crossed the Yellow River, defeated Liu Qiao. Sima Ji killed Zhang Fang and sent his head to Sima Yue. Sima Gai escorted the emperor back to the capital, worshiped as Situ. He died at thirty-seven, no sons, adopted Sima Mo's son Sima Li, who was killed in Chang'an.

Sima Sui, King Hui of Jinan, was son of Sima Xun, younger brother of Emperor Xuan. He was Marquis of Guannei in Wei, promoted to Marquis of Qianchang Ting, served as Dianjunlang. In the second year of Jingyuan, he was granted title of Rural Marquis of Wucheng, Governor of Yecheng and General Beizhonglang. He was named Zhu Abo, moved to Champion General. Emperor Wu made him King of Jinan. He died in the second year of Taishi. He had two sons: Sima Dan and Sima Ji. Sima Dan succeeded, renamed King of Zhongshan, died without sons, Sima Ji succeeded. Sima Ji served as General Jianwei, fought against Wang Chuang, died in battle. Without sons, the feudal state was cancelled. Later, Sima Sui's great-grandson Sima Xun, named Wei Chang, was adopted by Liu Yao's general Linghu Ni. He returned from Guanyou in the sixth year of Xianhe, claimed to be Sima Xun's descendant, paid homage to Pu She. He served as Governor of Liangzhou, attacked Chang'an, captured Hecheng. Guanzhong responded to him. During the Yonghe period, he entered Chang'an, killed Zhang Ju, but was defeated by Fu Xiong. He was promoted to General Conquering the Captives, Marquis of Tongjiting. He was tyrannical, killing wealthy families who disagreed with him. He planned to occupy Shu territory. Huan Wen sent troops to attack him, Sima Xun was captured and killed.

Sima Xun, King of Qiao Gang, was son of Sima Jin, younger brother of Emperor Xuan. He was Marquis of Wei Guannei, granted title of Marquis of Yangting of Chengcheng, served as General Qingche and Zuojian Yulin. He was named Jingyang Nan. Emperor Wu made him King Qiao with 4,400 households. He died in the second year of Taishi. He had two sons: Sima Sui and Sima Cheng. Sima Sui succeeded, died in Shile. Emperor Yuan asked Sima to succeed him. Sima Cheng, King Min, was loyal and virtuous young. He served as General Guangwei and Protector of Anyi. He returned to Luoyang with Emperor Hui, became Guerrilla General. During Yongjia period, he secretly attached himself to Shan Jian. When Shan Jian died, he entered Wuchang. When Emperor Yuan guarded Yangzhou, Sima Cheng returned to Jiankang, served as Military Counselor and Wine Worshiper. Emperor Yuan changed his title to King Qiao. He served as Captain of Tunqi School, General of the Auxiliary State and Left Army. He was frugal, did not take concubines. When Wang Dun was disloyal, the emperor summoned Sima Cheng at night, showed him Wang Dun's memorial. Sima Cheng advised early action. The emperor appointed him to guard Xiangzhou. Sima Cheng traveled to Wuchang, saw Wang Dun unarmed. Wang Dun tested him but let him go to his post. Sima Cheng was frugal, rode a reed cart, comforted people, earned good reputation. Wang Dun feared him, requisitioned his ships. When Wang Dun rebelled, Sima Cheng raised righteous deeds, but the city fell after more than a hundred days. Wei Yi sent him to Jingzhou, where Wang Yu killed him on the way at fifty-nine. After Wang Dun was pacified, Sima Cheng was posthumously awarded General of Chariots and Cavalry. His son Wuji succeeded him.

Sima Wuji, King Lie, escaped when young during Sima Cheng's trouble. He served as Sanqi Shilang, moved to Tunqi Xiaowei and Zhongshu. He tried to kill Wang Yu's son Qizhi for revenge but was stopped. The emperor pardoned him. He served as Sanqi Changshi and Censor Zhongcheng, then General of the Auxiliary State and Prime Minister of Changsha. He followed Dandan in expedition against Shu, given title of Duke Guangzhi. He died in the sixth year of Cheng Mian, posthumously awarded General Wei. He had two sons: Sima Tian and Sima Qing. Sima Tian succeeded him.

Sima Tian, King Jing, served as Sanqi Shilang, Sanqi Changshi, Huangmenlang, and Yushi Zhongcheng. When Duke Haixi was deposed, Sima Tian impeached Grand Sima Xun for disrespect. The emperor sighed at his courage. Sima Tian was loyal, upright, talented, feared by officials. He served as General of the Right Guard, Shangshu, Shizhong, and General of the Left Guard. He was appointed Domestic History of Wu and Prince Zhanshi. Emperor Xiaowu relied on him heavily, appointed him Military Governor of several states and General of Zhenbei. He died in the fifteenth year of Taiyuan, posthumously awarded General of Chariots and Cavalry. He had four sons: Shangzhi, Huizhi, Yunzhi, and Xiuzhi. Shangzhi succeeded him.

Sima Shangzhi, King Zhong, served as Secretary-General and Sanqi Shilang. He guarded Jingkou as General Zhenwei and Prime Minister Guangling. After his father's death, he served as Hussar Consultant. Within the clan, outstanding figures appeared generation after generation. When Wang Guobao was executed, Shangzhi advised King Daozi of Kuaiji to release Liu Zhenzhi and others. Daozi obeyed. Wang Gong, Governor of Yanzhou, jealous of Shangzhi's power, raised troops with Yu Kai and others. Daozi ordered Shangzhi to resist Yu Kai. Shangzhi defeated Yu Kai's forces. Daozi appointed Shangzhi as General Jianwei and Governor of Yuzhou. Shangzhi's power surpassed others. Later, Yuan Xian came to power and relied on him. Shangzhi scolded Yuan Xian's favorite Zhang Fashun, earning Yuan Xian's resentment. When Yuan Xian attacked Huan Xuan, Shangzhi was ordered to be vanguard. Huan Xuan attacked, Shangzhi's army collapsed, and Huan Xuan had Shangzhi killed. The emperor posthumously awarded General Wei to Shangzhi. His cousin Kangzhi was renamed King of Qiao County. Later, Wensi, Xiuzhi's eldest son, succeeded Shangzhi but was cruel and rebellious. Liu Yu killed him, and the feudal state was cancelled.

Sima Huizhi served as Commander of Hsiao Cavalry and Danyang Yin. After Shangzhi was killed, Huan Xuan moved Huizhi and others to Guangzhou, killing them on the way. Emperor An posthumously awarded him General Fujun. Sima Xiuzhi served as General Longxiang and Governor of Xiangcheng. When Shangzhi was defeated, Xiuzhi fought hard but failed, then joined Murong Chao. He returned to the capital hearing of the rebel army's rise. He was appointed to supervise military affairs of six states and serve as Governor of Jingzhou. Huan Zhen attacked Jiangling, Xiuzhi was defeated and fled to Xiangyang. He returned to his post but was later relieved. He served as General of the Empress and Internal Secretary of Kuaiji. He was demoted for violating the ban. When Lu Xun rebelled, he was appointed military commander. He was dismissed due to official crimes. Later, he was appointed Governor of Jingzhou. Because his son Wensi made trouble, Xiuzhi apologized and asked to be relieved. The emperor forbade it. Later, Xiuzhi colluded with Lu Zongzhi to kill those in power. Liu Yu personally conquered Xiuzhi. Xiuzhi was defeated and defected to Yao Xing with Zongzhi. Liu Yu pacified Yao Hong, and Xiuzhi defected to Wei but died on the road.

Sima Yunzhi passed to his uncle Sima Qing, inherited the title of Guangjin Bo, served as General of the Auxiliary State. When Wang Gong, Yu Kai, and Huan Xuan conquered the country, Yunzhi's brothers resisted but were defeated. He and his elder brother were moved to Luzhou and killed on the road. The rebel army arose, and he was posthumously awarded Taichang Qing. His cousin Chenzhi let his son Wenhui inherit the title. After Song accepted abdication, the feudal state was cancelled.

Sima Mu, King of Gaoyang, was younger brother of Sima Xun, King of Qiao. In Wei, he was Marquis of Anpingting, served as Imperial Censor. Emperor Wu made him King of Zhongshan with 5,200 households. Sima Mu petitioned to build an ancestral temple according to Liu Liao's sacrifice to Gao Tao. The matter was left to Taichang for review. Doctors offered wine to Liu Bang and others discussed the etiquette. The edict said the provisions were unclear and should be reviewed. In the third year of Xianning, Sima Mu recruited more than 700 migrants, and Du You, Governor of Jizhou, reported him. The relevant officials said the incident occurred before amnesty and should be forgiven. The emperor demoted Sima Mu to Marquis of Danshui County. After Wu was pacified, the edict restored his title. He was named King of Gaoyang. In the first year of Yuankang, he was appointed Zongzheng. He died in office, his eldest son Sima Wei died early, and his grandson Sima Yi succeeded. Sima Yi served as Sanqi Shilang, died in Shile during Yongjia period. In the first year of Long'an, Wen Shen, son of Huizhi, second son of Sima Tian, was appointed as Sima Yi's successor. He died without sons after five years, succeeded by Fa Lian, son of King Sima Chun of Gaomi. Song accepted abdication and the feudal state was cancelled.

Sima Ling, King of Renchengjing, was son of Sima Tong, younger brother of Emperor Xuan. He was Tinghou of Ancheng. Initially, he served as Yilang. In the first year of Taishi, he was granted title of King of Beihai with 4,700 households. In the third year, he was transferred to King of City. He died in the fifth year of Xianning, succeeded by his son Sima Ji. Sima Ji served as Sanqi Shilang and Auxiliary General. Following Sima Yue, King of Shuhai, in Xiangdi, he was killed by Shi Le, and his two sons died. He had two younger brothers: Sima Shun and Sima Bin. Sima Shun, courtesy name Zisi, was initially Marquis of Xiyangting. When Emperor Wu accepted abdication, Sima Shun sighed and cried sadly, saying the matter was contrary to Lu and Yu. He was deposed and moved to Guzang County, Wuwei. He persisted in his views until death. Sima Bin, King Mu of Xihe, was Zhonglang of Wei. Emperor Wu made him King Chen with 1,710 households. Three years later, it was renamed Xihe. He died in the fourth year of Xianning, succeeded by his son Yinli. When Yinli died, his son succeeded.

The historical commentary notes that in the early years of Taishi, the clan was established as a barrier to protect the country. Anping's demeanor was majestic and elegant, promoting morality internally and demonstrating loyalty externally. When Duke Gaogui was killed, he wept bitterly. When King Chenliu went to Jinyong City, he bid farewell tearfully. He could be ranked among top ministers, enjoy a long life, and be a model of extreme beauty. The King of Gaomi had a quiet style, simple and without desires, filial to relatives and loyal to the court. King Xincai and King Nanyang both ruled one side. During the royal family's trouble, they showed loyalty and made achievements in difficult times. King Qiao Min was brave and resourceful, guarding the south. When evil spirits rebelled, he raised righteousness in Xiangzhou. Although without meritorious service, he showed loyalty and left achievements to future generations. Sima Xun was entrusted with the clan but had a violent nature, betraying the king and failing to live up to kindness. His treacherous plot deepened, and he was eventually pacified. Marquis Xiyang Ting was frugal and reclusive, putting emotions aside from the world, valuing the principles of being knowledgeable and ethical.
