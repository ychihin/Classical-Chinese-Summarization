Fu Xuan, courtesy name Xiu Yi, was from Niyang in the north. His grandfather Fu Xie was a Han prefect, and his father Fu Qian was a Wei governor. Orphaned and poor in youth, Fu Xuan was erudite, skilled in writing, and knowledgeable in music. He had a strong, upright character and could not tolerate others' faults. He was appointed as a county planner, recommended as Xiaolian twice, and recruited by the Taiwei, but did not take the post. He passed the scholar examination, became a doctor, and was selected with Donghai Mushi to compile Wei books. He later held various military and civil posts, including prefect of Hongnong and captain of Diannong School, and was known for his competent governance and many petitions. After the fifth-grade system was established, he was granted the title of Baron Quail. Under Emperor Wu of Jin, he was promoted to viscount and Prince Consort and Commander-in-Chief.

As an admonishment officer, Fu Xuan submitted a memorial criticizing the decline of benevolence and righteousness since the Qin and Wei dynasties, praising Emperor Wu's virtue, and urging the recommendation of courteous ministers and removal of hypocritical ones. The emperor agreed and asked him to draft an edict. Fu Xuan further wrote on the importance of proper official appointments, advocating for long-term positions and the division of labor among scholars, farmers, workers, and merchants. He stressed the need for a system to plan the number of people in each class, emphasizing agriculture and Confucian education. He also proposed that idle officials should farm, and that the government should evaluate officials every three years, with promotions after nine years. The emperor praised his sincerity and ordered further study of his proposals.

In the fourth year of Taishi, Fu Xuan became Yushi Zhongcheng. During floods and droughts, he submitted five proposals: adjusting the share of harvest for soldiers using government cattle; enforcing Han-era laws to punish negligent officials; improving water conservancy by dividing responsibilities; reforming land tax to rest labor; and establishing a new county in Gaopingchuan to manage the Xianbei tribe and prevent border unrest. The emperor approved these as fundamental to governance. In the fifth year of Taishi, Fu Xuan became a servant. Despite grain shortages and border troubles, his sincere advice was often tolerated. He later became Sili Xiaowei, but was dismissed after a dispute over seating at a mourning ceremony. He died at home at age sixty-two, posthumously titled Gang.

Fu Xuan wrote "Fu Zi," a book on governance and the three religions, divided into internal, external, and middle chapters, with over 100 volumes. His son Fu Xian inherited his title. Fu Xian, courtesy name Yu, was upright, strict, and hated evil. He served as Prince Xima, Shangshu Youcheng, and governor of Jizhou, but resigned when his stepmother refused to accompany him. Later, as Situ Zuo Changshi, he criticized the emperor for overworking himself and proposed consolidating official positions, simplifying affairs, and promoting agriculture. He also petitioned against extravagance, citing ancient frugality. During Emperor Hui's reign, he advised Yang Jun on regency matters and later criticized Sima Liang for excessive rewards. Fu Xian was known for his fearless impeachments, including against Wang Rong, and his advocacy for long-term official tenures. He died in office at age fifty-six, posthumously titled Zhen. He had three sons: Fu Fu, Fu Rixi, and Fu Zuan.
