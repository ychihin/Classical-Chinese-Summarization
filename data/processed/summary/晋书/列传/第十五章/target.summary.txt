Liu Yi, courtesy name Zhongxiong, was from Ye in Donglai, a descendant of Liu Zhang, King of Yangjing. His father, Liu Zhe, served as Prime Minister. Liu Yi was filial in his youth and cultivated noble integrity, but he enjoyed commenting on people and was wary of princes and nobles. When Du Shu, the prefect of Qi, appointed him as a meritorious official, he eliminated over a hundred county officials, earning praise from the people of the Three Wei Dynasties. They remarked, "We only heard of Liu Gongcao, not Du Fujun." At the end of the Wei Dynasty, he was recommended as Xiaolian and appointed as Si Li Duguan, bringing order to the capital. When Liu Yi sought to impeach Yin of Henan, Sili objected, but Liu Yi insisted, saying, "A dog can catch animals and kill rats—what harm is there?" He then resigned.

Wang Ji recommended Liu Yi to the government, praising his uprightness and honesty. Liu Yi later served as an official in Pingyang and was known for his dignified conduct. Taichang Zheng Miao recommended him as a doctor, and Emperor Wen appointed him as a prime minister, but he declined due to illness. People said Liu Yi was loyal to the Wei family, and the emperor suspected him of nostalgia for the old dynasty, threatening death. Frightened, Liu Yi accepted the role of chief registrar. When Emperor Wu accepted the abdication, he made Liu Yi a minister and prince-in-law, later appointing him as an admonishment officer. Liu Yi served as city gate school captain, servant, and minister, but was dismissed for crimes. In the early years of Xianning, he was reappointed as a regular attendant and doctor, then as Sili school lieutenant, where he suppressed powerful families and brought order to the capital. Officials feared him, and he was compared to Zhuge Feng and Gai Kuanrao.

When the crown prince entered the court, Liu Yi blocked the band from entering Dongye Gate, deeming it improper, and impeached officials. The emperor pardoned them. After a ceremony at the southern suburbs, the emperor asked Liu Yi which Han emperor he compared to. Liu Yi replied, "Emperor Huan and Emperor Ling." The emperor was displeased, noting his own achievements, but Liu Yi retorted, "They sold offices for the treasury; you sell them for private gain." The emperor laughed, acknowledging Liu Yi's directness. Zou Zhan praised the emperor's tolerance, comparing it favorably to Emperor Wen of Han. Liu Yi later criticized the appearance of a dragon in the arsenal well, arguing against congratulations, citing historical precedents. The emperor accepted his view.

Liu Yi criticized the nine-rank Zhongzheng system, listing eight drawbacks: it relied on personal connections, ignored true talent, caused disputes, and led to corruption. He argued it failed to select virtuous officials and advocated for its abolition. The emperor did not implement his proposal. Liu Yi was dedicated to public service, speaking sincerely, and was respected by all. He was strict with his family, punishing his wife for visiting him during a fast. Despite his poverty, the emperor rewarded him generously. At seventy, he retired but was later recommended as Qingzhou's Grand Central Magistrate, though some officials opposed it. Sun Yin argued for his appointment, citing his integrity and ability. Liu Yi died in the sixth year of Taikang, and the emperor mourned him deeply, posthumously honoring him.

Liu Yi's son, Liu Tun, inherited his father's integrity. He served as a doctor and was punished for opposing the emperor's will. Later, he became Suanzao Ling and Shi Yushi, impeaching Wang Hun for misconduct. Liu Tun also criticized Guo Zhang for failing to put out a fire, causing Guo to reform. He served as Taiyuan's internal history, refused to serve under King Sima Lun, and participated in the three kings' revolt. He was appointed as Zuocheng and censor Zhongcheng, impeaching many officials. He was knighted for his role in attacking Sima Tong but dismissed after Sima Yi's death. He later served as Sili school lieutenant again, defending Queen Yang against King Sima Yong. He fled to Sima Lue and helped repel Liu Gen. After the emperor's return, he was honored but later killed by Shi Le's patrol cavalry.

Other figures include Cheng Wei, a strict official recruited by Liu Yi; He Xiao, a capable administrator praised for his integrity; Wu Yi, a perceptive official who criticized figures; Ren Kai, a loyal minister who opposed Jia Chong and was slandered; Cui Hong, a stern censor who punished misconduct; Guo Yi, a magnanimous official who recognized talent; Hou Shiguang, a loyal and frugal minister; and He Pan, a fair-minded official who helped clear injustices. The text concludes with a commentary praising these officials for their integrity and service, despite facing challenges and slander.
