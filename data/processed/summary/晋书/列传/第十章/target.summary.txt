Jia Chong, courtesy name Gonglu, was from Xiangling, Pingyang. His father, Jia Kui, served as Governor of Yuzhou under Wei and was the Marquis of Yangliting. Jia Kui fathered Jia Chong in his later years, naming him "Chong" (meaning "fill") in anticipation of future joyful celebrations filling the streets. Jia Chong lost his father young and was renowned for his filial piety in mourning. He succeeded his father's marquisate and began his official career as Shangshu Lang, where he analyzed classical rules and regulations for his work. He later held positions including Huangmen Shilang and Jijun Diannong Zhonglang.

Jia Chong participated in military campaigns under Emperor Jing against Qiu Jian and Wen Qin at Lejia. When the emperor fell ill and returned to Xuchang, Jia Chong was left to supervise military affairs, earning an additional 350 households in his fief. He later served as Sima under Emperor Wen and was transferred to You Changshi. Emperor Wen, newly in power, sent Jia Chong to Zhuge Dan under the pretext of discussing plans to attack Wu, but actually to observe his intentions. During their conversation, Jia Chong hinted at the possibility of a dynastic change, but Zhuge Dan sternly rebuked him, declaring his loyalty to Wei. Jia Chong reported to Emperor Wen that Zhuge Dan would surely rebel, advising that summoning him would trigger a minor rebellion, while delay would cause greater harm. Emperor Wen summoned Zhuge Dan as Sikong, and Zhuge Dan indeed rebelled. Jia Chong accompanied the expedition and proposed a strategy of digging deep moats and building high forts to besiege the rebels without battle, which succeeded.

After the city fell, Jia Chong was promoted to Marquis of Xuanyang Township with an additional 1,000 households. He became Tingwei, known for correcting wrongful cases, then Central Guards Army commander. When Gaogui Xianggong attacked the Prime Minister's Mansion, Jia Chong led troops at Nanque. As his forces faltered, Cheng Ji asked what to do, and Jia Chong replied, "I have raised you for just this day." Cheng Ji then killed Gaogui Xianggong. Under the new emperor, Jia Chong was made Marquis of Anyang Township with 1,200 additional households, commanding城外 armies. When Zhong Hui rebelled in Shu, Jia Chong was sent to supervise Guanzhong and Longyou, but Zhong Hui died before his arrival.

Jia Chong was deeply trusted by Emperor Wen, revising laws alongside Pei Xiu, Wang Chen, Yang Ku, and Xun Xu. He received the highest-class house and was named Marquis of Linyi. Initially, Emperor Wen considered passing the throne to Wuyang Hou Sima You, but Jia Chong praised Emperor Wu's virtues as the eldest son, convincing him to choose Emperor Wu. Upon Emperor Wu's succession, Jia Chong became General of the Guards of Jin, Yitong Sansi, and Marquis of Linying. He was later promoted to General of Chariots and Cavalry, Regular Attendant, and Minister of Public Affairs for his role in establishing the dynasty.

Jia Chong oversaw the revision of laws, which were praised for their leniency and simplicity. The emperor rewarded him with a marquisate for one of his children and 500 bolts of silk, which Jia Chong declined but was overruled. He succeeded Pei Xiu as Shangshu Ling while retaining his other posts. After his mother's death, he was recalled from mourning after 60 days due to affairs in the southeast. He devoted himself to agricultural savings and streamlining officials, earning imperial praise. Jia Chong recommended talents and treated others with sincerity, though he lacked integrity and relied on flattery, earning hatred from upright officials like Ren Kai and Yu Chun.

When Di and Qiang rebelled, Jia Chong was sent to guard Guanzhong as commander of Qin and Liang provinces. However, through Xun Xu's influence, his daughter was married to the crown prince, allowing him to remain in the capital. During the campaign to conquer Wu, Jia Chong was appointed supreme commander but was reluctant, fearing failure. He repeatedly advised against the campaign, but the emperor insisted. After Wu's surrender, Jia Chong was heavily rewarded but felt ashamed of his lack of strategy. He died in the third year of Taikang at age 66, receiving grand posthumous honors.

Jia Chong's wife, Guo Huai, was extremely jealous, causing the deaths of two sons by killing their wet nurses. After Jia Chong's death, his grandson Han Mi was made heir. His daughter Jia Wu secretly married Han Shou after an affair, which Jia Chong discovered but concealed. Jia Chong's descendants, including Jia Mi, became powerful but were eventually killed during political turmoil.
