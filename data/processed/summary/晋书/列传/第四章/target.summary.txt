**Summary of the Biographies of Yang Hu and Du Yu**

Yang Hu, a native of Mount Tai, came from a lineage of high-ranking officials renowned for integrity. Orphaned at twelve, he was known for his filial piety and erudition, standing seven feet three inches tall with a handsome appearance and eloquence. He repeatedly declined official appointments, demonstrating foresight by avoiding service under Cao Shuang, whose subsequent downfall vindicated his caution. After his mother and brother died, he observed mourning for over a decade. He later served Emperor Wen as a palace attendant and was enfeoffed as a marquis. Fearing the favored Zhong Hui, he was relieved when Zhong was executed. Under Emperor Wu, he rose to general and marquis, but humbly declined higher titles. As commander in Jingzhou, he reduced border troops, opened schools, and won the loyalty of the people through sincerity and leniency toward defectors. He implemented agricultural policies that amassed a decade’s worth of grain reserves. Despite his relaxed demeanor, a military officer once blocked his unauthorized night outing, and Yang Hu accepted the reprimand. He repeatedly refused promotions, citing his lack of merit and fear of disgrace. His campaign to support Wu defector Bu Chan failed due to slow mobilization, leading to his demotion. He later built strategic cities, expanded farmland, and treated Wu soldiers with humanity, returning captives and compensating for requisitioned crops. His virtuous conduct so impressed Wu general Lu Kang that they exchanged envoys and medicine without suspicion. Yang Hu hated sycophants like Xun Xu and Feng Tong, and predicted that his nephew Wang Yan would corrupt morals. He advocated for conquering Wu, arguing that Sun Hao’s tyranny and Wu’s internal discord made victory certain. Despite opposition, he persisted, but died at fifty-eight before the campaign succeeded. His death caused widespread mourning, even among Wu soldiers. He was buried with honors, and his posthumous title reflected his humility. Two years later, Wu was pacified, and the emperor credited Yang Hu’s plans.

Du Yu, from Duling, was knowledgeable and pragmatic, believing virtue could not be forced but deeds could be achieved. His father’s conflict with Emperor Xuan delayed his career, but he married a princess and served as a minister. He survived Zhong Hui’s rebellion through wisdom. He codified laws, emphasizing simplicity and clarity to prevent violations. As Henan Yin, he advocated for straightforward official assessments, arguing that overly detailed regulations bred hypocrisy. He was dismissed after a conflict with Shi Jian but later served as a military commander, correctly predicting the enemy’s actions. He proposed numerous reforms, including new tools, granaries, and tax regulations. He built a bridge at Fupingjin despite skepticism, and crafted a water clock. Appointed to replace Yang Hu in Jingzhou, he repaired weapons, trained troops, and defeated Wu general Zhang Zheng, then alienated Wu by returning captives. He urged immediate conquest of Wu, countering objections about summer rains by citing overwhelming momentum. His forces captured Jiangling and other cities, leading to Wu’s surrender. He was enfeoffed as a marquis. After the war, he focused on education, irrigation, and stabilizing the region, earning the people’s trust. He carved stone tablets to record his achievements, predicting geological changes. Though not a horseman or archer, he led effectively and was known for his humility and dedication to the *Zuo Zhuan*, which he annotated extensively. He died at sixty-three while traveling, leaving instructions for a simple burial. His son Du Xi served as a crown prince’s tutor, known for his integrity, and survived political turmoil. The chroniclers praised Yang Hu’s strategic vision and Du Yu’s multifaceted talents, noting their contributions to unifying the realm.
