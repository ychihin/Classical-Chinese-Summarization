# Summary of Historical Biographies of Wang Xiang, Zheng Chong, He Zeng, and Shi Bao

Wang Xiang, courtesy name Xiuzheng, was from Linyi, Langye, a descendant of Han official Wang Ji. His grandfather Wang Ren served as Qingzhou governor, while his father Wang Rong refused government service. Wang Xiang was exceptionally filial. After his mother died young, his stepmother Zhu treated him poorly and spoke ill of him, causing his father to lose affection for him. Despite being ordered to clean cattle pens, Wang Xiang remained respectful. When his parents fell ill, he personally tasted their medicine. During freezing weather, his stepmother desired live fish; Wang Xiang removed his clothes to break ice, and two carps leaped out spontaneously. When she wanted roasted oriole, dozens flew into his tent. Villagers attributed these events to his filial piety. During late Han warfare, he supported his mother and younger brother Wang Lan while hiding in Lujiang for over twenty years, refusing official appointments. After his mother's death, he became ill from grief. At nearly sixty, he initially refused Governor Lu Qian's invitation but eventually accepted after Wang Lan's persuasion. He defeated bandits, brought peace to the state, and was praised in song. Wang Xiang held various official positions including Wen Ling, Da Sinong, and was named Guannei Marquis. He served as Sili Xiaowei, Taichang, and Marquis of Wansui Ting. The emperor appointed him as third elder at the Imperial Academy, where he taught political education. He was promoted to Sikong, Taiwei, and Shizhong, eventually becoming Suiling Marquis with 1,600 households. When Emperor Wen became King of Jin, Wang Xiang refused to kneel, stating that as a Wei official, he would not bow to others. Emperor Wu appointed him Taibao and promoted him to Duke. Despite repeated retirement requests due to age, the emperor refused, granting him privileges including exemption from court attendance. Upon critical illness, Wang Xiang instructed his descendants on simple burial, forbidding elaborate ceremonies and expensive items. He died in the fourth year of Taishi at age eighty-five. His sons included Wang Zhao, Wang Xia, Wang Fu, Wang Lie, and Wang Fen.

Zheng Chong, courtesy name Wenhe, was from Kaifeng, Rongyang. Born poor, he established moral integrity, was quiet with few desires, and extensively studied Confucian classics. He followed etiquette strictly and did not seek local fame. Emperor Wen of Wei appointed him literary minister, later promoting him to Shangshulang and Chenliu prefect. He valued elegance over wealth, earning respect. Cao Shuang recommended him as lieutenant, then Sanqi Changshi and Guang Luxun. In Jiaping's third year, he became Sikong, later Situ. He was appointed Taibao and named Shouguang Hou. Though holding prime ministerial rank, he avoided worldly affairs. When Emperor Wei abdicated, Zheng Chong presented the policy letter. Emperor Wu appointed him Taifu and promoted him to Duke. Despite illness-related requests for removal, the emperor refused. In the sixth year of Taishi, the emperor praised Zheng Chong, He Zeng, and Xun Hao for their virtue and service. Zheng Chong repeatedly resigned, and the emperor eventually allowed him to return to his mansion as Duke Shouguang with full privileges. He died the following year, receiving posthumous honors. Zheng Chong compiled "Collected Commentary of the Analects of Confucius" with others. Having no sons, his nephew Zheng Hui inherited his title.

He Zeng, courtesy name Yingkao, was from Yangxia, Chen State. His father He Kui was Taipu of Wei. He Zeng inherited his title young, was eager to learn, and gained reputation. He served as Sanqi Changshi, Diannong General, and Huangmen Shilang. He submitted memorials advising careful selection of county governors. He served as governor of Hanoi with majestic reputation. As Sili Xiaowei, he impeached the favored official Yin Mo. He criticized Ruan Ji's dissolute behavior during mourning. He held various positions including Zhenbei General and was granted Marquis of Yingchang Township. Emperor Wu appointed him Taibao and later Taifu. He was known for filial piety, treating his wife with formal respect. However, he was extremely extravagant, spending ten thousand on daily meals, with luxurious carriages and clothing exceeding imperial standards. He died at age eighty, receiving the posthumous name Xiao. His sons He Zun and He Shao inherited his wealth. He Shao served as prince's grand master and Situ, maintaining similar extravagance.

Shi Bao, courtesy name Zhongrong, was from Nanpi, Bohai. Known for beauty and intelligence, he was discovered by Guo Xuanxin who predicted his future as minister. He served as Sima of Emperor Jing's Imperial Guard. Despite concerns about his character, Emperor Jing defended him, comparing him to Guan Zhong and Chen Ping. He served as Ye Diannong Zhonglang Jiang, governor of Donglai and Langye, and Xuzhou governor. During Zhuge Dan's rebellion, Shi Bao led troops and defeated Wu forces. He was granted Marquis of Shuguang and later promoted to Grand Sima and Duke of Leling County. Suspected of rebellion due to rumors, he was dismissed but later reinstated as Situ. He focused on agricultural policies and died in the eighth year of Taishi. His sons included Shi Yue, Shi Qiao, Shi Tong, Shi Jun, Shi Jun, and Shi Chong. Shi Chong, known for wealth and extravagance, competed with royal relatives in luxury, owning numerous slaves and treasures. He was executed at age fifty-two during political conflicts, with his entire family killed.
