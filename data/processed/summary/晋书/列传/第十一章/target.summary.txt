**Summary of Historical Records: Wei Shu, Li Xi, Liu Yi, and Gao Guang**

Wei Shu, courtesy name Yangyuan, was born in Renchengfan. Orphaned young, he was raised by his maternal grandfather’s Ning family. A feng shui master predicted a noble nephew would be born, and Wei Shu’s intelligence led his grandmother to believe he fulfilled this prophecy. Tall and majestic, he was slow and simple, not valued by villagers. His uncle Wei Heng underestimated him, but Wei Shu remained unbothered. He enjoyed riding, archery, and hunting. Wang Yi of Taiyuan predicted he would become prime minister and supported him. Wei Shu once witnessed a prophecy about a boy who died at fifteen from an ax wound, which later came true. At forty, he studied for a hundred days, passed exams, and became Chief of Mianchi. He later served as Shangshu Lang, where he voluntarily resigned when incompetent officials were dismissed. He excelled in archery, surprising his superior Zhong Yu. As Prime Minister’s aide, he planned wisely on major issues. Emperor Wen valued him, and he governed Yiyang and Rongyang with a good reputation. He served as governor of Jizhou for three years, known for simplicity and benevolence. Emperor Wu rewarded him for honesty. He married three times, all wives predeceased him. In the Taikang era, he advocated for the Fengshan ceremony but was refused. He served as Zuopushe and managed the official department, proposing reforms for imperial marriage gifts. He became Situ and was known for distributing his salary to relatives, leaving no surplus. He appointed Zhou Zhen, who had been avoided due to a superstitious nickname, without disaster. He repeatedly resigned due to illness, eventually being allowed to retire as Juyangzi with honors. He died at eighty-two, posthumously named Kang. His son Wei Hun died young, and his great-nephew Wei Huang succeeded him.

Li Xi, courtesy name Jihe, was from Shangdang Tongtian. He had good conduct, studied widely, and refused recruitment by Guan Ning and others. Emperor Xuan summoned him, but he declined due to his mother’s illness. He later served as a special chariot in Bingzhou, advising Bi Gui against improper conduct. Emperor Jing appointed him as a lieutenant; when asked why he accepted, Li Xi replied he feared the law. He became censor Zhongcheng, feared for his uprightness. He recommended Sun Pu, praised for identifying talent. He was dismissed as Grand Sima but later served as governor of Liangzhou, stabilizing the region and defeating Qiang invaders without prior approval, compared to Han generals. He returned to Beijing, then governed Jizhou and became Sili Xiaowei. When Wei surrendered to Jin, he assisted in ceremonies. He was made Marquis of Qi and accused officials of occupying public rice fields, leading to punishment for Liu You. He served as crown prince’s tutor, teaching diligently. He resigned due to old age, receiving honors. He advocated attacking Liangzhou invaders but was overruled, leading to Liangzhou’s destruction. He was honest and frugal, sharing with relatives. He died, posthumously named Taibao. His son Li Zan succeeded him.

Liu Yi, courtesy name Zizhen, was from Gaotang, Pingyuan. Poor in youth, he sold cow clothes but studied diligently. He was recommended as Xiaolian but refused. He served as Shangshu Lang and Tingweizheng. He predicted Zhong Hui and Deng Ai would conquer Shu but not return, which came true. He wrote “On Chong Rang Lun” advocating humility to correct worldly competition. He argued that humility reveals talent, reduces slander, and stabilizes governance. He criticized the lack of humility in official appointments, leading to mediocrity and corruption. He proposed that officials should recommend the worthy, with selection based on collective recommendations rather than seniority. He served as Taichang and Shangshu. His son Liu Xia’s bribery led to his dismissal. He later served as crown prince’s mentor and Taibao. He resigned at ninety-one, dying soon after. He was frugal, once mistaking Shi Chong’s luxurious toilet for a bedroom. He studied the “Three Biographies” and wrote “Spring and Autumn Regulations.” His sons Liu Ji and Liu Xia were noted, with Liu Xia disgraced for corruption.

Gao Guang, courtesy name Xuan Mao, was from Chenliu. He knew law from youth, serving as Shangshu Lang and governor of Youzhou and Yingchuan. He was appointed Huangsha Yushi for legal expertise. During Sima Lun’s rebellion, he stayed loyal. He served as Tingwei and Shangshu, later following Emperor Hui to Chang'an when others fled. He became Zuopushe and young master. He died of illness, posthumously named Sikong. His son Gao Tao was corrupt and plotted against Sima Yue, leading to his execution. The historian praised Wei Shu and Liu Yi for their wisdom, Li Xi for his boldness, and Gao Guang for his integrity, noting Liu Xia’s corruption as a flaw.
