Shantao, also known as Juyuan, was a man of Hanoi who lost his father early and grew up in poverty. He was thoughtful from a young age, favored the works of Zhuangzi and Laozi, and often concealed his talents. He formed deep friendships with Ji Kang, Lu An, and later Ruan Ji, becoming one of the Seven Sages of the Bamboo Forest. At forty, he began his official career, serving in various roles such as the princess's administrator and chief planner. He was known for his integrity and wisdom, once warning Shi Jian about the dangers of Cao Shuang's rebellion. After Cao Shuang's downfall, Shantao withdrew from public life for a time.

Shantao later served under Emperor Jing and Emperor Wen, holding positions such as Zhao Guoxiang and minister of official affairs. He was praised for his honesty and intelligence, receiving generous gifts from the emperor. In his later years, he interacted with high-ranking officials like Zhong Hui and Pei Xiu, maintaining harmony among them. He was promoted to general and lieutenant, and during the campaign against Shu, he was entrusted with guarding Ye. In the early years of Xianxi, he was named Xin Da Zi and later served as Prime Minister Zuo Changshi.

When Emperor Wu succeeded, Shantao advised against appointing Sima You, citing the importance of proper succession. He served as Dahonglu and later as Fengche Captain. As governor of Jizhou, he reformed local customs by recommending talented individuals. He resigned multiple times due to his mother's age, but was eventually persuaded to return to office. He served as Shangshu of the Ministry of Personnel, selecting officials based on merit. Despite facing criticism, he continued his work, earning the title "Monkey Announcement" for his screening methods.

In his later years, Shantao faced the dictatorship of the Hou party and advised the emperor without success. He resigned due to old age and illness, but was repeatedly called back to service. He was promoted to Youpuse and Guanglu doctor, but continued to decline due to health. After Wu was pacified, he advised against withdrawing military forces, a warning that proved prescient during later chaos. He was appointed Situ but refused, eventually dying at seventy-nine. He was given posthumous honors and his family was provided for by the emperor.

Shantao was known for his frugality and loyalty, never taking concubines despite his high status. He distributed his salary to relatives and friends. He once accepted silk from a corrupt official but kept it sealed, later returning it when the official's crimes were exposed. He had five sons: Shan Gai, Shan Chun, Shan Yun, Shan Mo, and Shan Jian. Shan Gai inherited his title and served as governor of Bingzhou. Shan Jian, known for his gentle temperament, became a prominent official, serving as minister and governor of various regions.

Wang Rong, courtesy name Junchong, was from Linyi. He was known for his intelligence and calmness from a young age, once standing unafraid during a tiger's roar. He was friends with Ruan Ji and others, and was known for his insight. He served in various official roles, including governor of Jingzhou and Yuzhou, and was involved in the conquest of Wu. He was known for his filial piety, though he did not strictly adhere to rituals. He was criticized for his greed and stinginess, often calculating his wealth day and night. He died at seventy-two, posthumously named Yuan.

Wang Yan, courtesy name Yifu, was known for his clear expression and elegance. He was a leading figure in the Qingtan movement, advocating the philosophy of nothingness. He held high positions but was criticized for his lack of integrity and self-preservation. During the chaos of the Jin dynasty, he advised his brothers to take up posts in strategic regions, a plan known as the "cunning rabbit's three burrows." He was captured by Shi Le and killed after refusing to take responsibility for the dynasty's fall. He was fifty-six at his death.

Le Guang, courtesy name Yanfu, was known for his calm and far-sighted nature. He was skilled in conversation and used simple words to explain profound truths. He served as Henan Yin and was known for his ability to resolve disputes. He once cured a guest's illness by explaining the illusion of a snake in a cup. He was praised for his integrity and adherence to ritual, even during times of chaos. He died of worry after being slandered. He had three sons: Le Kai, Le Zhao, and Le Chen.
