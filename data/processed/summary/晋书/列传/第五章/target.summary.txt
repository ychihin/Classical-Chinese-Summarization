Chen Qian, a native of Shuyang, Linhuai, was the son of Chen Jiao, a Situ of Wei. Chen Jiao, originally from the Liu family, was raised by his maternal grandfather and changed his surname. Chen Qian was known for his depth and resourcefulness. Early in his career, he advised his father during a false accusation by Liu Ye, which was later resolved. As a youth, he remained calm when insulted by Xia Houxuan, impressing him. He served as Shangshu Lang, Grand Administrator of Zhongshan and Anping, achieving remarkable success. He rose to Sima, Changshi, and Yushi Zhongcheng, then Shangshu, and was granted the title of Tinghou of Anguo. During the Shu invasion of Longyou, he led forces to victory. He also acted against the Zhuge Dan rebellion. After pacifying Shouchun, he was appointed envoy Chijie, commander of Huaibei military affairs, and General Andong, with his title promoted to Marquis of Guangling. He later governed Yuzhou and was transferred to command military affairs south of the Yangtze and in Jingzhou, becoming General of the Conquest of the South and Marquis of Tan.

When Emperor Wu accepted the abdication, Chen Qian was promoted to General of Chariots and Cavalry for his merits, granted the title of Duke of Gaoping County, and served as Shizhong, Grand General, and commander of Yangzhou military affairs. He captured Zhili in Wu and defeated Tu Zhong's forces. In the early Xianning years, he was promoted to Taiwei and then Grand Sima. He warned the emperor about Hu Lie and Qian Hong's overconfidence, but the emperor dismissed his concerns. Both later died in conflict with the Qiang Rong, leading the emperor to regret his decision. Chen Qian was magnanimous and achieved success in all positions. He surpassed colleagues like Maichong, Shi Bao, and Pei Xiu in resourcefulness. He held high office beyond retirement age and eventually retired, receiving honors and gifts from the emperor. He died at eighty-one, with the emperor attending his funeral.

Pei Xiu, courtesy name Ji Yan, was from Wenxi, Hedong. His grandfather Pei Mao and father Pei Qian were ministers. Pei Xiu was eager to learn and wrote articles at eight. His uncle Pei Hui had many guests, and Pei Xiu's mother was once humiliated by her aunt, but Pei Xiu's reputation grew. He was recommended to Cao Shuang, who recruited him. After Cao Shuang's death, he was dismissed but later served as Tingweizheng and Wei Sima, with his policies adopted. He participated in the campaign against Zhuge Dan and was promoted to Minister and Marquis of Luyang Township. He helped establish the throne and was promoted to County Marquis. In the Xianxi era, he reformed the official system, proposing the fifth-class title. He was granted the title of Marquis of Jichuan. He advised Emperor Wen on the heir, supporting Emperor Wu. After Emperor Wu's ascension, he became Shangshu Ling and Duke of Julu County. He faced accusations but was defended by the emperor. He was later made Sikong.

Pei Xiu was knowledgeable and compiled the "Yugong Regional Circle," a map with six principles: ratio, accurate observation, road, high-low, fangxie, and roundabout. He established court礼仪 and criminal laws. He died at forty-eight, and the emperor mourned deeply. His son Pei Jun died early, and his younger son Pei Dian succeeded him. Pei Dian, courtesy name Yimin, was elegant and knowledgeable. He served as crown prince's concubine and Sanqi Changshi. He advised against Yang Jun's rebellion and was promoted. He discussed the prince's education and medical standards. He wrote the "Chongyou Lun" against nihilism, arguing for the importance of existence and ritual. He was killed by Sima Lun at thirty-four. His sons Pei Song and Pei Gai were exiled but later restored.

Pei Kai, courtesy name Shuze, was Pei Hui's son. He was intelligent and famous young, proficient in "Laozi" and "Yijing." He served as Shangshu Lang and Ding Kelang. He was known as the "Jade Man" for his elegance. He advised Emperor Wu during a divination, calming him. He served as Sanqi Minister and other posts. He was generous, giving away possessions. He criticized Jia Chong's presence in court. He survived the Yang Jun rebellion by hiding. He later served as Zhongshu Ling. He died at fifty-five. His sons included Pei Yu, Pei Zan, Pei Xian, Pei Li, and Pei Xun. Pei Xian, courtesy name Jingsi, was upright and served as Yuzhou governor. He was captured by Shi Le but praised for his integrity. He later served under Shi Le and Jilong, but his sons were killed, and he was dismissed. He died in the Shi family's hands.

The Pei family was prominent in Wei and Jin, with eight members compared to the eight Wangs. The text praises Chen Qian and Pei Xiu as famous officials of the Jin Dynasty.
