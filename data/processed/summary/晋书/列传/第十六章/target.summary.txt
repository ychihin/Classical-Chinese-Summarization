# Summary of Historical Text on Liu Song and Li Chong

Liu Song, courtesy name Ziya, was born in Guangling and descended from Liu Xu, King Li of Guangling in the Han Dynasty. His family had been prestigious for generations, belonging to a group of four surnames—Lei, Jiang, Gu, and Lu—all originating from the Liu surname. His father, Liu Guan, served as the prefect of Pingyang. Liu Song demonstrated analytical abilities from childhood and was praised by contemporaries. Though recommended as a filial and honest scholar, he declined the position. Emperor Wen summoned him to serve in the Prime Minister's Mansion and sent him as an envoy to Shu, which had just been pacified. Finding the people hungry and the land desolate, Liu Song petitioned for relief but granted aid without waiting for approval, resulting in his removal from office.

When Emperor Wu of the Jin Dynasty ascended, Liu Song was appointed Shangshu Sangonglang, presiding over laws and redressing injustices. He gradually rose to Vice Minister of Zhongshu. During the Xianning period, he inspected Jingyang with Sanqi Lang Bai Bao and was transferred to Huang Menlang, then to Yilang and acting Tingwei. He reviewed the case of Shi Huyin, who was imprisoned without guilt, and proved his innocence, earning comparison to Zhang Shizhi. After six years in office, he was known for thoroughness and fairness. When Wu was conquered and generals competed for merit, Liu Song investigated and determined Wang Hun had highest merit and Wang Jun middle merit. The emperor considered this unreasonable and demoted Liu Song to prefect of Jingzhao, then transferred him to Hanoi. Before departing, he reported matters benefiting the country, petitioning to demolish water dams that caused disasters, bringing convenience to the people.

After his mother's death, Liu Song left office. Following the mourning period, he became Prime Minister of Huainan, where he was serious and organized with great political achievements. He reformed the construction of Shaopi, which previously required tens of thousands of laborers annually, by having the old and young work together with compensation based on repairs, earning praise for fair administration. Liu Song submitted a lengthy memorial discussing various state affairs, including the establishment of feudal states, the importance of enfeoffing princes to create stable foundations, and the dangers of relying solely on county systems. He argued that enfeoffment would ensure long-term stability, drawing comparisons between the Zhou Dynasty's long duration due to feudal systems and the Qin Dynasty's rapid collapse after abolishing them.

Liu Song also discussed the need for proper official evaluation, reducing labor burdens, developing agriculture, and maintaining grain prices. He criticized current governance for focusing on minor faults while ignoring major crimes, comparing this to leaving dangerous beasts on the road while strictly prohibiting mice. He advocated for simpler governance, long-term official appointments, and proper assessment of achievements. The imperial edict replied acknowledging his loyal service and requesting continued reports.

In the early Yuankang period, Liu Song followed Sima Yun into court. When Yang Jun was executed, Liu Song guarded the palace and was appointed Minister of the Three Lords. He later transferred to the Ministry of Civil Affairs, establishing a nine-class system to reduce official turnover, but Jia Guo's specialized government prevented implementation. When Sima Lun killed Zhang Hua, Liu Song wept bitterly and rejoiced upon learning Zhang Hua's son had escaped. When Sun Xiu and others praised Sima Lun's achievements and proposed adding Jiuxi, Liu Song opposed it, citing historical precedents. Zhang Lin was furious but spared Liu Song due to Sun Xiu's advice. Liu Song soon died of illness, receiving imperial condolences and gifts.

Li Chong, courtesy name Maozeng, was born in Zhongwu, Jiangxia. His father, Li Jing, was governor of Qinzhou. Li Chong was studious and talented, becoming orphaned early and living with his younger brothers, known for his friendship. He discussed the nine-grade system, arguing it was a temporary military measure, not a long-term governance method. He advocated for simpler systems with fewer grades and long-term official positions. He opposed limiting slaves and property, arguing that without restoring the well-field system, such restrictions were impractical. He recommended promoting reclusive talents like Huo Yuan, emphasizing the importance of selecting virtuous people regardless of background. Li Chong served as Protector of Expeditions and Prefect of Pingyang, respecting moral education and promoting talents. After his brother's death, he resigned. He died of illness at age forty-eight, leaving his family poor with no place for his coffin.

The historical commentary praised Liu Song for his loyalty, extensive discussions on feudal states, and refusal to compromise with the king of Zhao. Li Chong was commended for his integrity, uprightness, and recommendation of virtuous recluses. Both were considered cornerstones of the country.
