This text provides a detailed account of the empresses, consorts, and dowagers of the Jin Dynasty, chronicling their lives, titles, deaths, and roles in court politics. It begins with Empress Xia Yuan Jing Yu, who died young before her husband became emperor, and was later posthumously honored. Her rival, Concubine Zhang, bore Emperor Ming but was hated by Empress Yu and neglected by Emperor Yuan; she was later honored by her son. Empress Ming Mu Yu, known for her virtue, served as regent for her young son, Emperor Cheng, during a rebellion, and died in distress. Empress Cheng Gong Du, from a virtuous family, died young without children, and her funeral was kept simple by imperial decree.

The text then covers Empress Kang Xian Chu, who served as regent for Emperor Mu, and later for Emperor Ai and Duke Haixi, including her role in the deposition of the latter. Empress Mu Zhang He, from a notable family, was captured during Huan Xuan's usurpation and later returned to the capital. Empress Ai Jing, originally a concubine, died childless. The deposed Emperor's Empress Xiao Yu was demoted after her husband's deposition. Empress Dowager Jian Wen Xuan Zheng, a widow who remarried Emperor Yuan, was posthumously honored by her son, Emperor Jian Wen, and later by Emperor Xiao Wu, despite debates over her proper sacrificial status.

Empress Jian Wen Shun Wang, who lost favor and was deposed with her son, was posthumously restored. Empress Dowager Xiao Wu Wen Li, of humble origin, was chosen by a physiognomist and bore Emperor Xiao Wu and others, eventually being honored as empress dowager. Empress Xiao Wu Ding Wang, chosen for her beauty and virtue, was jealous and died young. Empress Dowager An De Chen, a concubine who bore two emperors, was posthumously honored. Empress An Xi Wang, married as crown princess, died childless. Empress Gong Si Chu, demoted after her husband's abdication, died later.

The text concludes with a historian's commentary, praising virtuous empresses who aided the dynasty's rise, while condemning those like Empress Jia Nanfeng for causing decline. It notes the unusual practice of empress dowagers acting as regents, which was not an ancient custom, and reflects on the rise and fall of dynasties being tied to the influence of women.
