Wang Chen, courtesy name Chudao, was born in Jinyang, Taiyuan. His grandfather Wang Rou served as a lieutenant general of the Huns in the Han Dynasty, and his father Wang Ji was the prefect of Wei Dong County. Orphaned young, Wang Chen was adopted by his cousin Sikong Wang Chang, whom he served as a biological father, gaining fame for his filial piety toward his stepmother and widowed sister-in-law. He was well-read and skilled in writing. General Cao Shuang recruited him as a subordinate officer, and he gradually rose to minister under Zhongshu's family. After Cao Shuang's execution, Wang Chen was dismissed as an old official but later reappointed as censor of the book administration department and transferred to secretary supervisor. During the Zhengyuan period, he was promoted to Sanqi Changshi and Shizhong, overseeing writings. Together with Xunjie and Ruan Ji, he compiled the "Book of Wei," though he often concealed taboos and did not record events as truthfully as Chen Shou.

Duke Gaogui of Wei, eager to learn and talented, frequently invited Wang Chen and Pei Xiu to lectures and banquets in Shutang. When the Duke planned to attack Emperor Wen, he summoned Wang Chen and Wang Ye to inform them, but Wang Chen and Wang Ye quickly reported to Emperor Wen. For this, they were granted the title of Marquis of Anping with two thousand households. Wang Chen's disloyalty to his master drew public criticism. He was soon promoted to minister and sent to supervise military affairs in Yuzhou as General Fenwu and governor. Upon arrival, he issued a decree encouraging criticism and recommendations, offering rewards of grain for those who exposed corruption or proposed beneficial policies. His chief registrars, Chen Chang and Chu Bi, advised caution, fearing that rewards might deter honest people or attract greedy ones. Wang Chen initially insisted on his sincerity but eventually relented, following Chu Bi's advice. He then focused on good governance, implementing legal prohibitions established since Jia Kui and promoting education to reform declining customs.

Wang Chen was promoted to General Zhenglu and Commander-in-Chief of all military affairs north of the Yangtze River. When the fifth-class title system was established, he was granted the title of Marquis of Boling. During the pacification of Shu, Wu forces threatened the border, but Wang Chen defended well, causing the enemy to retreat. He was later transferred to General Zhennan. After Emperor Wu ascended the throne, Wang Chen became imperial censor and abided by the Shangshu order. Known for his talent and fame, he consulted with Yang Hu, Xun Xu, Pei Xiu, and Jia Chong on state affairs. For his role in the abdication, he was promoted to general of hussars and granted the title of Duke of Boling County, which he initially refused but later accepted with 1,800 households. He died in the second year of Taishi, and the emperor mourned him with lavish gifts. Posthumously, he was given the title of Sikong Gong. Wang Chen was always honest and frugal, leaving no property. His son Wang Jun succeeded him.

Wang Jun, courtesy name Peng Zu, was born to a poor mother from a common family. Initially despised by his father, he became heir at fifteen after Wang Chen's death. He served as Prince Consort and later held various military and civil posts. During the chaos of the court, he allied with Yi Di, marrying his daughters to Xianbei leaders for safety. After King Sima Lun of Zhao usurped the throne, Wang Jun led troops to wait and see. He later killed He Yan, who plotted against him, and occupied Youzhou. He built equipment and led 20,000 Hu and Jin troops against Sima Ying, defeating his general and capturing Yecheng, where his soldiers looted brutally. Wang Jun's power grew, and he was promoted to General Hussar and Commander-in-Chief of Hebei. During the Yongjia period, he fought Shi Le and Liu Kun, but his harsh rule led to defections. He planned to become emperor based on prophecies, killing those who advised against it. Shi Le pretended to surrender, and Wang Jun, trusting him, was captured and killed. Wang Jun had no sons, and his title passed to his nephew Daosu.

Xunjie, courtesy name Jingqian, was from Yingchuan and the sixth son of Wei Taiwei Xun You. Known for filial piety and extensive knowledge, he served as Zhonglang and later Sanqi Shilang. He taught the young emperor of Wei and was granted the title of Marquis of Guannei. He rescued Fu Gu from Cao Shuang's harm and advised Emperor Jing to declare benevolence after Gao Guixiang Gong's ascension. He participated in suppressing rebellions and was promoted to Marquis of Wansuiting. Under Emperor Wen, he became Shangshu and later Sikong. He was filial, resigning for his mother's funeral, and was praised nationwide. He formulated Jin etiquette and was promoted to Duke of Linhuai. He served as Crown Prince Tutor and Taiwei, settling music before dying in the tenth year of Taishi. The emperor mourned him greatly. Xunjie understood the "Three Rites" but lacked upright moral integrity, catering to Xun Xu and Jia Chong. He had no sons, and his title passed to his grandnephew Xun Hui.

Xun Xu, courtesy name Gongzeng, was from Yingyin, Yingchuan, and the great-grandson of Han Sikong Xun Shuang. Orphaned young, he was raised by his uncle. He was intelligent and wrote articles in his teens. He served under Cao Shuang and later became Zhongshu Tongshilang. After Cao Shuang's death, he alone attended the funeral, earning respect. He served as Anyang Order and later Tingweizheng, participating in Emperor Wen's military affairs. He advised against assassins and warned of Zhong Hui's rebellion. He wrote documents to Sun Hao of Wu, which were praised as better than a hundred thousand troops. After Emperor Wu's abdication, he was granted the title of Duke of Jibei County but refused, becoming a marquis. He served as Zhongshu Supervisor and jointly formulated laws with Jia Chong. He helped secure Jia Chong's daughter as crown prince's concubine, drawing criticism. He revised music rhythms and compiled classics. He opposed the attack on Wu but was overruled. He advised against sending princes to feudal states and discussed reducing officials. He praised the prince's character, leading to respect for He Jiao and disdain for him. He died in the tenth year of Taikang, with ten sons, including Xun Ji, Xun Fan, and Xun Zu. Xun Xu's descendants continued his legacy, with Xun Sui and Xun He crossing the river eastward during the chaos.

Feng You, courtesy name Shaozhou, was from Anping. He read extensively and was eloquent. He served as prefect of Wei County and later General Zuowei, favored by Emperor Wu. He befriended Jia Chong and Xun Xu, helping Jia Chong's daughter become dowager. He opposed the attack on Wu but later followed Wang Jun into Moling. He advised the emperor to send Qi King Sima You to his feudal state, leading to Sima You's death. He hated Zhang Hua and prevented his promotion. He died in the seventh year of Taikang, leaving two sons, Feng Bo and Feng Xiong. The historian comments that Wang Chen's disloyalty and Wang Jun's violence brought disaster, while Xunjie and Xun Xu, despite their talents, contributed to court strife. Feng You's flattery led to Sima You's death and Jia's rise, causing endless civil unrest.
