# Summary of Historical Records on Emperor Xuan's Sons and Descendants

Emperor Xuan had nine sons from various consorts. Queen Mu Zhang bore Emperor Jing, Emperor Wen, and Sima Qian (King of Pingyuan). Mrs. Fu gave birth to Sima Liang (King of Wencheng in Runan), Sima Nian (King of Langye Wu), Sima Jing (Marquis of Huiting in Qing Dynasty), and Sima Jun (King of Fufeng Wu). Mrs. Zhang bore Sima Xu (King of Liang), while Mrs. Bai bore Sima Lun (King of Zhao).

Sima Qian, King of Pingyuan, was granted the title of Anyang Tinghou in the Wei Dynasty and later became King of Pingyuan under Emperor Wu with 11,300 households. He suffered from severe illness and was allowed to remain in the capital rather than go to his feudal state. He held various official positions including Guanglu大夫 and served as a servant. Although a great feudal lord, he did not care for government affairs. His salary and goods were left to rot in the open air. He was known for eccentric behavior, such as keeping his concubines' coffins unsealed in empty rooms and opening them to look at them periodically. During the rebellion of King Zhao Sima Lun, Sima Qian was appointed as General Wei and later Taibao. When Sima Tong of Qi pacified Sima Lun, Sima Qian congratulated him with a hundred coins. He died in the fifth year of Yongjia at age eighty.

Sima Dian, King of Langye Wu, was talented and famous from an early age. He served as General Ningshuo guarding Yecheng and later as General Zhenshu guarding Xiapi, where he defended well against the Wu people. During the pacification of Wu, Sun Hao surrendered to Sima Dian, who sent troops across the Yangtze River, capturing enemy officials and securing the surrender of 50,000 to 60,000 people. His two sons were made marquises. He died in the fourth year of Taikang at age fifty-seven. His descendants included Sima Rui, who later became Emperor Yuan, and several others who successively held the title of King of Langye.

Sima Dan, King of Wulingzhuang, was jealous and harmful by nature with no filial character. He hated his brother Sima Yao and spoke ill of him to Sima Liang. During Sima Lun's rebellion, Sima Dan served as leading general. He killed two men while drunk and gave their heads to Sima Lun. His mother complained of his unfilial behavior, and he was moved to Liaosai with his family. He was later killed by Shi Le in the late Yongjia period.

Sima Yao, King of Dong'an, was resolute, authoritative, and learned, with filial piety in serving relatives. He was granted 20,000 households and held various positions. He was arbitrary in punishments and rewards, leading to his dismissal and exile to Daifang. He was later restored but killed by Sima Ying after the emperor's army was defeated.

Sima Jun, King of Fufeng Wu, was clever from childhood, able to write memorials and recite scriptures at age five or six. He was quiet, upright, and had the best reputation in the clan. He served as General of Zhenxi, guarding Guanzhong, and was skilled at appeasing rulers. He promoted agriculture and shared labor with soldiers. He suppressed Qiang rebellions and received surrenders from various tribes. He was filial to his mother and eager to learn, writing treatises on benevolence and filial piety. When he died, people wept for him and erected monuments.

Sima Xu, King of Liang, practiced quietness and was respectful and prudent. He served as General of Zhengxi guarding Guanzhong and later as Grand Administrator and Situ. During Sima Lun's usurpation, he served as prime minister. After his death, there was debate over his posthumous name, with some criticizing him for failing to take a stand during crises. He had no sons, and his title passed through various relatives.

Sima You, King of Qi Xian, was wise from youth, calm, kind, and fond of classical books. He was made heir to Emperor Jing and inherited the title of Wuyang Hou. Under Emperor Wu, he was made King of Qi and managed military affairs. He was known for his filial piety and moral conduct. He advised on agricultural policies and government administration. He was especially favored by Emperor Wen, who nearly made him crown prince. However, due to the machinations of Xun Xu and Feng Shou, Emperor Wu sent him to his feudal state. Sima You became ill from resentment and died at age thirty-six while traveling. His son Sima Tong succeeded him.
