Wei Guan, courtesy name Boyu, was from Anyi, Hedong. His ancestor Wei Hao was recruited for his Confucian learning during the Han Dynasty, and his father Wei Jian served as a minister. Orphaned at ten, Wei Guan was known for his filial piety, quiet temperament, and fairness. He inherited his father's marquis title and began his official career at twenty. He served in various positions, including as a regular attendant, and was known for his competence and legal expertise, judging cases based on reason.

During the conquest of Shu, Wei Guan supervised the armies of Deng Ai and Zhong Hui. Zhong Hui, harboring rebellious intentions, plotted to have Deng Ai kill Wei Guan. Wei Guan, aware of the danger, arrested Deng Ai at night by issuing a false imperial edict. After Zhong Hui rebelled and was killed, Wei Guan, fearing repercussions from his involvement with Zhong Hui, ordered the assassination of Deng Ai and his son. Despite his role, Wei Guan refused credit for the conquest, attributing it to the generals. He was later appointed to high military positions, including General of Zhenxi and Governor of Qingzhou, where he achieved political successes and pacified border tribes like the Wuhuan.

Wei Guan was a strict administrator and a scholar, skilled in cursive calligraphy alongside Suo Jing. He advocated for the abolition of the nine-rank system in favor of ancient rural selection, but his proposal was not implemented. He also attempted to advise Emperor Wu against appointing the simple-minded Crown Prince, but his hints were dismissed. This led to resentment from Empress Jia. Later, due to his son's marital issues and political conflicts, Wei Guan requested retirement and was granted the title of Taibao.

After Emperor Hui ascended, Wei Guan was restored to power but clashed with Prince Sima Zhang of Chu. Empress Jia, resenting Wei Guan's integrity, falsely accused him of plotting rebellion. Sima Zhang arrested Wei Guan and his family, executing them despite his subordinates' advice to resist. Wei Guan was 72. His grandsons Wei Cao and Wei Jie escaped. Du Yu had predicted Wei Guan's downfall due to his role in Deng Ai's death.

Wei Guan's son Wei Heng, a calligrapher, wrote extensively on the history of Chinese scripts, from ancient characters to seal, clerical, and cursive styles. He was also killed in the purge. Wei Guan's other son Wei Zhan served as an official and petitioned to abolish cruel interrogation methods. Wei Jie, Wei Guan's grandson, was a renowned beauty and philosopher, known for his eloquence and admired by contemporaries. He died young at 27.

Zhang Hua, courtesy name Maoxian, was from Fangcheng. Orphaned and poor, he herded sheep but was recognized for his talent. He was learned and wrote the "Ode to the Quail," which gained him fame. He served as a key advisor to Emperor Wu, supporting the conquest of Wu and overseeing logistics. After Wu's fall, he was rewarded but later fell out of favor due to his support for Prince Sima You. He was sent to Youzhou, where he pacified border tribes and received tribute from distant states.

Recalled to court, Zhang Hua became a central figure under Emperor Hui, stabilizing the government despite Empress Jia's tyranny. He opposed the deposition of the crown prince, arguing for caution, but was overruled. He also thwarted a coup by Prince Sima Zhang of Chu. However, his refusal to join Prince Sima Lun of Zhao's rebellion led to his arrest and execution at 69, along with his family. He was known for his vast knowledge, collecting rare books, and his expertise in astronomy and strange phenomena, such as identifying dragon meat and predicting chaos from a bird feather.

Zhang Hua's son Zhang Yi was also killed. His grandson Zhang Yu survived. Liu Bian, a loyal official, warned Zhang Hua of Empress Jia's plots but was not heeded. He later died by poison to avoid execution. The text concludes with a lament for Wei Guan and Zhang Hua, praising their loyalty but noting their tragic ends in a time of chaos.
