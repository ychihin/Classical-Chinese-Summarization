# Summary of Historical Records on Jin Dynasty Empresses and Concubines

The text begins with a philosophical discourse on the hierarchical relationship between emperor and empress, comparing it to the natural order of heaven and earth, yin and yang, sun and moon. It traces the evolution of imperial consort titles from ancient times through the Zhou dynasty, when the emperor established one queen, three wives, nine concubines, twenty-seven secondary wives, and eighty-one royal wives to manage the inner palace. The text emphasizes that virtuous empresses served as maternal role models who promoted imperial teachings and ensured dynastic prosperity, while those who abused their power brought disaster upon their houses.

The biographies of individual empresses and concubines follow. Empress Xuanmu Zhang, from Pinggao, Hanoi, was known for her wisdom and decisiveness. She once killed a maid who witnessed her husband feigning illness to prevent exposure of the deception. Despite being neglected when another concubine gained favor, she maintained her dignity. She died at fifty-nine during the Wei Zhengshi era and was posthumously honored after Emperor Wu accepted the throne.

Empress Jinghuai Xiahou, from Pei state, was elegant and knowledgeable, often planning ahead for Emperor Jing. However, as she was a niece of the Wei imperial family, Emperor Jing grew suspicious of her, and she died by poison at twenty-four. Empress Jingxian Yang, from Mount Tai, was intelligent and virtuous. After Emperor Jing married and divorced another woman, he married Yang, who bore no sons. She lived to sixty-five and was buried with Emperor Jing.

Queen Wenming Wang, from Tan county, demonstrated exceptional intelligence from childhood, reciting classics at eight and managing household affairs at a young age. Her grandfather lamented she was not born male. She married Emperor Wen, bore several children including Emperor Wu, and served her parents-in-law devotedly. She warned against trusting Zhong Hui, who later rebelled. As Empress Dowager, she lived simply, weaving her own cloth and wearing washed garments. She died at fifty-two and was buried with Emperor Wen.

Empress Wu Yuanyang, named Yan, from Huayin, was naturally beautiful, skilled in calligraphy and crafts. Emperor Wen selected her for his son. She bore Emperor Hui and other children. When Emperor Wu doubted the crown prince's capability, she insisted on primogeniture. She was jealous, selecting only fair-skinned, tall women for the harem while rejecting beautiful candidates. On her deathbed at thirty-seven, she recommended her cousin to succeed her.

Empress Wu Daoyang, cousin of Wujiang, was established as queen in the second year of Shengkong. She personally collected mulberries and raised silkworms. After Emperor Wu's death, she became Empress Dowager but was falsely accused of conspiracy by Empress Jia, deposed as a commoner, and died at thirty-four after a hunger strike. Her title was posthumously restored in the first year of Yongjia.

Concubine Zuo Fen, known for literary talent despite plain appearance, wrote the melancholy "Lisi Fu" expressing sorrow at separation from family. She composed eulogies for Empress Yuan Yang and Princess Wannian. Hu Guifei, named Fang, was selected in the ninth year of Taishi and became the most favored concubine, once scratching Emperor Wu's finger during a game. Mrs. Zhuge, named Zhuge Wan, entered the palace in the ninth year of Taishi.

Queen Huijia, named Nanfeng, was married to the crown prince despite Emperor Wu's reservations. She was jealous and cruel, killing several people with her own hands and causing a pregnant concubine to miscarry. After becoming empress, she grew increasingly dissolute, engaging in affairs with physicians and luring young men into the palace. She conspired against the crown prince and was eventually deposed and forced to commit suicide by King Zhao Sima Lun.

Queen Huiyang, named Xianrong, was established as queen in the first year of Tai'an but was deposed and restored multiple times amid political turmoil. After Emperor Hui's death, she attempted to install a different successor but failed. When Luoyang fell, she was taken by Liu Yao, who made her his queen. Mrs. Xie, named Xie Jiu, from a poor family, was selected as a talented person and sent to serve the prince, bearing Prince Minhuai. Empress Jia kept her isolated, and she was eventually killed.

The text concludes with brief accounts of Empress Dowager Huai, named Xie Tan, who died young and was posthumously honored, and the concubine of Marquis Yuanxia, named Guangji, who married into the Langye royal family and bore Emperor Yuan.
