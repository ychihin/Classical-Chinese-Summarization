Wei Shangshulang Yang Wei submitted a memorial stating that he had studied ancient texts and the role of calendar numbers in judgment. He noted that seasons organize agriculture and months organize personnel affairs, a practice with a long history. During Shaohao's reign, days were divided based on phenological judgments like swallows' return; under Zhuanxu and Emperor Xue, Chongli managed celestial matters. The Japanese official promulgated the calendar to princes, who then distributed it within their territories. In the Xia Dynasty, Xihe's drunkenness caused calendar disorder, recorded in the "Yin Zheng" section of the "Book." This shows that awareness of farming seasons and personnel matters has been consistent across ages.

By the Zhou Dynasty's decline and the Warring States period, the lunar calendar's sheep were discarded, rituals for heaven and earth ceased, leap months were set incorrectly, the first month's arrangement was out of season, and Mars was still in the west while stinging insects remained active. The emperor did not coordinate seasons, officials failed to record taboos, princes neglected duties, and Japanese officials did not calculate the new moon, abandoning farming seasons. Zhongni tried to correct chaos in "Spring and Autumn" through praise and criticism, recording improper leap months for ridicule. Until the Qin and Han dynasties, Mengdong remained the year's start, with leap months in the ninth month. Solar terms were disordered, seasons and months were wrong, correction values lagged behind celestial phenomena, and solar eclipses did not occur at the new moon. This persisted for years without change.

In the seventh year of Emperor Wu's Yuanfeng reign, the error was recognized, leading to a change in the first month's position and calendar data. Talented individuals created the Taichu Calendar, using Jianyin month as the first month and Huangzhong month as the calendar's start. However, differences were too great, and errors increased. In the second year of Chenghe, the "Four Points Calendar" was adopted and used until now. Solar eclipses often occurred on dark days due to too many points, making it imprecise. Yang Wei used his spare time to calculate celestial movements, examine previous classics, verify with solar eclipses and new moons, and establish a more precise calendar.

He proposed changing the first month's location and calendar data, using Daru month as the year's start and Jianzi month as the calendar's initial month. He noted that in the Zhuanxu era, the calendar was called "Zhuanxu," and in the Xuanyuan era, "Huangdi." When Emperor Xiaoyuan of Han reformed Zhengshuo, the changed Yuan was called Taichu, so the calendar was named "Taichu Calendar." Now that the Yuan has been changed to Jingchu, the calendar should be called "Jingchu Calendar." Yang Wei's "Jingchu Calendar" is concise, precise, efficient, and easy to understand. He claimed that even mental calculations, plans, sundial control, and observation of celestial phenomena could not surpass his calendar's subtlety.

Past calendars have been rough and imprecise, constantly changing since the Yellow Emperor. From Renchen Yuan to Ding Si, the first year of Jingchu, the accumulation was 4046 years. This epoch begins with the Huangzhong month of Tianzheng Jianzi as the calendar's start. In the first year of each epoch, the winter solstice occurs at midnight on Jiazishuo day. The text then provides detailed numerical data for the calendar, including Yuanfa (11,058), Discipline (1,843), Jiyue (22,795), Chapter age (19), Zhang Yue (235), Zhang Run (7), Total number (134,630), Japan and France (4,559), Remainder (9,670), Zhou Tian (673,150), Middle age (12), Qi method (12), No points (67,315), No way (967), Month and week (24,638), General method (47), Huitong (790,110), Synodic total (67,315), Transaction limit (722,795), Tongzhou (125,621), Sunday balance (2,528), Zhou Xu (2,031), and Score (455).

The text describes six periods (Jiazi, Jiaxu, Jiashen, Jiawu, Jiachen, Jiayin) with their respective intersection error rates and late illness rates. It explains the calculation method for these data, using accumulated moon of one period (22,795) multiplied by the number (134,630), continuously subtracting the number of Huitong (790,110). The result less than Huitong is the intersection difference. Adding the intersection difference rate of the previous period yields the rate for the later period. If the result is still less than Huitong, the moon is in the south of the ecliptic; if greater, subtract it, and the moon is in the north. The Jiji difference is three lettuce and one hundred and eighty, calculated similarly using accumulated moon multiplied by total number, subtracting total number (125,621), with the remainder less than 95,441 subtracted by total number to get the chronic period difference.

The text provides methods for deducing accumulated month, new moon, string and looking days, twenty-four qi and solar terms, leap months, and various limits for each month. It includes detailed calculations for the sun's and moon's positions, conjunction and lunar eclipse, and the moon's north-south position relative to the ecliptic. The method for finding the degree of deintersection and the specific amount of food loss (using fifteen as denominator) is also given. The azimuth angle for solar eclipse and waning depends on whether the moon is in the outer or inner path.

The text includes a table of monthly progress with profit and loss rates, profit and loss points, and monthly progress points for 27 days plus a Sunday. It describes the method for deducing the combination of lunar eclipse into the lunar calendar, calculating fixed points, and adding time. The five-star technique for Jupiter, Mars, Saturn, Venus, and Mercury is detailed, including their conjunctions, movements, and degrees. The text also discusses Liu Zhi's modification of the calendar, Du Yu's "Chunqiu Changli," and various historical calendars' accuracy in predicting solar eclipses in the Spring and Autumn period. It concludes with Jiang Qi's "Jiazi Yuanli of the Three Epochs" and his "Huntian Lun," which calculated the sun's movement based on the ecliptic.
