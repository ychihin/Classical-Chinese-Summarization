The text traces the history of music and ritual in China from ancient times to the Jin Dynasty, emphasizing their spiritual and emotional significance. It begins by noting that spirituality and emotion are expressed through chanting and physical movement, with the heart's essence called Tao and its reflection in images called Yong. Music is used to spread sound, praise peace, and mourn chaos. Ancient sages like Shennong and Fuxi created instruments such as harps, bells, and chimes to express the soul and imitate nature. The Yin Shang Dynasty saw a decline in elegant music due to obscene influences, leading to the loss of works like "Five Heroes" and "Six Stems." Confucius remarked that people can promote Tao, but Tao cannot promote people.

From the Zhou Dynasty onward, various rulers composed music, including the Yellow Emperor's "Yunmen," Yao's "Xianchi," and others. The text describes how music and ritual were used to educate and govern, with the "Shang Shu" and "Zhou Guan" referencing the teaching of music to noble children. However, during the reign of King Ping, etiquette was destroyed, and elegant music was lost. Yanling Jizi noted that the Zhou Dynasty's decline still retained the legacy of previous kings. As nations rose, they created their own music, leading to a gradual decline in the way of kings. The Qin Dynasty unified the world but destroyed all musical works and instruments. The Han Dynasty revived music and ritual under Emperors Gaozu, Wen, and Wu, though Confucianism was abandoned. Emperor Ming of the Han Dynasty further promoted rituals and music, establishing a pagoda market and performing ceremonies in the Mingtang.

The text details various types of music, including great music for the world, sacrificial music for ancestral temples, and ceremonial music for different occasions. It explains the five tones (palace, shang, jiao, zheng, yu) and their associations with virtues and emotions, as well as the eight sounds (stone, leather, bamboo, wood, silk, earth, metal) and their corresponding winds. The twelve pitches (six yang and six yin) are linked to the twelve months and their characteristics. After the Han Dynasty's chaos, music was scattered. Emperor Wu of Wei appointed Du Kui to create Yale, but new music later changed its charm. Emperor Wu of Jin initially based rituals on the Wei Dynasty, with Fu Xuan composing lyrics for various sacrifices, including songs for heaven and earth, the five suburbs, and ancestral temples.

The text includes numerous songs and poems used in rituals, such as those for the first day of the first lunar month, sacrifices to heaven and earth, and ancestral temple ceremonies. It describes the evolution of music under different rulers, including the adaptation of old melodies by Zuo Yannian and the creation of new songs by Xun Xu and Zhang Hua. Xun Xu developed new rhythmic flutes and adjusted the pitch, though Ruan Xian criticized the high tone as sorrowful. The text concludes with the history of the "Bayu Dance," which originated from the Banshun people and was adapted by Wang Can during the Wei Dynasty. In the Jin Dynasty, it was renamed "Xuanwu Dance" and later replaced by "Zhengde" and "Dayu" dances.
