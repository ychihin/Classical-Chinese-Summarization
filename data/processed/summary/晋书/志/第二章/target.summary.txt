The text describes celestial bodies and their movements as omens reflecting the state of human affairs, particularly the ruler's virtue and the kingdom's stability. The sun, as strong yang energy, symbolizes the king. Its brightness or dimness, color changes, and phenomena like sunspots, eclipses, or multiple suns indicate the ruler's virtue, ministerial loyalty, or impending disaster. A bright sun signifies a prosperous country; a dim or red sun without light warns of unvirtuous rulers and chaotic ministers. Solar eclipses represent yin (ministers) invading yang (the ruler), foretelling national ruin. The moon, as lunar essence and symbol of the heroine (queen or empress), reflects the conduct of ministers and women in power. Its abnormal color, brightness during the day, or multiple moons signify treachery, power struggles, war, and famine.

The text then details the five planets—Year Star (Jupiter), Sparkling Deluder (Mars), Quelling Star (Saturn), Grand White (Venus), and Chronogram (Mercury)—each associated with a season, element, virtue, and aspect of governance. Their positions, movements, colors, and conjunctions predict the rise and fall of states, agricultural yields, war, and the fate of rulers and ministers. For example, Jupiter governs benevolence and agriculture; its proper movement brings harvests, while deviation causes trouble. Mars governs propriety and war; its abnormal appearance signals military chaos, banditry, and death. Saturn represents integrity and the emperor; its movement indicates the ruler's virtue or lack thereof. Venus governs morality and speech; its advance and retreat predict war outcomes. Mercury governs wisdom and punishment; its untimely appearance brings famine and military revolt. Conjunctions of planets, such as Jupiter and Saturn meeting, foretell civil strife and famine, while Venus and Mars meeting indicate war and mourning.

The text also covers comets, guest stars, meteors, and various atmospheric phenomena (clouds, rainbows, halos) as omens. Comets, or "broom stars," signify sweeping away the old and ushering in the new, often bringing war and floods. Specific comets like the "Red You Flag" or "Heavenly Spear" portend rebellion, military defeat, or the death of a ruler. Guest stars like the "Old Man Star" or "Zhou Bo Star" indicate famine or prosperity. Meteors, as "angels," foretell events based on their size, speed, and direction; their fall marks war or the death of a general. Atmospheric qi, such as the "Emperor's Qi" (red inside, yellow outside) or the "Qi of a Fierce General" (like a dragon or beast), signal the rise of a king or a military leader. Cloud formations, like "formation clouds" or "hook clouds," predict war, and their colors (white for mourning, red for war, etc.) determine the nature of the event.

The text concludes with historical records of celestial events and their supposed fulfillments, such as solar eclipses, sunspots, and planetary conjunctions, linked to specific dynastic events like usurpations, deaths of emperors, and military campaigns. For instance, a solar eclipse in the second year of Emperor Hui's Yuankang reign was interpreted as a sign of the empress's exclusive power. A white rainbow piercing the sun in the first year of Yongchang was seen as a precursor to Wang Dun's rebellion. The gathering of five stars in a constellation was considered a sign of a change in dynasty, as when the stars gathered in Dongjing before Liu Yu's conquest of Guanzhong and the subsequent transition from Jin to Song. The text emphasizes that these celestial signs are warnings from Heaven, urging rulers to cultivate virtue and correct their governance to avert disaster.
