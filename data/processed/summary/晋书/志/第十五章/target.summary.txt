The text provides a detailed historical account of chariots, clothing, and ceremonial regalia from ancient times through the Jin Dynasty, emphasizing their symbolic and functional roles in governance, ritual, and social hierarchy. It begins by noting that since antiquity, gods and ancestors used specific vehicles and attire, such as the Yellow Emperor’s black top and light red lower clothes, and Fang Xun’s vermilion chariot with white horses, aligning with calendrical systems. The "Moon Order" describes women dyeing satin in regulated shades, while flags bearing sun and moon images and jade-adorned garments were meant to inspire awe and suppress evil. The text cites classical texts like "Shangshu" and "Book of Rites" to justify rewarding merit with chariots and clothing, and notes that saints derived designs from nature, such as birds and plants, for garments and wheels.

The narrative traces the evolution of these systems through dynasties. During the Zhou and Yin periods, creative designs emerged, but later, as ritual order declined, princes violated norms, leading to confusion like purple garments in Qi and long tassels in Zou. Confucius advocated for clothing matching status. The Qin Dynasty unified practices, collecting furs and decorating chariots, but burned texts and simplified regalia. Emperor Gaozu followed Qin systems, while Emperor Shizong expanded ceremonies with grand processions. However, later rulers like Emperor Sheng favored concubines, and the Wang family’s dominance led to war and destruction of artifacts. After the Chimei Rebellion, Emperor Ming restored costumes based on "Zhou Guan" and "Book of Rites," and Emperor Wei Ming reduced lavish furs. Gao Tanglong advised changing Zhengshuo to align with gods, leading to yellow as the dominant color in the Jingchu era.

The text details specific chariot types, such as the "five chariots" (jade, gold, elephant, leather, wood), each with distinct decorations and uses. The jade chariot, most noble, featured a Taichang flag with twelve strands for sky worship. The golden chariot had nine strands for guests, the elephant chariot was plain red for court, the leather chariot was for military campaigns, and the wood chariot for hunting. Chariots were drawn by horses with elaborate trappings, including gold ornaments, bells, and jade fittings. The emperor’s ceremonial procession included various vehicles like the Sinan chariot (a compass guide), drum cart (measuring distance), and sheep cart, each with specific functions. The text also describes the Five Oxen Flag, plowing cart, and hunting cart, noting changes in color and flags under different rulers, such as Wei Jingchu’s shift to yellow and white.

The account continues with the emperor’s travel sequence, listing dozens of vehicles and officials in a strict order, from the elephant chariot and drummers to the golden root chariot and imperial guards. This procession included multiple ranks of officials, cavalry, and ceremonial items like flags, halberds, and drums. The text notes that after the Liao Dynasty, many old regulations were lost, and by the Yuantong era, only a few chariots remained, with temporary decorations for sacrifices. The compass and other chariots were rediscovered later, such as Liu Yu finding the Sinan chariot in the 13th year of Yixi. The crown prince’s chariot and the empress dowager’s golden root chariot are also described, with specific rules for women’s vehicles, such as the queen’s mica chariot for silkworm worship.

The second half focuses on clothing and headwear. Different occasions required specific garments, such as a soap-colored robe for ancestral sacrifices or a crimson robe for court. Crowns varied by rank: the Tongtian Crown for the emperor, Pingmian for princes, and Jinxian Crown for scholars, with beams indicating status (e.g., three beams for princes, one beam for low officials). Other crowns like the Wu Guan (martial), Alpine Crown (for envoys), and Dharma Crown (for law officials) had distinct shapes and origins, often linked to historical figures or legends. The text also describes caps, scarves, and hats, noting that the clam (a type of headgear) evolved from ancient practices and was used by all classes.

The text concludes with accessories and women’s attire. Swords were once universal but later limited to court, with wooden swords replacing metal. The emperor’s six seals, including the legendary Imperial Seal, were passed down, though the sword of the white snake was lost. Belts, pouches, and wats (tablets) were used for official duties, with specific colors and materials for ranks. Women’s clothing, such as the queen’s soap-colored top for temple visits or cyan for weddings, included elaborate headdresses with golden flowers, jade ornaments, and step decorations. Noble ladies and concubines had graded seals, ribbons, and jade, with specific attire for silkworm ceremonies. The text emphasizes that these systems were meant to reflect virtue and status, but many were lost or simplified over time, especially after the Eastern Jin Dynasty.
