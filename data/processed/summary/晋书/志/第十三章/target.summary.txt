The Yongjia Rebellion caused the collapse of the Jin state, destroying all Lingguan and musical instruments. When the ancestral temple was first established in Jiangdong, the Shangshu ordered Taichang to submit the names of sacrificial music. Taichang He Xun replied that Wei had modified Han music, but the name of Jin music differed from before. After the disaster, old scriptures were lost. However, music could be harmonized with bells, expressed in text with five tones, added with lyrics for singing, and presented with dance. The palace hung instruments in the hall, with eight-tone instruments playing in sequence, elegant music ensemble, and singing performed below, all in line with the old Zhou system. Since the Mo Dynasty, new words were composed based on this music. The old capital was deserted, and music was lost, with twists and turns unrecognizable.

Due to a lack of elegant music instruments and actors, there was no Taiyue and drumming order. Later, songs about climbing were collected, but the joy of eating and drinking was incomplete. In the last years of Taining, Emperor Ming ordered Ruan Fu and others to supplement it. During the Xianhe period, Emperor Cheng reinstated the Taiyue Official to collect lost music, but it still lacked gold and stone music. When Yu Liang was in Jingzhou, he arranged elegant music with Xie Shang, but Yu Liang died before completion. Yu Yi and Huan Wen were engaged in war, and musical instruments rotted in the storehouse. When Murong Jun pacified Ran Min, many followers sought refuge. In the eleventh year of Yonghe, Xie Shang guarded Shouyang, searched for music masters to serve as Taiyue Mansion, and made stone chimes, so Yayue was slightly complete. However, the music gained by Wang Mengzi Ye and the Murong family flowed into Guanxi. During the Taiyuan period, Fu Jian was defeated, and musicians like Yang Shu were captured, becoming proficient in old music, and the four-chamber gold and stone music began to be complete. So Cao Pi, Wang Shi, and others were ordered to write more lyrics for the ancestral temple, but there was no longer any music for sacrifices in the surrounding suburbs.

The text lists songs for various emperors, including Emperor Xuan, Emperor Jing, Emperor Wen, Emperor Wu, Emperor Yuan, Emperor Ming of Song, Emperor Cheng, Emperor Kang, Emperor Mu, Emperor Ai, Emperor Jianwen, and Emperor Xiaowu, all composed by Cao Pi or the king who died. These songs praise the emperors' virtues, achievements, and divine favor, often referencing their roles in stabilizing the state, defeating enemies, and promoting education and harmony. The songs also describe rituals, music, and the joy of the gods.

In the Han Dynasty, there was the music of "Short Xiao and Chong Song," with songs like "Ibis," "Si Bei Weng," and others listed in drum music, mostly describing battle formations. In the Wei Dynasty, the names of twelve songs were changed, and Miao Xi composed lyrics to describe the merits and virtues of the Wei Dynasty. For example, "Ibis" was changed to "Chu Zhiping," describing the Wei Dynasty, and "Si Bei Weng" was changed to "Zhan Rongyang," describing Cao Gong. Similarly, other songs were changed to describe specific events, such as "Capture Lu Bu," "Conquering Guandu," and "Slaughter of Liucheng." The rest had the same old names. At this time, Cheng also ordered Wei Zhao to compose twelve song names to describe his merits and virtues, changing songs like "Ibis" to "Yan Jing Que," describing Sun Jian's ambitions.

When Emperor Wu was ordered to ascend the throne, he ordered Fu Xuan to compose twenty-two poems, describing the merits of Jin in replacing Wei. These poems changed song names like "Ibis" to "Ling Zhixiang," describing Emperor Xuan's assistance to Wei, and "Si Bei Weng" to "Xuan Shou Ming," describing Emperor Xuan's resistance to Zhuge Liang. Other songs were changed to describe events like "Conquering Liaodong," "Xuan Fu Zheng," and "The Times Are Troubled." The poems list the lyrics for each song, praising the emperors' virtues and achievements.

There are five old songs: "There is a Wise Woman in Guandong," "In the Second Year of Zhanghe," "The Music Will Last for a Long Time," "Emperor from Four Directions," and "The Osmanthus Tree Grows in Front of the Palace," all lost. Cao Zhi's "Preface to the Poems on the Chao Dance" mentions that Li Jian, skilled in Chao dance, was summoned by the late emperor. Li Jian, over seventy, wrote five new songs based on old ones. At the beginning of Taishi, another speech was made. The dance was performed with two yi, but Huan Xuan increased it to eight yi. The poems from Taishi include "Hong Ye Pian," "The Destiny Pian," "Jinghuang Pian," "Da Jin Pian," and "Ming Jun Pian," each equivalent to Wei or ancient songs.

The text also describes various dances and songs, such as the "White Dove" dance, "Ji Ji Chapter," "Dulu Chapter," "Jieshi Chapter," "Viewing the Sea," "Winter October," "Different Soils," "The Turtle Lives Longevity," and "The King of Huainan." These songs and dances are associated with specific historical figures and events, often praising virtues and achievements.

Drum and horn horizontal blowing music is discussed, with the drum used to promote military affairs and the horn to ward off enemies. The music evolved from Hu music, with Zhang Bowang transmitting playing skills to Xijing. During the Wei and Jin Dynasties, only ten songs were used, such as "Huang Hu" and "Longtou." The Song of Harmony is an old song from the Mo Dynasty, with silk and bamboo overlapping to harmonize. Originally one part, Emperor Wei Ming divided it into two parts. There are four Danges from the Han Dynasty, with no string accompaniment, and the lead singer sings first. Since the Jin Dynasty, this music has been extinct.

Wu songs and miscellaneous songs come from south of the Yangtze River, with songs like "Midnight Song," "Phoenix General's Young Song," and "Qianxi Song" composed by various individuals. Cup Loop Dance and "Gong Mo Wu" are also described, with the latter associated with Xiang Zhuang and Xiang Bo. Bai Yu Wu and other dances are mentioned, with lyrics and performances presented at New Year's Day gatherings. In the seventh year of Emperor Cheng's reign, Shangshu Cai Mo wrote about the use of music, with debates on whether to play bells and drums. Eventually, some acrobatic music was abolished, but later restored.
