Based strictly on the provided text, here is a summary of the historical records concerning the provinces of Qingzhou, Xuzhou, Jingzhou, Yangzhou, Jiaozhou, and Guangzhou.

**Qingzhou** is recorded in the "Yu Gong" as one of twelve pastoralist areas between Shuhai and Mount Tai. It was named for its eastern polar location and green color. Emperor Wu of Han established it as one of thirteen states, a designation that continued through the Jin Dynasty. The province governed six counties, 37 counties, and 53,000 households. Its history includes periods under the Shi family, occupation by Duan Chong, and control by Murong Ke and Fu's forces. After the Yongjia Rebellion, the imperial court established Youzhou here. Later, Murong De made it the capital of Nanyan, and Liu Yu eventually eliminated Murong Chao, leaving Chang Shi Yangmuzhi as governor. After the Yuan Emperor crossed the Yangtze, Qingzhou was established in Guangling, and later a Northern Qingzhou was created, with the overseas Chinese establishing a Southern Qingzhou. Eventually, Southern Qingzhou was abolished, and Northern Qingzhou was simply called Qingzhou.

**Xuzhou**, also from the "Yu Gong," was one of the twelve pastoralist areas. Its name is said to mean "soothing." The Han Dynasty divided Shuhai County and established various commanderies and states here. When the Thirteen Prefectures were established, Xuzhou governed Chu State and four counties. The whole state governed seven counties, 61 counties, and 81,021 households. During the Yongjia Rebellion, parts of Xuzhou fell to the Shi family. After Emperor Yuan crossed the river, Xuzhou only gained half its territory, leading to the establishment of overseas counties. Emperor Ming also established several southern commanderies. During the reign of Emperor Mu, seven counties in the South East China Sea were assigned to Jingkou. In the seventh year of Yixi, Huaibei was divided into North Xuzhou, and Huainan was only called Xuzhou.

**Jingzhou**, recorded in the "Yu Gong," was one of the twelve pastoral areas. Its name means "strong" or "police," referring to the spirit of the region or the need to guard against southern barbarians. During the Six Kingdoms period, it was Chu territory. The Qin and Han Dynasties established various commanderies here. When the Thirteen Prefectures were established, Jingzhou governed seven counties. After the Battle of Chibi, Wu and Shu divided Jingzhou, leading to a northern and southern Jingzhou. After the Jin pacified Wu, the whole state governed 22 counties, 169 counties, and 357,548 households. Emperor Hui divided some counties into Jiangzhou and Liangzhou. Emperor Huai divided nine counties into Xiangzhou. After Emperor Yuan crossed the river, he established new counties. During the reign of Emperor Mu, Lingling was divided into Yingyang County. In the thirteenth year of Yixi, Xiangzhou was abolished and its counties were assigned to Jingzhou.

**Yangzhou**, from the "Yu Gong," was one of the twelve pastoral areas. Its name is believed to refer to the restless energy of Jiangnan or the strong water waves. In ancient times, it was the most remote region from Wang Ji. After the Qin unified the world, it established three counties. The Han Dynasty created various kingdoms and commanderies. When the Thirteen Prefectures were established, Yangzhou governed six counties. After the Jin pacified Wu, the whole state governed 18 counties, 173 counties, and 311,400 households. Emperor Hui divided parts of Yangzhou and Jingzhou to establish Jiangzhou. After Emperor Yuan crossed the river and established his capital in Yangzhou, the Si, Ji, Yong, Liang, Qing, Bing, Yan, Yu, You, and Ping prefectures were all occupied. During the war and division, people were sent south to cross the Yangtze River and established overseas counties.

**Jiaozhou** is recorded as the Yangzhou area, the land of South Vietnam. After Qin Shihuang pacified Yangyue, he set up three counties. In the early Han Dynasty, the three counties of Lingnan were granted to Wu Rui. Emperor Wu of Han later conquered the area and set it as seven counties. During the Yuanfeng period, Dan'er and Zhuya counties were established. Emperor Shun established the area as a state. The province governs seven counties, 53 counties, and 25,600 households. The text lists the commanderies of Hepu, Jiaozhi, Xinchang, Wuping, Jiuzhen, Jiude, and Rinan.

**Guangzhou** is recorded as the Yangzhou area, the land occupied by Zhao in the late Qin Dynasty. By the time of Emperor Wu of Han, it was designated as Jiaotou County. In the fifth year of Wu Huangwu, four counties were separated from Jiaozhou and established as Guangzhou. During the Taikang period, three counties from Jingzhou were assigned to Guangzhou. It governs ten counties, 68 counties, and 43,120 households. The text lists the commanderies of Nanhai, Linhe, Shi'an, Shixing, Cangwu, Yulin, Guilin, Gaoliang, Leyi, and Ningpu.
