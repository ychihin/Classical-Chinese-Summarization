The emperor's virtue must align with heaven and earth, harmonizing yin and yang, as signs of disaster or auspiciousness appear through induction. The "Shangshu" states that benevolence brings good fortune, while evil brings misfortune, like a shadow following form. In antiquity, the Fuyi family received the "River Map" to create the Bagua, and Yu was given the "Luo Shu" during flood control, forming the "Hong Fan." Sages who follow this path are blessed by God. From the Three Sovereigns and Five Emperors onward, officials managed affairs. In the Yin Dynasty, Jizi served as a teacher, and after the Zhou replaced Yin, King Wu consulted Jizi, who taught him from the "Luoshu." Thus, the "He Yuan" and "Luo Shu" are intertwined, as are the Bagua and Jiu Zhang. When Yin's Taoism declined, King Wen deduced the Book of Changes; when Zhou's Taoism waned, Confucius wrote the Spring and Autumn Annals. By respecting yin and yang and imitating Hong Fan's disasters and auspiciousness, the correspondence between heaven and man becomes clear.

The Han Dynasty revived scholarship after the Qin. Under Emperor Wen, Liu Sheng created the "Da Zhuan," discussing the five elements. Later, Dong Zhongshu studied "Gongyang Chunqiu," deducing yin and yang, becoming a Confucian master. During Emperor Xuan and Yuan's reigns, Liu Xue studied "Gu Liang Chun Qiu," calculating fortunes and writing annotations for "Hong Fan," differing from Dong Zhongshu. Su Ta studied "Zuo Shi Zhuan," discussing the "Spring and Autumn" and Five Elements, but with errors. Ban Gu compiled the "Five Elements Chronicles," incorporating comments from Dong Zhongshu, Liu Xiang, and Liu Xin, along with records from others, ending with Wang Mang, to provide evidence for the Spring and Autumn Annals. Generally, there are three methods: if the king governs according to the Tao, harmony and auspicious signs appear; if he disobeys, ominous omens and disasters occur; when emperors and ministers see disasters and reflect, disasters can be eliminated.
