Among the five types of etiquette, the third pertains to guest rituals, including meetings with emperors and gatherings of princes. Since the Zhou Dynasty, the etiquette system for sending envoys became increasingly complex. After the Qin Dynasty's book burning and harassment of Confucian scholars, laws and regulations from past dynasties were incomplete. With the rise of the Han Dynasty, Shusun Tong began formulating etiquette, referencing previous generations but often making changes. A formal ceremony was held on the first day of the first lunar month, where bells rang to accept gifts, dukes and princes stood with offerings, and officials shouted long live before music and feasting. Emperor Wu of Wei held a similar ceremony in Wenchang Hall, using Han Dynasty etiquette and setting up hundreds of lanterns. The Jin Dynasty revised this ceremony, as documented in the "Xianning Notes" and Fu Xuan's "Yuan Hui Li," which integrated rituals from Xia, Yin, Shang, Zhou, Qin, and Han dynasties.

The "Xianning Note" details the ceremony: on the day before the first day of the first lunar month, officials gathered, torches were lit, and they entered the court to congratulate the queen. At less than seven quarters of the night, officials stood in order, and the emperor arrived with bells and drums. Officials knelt and worshipped, and the emperor ascended the throne. The master of ceremonies sang praises, and vassal kings presented jade, bowing twice. The emperor stood and bowed, then sat and bowed again. After the ceremony, the king returned to his seat. The emperor then invited the Taiwei and others, who presented jade, fur, silk, and animals, bowing twice. The ceremony continued with gifts distributed, music played, and the emperor entering a chariot. Guests from various ethnic groups entered, bowed, and sat. The visitor guided the king to pour longevity wine, and music played as ministers bowed. After drinking, they bowed again. The ceremony included distributing wine, playing songs, and serving meals. After the banquet, the ministers left the palace.

The Yangtze River region was unsettled, so morning greetings ceased. The crown prince met with descendants of previous dynasties and nobles. During New Year's gatherings, a white tiger bottle was set up for those who dared to speak out. Under the Wei Dynasty, vassal kings could not meet the emperor, except during Emperor Wei Ming's reign. During the Taishi period, officials reported that princes and nobles should pay court visits every three years, with temporary meetings allowed. The etiquette for court meetings involved holding jade, similar to the old system. In years without court visits, ministers were sent to pay respects. The emperor approved this. However, princes east of the Yangtze River did not return to their own countries, and some accepted appointments elsewhere, rendering this etiquette invalid.

In the Han Dynasty, October was tentatively set as the beginning of the year due to Emperor Gao's pacification of Qin. Emperor Wu later adopted the first month as the beginning of the year but still held banquets on the first day of October and the first day of each month. The banquet etiquette involved receiving congratulations and gifts at night, with princes presenting jade and officials presenting animals. The third prince offered jade to the throne, and the emperor stood for them. Officials congratulated, and those with high salaries shouted long live and raised glasses. Music played during meals, similar to the first day of the first lunar month. Wei and Jin held small court gatherings on the winter solstice, second in scale to New Year's Day.

Ancient emperors patrolled their territories, but during Emperor Wei Wen's reign, border wars prevented this. Emperor Ming visited the east three times, providing relief to the poor and rewarding the elderly. In the first year of his reign, he visited Luoyang County and gave rewards. In the fourth year of Emperor Wu's reign, he issued an edict to officials, emphasizing the importance of inspections and sending envoys to investigate political affairs and the people's sufferings. The new etiquette for patrolling mountains, offering sacrifices, and reporting on palace construction remained the same as in the past. When princes met the emperor, they set up flags to distinguish their ranks, as per the ancient ritual. However, this etiquette was not implemented until the end of the Jin Dynasty.

The ritual system included sacrifices to heaven and earth. The emperor patrolled famous mountains, burned firewood to worship heaven, and reported success. During the Taihe period of Emperor Wei Ming, Jiang Ji reported that the emperor's grand ceremonies prioritized patrols and sacrifices. He argued that since ancient times, dynastic changes involved climbing Mount Tai and writing down names. He urged the emperor to perform the sealing ceremony, but the emperor declined, citing humility and the need to unify the world. Later, Emperor Wu pacified Wu and unified the country. Officials urged him to perform the sealing ceremony, but he repeatedly declined, citing the need for peace and stability. After several memorials, the emperor finally refused, stating that the time was not right.

Emperor Ai wanted to honor Princess Zhang, but officials debated the etiquette. Some argued that a son should not knight his father, while others cited historical precedents. The discussion concluded that the emperor should not confer titles on his mother, as it would violate the order of human relations. The emperor eventually issued an edict to worship the imperial concubine, granting her the same status as the Queen Mother. Further debates arose about the etiquette for the crown prince, with some arguing that he should call himself a minister, while others disagreed. The emperor ultimately decided to follow the old system.

The fourth type of etiquette is military, used to achieve peace abroad and tranquility at home. In the Han Dynasty, military exercises were held on the day of the Beginning of Autumn, with the emperor personally shooting sacrifices. The ceremony included training in battle formations and beheading sacrifices. In the 21st year of Emperor Xian's reign, Wei officials proposed a military parade in autumn, which was approved. During the Wei Dynasty, military parades were held, but after Emperor Hui, this etiquette was abandoned. Later, local governors conducted military parades, but the imperial court no longer did so.

The fifth type of etiquette is Jia, covering banquets, crown ceremonies, and weddings. At the end of the Zhou Dynasty, these rituals were rarely practiced. The crown ceremony for emperors was not recorded in the "Zhou Rites," but it was performed for princes. The emperor's crown ceremony involved three crowns, while the crown prince was crowned twice. The ceremony was held in the ancestral temple, and the emperor personally attended. The wedding etiquette for emperors included gifts of black coins, silk, and horses. The ceremony involved sending envoys to marry the queen, with various rituals such as asking for names, accepting gifts, and personal greetings. The emperor's wedding did not include music or congratulations, as per the rites. The crown prince's wedding also followed specific protocols, with gifts and ceremonies similar to those of the emperor.
