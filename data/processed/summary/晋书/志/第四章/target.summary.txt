# Summary of Historical Text on Chinese Territorial Administration

The text begins by describing ancient times when primitive people lived in caves and earthen chambers, eating raw food without clothing. It then traces the development of civilization through legendary figures: Suirenshi who drilled wood for fire, Paoxishi who created the eight trigrams, Nüwa who repaired the heavens, and Shennong who developed agriculture and divided territories. The Yellow Emperor traveled extensively, reaching the eastern seaside, the Yangtze River in the south, and Mount Daiyue. Zhuanxu ensured everyone's needs were met, while the Gaoxin family followed heavenly ways. Emperor Yao's reign saw Dayu control floods and divide the land into nine provinces. Yu Shun formulated calendars, divided star districts, and established provinces including Bingzhou, Youzhou, and Yingzhou.

The text details the evolution of administrative divisions through successive dynasties. During the Xia and Shang dynasties, territorial systems were established and maintained. King Wu of Zhou moved the capital from Feng to Haojing. Under King Cheng of Zhou, the "Yu Gong" was revised, merging some provinces and dividing others. Officials were appointed to manage lands and observe astronomy, distinguishing the nine provinces with their corresponding star regions. The southeast was Yangzhou, due south was Jingzhou, Henan was Yuzhou, due east was Qingzhou, east of Hedong was Yanzhou, due west was Yongzhou, northeast was Youzhou, north of the Yellow River was Jizhou, and due north was Bingzhou.

When Qin Shihuang unified the world, he abolished the feudal princes and divided the empire into thirty-six counties, including Sanchuan, Hedong, Nanyang, Nanjun, Jiujiang, and others. The Qin territory extended to Dianfeng in the west, the desert in the north, and the sea in the east. After the Qin dynasty fell, the Han dynasty established new administrative systems. Emperor Gaozu of Han added twenty-three counties, while Emperor Wu established seventeen counties after conquering Vietnam and the Hu lands, and six more after pacifying the southwestern barbarians. By the second year of Emperor Ping's Yuanshi period, there were 111 counties total, divided into thirteen divisions including Liang, Yi, Jing, Yang, Qing, Yu, Yan, Xu, You, Bing, Ji, Jiaozhi, and Shuofang.

The text describes how later dynasties continued to modify territorial divisions. During the Later Han, Emperor Guangwu merged eight counties due to population decline. Subsequent emperors added or changed various counties. During the Three Kingdoms period, Wei, Shu, and Wu each established new counties. By the first year of Taikang under Emperor Wu of Jin, after pacifying the Wu state, twenty-three additional counties were established, bringing the total to 173 counties across nineteen provinces. The text notes that this represented a great civilized country owning all territories of Yin and Zhou.

The document provides extensive detail on the geographical measurements and cosmological understanding of the ancient world. It describes the eight poles (Baji) as being 203,1300 miles from east to west and the same from north to south. The distance from earth to heaven was half the width of the eight poles. The text explains that there are seven stars in the Big Dipper and seven layers of earth, with four corners in the sky and four great rivers flowing eastward into the sea. The terrain becomes lower in the southeast while the sky tilts toward the northwest.

The text extensively describes the feudal system established by various dynasties. Under the Xia dynasty, the territory was divided into five service zones (five fu) extending 5000 miles in radius. The system included Dianfu (imperial domain), Houfu (marquis domain), Suifu (submission domain), Yaofu (essential domain), and Huangfu (wilderness domain), each with specific tax and service requirements. The Zhou dynasty established five ranks of nobility—Duke, Marquis, Earl, Viscount, and Baron—with specific land allocations. The emperor's field was 1000 li in circumference, while dukes received 100 li, earls 70 li, and viscounts 50 li. The text notes that during the Zhou dynasty's peak, there were 13,714,933 people and no crimes were committed.

The document traces the decline of the Zhou royal family and the rise of the Spring and Autumn period, when 36 monarchs were killed and 52 countries perished over 242 years. It lists 139 known countries including Lu, Zhu, Zheng, Song, Ji, Wei, and others, plus 31 countries that disappeared. The five hegemons emerged, followed by the seven warring states of Han, Wei, Zhao, Yan, Qi, Qin, and Chu. The text describes the well-field system in detail, where land was divided into nine squares of 100 mu each, with eight families jointly cultivating the central public field.

The text concludes by describing the administrative systems of the Qin and Han dynasties. After Qin Shihuang abolished the five-rank nobility system, the Han dynasty established second-class titles, granting fiefs to meritorious officials. By Emperor Ping's reign, there were 12,233,062 households and 59,594,978 people, with territory extending 9,302 miles east to west and 13,368 miles north to south. The document details the system of pavilions every ten miles, townships every ten pavilions, and the roles of village elders and other officials. It notes that by the third year of a later reign, there were 10,677,960 households with 56,486,856 people, representing an era of increasing population.
