This allowed sages to review the past, predict the future, and align human affairs with natural laws. The *Book of Changes* states that Heaven reveals signs of good and bad luck through celestial phenomena, which sages imitate. Similarly, the *Shangshu* emphasizes that Heaven's wisdom is reflected in the people's understanding. The quality of governance is linked to the harmony of people's sentiments and celestial movements. The three emperors were diligent, and as a result, the sun, moon, and stars followed their proper courses without anomalies. Huangdi studied the "River Map" to understand good and bad luck, and his "Star Biography" survives. Later rulers appointed officials to oversee astronomy and geography, but many ancient texts were lost over time.

During the Yin Shang and Zhou dynasties, various historians and astronomers, such as Wu Xian, Shi Yi, and others from different states, were responsible for interpreting celestial signs. Their theories, particularly those of Wu Xian, Gan De, and Shi Shenfu, were highly regarded. Despite Qin Shihuang's book burning, astronomical texts were preserved. The text then introduces three ancient cosmological theories: the Gaitian (hemispherical sky) theory, the Xuanye (infinite void) theory, and the Huntian (celestial sphere) theory. Cai Yong favored the Huntian theory, which was used in the armillary sphere observatory. The Gaitian theory, described in the "Zhou Bi," posits a round sky like an umbrella and a square earth like a chessboard, with the sun and moon moving in relation to the celestial body. The Xuanye theory, promoted by Ximeng, suggests the sky is a boundless void with stars floating freely, their movements governed by atmospheric forces.
