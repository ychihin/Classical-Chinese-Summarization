In the past, sages used the xuanji to simulate the North Star's movement, observing the sky to measure the sun's shadow and the motions of the sun, moon, and five planets. They divided celestial areas on earth, established calendars, taught agricultural seasons, and promoted prosperity, linking yin and yang harmony with governance. Observing celestial phenomena to form hexagrams and adding leap months to describe seasonal changes became the essence of calendrical science. Emperor Yan distinguished eight seasons for agriculture, Xuanyuan established principles for writing, and appointed Xihe to count the sun, Chang Yi to track the moon, and astrological Qi in specific regions. Rong Cheng synthesized these methods, examined solar terms and celestial phenomena, established the five elements, studied solar and lunar movements, and wrote "Adjusting the Calendar." During the Shaowu period, Fengniao managed the calendar; in Zhuanxu's time, Nanzheng oversaw astronomy; in the Tao and Tang Dynasties, Xi Yuhe was in charge; and in Yao's era, Yu and Shun inherited the calendar. By the Xia, Shang, and Zhou dynasties, their Zhengshuo and calendars differed. The "Biography" notes that when the big Mars appears, it is March in Xia, April in Shang, and May in Zhou. Emperors set sun officials, and princes set sun emperors to harmonize nations and coordinate celestial bodies, ensuring cold and heat, yin and yang cycles, and solar terms aligned with the sun's position without error.

With the Zhou Dynasty's decline, historians failed in their duties, and astronomical personnel dispersed, leaving good and bad omens unmonitored. The Qin State annexed the world, respected the five elements' mutual interdependence, believed in water virtue's auspicious response, and took October as the first month. The Han family followed the Qin calendar for over a hundred years until Emperor Wu ordered Sima Qian and others to create the "Han Calendar," adopting Xia Zheng. Later, Liu Xin created the "Santong Li" to explain the "Zuo Zhuan," but his explanations were untrue, and Ban Gu was misled. Emperor Guangwu established Shuhan, and Zhu Fu repeatedly noted calendar errors, but the world's instability prevented detailed examination. In the late Yongping period, the "Four Point Calendar" was used, completed over seventy years later. During the Guanghe period, Liu Hong and Cai Yong revised the legal calendar, and Sima Biao compiled the "Book of the Later Han Dynasty" based on their work. Liu Hong studied ancient calendars, investigated solar, lunar, and planetary movements, and found the "Four Points Calendar" inaccurate due to too many points. He used 589 as the rule and 145 as fighting points, writing the "Qian Xiang Method," which aligned with ancient records and current observations. He created techniques for delayed solar movement and examined lunar motion, with the white path intersecting the ecliptic, achieving greater precision than previous calendars.

In the first year of Jian'an, Zheng Xuan studied Liu Hong's calendar and annotated it. During the Huangchu period, Gao Tanglong discussed the calendar, and Han Yi believed the "Qianxiang Calendar" reduced too many dou points, creating the "Huang Chu Li" with 4,883 as the rule and 1,205 as dou points. Chen Qun submitted a report noting the calendar's complexity and debates among Confucian scholars. In the first year of Huangchu, the "Four Points Calendar" was replaced due to errors, and Han Yi compared his calendar with the "Qianxiang Calendar" for three years, studying solar and lunar motions but unable to decide differences. The emperor ordered astronomical instruments to test accuracy over a year. Xu Zhi believed Liu Hong's lunar motion method had been used for over 40 years with errors exceeding an hour. Sun Qin noted that Sima Qian's Taichu Calendar was rough, and Liu Xin created the Santong Calendar. The "Four Points Calendar" often erred, with eclipses differing by half a day. Liu Hong's Qianxiang Calendar calculated lunar and planetary motions consistently with actual phenomena. Dong Ba stated that sages used sundial shadows, strings, and angles to test calendars, with Xian, Wang, Jian, and Fu as key points. Xu Yue noted that Liu Xuan researched for over 20 years, referencing Han calendars and using Liangyi Guojian to examine moon phases. The moon runs for nine years with an end called the Nine Paths; nine chapters include 171 years, and nine-nine 81 chapters with 567 divided into nine endings, advancing and retreating four degrees and five minutes in front of the Ox constellation. To align with the "Four Points Calendar," subtract one sixty-three points. Examining Xian and Wang requires measuring the moon's position at dawn and dusk.

The key to testing the calendar lies in solar eclipses. During the Xiping period, Liu Hong predicted a solar eclipse at Chen hour, eclipsing two-thirds from bottom to top, which proved accurate, making him famous. Five solar and lunar eclipses were tested: four in the "Qian Xiang Calendar" and one in the "Huang Chu Li." Han Yi challenged Xu Yue, who replied that the "Qianxiang Calendar" had a unique message correction method. Jupiter, Saturn, Venus, and Mercury appearances and concealments were tested 15 times, with the "Qian Xiang Calendar" close to the sky seven times and the "Huang Chu Li" five times. Li En noted differences in celestial degrees and lunar eclipses. Dong Ba recalled that Fuxi created the Bagua, Huangdi wrote "Calendar Calendar," and after 11 generations and 5,000 years, seven calendars were used. Zhuanxu used Mengchun as the calendar year, while Tang's "Yin Calendar" used the winter solstice. Xia inherited Yao and Shun's methods. Yang Wei argued that within 60 days, calendar precision can be known without waiting ten years, but without following calendar laws, it is like using no rules or scales. Han Yi formulated his calendar based on Liu Hong's method but abandoned his theories, preventing Liu Hong's wonders from being passed down. The verification proposal was not finalized due to the emperor's death.

In the first year of Jingchu, Yang Wei formulated the "Jingchu Calendar," which was implemented with Chou month as the first month. After the emperor's death, Xia Zheng was reinstated. The Liu family in Shu used the "Four Points Calendar," while Wu used the "Qianxiang Calendar" until its fall. Emperor Wu of Jin followed the "Jingchu Li," renaming it "Taishi Li." Yang Wei's calendar was inaccurate for the five planets, so the Jin replaced its calculation method with the "Qianxiang Calendar." All subsequent calendars referred to the "Qianxiang Calendar," adding or subtracting basic data. Liu Hong's calendar technique became the model for later generations. It includes formulas for determining the winter solstice, twenty-four Qi, leap months, and the positions of the sun, moon, and five planets (Jupiter, Mars, Saturn, Venus, Mercury). The methods involve multiplying and dividing specific constants to find degrees, remainders, and celestial positions, with adjustments for retrograde motion, conjunctions, and eclipses. The text concludes with detailed steps for calculating planetary appearances, conjunctions, and movements, including direct and retrograde motion, with specific numbers for each planet's cycles and degrees.
