The text begins by defining the Tao as intangible and abstract, while utensils are concrete and real. The sage observed seasonal changes to create a standard watch and understood the five elements to make bells, aligning music with state affairs. Shen Gu used bamboo to standardize pitch, establishing the twelve laws (Huang Zhong, Gu Xi, Yi Ze, etc.) as an embodiment of natural laws. The sundial shadow and rhythm tube were used to harmonize yin and yang, with the rhythm pipe producing a sound of harmony between heaven and earth, standardizing measurements as stated in "Yu Shu." Virtuous music, grand and harmonious, could move heaven, earth, ghosts, and gods, guiding emotions and customs. Ye Shi noted that aspirations are expressed in chanting, and a gentleman listens to sound to choose music, then to grasp music, and finally to examine political affairs.

Tai Shigong's "Lvshu" states that kings follow the six laws as the foundation of all things, especially in war. Observing the enemy's situation and listening to music can predict outcomes. After Qin Shihuang burned books, these laws declined. During the Han Dynasty, Prime Minister Zhang Cang examined music and temperament but failed to complete the assessment. Emperor Xiaowu set up an official position for coordinating music, and Sima Qian investigated the order of music and rhythm. Wang Mang also studied musical rhythm, with Liu Xin presenting five items: complete mathematics, harmony of five tones, high and low pitch, length and shortness, and capacity of round and square. Ban Gu compiled these into "Lü Li Zhi." Cai Yong recorded later scholars' work in "Xu Hanshu." During the end of Mo, musicians fled, and laws were destroyed. Emperor Wu of Wei acquired musician Du Kui to test musical instruments, and Du Kui balanced the legal system. After Emperor Wu of Jin founded the country, the system remained unchanged until Xun Kan rebuilt new standards in the tenth year of Jin. During Yuankang, Xun Xun's son Yingpeng continued the work, but the Yongtai Rebellion caused the loss of rituals and music to Shi Le. When Emperor Jin and Yuan moved to Jiankang, ceremonial utensils and musical instruments were scarce.

The text records that the twelve laws were produced during the Huangdi era. Linglun cut bamboo from a valley west of Daxia and north of Kunlun, selecting a tube three inches and nine minutes long, naming the sound Huang Zhong's Palace, called Han Shao. Twelve bamboo tubes were made to imitate the phoenix's cry, with six sounds of the phoenix and six of the phoenix, all from the Yellow Bell Palace rhythm, forming the twelve rhythms. Bamboo tubes were used initially due to their naturally formed cavity size. Huangdi also used jade to make rhythm tubes, one foot long with six holes, one for each month. During Shun's reign, the king of the West presented Zhaohua's lüguan made of jade. During Emperor Mozhang's reign, Xi Jing unearthed a white jade rhythm tube under the Shun Temple. In the first year of Emperor Wu's reign, a jade rule was obtained from the tomb of King Xiang of Wei. Jade was used for its clear and moist tube body. During Emperor Moping's reign, Wang Ling used copper to make musical instruments, symbolizing unity and stability, as copper does not deform with dryness, moisture, cold, or heat.

"The Rites of Zhou" records that the Taishi is in charge of the six laws and six lu, coordinating yin and yang sounds. The six rhythms (Huangzhong, Taigu, Guxi, Ruibin, Yize, Wushe) are yang sounds, while the six lu (Dalu, Yingzhong, Nanlu, Linzhong, Zhonglv, Jiazhong) are yin sounds. The imperial master used twelve laws to predict military affairs and good or bad luck. Dian Tong was in charge of the sum of six rhythms and six lu to analyze yin and yang sounds and make musical instruments, using the twelve rhythms as length standards and twelve tones as test standards. King Zhou Jing wanted to make a Wushe bell and asked about the rhythm. Shi Xi explained that the yellow bell corresponds to earth, and from it, the second rhythm of Tai Cun assists Yang Qi. The third law, Guxi, is used for worship. The fourth, Ruibin, is used in the ancestral temple. The fifth, Yi Ze, sings the virtues of the Nine Gongs. The sixth, Wushe, declares the virtues of philosophers. There are also six rooms: Da Lu assists the Yang Law, Jia Zhong harmonizes the four seasons, Zhonglu nourishes neutrality, Lin Zhong brings peace, Nanlu assists formation, and Yingzhong restores utensils to their normal state.

The First Emperor of Qin burned books, scattering classics. Lu Buwei's "Spring and Autumn Annals" stated that Huangzhong Palace is the foundation of the law, with Lin Zhong born under it, Tai Cun above Lin Zhong, and so on, following the rule of three-thirds increase or decrease. After the Han Dynasty, Zhang Cang studied the legal calendar but it was incomplete. Emperor Xiaowu set up the official position of Xielu, standardizing pitch, but the number of notes remained five. King Liu An of Huainan invited scholars to study Lu Lu, stating that Huang Zhong's rhythm is nine inches, with the palace sound determined. The twelve laws correspond to the twenty-four seasonal changes, with Jiazi corresponding to Zhonglu, Bingzi to the bell's feather, Wuzi to Huangzhong's palace, Gengzi to Wushe's quotient, and Renzi to Yize's corner. The twelve laws produce sixty tones, and three hundred and sixty tones correspond to the number of days in a year.

Sima Qian's "Eight Books" discussed Lu Lu, stating that the most primitive chaotic energy contains the three qi of heaven, earth, and humans, activated in Zi. The twelve laws' mutual generation originates from here. The calculation method uses the nine-inch length of the Huang Zhong rule, with lower rules multiplying two by nine and dividing by three, and upper rules multiplying the lower rule by four and dividing by three. The law corresponds to the twelve celestial beings, with six laws as yang and six lu as yin. Sima Qian noted that if the length of the twelve laws is calculated according to "Huainanzi," Ruibin is a repeated ascendant. In the first year of the Yuan Dynasty, Wang Mang recruited scholars to examine music and rhythm, with Liu Xin sorting out the memorials. Ban Gu's "Book of Han" compiled these into annals, with the order of gain and loss based on the nine-inch yellow bell. During Emperor Yuan's reign, Jingfang was proficient in five tones and sixty temperaments. He explained that the method of mutual generation of sixty laws uses the upper law to produce the lower law with a ratio of two-thirds, and the lower law to produce the upper law with a ratio of three-fourths. The twelve laws change into the sixty laws, like the eight trigrams change into the sixty-four hexagrams. Jingfang added that a precision instrument, shaped like a zither with thirteen strings, is used to determine temperament data. The pitch of the pitch instrument is clear, but the pitch deviation caused by string tension is difficult to standardize without a temperament to calibrate.

In the first year of Yuanhe, Emperor Zhang of Han, Yin Xi reported that no one in the court knew how to use accurate instruments to adjust the sixty rhythms. Shao Yanchong taught his son Yan Xuan, but when tested, Yan Xuan could only identify two of the twelve laws accurately, so he could not be appointed. From then on, lawyers could no longer adjust the instrument. In the sixth year of Xiping, Emperor Ling summoned Zhang Guang and others, but they did not know the meaning of the accurate instrument. The problem of music could not be summarized, and there were no people in the palace who could distinguish pitch. At the end of the Han Dynasty, wars caused the loss of Yale San. During Emperor Wu of Wei's reign, Du Kui was proficient in music and cast bells, but the pitch was often off. King Wu of Wei checked and found Du Kui's opinion accurate. In the tenth year of Taishi, Zhongshu supervisor Gou Xu and Zhongshu ordered Zhang Hua to produce twenty-five copper and bamboo flutes. Three were consistent with Du Kui's laws, while the others were flute flutes. Liehe explained that the flute was used for practice, and the whole group could play in tune if the names and dimensions were known. Xun Xu and others reported that the flute making was not standardized, and they proposed making twelve flutes according to the twelve laws, with each hole tuned to the same tune. The emperor agreed, and Gou Xu asked Liehe about the method. Liehe replied that the flute length should be based on the rhythm, and the holes should correspond to the rhythms. The flute workers made the flutes, and the rhythmists adjusted their temperament, resulting in standardized instruments and harmonious sounds.

The text describes the construction of twelve flutes, each with a specific length and fingering method. For example, the Huang Zhong flute is two feet eight inches long, with the upright tone corresponding to Huang Zhong and the lower tone to Lin Zhong. The fingering method for the upright tone uses Huang Zhong as the palace, and the flute's holes are positioned according to the mutual generation of the laws. The lower Zheng tone uses Lin Zhong as the palace, and the Qingjiao tone uses Guxi as the palace. The flute's length is determined by the angular rhythm, with longer flutes being eight times the angle rhythm and shorter flutes four times. The flute tube's volume is ten times its angular volume. The text also discusses the three palaces (positive tone, downward sign, and clear angle) with twenty-one changes, each palace having seven tones. The four fu holes are used to change pitch but are not actually opened, only sized to meet the needs of pronunciation.

The text explains the correspondence between musical sounds and the five elements: earth sound is the palace (number 81), fire sound is the sign (number 54), gold sound is the quotient (number 72), water sound is the feather (number 48), and wood sound is the horn (number 64). Each sound is associated with a season and a symbol (monarch, things, ministers, etc.). The twelve laws correspond to the months, with Huang Zhong in November, Da Lu in December, Tai Cun in January, etc. The text also discusses the method of weathering, where a triple secret room is built, and rhythm tubes are placed on wooden cases to wait for the Qi to arrive. The correct length standard is described in "Mozhi," and Xun Xu made a new ruler based on ancient standards, which was consistent with ancient artifacts. The new clock rhythm was praised, but some criticized it for being too high. The text concludes with a discussion of weighing and capacity units, referencing "Zhou Li" and "Spring and Autumn Annals," and noting the changes in systems over time.
