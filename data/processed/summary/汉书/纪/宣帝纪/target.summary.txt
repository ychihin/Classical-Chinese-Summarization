# Summary of Emperor Xuan of Han's Reign

Emperor Xuan, great-grandson of Emperor Wu, was born into tragedy. His grandfather Prince Ru, grandmother Shi Liangdi, and parents were all killed in the "witchcraft incident" when he was an infant. Imprisoned, he was saved by official Bingji, who arranged for female convicts to nurse him. After a general amnesty, Bingji took him to his grandmother's home. Later, Yeting official Zhang He, remembering his loyalty to Prince Li, supported the boy's education and arranged his marriage to Xu Guanghan's daughter.

As he grew, Emperor Xuan studied poetry under Zhong Weng, showed talent, and became familiar with both common people's lives and official governance. He traveled extensively, visited ancestral tombs, and experienced hardship in Lianshao County. Strange phenomena surrounded him—his body glowed during sleep, and bakeries prospered when he visited.

When Emperor Zhao died without heirs, General Huo Guang initially appointed the King of Changyi, but soon deposed him for lewd behavior. Huo Guang then recommended Emperor Xuan, then eighteen, citing his virtue, education, and suitability. The Empress Dowager agreed, and after proper ceremonies, he ascended the throne.

Emperor Xuan's reign was marked by careful governance. He initially declined Huo Guang's offer to return power, entrusting him with major responsibilities. He rewarded meritorious officials generously, including Huo Guang, Yang Chang, and others. He issued edicts promoting agriculture, reducing waste, and caring for the poor. When earthquakes struck, he wore plain clothes, avoided the main hall, and sought advice from officials.

After Huo Guang's death, Emperor Xuan took personal control of government. He established regular reporting systems and tested officials' abilities. He promoted virtuous officials like Wang Cheng, who resettled refugees. He established the Crown Prince and granted amnesties. He reformed laws to protect filial piety, allowing parents to hide children's crimes and exempting mourners from corvée labor.

The Huo family later rebelled, and Queen Huo was deposed after being implicated in poisoning plots. Emperor Xuan continued his reforms, lowering salt prices, improving prison conditions, and requiring annual reports of deaths in custody. He built tombs, established temples, and rewarded those who had helped him in his youth, including Bingji, Zhang He, and the female convicts who nursed him.

Under his rule, the Xiongnu were pacified. Huhanxie Chanyu submitted as a vassal, visiting the court with great ceremony. Emperor Xuan treated him as a guest, rewarding him generously and establishing protectorates for surrendered tribes. The Western Qiang were subdued through military campaigns and farming strategies proposed by General Zhao Chongguo.

Emperor Xuan died in Weiyang Palace after a reign marked by competent administration, military success, and cultural achievements. Ban Gu commented that he rewarded merit, punished guilt, and examined names and facts thoroughly, making officials proficient in their duties and people content in their work. His reign was considered a restoration, with virtues comparable to those of King Xuan of Zhou.
