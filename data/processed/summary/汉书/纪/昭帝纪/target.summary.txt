Emperor Zhao of Han, youngest son of Emperor Wu, ascended the throne at age eight after his father’s death in 87 BCE. Huo Guang, appointed regent, managed state affairs with deputies Jin Rixi and Shangguan Jie. Early reign events included amnesties, a comet sighting, and Xiongnu raids repelled by garrisons. Auspicious signs like yellow swans prompted celebrations, while rebellions in Yizhou and by Liu Ze were suppressed. Reforms reduced taxes, exempted land rent, and provided relief to the poor. In 81 BCE, Emperor Zhao ended state monopolies on salt, iron, and wine after consulting virtuous scholars. Su Wu returned from 19 years of captivity among the Xiongnu and was rewarded.

In 80 BCE, a major conspiracy involving King Liu Dan of Yan, Princess Eyi, Shangguan Jie, and others to assassinate Huo Guang was foiled. Emperor Zhao, then 14, exposed the plot, leading to executions of the conspirators. Huo Guang’s loyalty was vindicated, and he continued as regent. Subsequent years saw further reforms: tax reductions, amnesties, and agricultural support. Emperor Zhao died in 74 BCE at age 21, buried in Pingling. Ban Gu praised his reign for restoring stability after Emperor Wu’s costly campaigns, implementing light corvée and low taxes, and fostering peace with the Xiongnu while enriching the people.
