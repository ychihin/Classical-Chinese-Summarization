Emperor Xiaowen, the fourth son of Emperor Gaozu of Han, was made King of Dai after the execution of Chen Xi. Following Empress Lu’s death, the Lu clan plotted to seize power but were killed by ministers Chen Ping, Zhou Bo, and others, who then decided to welcome King Dai as emperor. King Dai hesitated, with advisor Zhang Wu warning that the ministers were veteran generals skilled in intrigue and might not be trustworthy. However, Song Chang argued that the Han dynasty’s stability, the people’s loyalty to Liu, and the failure of the Lu clan’s usurpation proved divine support for Liu rule. After divination yielded a favorable omen, King Dai sent Bo Zhao to verify the ministers’ intentions. Zhou Bo confirmed their sincerity, and King Dai proceeded to Chang’an. At Weiqiao, Zhou Bo offered the imperial seal, but King Dai declined, insisting on discussing the matter at the palace. The ministers formally petitioned him to accept the throne, citing his lineage as Emperor Gaozu’s son. After three refusals, he agreed and became emperor.

Upon ascending the throne, Emperor Wen issued a general amnesty, rewarded those who had suppressed the Lu clan, and restored lands taken by the Lu family. He abolished the Qin law of collective punishment. When officials urged him to appoint a crown prince, he initially declined, citing his own unworthiness and suggesting a virtuous successor from among his relatives or ministers. After repeated requests, he appointed his eldest son, Prince Qi, as crown prince. He also established his consort Dou as empress. The emperor implemented benevolent policies, including tax relief for farmers, care for the elderly, and pardons for minor offenses. He abolished the crime of slander and attempted treason, emphasizing the importance of agriculture and reducing government expenditures.

During his reign, Emperor Wen faced challenges such as the rebellion of King Xingju of Jibei, which was swiftly suppressed, and conflicts with the Xiongnu, whom he managed through defense rather than costly campaigns. He promoted frugality, wearing simple clothes and forbidding lavish decorations. He also reformed the legal system, abolished corporal punishment, and encouraged the recommendation of virtuous officials. In his later years, he issued an edict on his deathbed, advocating simple funerals and urging the people not to mourn excessively. He was buried in Ba Ling with minimal ceremony, using earthenware vessels and no precious metals. Ban Gu praised Emperor Wen for his benevolence, noting that his reign saw prosperity, few imprisonments, and a flourishing of moral virtue.
