Emperor Xiaoai, grandson of Emperor Yuan and son of Prince Dingtao Gong, became king at age three and developed an interest in literature and law. In the fourth year of Yuanyan's reign, he visited Emperor Cheng with his officials, while Prince Xiao of Zhongshan came with only one attendant. Emperor Cheng, impressed by King Dingtao's knowledge of the "Poetry" and his explanations, favored him over the incompetent King Zhongshan. Queen Mother Fu, King Dingtao's grandmother, bribed Emperor Cheng's favorites Zhao Zhaoyi and Wang Gen, who, seeing the emperor had no heirs, praised King Dingtao. Emperor Cheng crowned him at seventeen and later made him crown prince. King Dingtao humbly declined but accepted. After Emperor Cheng's death in the second year of Suihe, the prince became emperor, honoring the Queen Mother and issuing amnesties. He honored his grandmother and mother, granted titles to relatives, and dismissed Yuefu for lewd music. He rewarded Wang Gen and others for supporting his succession and granted lands to Wang Liang for mourning. He issued edicts to curb extravagance, limiting land and slave ownership, banning merchants from land and office, and stopping complex embroidery. He revoked certain laws, allowed inner palace residents under thirty to marry, freed slaves over fifty, and banned rare animal tributes. He increased salaries for low-ranking officials and punished corrupt officials. After disasters like earthquakes and floods, he sent officials to aid victims and exempted taxes for the poor. In the first year of Jianping, he pardoned the world, deposed guilty marquises, and gave Wang family lands to the poor. He sought talent, granted money and cloth to officials, and saw the suicide of Empress Dowager Feng and her brother. In the second year, he dismissed the chief minister, restored the imperial censor, and honored his ancestors with new titles and temples. After Empress Dowager Ding's death, he followed Zhou rites for burial, recruiting 50,000 workers. He initially accepted prophecies to change the era name but later revoked them, punishing the prophets and officials. He faced droughts, fires, and comets, restored sacrifices, and dealt with rebellions. He granted titles for reporting crimes, honored his wife, and sought military talent. In the first year of Yuanshou, a solar eclipse led to an edict blaming himself and seeking virtuous officials. After Empress Dowager Fu's death, he dismissed officials and saw portents. In the second year, Xiongnu envoys visited, and he divided official duties. Emperor Ai died in Weiyang Palace at age 20 after six years as emperor, buried in Yiling. Ban Gu noted his early talent and efforts to strengthen authority but lamented his short reign.
