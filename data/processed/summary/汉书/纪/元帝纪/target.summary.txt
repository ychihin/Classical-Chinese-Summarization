Emperor Yuan of Han, son of Emperor Xuan, was made prince at age eight. He was generous and respected Confucianism, often criticizing his father's reliance on legal officials and harsh punishments. Emperor Xuan warned that Confucian scholars would disrupt the Han dynasty's system, which blended kingly and hegemonic rule. Though Emperor Xuan favored another son, he ultimately kept Emperor Yuan as heir due to loyalty to his mother.

Upon ascending the throne in 48 BCE, Emperor Yuan honored his mother and wife as empresses, granted amnesty, and distributed rewards. He issued edicts addressing natural disasters, earthquakes, and famines, ordering relief efforts, tax exemptions, and land grants to the poor. He reduced palace expenses, dismissed entertainers, and lent public lands to farmers. He also sent officials to inspect local governance and seek wise men.

In subsequent years, Emperor Yuan continued responding to crises: earthquakes, floods, plagues, and famines led to further tax exemptions, amnesties, and relief measures. He abolished certain laws, reduced palace staff, and encouraged remonstrance. He appointed Confucian scholars like Gong Yu and Kuang Heng as officials, but his indecisiveness weakened the state. He also dealt with rebellions, such as the Xiqiang uprising, and military campaigns against the Xiongnu, including the execution of Zhizhi Chanyu.

Emperor Yuan's reign saw numerous natural disasters and social unrest. He repeatedly issued edicts blaming himself for poor governance and seeking advice. He reformed policies, including abolishing the death penalty for certain family crimes and reducing corvée labor. He also established his own mausoleum, Weiling, and ordered that no new migrants be forced to settle there.

Emperor Yuan died in 33 BCE after 16 years of rule. Ban Gu's commentary notes that Emperor Yuan was talented in music and calligraphy, respected Confucianism, and appointed many Confucian scholars as officials. However, his indecisiveness and adherence to literary principles led to the decline of Emperor Xuan's achievements. Despite this, his generosity, thrift, and gentle edicts reflected the legacy of ancient wise kings.
