Emperor Jing of Han, son of Emperor Wen and Queen Dou, ascended the throne on June 9 after his father's death in the seventh year of the Second Yuan Dynasty. He honored Empress Dowager Bo and Empress Dowager Dou. In his first year, he issued an edict praising Emperor Wen's virtues—abolishing slander laws, corporal punishment, and palace punishment; rewarding the elderly; and repatriating palace women. He ordered the "Zhao De" dance for Emperor Wen's temple. Prime Minister Shen Tujia proposed establishing Emperor Gao's temple as Taizu and Emperor Wen's as Taizong, with perpetual ancestral worship, which Emperor Jing approved.

In subsequent years, Emperor Jing addressed food shortages by encouraging migration to fertile lands, granted amnesties, reduced farmland rent by half, and reformed laws on official bribery and gift acceptance. Comets appeared, and the Empress Dowager died in 144 BC. The Seven Kingdoms rebellion led by Liu Bi of Wu and others erupted; Emperor Jing sent Zhou Yafu and Dou Ying to suppress it, resulting in over 100,000 rebel deaths and the suicides of the rebel kings. He pardoned deceived officials and civilians.

He appointed princes, resumed checkpoint inspections, made Liu Rong crown prince (later deposed), and Liu Che crown prince. He reformed mourning rites for princes, changed execution methods, and renamed officials. He prohibited tall horses from leaving the empire, faced locust plagues, and built Yangling. He issued edicts against bribery and harsh judgments, reformed official attire, and reduced whipping penalties. The Xiongnu invaded, and Zhou Yafu died in prison. He abolished the rule requiring princes to visit fiefs, promoted agriculture, and allowed those with 40,000 yuan assets to become officials. Emperor Jing died in Weiyang Palace in the first month of the third year of Houyuan, was buried in Yangling, and was praised by Ban Gu for continuing Emperor Wen's frugal policies, leading to a prosperous era comparable to the Zhou dynasty's Chengkang period.
