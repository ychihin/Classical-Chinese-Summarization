Emperor Xiaocheng, the son of Emperor Yuan and Empress Wang, was born while his father was crown prince and was greatly favored by his grandfather, Emperor Xuan, who named him Taisun. After Emperor Xuan’s death, Emperor Yuan ascended the throne and made him crown prince. As an adult, he was studious, broad-minded, and cautious. Once, when summoned urgently by his father, he avoided crossing the emperor’s road, taking a detour instead, which pleased Emperor Yuan, who then allowed him to cross. Despite later concerns over his drinking and pleasure-seeking, Emperor Yuan considered deposing him in favor of Prince Tao Gong, but was dissuaded by Shi Xian and the memory of Emperor Xuan’s affection.

Upon Emperor Yuan’s death in the first year of Jingning, the prince ascended the throne, honoring his mother and grandmother as empress dowagers. He appointed his uncle Wang Feng as Grand Marshal and Grand General, overseeing secretarial affairs. He agreed to stop burying chariots and animals with the dead, granted a general amnesty, and faced various omens: a fire in the ancestral temple, a comet, and a storm that killed envoy Yao Yin. He rewarded officials and the people, issued an edict acknowledging his faults, and promoted tolerance. He granted titles to his uncles and faced natural phenomena like yellow mist, swarms of flies, and a shooting star.

In subsequent years, he conducted suburban sacrifices, established Queen Xu, and dealt with droughts, floods, and rebellions. He reduced taxes, pardoned prisoners, and encouraged agriculture. He faced earthquakes, solar eclipses, and a comet, issuing edicts to seek advice and reform. He moved wealthy households to Changling, deposed Queen Xu, and suppressed uprisings like Zheng Gong’s rebellion. He stopped the costly Changling project, punished the architect Xie Wannian, and rewarded those who advised against it. He continued to face disasters, including fires and floods, and issued edicts against extravagance.

He appointed Liu Xin of Dingtao as crown prince, granted titles to descendants of previous dynasties, and reorganized offices. He died in Weiyang Palace and was buried in Yanling. Ban Gu noted his dignified appearance, knowledge, and eloquence, but lamented his indulgence in drink and women, leading to the rise of the Wang family and Wang Mang’s eventual usurpation.
