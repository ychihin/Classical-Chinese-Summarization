Wei Qing, courtesy name Zhongqing, was the illegitimate son of Zheng Ji, a county official in Pingyang, and a servant named Wei. He assumed the surname Wei after his sister Wei Zifu gained favor with Emperor Wu. As a youth, Wei Qing herded sheep and was treated poorly by his relatives. A prisoner once prophesied he would become a marquis, but Wei Qing dismissed this, content to avoid beatings. He later became a knight for the Pingyang Hou family.

Wei Zifu's entry into the palace and pregnancy provoked jealousy from Empress Chen and her mother, the Grand Princess, who imprisoned Wei Qing. His friend Gongsun Ao rescued him, and the emperor subsequently appointed Wei Qing as a eunuch and minister. His rise brought wealth to his family. In 129 BC, Wei Qing led a cavalry attack on the Xiongnu, reaching Longcheng and killing hundreds. He was made Marquis of Guannei, while other generals faced punishment for failures.

In subsequent campaigns, Wei Qing defeated the Xiongnu, capturing livestock and driving out kings. He was granted the title of Marquis of Changping with 3,800 households. The emperor praised his victories, which included pacifying the Henan area and building Shuofang City. Despite continued Xiongnu raids, Wei Qing's successes led to additional rewards.

In 124 BC, Wei Qing led a surprise attack on the Xiongnu's Right King, capturing over 15,000 people and millions of livestock. He was promoted to General-in-Chief, and his sons were made marquises. Wei Qing declined the honors, crediting his officers. The emperor then rewarded several subordinates with marquisates.

In 123 BC, Wei Qing's campaign saw the defection of former Xiongnu marquis Zhao Xin and the destruction of Su Jian's force. Wei Qing spared Su Jian, sending him to the emperor for judgment. That year, Huo Qubing, Wei Qing's nephew, began his rise, leading a successful raid with 800 cavalry and being made Marquis of Champion.

Huo Qubing's subsequent campaigns were highly successful, including capturing the Xiongnu's golden statue and defeating the Hunxie King, who surrendered with 100,000 people. Huo Qubing was rewarded with additional households, and the surrendered Xiongnu were settled as vassal states.

In 119 BC, Wei Qing and Huo Qubing led a massive campaign. Wei Qing defeated the Xiongnu Chanyu but failed to capture him. Huo Qubing's forces killed over 70,000 enemies. Both were made Grand Sima, but Huo Qubing's star rose while Wei Qing's declined. Huo Qubing died in 117 BC, deeply mourned by the emperor.

Wei Qing died in 106 BC, having attacked the Xiongnu seven times and killing over 50,000 enemies. He recovered the Hetao region and established Shuofang County. His three sons were marquises, but all lost their titles. Huo Qubing's six campaigns killed over 110,000 enemies and opened the Hexi region. His subordinates also received honors. The text concludes with brief biographies of other generals and notes that Wei Qing and Huo Qubing refrained from recruiting scholars, following the emperor's wishes.
