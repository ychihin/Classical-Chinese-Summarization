Zhang Shizhi, a native of Nanyang, served Emperor Wen of Han but remained unpromoted for years. He sought to resign, but Lieutenant-General Yuan Ang recognized his talent and arranged a transfer. During an audience, Emperor Wen advised him to focus on present matters rather than ancient ones. Zhang Shizhi discussed the fall of Qin and the rise of Han, earning promotion to Servant. While visiting a tiger enclosure, the emperor questioned officials about animal records. A lowly keeper answered fluently, impressing the emperor, who wanted to promote him. Zhang Shizhi cautioned against valuing eloquence over substance, citing Qin's decline due to such practices. The emperor agreed and halted the promotion. Later, Zhang Shizhi stopped the prince and King of Liang from entering the palace gate without dismounting, accusing them of disrespect. Empress Dowager Bo pardoned them, but Emperor Wen appointed Zhang Shizhi as a senior official. At Ba Ling, the emperor lamented the difficulty of securing his tomb, but Zhang Shizhi argued that greed, not materials, determines security. Promoted to Tingwei, he handled cases with fairness. When a man startled the emperor's horse, Zhang Shizhi imposed a fine, not execution, insisting on legal consistency. In another case, he opposed executing a thief's entire clan for stealing from Gaozu Temple, arguing proportionality. Emperor Wen approved his verdict. After Emperor Jing's accession, Zhang Shizhi feared retribution for past offenses but was forgiven. He later died of illness.

Feng Tang, known for filial piety, served Emperor Wen. The emperor lamented not having generals like Lian Po and Li Mu to fight the Xiongnu. Feng Tang boldly claimed the emperor could not use them, citing harsh laws and unfair rewards. He used the example of Wei Shang, a capable governor punished for minor discrepancies in battle reports. Emperor Wen, initially angered, later pardoned Wei Shang and appointed Feng Tang as chariot and cavalry captain. Under Emperor Jing, Feng Tang became prime minister of Chu. Emperor Wu later recommended him, but he was too old to serve.

Ji An, from Puyang, served Emperor Wu. He was known for his blunt advice and disdain for trivialities. Sent to inspect conflicts, he deemed them unworthy of imperial attention. During a famine, he distributed grain without authorization, earning the emperor's favor. As a governor, he favored quiet governance, delegating tasks and achieving stability. In court, he criticized the emperor's hypocrisy and opposed Confucian scholars. He clashed with officials like Zhang Tang, accusing them of manipulating laws. Despite his integrity, he was demoted for minor offenses. Later appointed governor of Huaiyang, he warned General Li Xi against Zhang Tang's cunning. Ji An died after ten years, leaving a legacy of principled governance.

Zheng Zhuang, from Chen County, admired Huangdi and Laozi teachings. He treated guests humbly, regardless of status, and lived frugally, using his salary to support friends. He recommended scholars enthusiastically, often praising them as superior to himself. However, he avoided taking clear stances in court, echoing the emperor's wishes. During the Xiongnu campaigns, his recommendations led to financial mismanagement, resulting in his demotion. He died as governor of Runan, leaving no property. The text reflects on the fickleness of human relationships, noting that guests flock to the powerful but abandon the fallen.
