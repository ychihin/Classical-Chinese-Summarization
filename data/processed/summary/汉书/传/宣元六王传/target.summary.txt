Emperor Xiaoxuan had five sons. The empress gave birth to Emperor Xiaoyuan, while other consorts bore Huaiyang Xianwang Liu Qin, Chu Xiaowang Liu Xiao, Dongping Siwang Liu Yu, and Zhongshan Aiwang Liu Tong, who died young. Liu Qin, made king in the third year of Yuankang, was favored by Emperor Xuan due to his mother Zhang Shidan’s influence. After Empress Huo was deposed, Xuan initially wanted to make Zhang queen but feared for the crown prince’s safety, instead choosing Wang Jiantun, a childless and cautious woman, to raise the prince. Zhang remained the most favored consort. Liu Qin, intelligent and fond of scriptures, was admired by Xuan, who considered replacing the crown prince but relented due to the prince’s early loss of his mother. After Xuan’s death, Emperor Yuan ordered Liu Qin to his fief. Zhang Shidan died, and Liu Qin’s maternal uncle Zhang Bo and his brothers were rewarded for visits. Liu Qin requested the Zhang family’s relocation to Huaiyang, but Zhang Bo refused, causing resentment. Zhang Bo later demanded debt repayment and, after receiving gold, flattered Liu Qin in letters, urging him to seek court audience and assist the emperor. Liu Qin initially declined but eventually agreed, sending gold and gifts. Zhang Bo’s son-in-law Jingfang, favored by Emperor Yuan for his knowledge of the *I Ching*, shared court secrets with Zhang Bo, who fabricated claims of influence with officials. Liu Qin was persuaded to pursue audience, but Jingfang’s departure and Shi Xian’s exposure led to arrests. The emperor, sparing Liu Qin, sent an admonishing official to rebuke him, citing his correspondence with Zhang Bo as treasonous. Liu Qin apologized, and Zhang Bo and his brothers were exiled. Under Emperor Cheng, Liu Qin requested the exiles’ return, which was granted despite official objections. He died in his thirty-sixth year, succeeded by his son Liu Xuan, then grandson Liu Yan, whose line ended under Wang Mang.

Liu Xiao, Chu Xiaowang, was made king in the second year of Ganlu. Emperor Cheng, during a court visit, praised his filial piety and kindness, granting his son Liu Xun a marquisate. Liu Xiao died the next year, succeeded by his son Liu Wen, who died without heirs. Emperor Cheng then appointed Liu Wen’s brother Liu Yan as king, whose line ended under Wang Mang. Liu Jing, Liu Yu’s brother, was made King of Dingtao. Liu Xun’s descendant Liu Xian had a son, Liu Ying, whom Wang Mang made Duke of Ding’an after Emperor Ping’s death. During the Gengshi era, Liu Ying was proclaimed emperor by rebels but was killed by Gengshi’s forces.

Liu Yu, Dongping Siwang, was made king in the second year of Ganlu. He committed crimes and was leniently treated by Emperor Yuan. Later, he served his mother poorly, leading to an imperial reprimand. He repented but, after Emperor Yuan’s death, showed disrespect by drinking and eating meat during mourning. He hanged a concubine who recorded his faults, leading to the reduction of his fief. He later reformed, and the emperor restored his lands. During a court visit, he requested books of the philosophers and *Historical Records*, but Wang Feng advised against it, citing their unsuitability for princes. Liu Yu died in his thirty-third year, succeeded by his son Liu Yun. During Emperor Ai’s reign, Liu Yun and his queen performed sacrifices at a stone formation, which was reported as treason. They were accused of cursing the emperor, and Liu Yun committed suicide. His fief was abolished but later restored by Wang Mang, who appointed Liu Kaiming as king. Liu Kaiming died without heirs, and his brother’s son Liu Xingkuang succeeded, but was killed by Wang Mang after a failed rebellion.

Liu Tong, Zhongshan Aiwang, was made King of Qinghe and later moved to Zhongshan but died young without heirs. Emperor Xiaoyuan had three sons: Empress Wang gave birth to Emperor Cheng, Fu Zhaoyi to Liu Kang of Dingtao, and Feng Zhaoyi to Liu Xing of Zhongshan. Liu Kang, favored by Emperor Yuan, was treated specially by Emperor Cheng. He died after nineteen years, succeeded by his son Liu Xin, who became Emperor Ai. Emperor Ai honored his father as Emperor Gong. Liu Xing, Zhongshan Xiaowang, was made king and later moved to Zhongshan. Emperor Cheng considered him as heir but chose Liu Xin instead. Liu Xing died after thirty years, succeeded by his son Liu Yan, who became Emperor Ping. Emperor Ping’s line ended under Wang Mang.

The commentary notes that Emperor Xiaoyuan’s descendants spread widely but were cut off in his grandchildren’s generation, perhaps by fate. Liu Qin was intelligent but nearly fell into immorality due to Zhang Bo’s greed, echoing the *Book of Songs*’ warning about greedy people.
