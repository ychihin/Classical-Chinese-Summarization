# Summary of Historical Records: Biographies of Li Shiqi, Lu Jia, Zhu Jian, Lou Jing, and Shusun Tong

Li Shiqi of Gaoyang was a learned man from a poor family who worked as a village gatekeeper. When various rebel generals passed through Gaoyang during the uprisings against Qin, he hid from them, considering them narrow-minded. Upon hearing that Pei Gong (later Emperor Gaozu of Han) was far-sighted, Li Shiqi arranged an introduction through a fellow villager serving under Pei Gong. Despite Pei Gong's disdain for Confucian scholars, Li Shiqi boldly criticized him for washing his feet while receiving visitors. Pei Gong apologized, and Li Shiqi advised him to seize Chenliu County, which had abundant food stores. Li Shiqi persuaded the magistrate to surrender, and Pei Gong captured Chenliu, granting Li Shiqi the title of Lord of Guangye. Later, when the Han army was besieged, Li Shiqi urged the King of Han to seize Aocang's grain supplies and strategic positions. He then volunteered to persuade the King of Qi to submit to Han, successfully convincing Tian Guang to surrender. However, Han Xin attacked Qi anyway, and Tian Guang, believing Li Shiqi had deceived him, executed him.

Lu Jia of Chu was a skilled orator who served as an envoy for Emperor Gaozu. He was sent to South Vietnam to grant Wei Tuo the king's seal. When Wei Tuo received him disrespectfully, Lu Jia warned him that the Han emperor could easily destroy him. Wei Tuo apologized and acknowledged Han's superiority. Lu Jia successfully secured South Vietnam's submission. Later, when Emperor Gaozu dismissed the value of studying the classics, Lu Jia argued that while one could conquer the world on horseback, one could not govern it that way. He wrote twelve essays explaining why Qin fell and Han succeeded, which became known as the "New Speeches." During Empress Dowager Lu's regency, Lu Jia retired but later advised Chancellor Chen Ping to ally with General Zhou Bo to counter the Lu family's conspiracy. His strategy helped preserve the Liu imperial house.

Zhu Jian of Chu served as prime minister to Yingbu, King of Huainan, and advised against rebellion. After Yingbu rebelled and was suppressed, Zhu Jian was rewarded. He was known for his integrity and refused to associate with the corrupt Marquis of Piyang, who was favored by Empress Dowager Lu. When Zhu Jian's mother died, Lu Jia advised the Marquis of Piyang to offer generous funeral gifts, which led to reconciliation. Later, when the Marquis of Piyang was imprisoned, Zhu Jian secretly instructed the emperor's favorite minister to plead for his release, saving his life. After Empress Dowager Lu's death, Zhu Jian's earlier advice helped protect the Marquis of Piyang from execution. During Emperor Xiaowen's reign, Zhu Jian was implicated in a case involving the Marquis of Piyang and committed suicide to protect his family.

Lou Jing of Qi advised Emperor Gaozu to establish the capital in Guanzhong rather than Luoyang, arguing that Qin's former territory had natural defenses and abundant resources. Despite opposition from ministers from Shandong, Zhang Liang supported this plan, and the emperor adopted it, granting Lou Jing the surname Liu and the title of Feng Chunjun. Later, when the emperor planned to attack the Xiongnu, Lou Jing warned that the Xiongnu were deliberately showing weakness to ambush the Han army. The emperor ignored this advice, was besieged at Baideng, and later released Lou Jing, rewarding him. Lou Jing then proposed a marriage alliance with the Xiongnu, suggesting the emperor send his eldest princess to marry the Chanyu. Although Empress Lu objected, a substitute princess was sent. Lou Jing also recommended relocating powerful clans from various states to Guanzhong to strengthen the region.

Shusun Tong of Xue served under Qin II, cleverly avoiding punishment by calling rebels mere thieves. He later served Xiang Liang, King Huai, and finally surrendered to the King of Han. Noticing the disorderly behavior of ministers at court, he offered to create court rituals. Despite criticism from conservative Confucian scholars, he adapted ancient and Qin rituals to create a practical system. After the completion of Changle Palace, the new rituals were implemented, impressing Emperor Gaozu, who remarked, "Now I know the dignity of being emperor." Shusun Tong was appointed Fengchang and rewarded with gold, which he distributed to his disciples. He later served as Crown Prince Tutor and successfully dissuaded Emperor Gaozu from replacing the crown prince. Under Emperor Xiaohui, he continued to formulate rituals and established the practice of offering fruits at ancestral temples.
