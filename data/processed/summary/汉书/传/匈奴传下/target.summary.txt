After Huhanxie Chanyu returned to his court, he ended the war and appointed his brother Hutuwusi as King Zuoguli. He also ordered the killing of King Youxian. That winter, Dulongqi and King Youxian supported Zhu Wanglian as a rival chanyu and attacked Huhanxie, who was defeated and fled. Tuqi Chanyu then took control, appointing his sons as kings. The following autumn, Tuqi Chanyu stationed troops to guard against Huhanxie. Internal strife escalated when King Hujie slandered King Youxian, leading to his execution. This caused King Hujie to flee and declare himself Chanyu, followed by others, resulting in five rival chanyus.

Tuqi Chanyu attacked Cheli Chanyu and Wujii Chanyu, both of whom fled to join Hujie Chanyu. Tuqi then sent forces east and west, but Huhanxie Chanyu attacked and killed over 10,000 of Tuqi's troops. Tuqi retaliated with 60,000 cavalry but was defeated and committed suicide. Huhanxie Chanyu's general Dan Quli surrendered to the Han Dynasty. Huhanxie returned to his court with only tens of thousands of followers. Runzhen Chanyu and Zhizhi Chanyu emerged, with Zhizhi killing Runzhen and attacking Huhanxie, who was defeated.

King Zuoyi Zhizi advised Huhanxie to surrender to the Han Dynasty for stability. Despite opposition from ministers who valued Xiongnu martial traditions, Huhanxie agreed and led his people south, sending his son as a hostage. The Han emperor received Huhanxie with great honor, granting him titles, gifts, and troops for protection. Huhanxie requested to stay near the Han border for defense. The Han provided food and military support. Zhizhi Chanyu, meanwhile, moved west, attacking Wusun and other tribes, establishing his capital in Jiankun.

Emperor Yuan succeeded and Huhanxie requested aid due to poverty. The Han sent grain. Zhizhi, resentful of Han support for Huhanxie, killed a Han envoy. The Han sent envoys to escort Huhanxie's son back and made an alliance with him, pledging mutual defense. However, Han officials criticized the alliance as unauthorized. Huhanxie eventually returned north, stabilizing his rule. Zhizhi, fearing Han retaliation, moved further west and allied with Kangju, but was later killed by Han forces. Huhanxie, relieved, requested to visit the Han court again.

In the first year of Jingning, Huhanxie visited the Han court and received generous gifts. He requested to become a Han son-in-law, and Emperor Yuan gave him Wang Zhaojun as a wife. Huhanxie offered to defend the Han border from Shanggu to Dunhuang and asked for the withdrawal of Han border troops. The emperor consulted officials, and Hou Ying argued against withdrawal, citing ten reasons: the strategic value of the Yinshan Mountains, the Xiongnu's untrustworthy nature, the need to prevent internal crime and defections, and the risk of future emergencies. The emperor agreed and declined Huhanxie's request.

King Zuoyi Zhizi, who had advised Huhanxie to submit to Han, later fell under suspicion and surrendered to Han. Huhanxie later apologized and asked him to return, but he refused. Huhanxie died after 28 years, succeeded by his son Diao Tao Mogao as Fuzhuanruo Qianchanyu, following the advice of his mother Zhuanquban to avoid instability. The new chanyu appointed his brothers as kings and continued relations with Han. Subsequent chanyus maintained ties, sending sons as hostages and visiting the Han court.

During the reign of Wuzhuliu Chanyu, Han envoy Xiahou Fan requested land from the Xiongnu, but the chanyu refused, citing ancestral rights. The Han emperor punished Xiahou Fan. Later, conflicts arose over Wusun raids and Wuhuan tax issues. Wang Mang, after usurping the Han throne, changed the Xiongnu seal and imposed new regulations, causing resentment. The Xiongnu resumed border raids. Wang Mang's military campaigns failed, leading to chaos. The Xiongnu chanyu died, and his successor Xian sought peace with Han but continued raiding. Wang Mang's attempts to control the Xiongnu through titles and gifts ultimately failed, and the border remained unstable.

After Wang Mang's death, the Han Dynasty restored relations with the Xiongnu, but the chanyu remained arrogant. The historian concludes that peace treaties and military campaigns both had limitations, and the Xiongnu's nature made lasting peace difficult.
