Sima Xiangru served as a Langguan when Tang Meng was ordered to open Yelang and Fenzhong in the west, levying soldiers from Bajun and Shujun and executing leaders who disobeyed, causing great fear among the people. The emperor sent Xiangru to blame Tang Meng and inform the people that these actions were not the emperor's original intent. The message explained that the barbarians had long invaded the border, but the emperor had brought peace to China, attacked the Huns, and pacified Minyue and Nanyue. The recruitment of soldiers was meant to prevent war, not to cause fear. The emperor condemned those who committed suicide or fled from conscription, emphasizing the duty of subjects to serve their country. He also criticized the lack of moral education among the people and their fathers and brothers.

Xiangru returned to report. Tang Meng had opened Yelang and recruited tens of thousands of soldiers from Ba, Shu, and Guanghan to build a road, which took two years and cost hundreds of millions, with many soldiers dying. Many in Shu and the Han court opposed this. The commanders of Yinyi and Zha, seeing Nanyi's rewards, wished to become Han vassals. The emperor consulted Nuojia, who noted that the Yi tribes near Shu had been opened during Qin but abandoned after Han. The emperor agreed to reopen them, appointing Xiangru as Zhonglang General to pacify the southwestern barbarians. Xiangru used officials and property from Ba and Shu to bribe the barbarians, and the people of Shu were proud of his mission. Zhuo Wangsun, Xiangru's father-in-law, distributed property equally to his daughter. Xiangru pacified the barbarians, who submitted to Han, and the emperor was pleased.

However, many in Shu and some ministers opposed opening the southwestern barbarians. Xiangru wrote an article to admonish the emperor, using a conversation with the elders of Shu to question their views. He argued that the Han dynasty's virtues were vast, and the barbarians should be brought under control. He criticized the elders for their shortsightedness, comparing the emperor's efforts to those of Xia Yu in controlling floods. He emphasized that the emperor's goal was to extend benevolence and stop conquest, and that the people's labor was justified for such a grand cause. The elders eventually agreed with him.

Later, Xiangru was accused of accepting bribes and dismissed, but was reinstated as Langguan after a year. He suffered from diabetes and often stayed home due to illness. He once accompanied the emperor to hunt at Changyang Palace and advised against dangerous hunting, warning of potential disasters. The emperor appreciated his advice. Xiangru also wrote a eulogy for Qin II's mistakes and composed "Adult Fu" to express the emperor's desire for immortality. The emperor was delighted with the fu.

After Xiangru's death, the emperor sent for his writings but found none. His wife said he often wrote but had no books left. Xiangru had left a letter about Fengshan, which the emperor found strange. The letter discussed ancient monarchs and the importance of Fengshan rituals, arguing that the Han dynasty's virtues surpassed those of Zhou and that the emperor should perform the rituals. The emperor eventually performed the Fengshan ceremony eight years after Xiangru's death.

The historian praised Xiangru's works for their moral lessons, despite their exaggerations, comparing them to the satires in the Book of Songs. However, Yang Xiong criticized Xiangru for encouraging extravagance with his ornate language.
