Zhao Guanghan, a native of Zhuo Commandery, began his career as a county and commandery official, known for his integrity and talent. He was elected as Maocai and served as a market price regulator before becoming the magistrate of Yangdi. His exceptional governance led to his promotion to Lieutenant of Jingfu and acting Jing Zhaoyin. After Emperor Zhao's death, he executed the corrupt Du Jian despite powerful intercessions, earning praise from the capital. He participated in deposing King Changyi and establishing Emperor Xuan, receiving the title of Marquis of Guannei. As Governor of Yingchuan, he suppressed the powerful Yuan and Chu clans and used innovative methods like the "snitch bucket" to break up local factions, restoring order. His reputation spread even to the Xiongnu.

As Jing Zhaoyin, Guanghan was diligent and generous, attributing successes to his subordinates. He was skilled in the "hook distance" method of investigation, uncovering crimes with precision. He once prevented a kidnapping by negotiating with the kidnappers and later treated them with dignity before their execution. He also exposed private affairs with uncanny accuracy, such as knowing a pavilion chief's message before it was delivered. He increased the salary of traveling wardens to prevent corruption, leading to a well-governed capital. However, after General Huo Guang's death, Guanghan raided his son's residence, offending the queen and powerful relatives. He eventually fell from favor after a series of conflicts, including a false accusation against the prime minister's wife. Despite his integrity and popularity among the people, he was executed by waist-cutting.

Yin Wenggui, from Hedong, lost his father early and worked as a prison clerk. As a market official, he curbed the violence of Huo Guang's retainers. Governor Tian Yannian recognized his talent and appointed him to oversee half of the commandery's counties. He later served as Governor of Donghai, where he maintained detailed records of officials' conduct and arrested criminals during official meetings, instilling fear. He executed the cunning Xu Zhongsun, pacifying the region. As You Fufeng, he continued his strict governance, targeting the powerful while being lenient with the weak. He was known for his integrity and modesty, dying in office with no surplus property. The emperor praised him and granted gold to his family.

Han Yanshou, from Handi, was promoted after his father died admonishing King Yan. As Governor of Yingchuan, he replaced Zhao Guanghan's confrontational methods with education in rites, consulting local elders and standardizing ceremonies. The people followed his teachings, abandoning lavish funerals. He later governed Shujun, hiring local worthies and promoting filial piety. He treated officials with kindness, blaming himself for their failures. As Zuo Fengyi, he resolved a land dispute by blaming himself, leading to the brothers' reconciliation. However, he was impeached for exceeding his authority and executed, despite public mourning. His sons resigned from office as he advised.

Zhang Chang, from Hedong, gained fame for admonishing King Changyi. He warned Emperor Xuan about the Huo family's power, advocating for their removal. As Governor of Shanyang, he requested to suppress bandits in Bohai, using rewards and amnesty to disperse them. He also advised the Empress Dowager to stop hunting. As Jing Zhaoyin, he recruited thief leaders to capture others, arresting hundreds in one day. He was known for his wit and leniency, but his frivolous behavior, like thrushing his wife's eyebrows, drew criticism. He served for nine years but was dismissed after killing a subordinate. He later governed Jizhou and Taiyuan, dying of illness. His brother Zhang Wu also governed Liang effectively.

Wang Zun, from Zhuo Commandery, began as a prison clerk and studied the classics. As magistrate of Guo County, he executed an unfilial son by shooting. As Governor of Anding, he issued strict notices and killed the corrupt Zhang Fu, shocking the commandery. He was dismissed for excessive killings but later served as Governor of Yizhou, famously scolding his horse at a dangerous pass. As Prime Minister of Dongping, he confronted the arrogant king, who eventually avoided him. He impeached Prime Minister Kuang Heng for misconduct but was demoted. As Jing Zhaoyin, he eliminated bandits in Nanshan and executed powerful criminals, but was dismissed again. He later prevented a Yellow River flood by personally staying on the embankment, earning a promotion. He died in office, remembered by the people.

Wang Zhang, from Taishan, was a blunt admonisher who slandered Zhongshu Ling Shi Xian, leading to his dismissal. Recalled by Emperor Cheng, he became Sili Xiaowei and later Jing Zhaoyin. He accused General Wang Feng of monopolizing power, leading to his execution for treason despite his wife's warnings. His family was exiled but later returned. He served for two years and was mourned by the people. The text concludes that Zhao Guanghan, Han Yanshou, Yin Wenggui, Zhang Chang, Wang Zun, and Wang Zhang were notable officials, though some met tragic ends due to their integrity or conflicts with power.
