Jia Yi, a native of Luoyang, was renowned for his literary talents at eighteen. Governor Wu Gong of Henan summoned him after hearing of his abilities. When Emperor Wen of Han ascended the throne, he appointed Wu Gong as a court captain due to his excellent governance and connection to Li Si. Wu Gong recommended Jia Yi, who was then made a doctor at around twenty years old, the youngest among them. Jia Yi excelled in court discussions, impressing the emperor, who promoted him to Taizhong doctor within a year. Jia Yi proposed reforming the calendar, legal system, and rituals, drafting regulations with yellow as the noble color and a five-character seal. Emperor Wen hesitated to implement these changes, but many legal reforms and the relocation of princes were Jia Yi's ideas.

Jealous officials slandered Jia Yi, leading to his demotion as Tutor to the Prince of Changsha. On his journey, he wrote a poem mourning Qu Yuan, comparing their fates. The poem lamented Qu Yuan's unjust exile and suicide, criticizing a world that values the worthless over the worthy. Jia Yi advised retreat and self-preservation, using metaphors of dragons and phoenixes to urge distancing from corruption. He argued that Qu Yuan should have sought a wise ruler rather than cling to Chu.

In his third year in Changsha, an owl, an unlucky bird, entered Jia Yi's room. He wrote a poem exploring fate and fortune, reflecting on the cyclical nature of life and the futility of human concerns. The poem discusses the interplay of fortune and misfortune, using examples like Li Si and Fu Yue to illustrate unpredictability. It advocates detachment from worldly desires, embracing the Dao, and accepting life's transience. Jia Yi concluded that the owl's omen was trivial.

Emperor Wen later recalled Jia Yi, discussing ghosts and gods late into the night. Impressed, he appointed Jia Yi as Tutor to his favored son, King Huai of Liang. Jia Yi frequently advised on state affairs, highlighting crises: the Xiongnu threat, rebellious princes, and corrupt customs. He argued that the realm was not truly peaceful, comparing it to sleeping on a pyre. He proposed dividing large fiefdoms to weaken princes, using historical examples to show that power leads to rebellion. He urged the emperor to adopt a system of smaller states to ensure stability, warning that inaction would lead to disaster.

Jia Yi criticized the Xiongnu policy of appeasement, calling it humiliating and ineffective. He offered to subdue them through strategy. He also condemned lavish dress among commoners, which drained resources and undermined social hierarchy. He lamented the decline of moral standards, tracing it to Qin's abandonment of propriety. He advocated for early education of the crown prince, citing the long reigns of Xia, Shang, and Zhou, which prioritized ritual and moral instruction, unlike Qin's harsh laws that led to rapid collapse.

Jia Yi emphasized the importance of ritual over punishment, arguing that ritual prevents wrongdoing subtly, while punishment only reacts. He urged the emperor to respect ministers, using historical examples to show that honor fosters loyalty. He advised against humiliating officials, as it erodes their integrity. He used the case of Zhou Bo, falsely accused of treason, to argue for leniency. Emperor Wen heeded this, and thereafter, guilty ministers committed suicide rather than face execution.

Jia Yi later proposed strengthening the kingdoms of Huaiyang and Liang to counter powerful princes like Huainan. He warned against granting titles to the sons of the rebellious King Li of Huainan, predicting they would seek revenge. Emperor Wen partially adopted his advice, appointing Liu Wu as King of Liang. After Jia Yi's death at thirty-three, his predictions came true: the Seven Kingdoms rebelled, but Liang's strength helped suppress them. Emperor Wu later executed some of King Li's sons for rebellion.

The historian praised Jia Yi's insights on governance, comparing him to Yi Yin and Guan Zhong. Though his ideas were only partially implemented, his writings on statecraft were influential. His early death prevented him from reaching high office, but his legacy endured through his fifty-eight essays.
