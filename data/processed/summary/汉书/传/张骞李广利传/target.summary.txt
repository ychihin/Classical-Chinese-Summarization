Zhang Qian, a native of Hanzhong, was appointed as Lang Guan during the Jianyuan period. Hearing from surrendered Xiongnu that the Yuezhi had been defeated by the Xiongnu and sought allies for revenge, the Han Dynasty, planning to attack the Xiongnu, recruited envoys to the Yuezhi. Zhang Qian was recruited and departed with Gan Fu, a servant from Tangyi. Captured by the Xiongnu, he was detained for over ten years, married, and had a son, but he retained his Han envoy status. Escaping westward, he reached Dawan, which aided him due to interest in Han wealth. He was sent via Kangju to Dayuezhi, but the Yuezhi, now settled in fertile Daxia, had no desire for revenge. After over a year without a clear response, Zhang Qian returned but was recaptured by the Xiongnu. He escaped after a year during Xiongnu turmoil, returning to Han with his wife and Gan Fu. He was appointed Taizhong Taifu.

Zhang Qian, known for his perseverance and sincerity, visited Dawan, Dayuezhi, Daxia, and Kangju. He reported to Emperor Wu that Daxia had Shu products, suggesting Shendu was near Shu. He proposed a route via Shu to Daxia, avoiding dangerous Qiang and Xiongnu territories. Emperor Wu, intrigued by the wealth of western states, sent envoys to explore Shu routes, but they were blocked by local tribes. Zhang Qian later joined General Wei Qing against the Xiongnu, becoming Marquis of Bowang. After a failed campaign with Li Guang, he was reduced to commoner status. Following Han victories over the Xiongnu, Zhang Qian advised allying with Wusun to weaken the Xiongnu and recruit western states. Emperor Wu agreed, sending Zhang Qian with gifts to Wusun. Though Wusun did not commit, Zhang Qian sent deputies to other states. Wusun later sent envoys to Han, and Zhang Qian died over a year after returning.

After Zhang Qian's missions, Han envoys frequently traveled to the Western Regions, often exaggerating to gain favor. Emperor Wu, seeking Dawan horses, sent Li Guangli as General of Ershi to attack Dawan. The first campaign failed due to food shortages, and Li Guangli was ordered to halt at Dunhuang. Despite opposition, Emperor Wu launched a massive second campaign with 60,000 troops, 100,000 cattle, and 30,000 horses. After a 40-day siege, Dawan nobles killed their king and offered horses, ending the war. Li Guangli was made Marquis of Haixi. The campaigns opened routes to the Western Regions, with Han establishing Jiuquan County and building fortresses. Envoys continued, but conflicts arose with small states like Loulan and Gushi, leading to Han military actions. The Han Dynasty's influence expanded, though some states remained distant.
