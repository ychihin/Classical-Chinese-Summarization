The Xiongnu, whose ancestor Chunwei was a descendant of the Xia dynasty, were a nomadic people living in the northern border of China. They raised horses, cattle, sheep, and exotic livestock, migrating with water and grass without permanent cities or agriculture. They had no written language, relying on oral agreements. Men learned archery from youth, and in adulthood served as armored cavalry. Their customs prioritized the strong, who ate well while the elderly ate leftovers. Upon a father's death, a son married his stepmother; upon a brother's death, the surviving brother married the widow. They had no naming taboos or posthumous titles.

The text traces the history of interactions between the Xiongnu and various Chinese dynasties, beginning with the Zhou dynasty. The Zhou declined, and Rong and Di tribes frequently attacked. King Xuan of Zhou attacked the Xiongnu, but later King You was killed by Quan Rong. Qin and Jin became powerful, with Duke Mu of Qin conquering Xirong and Duke Wen of Jin expelling Rong Di. During the Warring States period, Qin, Zhao, and Yan built walls to resist the Xiongnu. Qin Shihuang sent Meng Tian to attack the Xiongnu, recovering land south of the Yellow River and building the Great Wall. After Meng Tian's death, the Xiongnu regained strength under Chanyu Touman.

Touman's son, Maodun, trained his cavalry with a signaling arrow, killing his father and establishing himself as Chanyu. Maodun defeated the Donghu, drove away the Yuezhi, and annexed Loufan and the Aries King of Henan, becoming powerful with over 300,000 warriors. The Xiongnu's official structure included left and right kings, generals, and governors, with the Chanyu's surname being Luanti. They held annual meetings, had strict laws, and practiced burial with concubines. Maodun later conquered northern countries, and the Han dynasty, under Emperor Gao, made peace through marriage alliances and tribute.

During the reign of Empress Dowager Lu, Maodun sent an insulting letter, but she responded diplomatically. Emperor Wen continued marriage alliances, though the Xiongnu frequently invaded. The Xiongnu Chanyu wrote to the Han, claiming to have conquered the Yuezhi and other states, and sought peace. The Han agreed, sending gifts. After Maodun's death, his son Laoshang Chanyu succeeded. A eunuch, Zhongxing, defected to the Xiongnu and taught them Chinese customs, advising against adopting Han ways. He argued that Xiongnu simplicity and mobility were strengths, while Han complexity led to internal strife.

Under Emperor Wu, the Han attempted to ambush the Xiongnu at Mayi but failed, leading to open conflict. The Han sent multiple campaigns, with generals like Wei Qing and Huo Qubing achieving victories, killing tens of thousands of Xiongnu and capturing their kings. The Xiongnu suffered heavy losses and fled north. The Han also expanded into the Western Regions, isolating the Xiongnu from allies. Despite peace negotiations, the Xiongnu continued border raids. The Han detained Xiongnu envoys, and the Xiongnu retaliated.

Later, the Xiongnu faced internal strife and external attacks from Dingling, Wuhuan, and Wusun, leading to decline. The Han sent multiple expeditions, further weakening the Xiongnu. The Xiongnu Chanyu sought peace, but the Han demanded the prince as a hostage, which the Xiongnu refused. The Xiongnu continued to raid, but the Han's military campaigns and alliances with Wusun and other states devastated them. The Xiongnu suffered from famine, disease, and livestock losses, with many tribes rebelling or surrendering to the Han.

The text concludes with the Xiongnu's continued decline, internal power struggles, and eventual submission to the Han. The Xiongnu Chanyu's lineage and official titles are recorded, highlighting their complex relationship with the Han dynasty, marked by periods of war, peace, and tribute.
