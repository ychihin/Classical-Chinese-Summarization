Emperor Xiaowen had four sons. Empress Dou bore Emperor Xiaojing and Liu Wu, King Xiao of Liang, while Zhu Ji bore Liu Shen, Prince Xiao, and Liu Yi, King Huai of Liang. Liu Wu was initially made King of Dai, later transferred to Huaiyang, and finally to Liang in the twelfth year of Emperor Xiaowen’s reign, serving as king for eleven years. He frequently entered the court, and during one banquet, Emperor Xiaojing casually offered to pass the throne to him, which Liu Wu declined but was pleased by. In the spring of the twenty-fourth year, the seven kingdoms of Wu, Chu, Qi, and others rebelled, attacking Liang. Liu Wu defended Suiyang with generals Han Anguo and Zhang Yu, holding off Wu and Chu for three months until they were defeated. Liang’s military exploits and proximity to the emperor made it a large, fertile kingdom with over forty cities.

Liu Wu, favored by the Queen Mother, built a grand garden and palace, amassed vast wealth, and recruited heroes like Yang Sheng, Gongsun Gui, and Zou Yang. He manufactured weapons and stored treasures exceeding the capital’s. In the twenty-ninth year, he entered the court and was honored by Emperor Jing, riding together and hunting. After the emperor deposed Prince Li, the Queen Mother wanted Liu Wu to succeed, but ministers like Yuan An dissuaded the emperor, and Liu Wu left. Later, Liu Wu conspired to assassinate Yuan An and others, but the plot failed. The emperor investigated, and Liu Wu’s men hid. After pressure, Liu Wu ordered them to commit suicide. The emperor grew distant, and Liu Wu, in panic, apologized through the eldest princess. He later hid in the princess’s garden, causing the Queen Mother to weep, but reconciled with the emperor. However, Emperor Jing stopped riding with him.

In the thirty-fifth year, Liu Wu entered the court but was denied stay, becoming unhappy. He died of fever on June 6 after hunting and seeing a strange cow. He was filial, often wanting to stay with the Queen Mother when she was ill. Her grief led the emperor to divide Liang into five kingdoms for Liu Wu’s sons and grant land to his daughters. Liu Wu’s wealth was immense, with over 400,000 kilograms of gold at death.

Liu Shen, King of Taiyuan, visited court three times before dying. His descendants ruled until Liu Nian was deposed for incest with his sister. Liu Yi, King Huai of Liang, died from a fall, leaving no heirs. Liu Wu’s five sons were all made kings on the same day. Liu Mai, King Gong of Liang, died after seven years. Liu Ming was deposed for murder. Peng Li, King of Jidong, was deposed for robbery and murder. Liu Ding, King Ai of Shanyang, died without heirs. Another son died without issue.

Liu Xiang, King Ping of Liang, favored Queen Ren, who coveted a wine bottle left by Liu Wu. Queen Mother Li refused, but Liu Xiang took it. Conflicts arose, and Queen Mother Li’s adultery was exposed. After her death, Queen Ren failed to mourn. During the Yuanshuo period, a fugitive revealed palace scandals. The emperor investigated, found Queen Ren guilty, and reduced Liang’s territory. Liu Xiang died after forty years. His descendants ruled until Liu Li, King Huang, committed adultery with his aunt and murdered officials. Despite multiple crimes, the emperor showed mercy, only reducing territory. Liu Li later committed suicide after being deposed. The kingdom was eventually abolished.
