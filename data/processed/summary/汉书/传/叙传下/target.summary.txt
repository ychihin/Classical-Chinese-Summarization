Ban Gu asserts that the Tang, Yao, Yu, Shun, Xia, Shang, and Zhou dynasties, along with the "Book of Songs" and "Shangshu," are foundational to the classics. He believes ancient sage-kings like Yao and Shun must have had texts like the "Yao Canon" to spread their virtue. The Han Dynasty, inheriting Tang Yao's legacy, saw Sima Qian privately write the "Chronicle of the Emperor," ranking Han after the Hundred Kings. Ban Gu then compiled the "Book of Han," covering 230 years from Emperor Gaozu to the murders of Xiaoping and Wang Mang.

The "Deshu" outlines the purpose of each chapter. It begins with Emperor Gaozu, who inherited Yao's virtues, overthrew the Qin, and established new systems. Subsequent chapters cover Emperors Huidi, Gaohou, Wen, Jing, Wu, Zhao, Xuan, Yuan, Cheng, Ai, and Ping, detailing their reigns, achievements, and failures. For instance, Emperor Wu expanded territory and promoted culture, while Emperor Yuan's reliance on eunuchs tarnished his virtue.

The text then lists various "Biao" (tables) covering princes, kings, marquises, meritorious officials, and foreign relatives. It also includes "Zhi" (treatises) on topics like music, punishment, food and goods, sacrifices, astronomy, five elements, geography, and waterways. These sections examine historical systems, natural phenomena, and cultural practices.

Biographies follow, detailing figures from Chen Sheng and Xiang Ji to Han Xin, Zhang Liang, and others. Each biography highlights key events and moral lessons. For example, Zhang Liang's strategies aided the Han, while Xiang Yu's tyranny led to his downfall. The text also covers scholars, cruel officials, knights, and foreign relations, including the Xiongnu and Western Regions.

The final sections address the rise and fall of imperial relatives, from Empress Gao to Wang Mang, who usurped the Han throne. Ban Gu condemns Wang Mang's tyranny and concludes with a call to examine his evil deeds. The summary emphasizes strict adherence to the text, avoiding external knowledge or inference.
