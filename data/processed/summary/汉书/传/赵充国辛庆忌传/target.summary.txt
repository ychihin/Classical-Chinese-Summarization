Zhao Chongguo, styled Wengsun, was from Shangbang in Longxi and later moved to Jincheng Lingju. He began as a knight, was promoted to a guard of the feather forest for his skill in horseback riding and archery, and was known for his calm courage and strategic depth. He studied military strategy and became familiar with barbarian affairs. Under Emperor Wu, he served as a fake Sima under the Second Division General against the Xiongnu, breaking through an enemy siege with over 100 strong men despite heavy casualties and over 20 wounds. Emperor Wu personally honored him, appointing him Zhonglang and later General of Chariots and Cavalry. Under Emperor Zhao, he quelled a Di rebellion in Wudu, was promoted to Zhonglang General, and later captured Xiongnu King Xiqi, becoming a post-general. He supported Emperor Xuan’s ascension with Huo Guang and was named Yingpinghou. During the Benshi period, he led a campaign against the Xiongnu, killing hundreds, and was appointed Hou General and Shaofu.

When the Xiongnu mobilized over 100,000 cavalry near the border, Zhao Chongguo led 40,000 cavalry to garrison nine counties, causing the Shanyu to retreat. He later impeached Yiqu Anguo for disrespect after Anguo mishandled Qiang affairs. In the third year of Yuankang, Qiang chief Xianling formed alliances, and Zhao Chongguo warned of potential rebellion, citing past Qiang uprisings and Xiongnu collusion. He advised preparations, but Anguo’s subsequent actions—killing over 30 Qiang leaders and attacking their tribes—provoked a widespread Qiang revolt. Zhao Chongguo, over 70, was tasked with leading the campaign. He insisted on personal command, saying “seeing once is better than hearing a hundred times,” and Emperor Xuan agreed.

At Jincheng, Zhao Chongguo crossed the Yellow River cautiously, avoiding enemy traps. He prioritized reconnaissance, fortifications, and soldier welfare, refusing to engage in small skirmishes. His son Zhao Yin led reinforcements, but Zhao Chongguo advocated a strategy of kindness and power, recruiting Qiang tribes like Han and Kai to disrupt enemy plans. Emperor Xuan assembled 60,000 troops, but Zhao Chongguo opposed a proposed attack by Xin Wuxian, arguing it risked logistical failure and enemy ambush. He recommended a defensive approach, focusing on farming and waiting for the enemy to weaken. Despite imperial pressure, Zhao Chongguo submitted a memorial detailing a plan to withdraw cavalry and station 10,281 soldiers for farming, listing twelve benefits including cost savings, enemy attrition, and border security. Emperor Xuan eventually approved his strategy.

Zhao Chongguo’s campaign succeeded: over 10,000 Qiang surrendered, and by the following year, only about 4,000 enemies remained. He was praised for his foresight, though his son Zhao Yin committed suicide after a dispute. Zhao Chongguo retired with honors and died at 86. His portrait was placed in Weiyang Palace, and a eulogy by Yang Xiong celebrated his achievements. The text also covers Xin Wuxian and his son Xin Qingji, noting their military roles and eventual decline under Wang Mang. The commentary reflects on the martial traditions of western regions like Tianshui and Longxi, attributing the prevalence of generals there to proximity to Qiang and Hu peoples.
