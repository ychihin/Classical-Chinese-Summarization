The text recounts the histories of three prominent figures from the Six Kingdoms period: Wei Bao, Tian Dan, and Han Wangxin, detailing their rise, fall, and the fates of their descendants.

Wei Bao, son of the Wei state, saw his brother Wei Jiu made king by Chen Sheng after the Qin collapse. Wei Jiu later surrendered to Qin to save his people and committed suicide. Wei Bao fled to Chu, recaptured Wei lands with King Huai's aid, and was made King of Wei by Xiang Yu. After Xiang Yu moved him to Hedong, Wei Bao initially surrendered to the Han king but later betrayed him, blocking the Yellow River. The Han king sent Han Xin to capture him, and Wei Bao was eventually killed at Rongyang by Zhou Ke, who deemed him untrustworthy.

Tian Dan, a member of the Qi royal clan, declared himself king after killing a county magistrate and expanding Qi's territory. He died in battle against Qin's Zhang Han while aiding Wei. His cousin Tian Rong later made Tian Dan's son Tian Shi king, but conflicts arose with Xiang Yu over allegiances. Tian Rong was killed, and his brother Tian Heng restored Qi, making Tian Rong's son Tian Guang king. Tian Heng ruled Qi for three years until Han Xin's invasion. After a series of battles, Tian Guang was captured, and Tian Heng fled to the sea with 500 followers. Summoned by Emperor Gaozu of Han, Tian Heng committed suicide near Luoyang out of shame, and his followers also died, demonstrating his virtue.

Han Wangxin, grandson of the original Han king, was made King of Han by Xiang Liang. He later followed the Han king, was captured by Chu, but escaped and was reinstated. After the Han dynasty was established, Emperor Gaozu moved him to Taiyuan to resist the Xiongnu. Suspected of treason for negotiating with the Xiongnu, Han Wangxin surrendered to them and attacked Han. The emperor personally campaigned against him, and after a siege at Pingcheng, the Xiongnu retreated. Han Wangxin continued raiding until he was killed by General Chai in Shenhe County. His descendants, including Han Dangdang and Han Ying, later surrendered to Han and were granted titles, with some serving as generals and officials through subsequent reigns.

The text concludes with a reflection that while Wei Bao, Tian Dan, and Han Wangxin were descendants of the Six Kingdoms, their lineages ended. Tian Heng's integrity and his followers' loyalty did not lead to a lasting state, suggesting divine will. The Han family's resurgence through the Marquis of Gonggao is noted as possibly linked to ancient lineage.
