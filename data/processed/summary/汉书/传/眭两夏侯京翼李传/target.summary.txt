# Summary of Historical Records on Classical Chinese Scholars and Their Interpretations of Omens

Suihong, courtesy name Meng, was from the state of Lu. Initially fond of cockfighting and horse racing, he later transformed his character and studied the "Spring and Autumn" with Ying Gong. Through his mastery of Confucian classics, he became a Yilang and reached the position of Fuli Jieliang.

In the first month of the third year of Emperor Zhao's Yuanfeng reign, a great roar was heard south of Laiwu Mountain in Mount Tai. A large stone stood up by itself, measuring one to five feet high, as thick as forty-eight arm spans, and eight feet deep into the ground, with three other stones serving as its feet. Thousands of white crows gathered around it. Simultaneously, dead trees in the Changyi Shrine revived, and a broken willow in Shanglin Garden stood up and regained life, with insects eating its leaves into shapes resembling the words "Gongsun Bing has been established." Suihong interpreted these events through the "Spring and Autumn," concluding that stones and willows symbolized common people, while Mount Tai represented the site of imperial ceremonies. He argued that these phenomena indicated commoners would become emperors and the deposed Gongsun clan would revive. He advocated for the Han emperor to abdicate in favor of a worthy successor. His friend, an internal official, submitted this memorial. Since Emperor Zhao was young and General Huo Guang governed, Huo Guang was angered and had Suihong and his friend executed for treason. Five years later, when Emperor Xiaoxuan rose from the people to the throne, he recruited Suihong's son as a Lang.

Xiahou Shichang, from Lu, was proficient in the Five Classics and taught Qi Shi and Shangshu. After Dong Zhongshu and Han Ying died, Emperor Wu valued him greatly. Shichang understood yin and yang, once predicting the exact date of a fire at Bailiang Terrace. He served as tutor to King Changyi and died of old age. His nephew Xiahou Sheng, courtesy name Changgong, was also a renowned Confucian scholar. Xiahou Sheng lost his father young but studied diligently, learning "Shang Shu" and "Hong Fan's Five Elements" from Shichang, and later studied under Jian Qing and Ouyang. He was sophisticated and learned from multiple teachers.

When King Changyi succeeded Emperor Zhao, he frequently traveled. Xiahou Sheng once blocked the king's carriage to remonstrate, warning that prolonged overcast weather without rain indicated ministers plotting against the emperor. The king was furious and had him bound, but Huo Guang did not punish him. When Huo Guang and Zhang Anshi were discussing deposing the king, Xiahou Sheng explained his prediction came from the "Hong Fan" biography, which stated that when an emperor rules improperly, subordinates will rebel. Huo Guang and Anshi were impressed and valued Confucian scholars more thereafter. They eventually petitioned the Queen Mother to depose King Changyi and establish Emperor Xuan. Xiahou Sheng was appointed Shaofu of Changxin and given the title of Marquis of Guannei.

When Emperor Xuan wanted to honor Emperor Wu's achievements, Xiahou Sheng opposed, arguing that although Emperor Wu defeated barbarians and expanded territory, he killed many ministers, exhausted resources, and caused widespread suffering and cannibalism. The ministers impeached Xiahou Sheng for slandering the late emperor, and he was imprisoned. After an earthquake killed over six thousand people, Emperor Xuan issued a general amnesty, and Xiahou Sheng was pardoned and served as an admonition official. He was known for his forthrightness, addressing the emperor as "king" and calling colleagues by name in court. Emperor Xuan trusted him for this honesty. Xiahou Sheng died in office at ninety, and the Queen Mother mourned him for five days.

Jingfang, courtesy name Junming, studied "The Book of Changes" under Jiao Yanshou. Jiao Gan, his teacher, was known for predicting evil events and governing Xiaohuang County well. Jingfang was particularly skilled in connecting hexagrams with daily events and using meteorological changes as symptoms. During Emperor Yuan's reign, he predicted events accurately and was frequently consulted. He warned about disasters and criticized the appointment of flattering officials like Shi Xian. Jingfang recommended implementing a system to inspect officials' performance, but faced opposition from powerful ministers. He was eventually appointed prefect of Wei County but feared being isolated from the emperor. After sending several sealed memorials warning about yin energy overwhelming yang and predicting disasters, he was imprisoned and executed at age forty-one.

Yi Feng, courtesy name Shaojun, studied "Qi Shi" and was diligent in learning, preferring calendar and yin-yang divination to officialdom. Emperor Yuan respected him greatly. Yi Feng wrote extensively about using the twelve laws to understand human emotions and predict disasters. He warned about the dangers of powerful relatives and advocated for frugality, criticizing excessive palace construction. He proposed moving the capital to Chengzhou to reform institutions and reduce expenses. Although Emperor Yuan found his views unusual, Yi Feng became a doctor and admonishing official, dying in old age.

Li Xun, courtesy name Zichang, studied "Shangshu" and liked theories of disasters and strange phenomena. He served under various officials and warned about impending floods and chaos. During Emperor Ai's reign, he was summoned to discuss disasters and advised restraining relatives, selecting virtuous officials, and following seasonal orders. He later supported Gan Zhongke's forged texts about the Han dynasty's fate declining, advocating for changing the reign name to prolong the dynasty. Emperor Ai, ill for a long time, agreed and issued an edict changing the reign name to Taichu. When this had no effect, He Liang and others were executed for treason, and Li Xun was exiled to Dunhuang County.

The historian's commentary notes that since the Han dynasty's founding, those who discussed disasters and yin-yang differences included Dong Zhongshu, Xiahou Shichang, Suimeng, Xiahou Sheng, Jingfang, Yifeng, Liu Xiang, Gu Yong, Li Xun, and Tian Zhongshu. Their theories were sometimes adopted by rulers but often led to imprisonment or death. Suimeng was brutally killed, and Li Xun was exiled—warnings for scholars who spoke frankly about current affairs, offending powerful ministers and meeting tragic ends.
