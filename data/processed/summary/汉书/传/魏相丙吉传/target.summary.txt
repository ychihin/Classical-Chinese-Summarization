Wei Xiang, courtesy name Wenweng, was a native of Dingtao, Jiyin, later moving to Pingling. He studied the Book of Changes in his youth, became a county governor, and was praised for his virtue. After ranking first in policy questions, he became magistrate of Maoling County. When a guest of Censor Sang Hongyang falsely claimed Sang was arriving and tied up the magistrate, Wei Xiang suspected foul play, arrested the guest, tortured him to reveal his crime, and executed him in the street, leading to excellent governance in Maoling. Promoted to governor of Henan, he prohibited evil deeds, instilling fear in local powers. When Prime Minister Che Qianqiu died, his son, commander of the Luoyang Armory, resigned fearing Wei Xiang’s strictness. Wei Xiang regretted this, fearing it would cause resentment. General Huo Guang later scolded Wei Xiang for dismissing the prime minister’s son after his death, calling it shallow. Accused of killing an innocent person, Wei Xiang faced investigation, but over 10,000 Henan residents petitioned for him. Huo Guang handed him to Tingwei for punishment, but he was pardoned and reappointed as magistrate of Maoling, then governor of Yangzhou. After demotions in an imperial inspection, Wei Xiang, close to Bingji, heeded his advice to restrain his dignity. He served as governor for two years, then became an admonishing official and later governor of Henan again. After Emperor Xuan ascended, Wei Xiang became chief minister of agriculture, then imperial censor. Four years later, Huo Guang died. Wei Xiang wrote to the emperor, warning against the Huo family’s power, citing historical precedents of autocratic rule causing chaos. He suggested weakening their influence to strengthen the Han dynasty. Emperor Xuan adopted his advice, deposing Huo Yu, Huo Yun, and Huo Shan. Wei Xiang became prime minister, replacing Wei Xian, and was named Gaopinghou. The Huo family later plotted to kill him but were discovered and executed. Emperor Xuan then personally governed, selecting good ministers, and Wei Xiang was popular as prime minister. During the Yuankang period, when the Xiongnu attacked Han forces, Wei Xiang advised against military retaliation, citing border poverty and internal social issues like 222 cases of familial killings. He urged focus on domestic stability. Emperor Xuan followed his advice. Wei Xiang, well-versed in the Book of Changes, studied Han history and advocated following past systems. He presented 23 items from the late emperor’s edicts, emphasizing agriculture, grain storage, and frugality to prepare for disasters. He warned of current grain price hikes and border conflicts, urging the emperor to care for the people. Emperor Xuan implemented his suggestions. Wei Xiang also presented chapters on Yin-Yang and seasonal governance, arguing that proper seasonal management prevents disasters and ensures prosperity. He recommended selecting ministers knowledgeable in Yin-Yang to oversee seasonal affairs. The emperor adopted his proposals. Wei Xiang’s commissioners reported irregularities from counties, often revealing unreported rebellions or disasters. He was severe and resolute, unlike the tolerant Bingji. He served as prime minister for nine years, dying in the third year of the Shenjue era, and was posthumously named Xianhou. His son Wei Hong inherited the title but was demoted to Marquis of Guannei during the Ganlu era due to a crime.
