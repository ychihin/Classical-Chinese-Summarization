The Ban family, originally sharing the surname of Chu, descended from Ling Yin Ziwen. Ziwen, abandoned as an infant, was nursed by a tiger, leading to his name "Guyugan" from Chu words for nursing and tiger. The tiger was called "Ban," and his son adopted this as their surname. After Qin conquered Chu, the family moved between Jin and Dai, taking Ban as their surname. During the late Qin, Ban Yi fled to Loufan, amassing livestock. In early Han, the Bans dominated border areas with wealth, hunting and living lavishly, with Ban Yi living over a hundred years.

Ban Yi's son, Ban Ru, was praised for chivalry. His son, the squad leader, became Shanggu Prefect. Ban Hui, the squad leader's son, became a county magistrate. Ban Kuang, Ban Hui's son, served as a Lang and rose to Captain of Henong, later retiring with wealth to Changling. He had three sons: Banbo, Banbo, and Banzhi. Banbo studied the Book of Songs, was recommended as an attendant, and became a regular attendant. He lectured on classics, was promoted to Fengche Captain, and later requested to govern Dingxiang, where he captured all thieves within ten days. He was summoned back, suffered a stroke, and died at thirty-eight.

Ban Zhen, knowledgeable and talented, served as Yilang and collated books. Ban Zhi, upright and clean, served as Huangmen Langzhong. During Wang Mang's rise, Ban Zhi avoided presenting carols, and the Ban family remained uninfluential but safe. Ban Zhi's son, Ban Biao, studied with his cousin Ban Si. Ban Si, a Confucian who admired Lao-Zhuang, refused to lend books, fearing Ban Biao would lose his way. Ban Biao devoted himself to sage teachings.

During the chaos of Wang Mang's fall, Wei Xiao asked Ban Biao about the likelihood of another Warring States period. Ban Biao argued that the Han dynasty's centralized system prevented such fragmentation, and the people's hearts remained with Han. He wrote "The Theory of King's Mandate," asserting that the Liu family's rise was destined by heaven, not human effort. He fled to Hexi, where Dou Rong admired him. Ban Biao had a son, Ban Gu, who wrote "Ode to You Tong" to express his aspirations.

During the Yongping period, Ban Gu served as Lang, collating books. When ridiculed for his focus on writing, he responded that in peaceful times, unlike the chaotic Warring States, one should not seek fame through deceit. He argued that the Han dynasty's stability made such pursuits unnecessary, and true virtue and writing were the proper path.
