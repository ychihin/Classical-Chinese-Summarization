Yan An, from Linmiao, wrote a letter as the late Prime Minister Shi, criticizing the extravagance of the wealthy, who decorated their carriages, clothes, and palaces excessively, leading to social ills like greed, lawlessness, and moral decay. He argued that luxury without restraint causes dissatisfaction, drives people from their proper occupations, and fosters treachery and violence. Yan An proposed laws to curb extravagance, reduce envy, and promote harmony, which would lead to peace, reduced crime, and natural prosperity. He contrasted the Zhou Dynasty's long rule with its decline due to weak leadership, leading to the Warring States period and the rise of the Five Hegemons, who upheld the emperor's authority. The Qin Dynasty, under Emperor Qin Shi Huang, unified China but failed to reform its harsh laws and heavy taxes, instead expanding aggressively against the Xiongnu and Baiyue, causing widespread suffering and rebellion. Yan An warned the Han court against similar overreach, advocating for domestic stability over foreign conquests, and noted that the Zhou's weakness and Qin's strength both led to their downfalls due to inflexibility.

Zhongjun, from Jinan, was a talented scholar who became Emperor Wu's envoy. He wrote a response to Emperor Wu's discovery of a white unicorn and a strange tree, interpreting them as auspicious signs of the Han dynasty's virtue and the submission of foreign peoples. He advised the emperor to perform sacrifices and change the reign name, which Emperor Wu did, renaming it Yuanshou. Later, Zhongjun interrogated Dr. Xu Yan for falsely claiming imperial orders to operate salt and iron in Jiaodong and Lu, exposing his deceit and securing his conviction. Emperor Wu praised Zhongjun's handling of the case. Zhongjun also volunteered to envoy to the Xiongnu and South Vietnam, famously requesting a "long tassel" to bind the King of South Vietnam. He successfully persuaded the king to submit to Han rule, but was killed by the South Vietnamese prime minister Lu Jia during a rebellion.

Wang Bao, from Shu, was a poet summoned by Emperor Xuan to compose songs and odes. He wrote an ode praising wise rulers and ministers, using metaphors like the Ganjiang sword and fine horses to illustrate the harmony between talent and leadership. He argued that virtuous ministers naturally appear under sage kings, citing historical examples like the Duke of Zhou and Duke Huan of Qi. Emperor Xuan enjoyed Wang Bao's works, which included references to immortals, and promoted him to advisory doctor. Wang Bao also served the prince during illness, reading his compositions to cheer him. He died while on a mission to Yizhou to obtain treasures.

Jia Ganzhi, great-grandson of Jia Yi, advised Emperor Yuan against attacking Zhuli County (in modern Hainan), which had repeatedly rebelled since its establishment. He argued that the territory was not worth the cost, citing historical precedents like Yao, Shun, and the Zhou Dynasty, which did not forcibly conquer distant lands. He contrasted Emperor Wen's frugality and peace with Emperor Wu's costly expansions, which led to widespread suffering and rebellion. Emperor Yuan accepted his advice and abandoned Zhuli County. Later, Jia Ganzhi and Yang Xing, a favored official, conspired to recommend each other for high positions, but Shi Xian reported their scheme. They were tried for deceit and leaking palace secrets; Jia Ganzhi was executed, and Yang Xing was reduced to penal servitude.
