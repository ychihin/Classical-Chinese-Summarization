Gongsun Hong, born in Xue County, Sichuan State, began his career as a jailer but was dismissed for a crime. Left impoverished, he herded pigs until his forties, when he started studying the "Spring and Autumn" and various philosophical texts. At sixty, he was recruited as a virtuous and literary person under Emperor Wu of Han, but his mission to the Huns displeased the emperor, leading to his dismissal. In the fifth year of Yuanguang, he was recommended again and, despite initial reluctance, presented a detailed policy response. His essay emphasized governance through righteousness, trust, and eight principles: appointing by talent, avoiding useless discussions, reducing taxes, not harming agriculture, promoting the virtuous, rewarding merit, punishing crime appropriately, and rewarding virtue. He argued that harmony between heaven and earth arises from unified virtue, and that floods and droughts under Yu and Tang were due to prior misrule. His response was ranked first by the emperor, who appointed him as a doctor.

Gongsun Hong rose quickly, becoming Zuo Neishi within a year, known for cautious debate and Confucian decorum. He avoided court confrontations, often aligning with the emperor’s wishes, which drew accusations of hypocrisy from Ji An. Gongsun Hong defended himself, and the emperor favored him more. He served as imperial censor and later prime minister, receiving the title Marquis of Pingjin. Despite his high rank, he lived frugally, eating simple meals and using cloth quilts, spending his salary on guests and relatives. However, he was inwardly vengeful, contributing to the downfall of rivals like Dong Zhongshu. During the Huainan and Hengshan rebellions, he fell ill and offered to resign, but the emperor refused, granting him leave. He died in office at eighty.

Bu Shi, a farmer and herder from Henan, donated half his wealth to support border campaigns against the Xiongnu, but Gongsun Hong advised against accepting, calling it irregular. Later, during a famine, Bu Shi gave 200,000 yuan to aid refugees, earning imperial recognition. He was appointed as a langzhong to herd sheep in Shanglin Garden, where he excelled. The emperor, impressed, made him a magistrate, then tutor to the King of Qi, and eventually prime minister. During the Lu Jia rebellion, Bu Shi offered to fight, but the emperor honored him instead. As imperial censor, he criticized salt and iron taxes, displeasing the emperor, and was demoted.

Ni Kuan, a scholar of "Shangshu," worked as a farmer while studying. He became a Tingwei official, known for his writing and legal expertise. Promoted by Zhang Tang, he served as Zuo Neishi, where he encouraged farming, reduced punishments, and built irrigation canals. When dismissed for poor tax collection, the people paid their debts, restoring his position. He advised on the Fengshan ceremonies, supporting the emperor’s plans, and helped establish the Taichu calendar. As imperial censor, he became complacent and died in office after nine years.

The text concludes that Gongsun Hong, Bu Shi, and Ni Kwan rose from humble origins due to the Han dynasty’s prosperity under Emperor Wu, who recruited diverse talents. This era saw a flourishing of Confucians, loyalists, legalists, writers, and generals, achieving great deeds. Later, under Emperor Xuan, similar talents emerged, but none matched the brilliance of Emperor Wu’s reign.
