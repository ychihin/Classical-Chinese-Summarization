Zhang Cang, from Yangwu, Henan, was a scholar of writing, music, and calendars. He served as a censor under the Qin Dynasty, managing household registries, but fled home after breaking the law. He later joined Pei Gong (Liu Bang) as a guest, and was spared execution after Wang Ling admired his physique. He followed Pei Gong to Xianyang, served as governor of Changshan, and helped capture Chen Yu. He became prime minister of Zhao and Dai, and was made Marquis of Beiping for suppressing a rebellion. As censor-in-chief, he used his expertise in calendars and registries to serve in the prime minister's office. He later became prime minister of Huainan and then censor-in-chief. He corrected the calendar, adopting the Qin system with October as the new year, and advocated for water virtue. He served Wang Ling like a father after being saved by him. He was dismissed after opposing Gongsun Chen's proposal for Han's earth virtue, which was later confirmed by a yellow dragon sighting. He died around age 100, leaving writings on yin-yang and calendars.

Zhou Chang, from Pei, served as a soldier under Pei Gong with his cousin Zhou Ke. Zhou Ke died defending Xingyang against Xiang Yu, and Zhou Chang became censor-in-chief. He was known for his bluntness, once calling Emperor Gaozu a tyrant. He opposed deposing the crown prince, earning gratitude from Empress Lü. He was appointed prime minister of Zhao to protect Prince Ruyi, despite his reluctance. After Gaozu's death, Empress Lü summoned and killed Ruyi; Zhou Chang died of illness three years later. His descendants lost the marquisate.

Ren Ao, from Pei, was a jailer who protected Empress Lü from abuse. He followed Gaozu, became censor-in-chief, and was made Marquis of Guang'a. He was later dismissed.

Shen Tujia, from Liang, rose from a crossbowman to censor-in-chief and then prime minister. He was upright, refusing private visits. He once ordered the execution of the favored courtier Deng Tong for disrespect, but Emperor Wen intervened. As prime minister, he clashed with Chao Cuo over legal changes. When Chao Cuo opened a gate through a temple wall, Shen Tujia sought his execution but was thwarted. He died of anger after being outmaneuvered. Subsequent prime ministers were competent but unremarkable.
