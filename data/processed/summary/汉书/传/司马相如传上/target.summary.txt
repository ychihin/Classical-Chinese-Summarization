# Summary of Sima Xiangru's Biography

Sima Xiangru, courtesy name Changqing, was born in Chengdu, Shu County. As a youth, he studied literature and practiced fencing. After completing his studies, he admired Lin Xiangru's character and changed his name accordingly. He purchased a government position serving Emperor Jing of Han, but found the role unsatisfying. Emperor Jing did not appreciate poetry, so when King Xiao of Liang visited the capital with scholars including Zou Yang and Meicheng, Xiangru associated with them. After King Xiao died, Xiangru returned home impoverished.

His friend Wang Ji, magistrate of Linyin County, invited Xiangru to stay. Xiangru lived outside the city, and though the magistrate visited daily, Xiangru eventually claimed illness and refused to see him, which only increased Wang Ji's respect. Wealthy locals Prince Zhuo and Cheng Yin, noticing the magistrate's distinguished guest, arranged a banquet. When invited, Xiangru initially declined, forcing the magistrate to personally fetch him. At the banquet, Xiangru's demeanor impressed everyone. When the magistrate brought out a zither, Xiangru played several tunes.

Zhuo Wangsun's daughter Wenjun, recently widowed, loved music. Xiangru used the zither to flirt with her. Wenjun secretly watched him from a doorway, admiring him. After the banquet, Xiangru sent gifts to Wenjun's servant to convey his feelings. Wenjun eloped with Xiangru that night, and they fled to Chengdu, where his home was bare except for four walls. Zhuo Wangsun was furious and refused to give them any money. Eventually, Wenjun persuaded Xiangru to return to Linyin, where they sold their possessions and opened a tavern, with Wenjun serving wine. Ashamed, Zhuo Wangsun was persuaded by relatives to give them a hundred slaves, a million in money, and wedding belongings. They returned to Chengdu, bought land, and became wealthy.

Later, Yang Deyi, a native of Shu serving as a dog keeper, praised "Zixu Fu" to Emperor Wu. When the emperor learned Xiangru wrote it, he summoned him. Xiangru confirmed this but offered to compose a hunting rhapsody for the emperor. He created three fictional characters—Zixu, Mr. Wuyou, and a deceased man—to discuss hunting. Zixu boasted of Chu's Yunmeng Marsh, describing its vast mountains, minerals, plants, animals, and the King of Chu's grand hunting expeditions. Mr. Wuyou criticized Zixu for boasting rather than praising virtue. The deceased man then described the emperor's Shanglin Garden, far surpassing anything in Qi or Chu, with its eight rivers, diverse terrain, abundant flora and fauna, and imperial hunts. He concluded by advocating frugality and virtue, warning against excessive luxury. The emperor was pleased and appointed Xiangru as a court gentleman.
