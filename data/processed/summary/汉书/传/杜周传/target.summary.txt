Du Zhou, a native of Du Yan County in Nanyang, rose from being an assistant to Governor Yi Zong to an official historian under Zhang Tang. He gained the emperor's trust by convicting and executing many during investigations of border counties, serving as Zhongcheng for over a decade. Taciturn and slow-tempered but kind-hearted, Du Zhou succeeded Xuan as Tingwei, modeling his governance after Zhang Tang and adeptly reading the emperor's intentions. He would frame those the emperor wished to exclude and prolong imprisonment for those the emperor wished to forgive, secretly revealing their grievances. When criticized for not following established laws, Du Zhou argued that laws were created by monarchs and should adapt to current circumstances. As Tingwei, he handled over a thousand cases annually, with large cases involving hundreds of witnesses. Many fled or hid upon hearing of impending arrests, and cases could drag on for over a decade through multiple amnesties. Prisons held 60,000 to 70,000 criminals, with ordinary officials adding up to 100,000. Du Zhou was later deposed but returned as Jinwu, harshly pursuing thieves and interrogating Sang Hongyang's sons and Queen Wei's brothers. Promoted to imperial censor, his family wealth accumulated to hundreds of millions. His two sons served as governors, but only his youngest, Du Yannian, was kind-hearted.

Du Yannian, well-versed in law, served under Emperor Zhao with General Huo Guang in power. He led Nanyang soldiers to suppress a rebellion in Yizhou and later reported the rebellion of Shangguan Jie and others, earning the title of Marquis of Jianping. As a subordinate of Huo Guang, Yannian advocated lenient punishments. When Hou Shiwu was implicated for harboring Sang Qian, Yannian argued against harsh penalties, balancing the court's harmony. He advised Huo Guang to promote frugality and tolerance, leading to the abolition of monopolies on wine, salt, and iron. During Emperor Zhao's illness, Yannian managed prescriptions. After the emperor's death, he helped establish Emperor Xuan, gaining additional fiefs. Despite being an old member of the Huo family, Yannian was dismissed after Huo Guang's death due to legal violations but later reinstated as governor of Xihe, where he achieved peace. He served as censor during the Wufeng period but resigned due to old age and illness, dying a few months later. His son Du Xu succeeded him as marquis.

Du Xu served as a Langguan, followed General Pulei against the Xiongnu, and later became Taichang. He was known for his kindness, often drinking and saving food during winter verdicts. During Emperor Yuan's reign, he donated money and grain to support the army. Du Xu had six younger brothers, five of whom became senior officials. His brother Du Qin, though blind, was renowned for his knowledge and strategy. Du Qin advised General Wang Feng on establishing a system of nine concubines to ensure heirs and longevity, warning against lust and its consequences. He also addressed solar eclipses and earthquakes as signs of weak yang and strong yin, urging the emperor to restrain female favors and promote frugality. His countermeasures at the White Tiger Palace emphasized filial piety, righteousness, and the importance of knowing and employing people. Du Qin later warned Wang Feng against excessive power, citing historical examples. After Wang Zhang's death, Du Qin advised Wang Feng to clarify the matter to dispel rumors. Du Qin never became an official and died of old age.

Du Ye, Du Qin's nephew, inherited the marquis title and served as Taichang. He criticized Prime Minister Zhai Fangjin for bias and corruption, and later defended Dong Hong's proposal to honor the emperor's mother. Du Ye was demoted to captain of Shangdang and eventually dismissed from office. When Wang Mang came to power, Du Ye, fearing punishment, fell ill and died. The Du family, starting with Du Zhou and Du Yannian, were known as harsh officials but produced virtuous sons. Their lineage continued until the end of the Han Dynasty, with no one surpassing them after the great heroes and scholars.
