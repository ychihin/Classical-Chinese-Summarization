The text traces the lineage of the Sima family, who were descendants of the ancient officials Zhong and Li, responsible for astronomy and geography during the reigns of Zhuanxu, Yao, Shun, and through the Xia and Shang dynasties. During the Western Zhou, their descendant Xiu Fu was named Cheng Bo. By King Xuan of Zhou, they lost their official positions and became the Sima clan, who then served as historians for the Zhou dynasty. During the reigns of King Hui and King Xiang of Zhou, the Sima family moved to Jin, and later scattered to Wei, Zhao, and Qin. In Wei, a descendant became prime minister of Zhongshan; in Zhao, they were known for sword theory; in Qin, Sima Cuo argued with Zhang Yi about conquering Shu, led the campaign, and became governor of Shu. His grandson Sima Qi served under Bai Qi and was involved in the Battle of Changping, after which both were ordered to die. Sima Qi's descendants served as iron officials in Qin, and later Sima Yan was made King of Yin by Xiang Yu. Sima Yan's son Sima Wutang served as mayor of Chang'an in Han, and his descendants included Sima Tan, who became Grand Historian.

Sima Tan studied astronomy, the Book of Changes, and Taoism, and served under Emperor Wu of Han. He worried that scholars misunderstood the six schools of thought—Yin-Yang, Confucianism, Mohism, Legalism, Mingjia, and Taoism—and discussed their principles. He noted that Yin-Yang had many taboos but its seasonal order was useful; Confucianism was broad but lacked essentials, though its rituals were important; Mohism was frugal but hard to follow, yet its emphasis on economy was valuable; Legalism was harsh but upheld the ruler-minister distinction; Mingjia was too formal but its name-reality dialectic was worth considering; and Taoism united spirit and action, absorbed strengths of other schools, and was concise and effective. He criticized Confucianism for tiring the ruler and praised Taoism for its simplicity and adaptability.

Sima Tan had a son, Sima Qian, who was born in Longmen, farmed and herded, and could read ancient texts at ten. At twenty, he traveled extensively, visiting historical sites and studying in Qi and Lu. He became a doctor and was sent on a mission to the southwest. Upon returning, he found his father ill in Luoyang. His father, unable to participate in the imperial Fengshan ceremony, urged Sima Qian to continue their ancestral work as historians, emphasizing the importance of recording history. Sima Qian promised to complete his father's plan. After his father's death, Sima Qian became Grand Historian and began compiling historical records. He later suffered the Li Ling disaster, was imprisoned, and castrated. Despite this, he continued his work, writing the "Records of the Grand Historian" from the Yellow Emperor to Emperor Wu. The text includes a detailed table of contents for the 130 chapters, covering annals, tables, treatises, hereditary houses, and biographies.

Sima Qian wrote a letter to his friend Ren An, explaining his reasons for enduring humiliation rather than committing suicide. He emphasized the importance of completing his historical work, comparing himself to ancient figures like King Wen, Confucius, Qu Yuan, and others who wrote under duress. He argued that death could be heavier than a mountain or lighter than a feather, and that his duty was to finish his book to express his thoughts and preserve history. After his death, his works gradually spread, and his grandson Yang Pang helped publish them. The text concludes with a commentary praising Sima Qian as a talented historian but noting his deviations from orthodox views, such as favoring Huang-Lao over the Six Classics and promoting knights and wealth.
