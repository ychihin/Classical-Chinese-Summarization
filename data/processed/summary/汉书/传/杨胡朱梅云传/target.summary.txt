# Summary of Historical Records

Yang Wangsun, a man from Emperor Wu's reign, studied Huang-Lao teachings and focused on health preservation. When terminally ill, he ordered his son to bury him naked, directly in contact with soil, to return to nature. His son consulted their friend Qi Hou, who argued against this, citing filial piety traditions requiring coffins and burial clothes. Yang Wangsun responded that lavish burials were wasteful and harmful, preventing the dead from returning to their natural state. He argued that the spirit returns to heaven and the body to earth; elaborate burials only create mummies that cannot decompose properly. He insisted on naked burial, which was ultimately carried out.

Hu Jian served as a military official during Emperor Wu's Tianhan period. When a military censor illegally built a shop within the army fortress for profit, Hu Jian gathered his officers, seized and executed the censor on the spot. He submitted a memorial citing military law, arguing the censor's crime warranted death and that military officers could handle such matters without civilian court involvement. The emperor approved his actions, citing the principle that military and civil laws differ. Hu Jian later became magistrate of Weicheng County. During Emperor Zhao's reign, he arrested an assassin hiding in a noble's villa, angering Princess Gaichang and General Shangguan An. After General Huo Guang fell ill, Hu Jian was forced to commit suicide, though people considered his death unjust.

Zhu Yun, originally a chivalrous man, later studied the Book of Changes and Analects. During Emperor Yuan's reign, he debated Wulu Chongzong on the Liangqiu interpretation of the Book of Changes, defeating him soundly. He became a doctor and later magistrate of several counties. He criticized powerful officials including Shi Xian and Prime Minister Wei Xuancheng. After being convicted of murder, he fled and sought help from Chen Xian, who was also imprisoned. During Emperor Cheng's reign, Zhu Yun requested the emperor's sword to kill sycophantic minister Zhang Yu. The emperor was furious and ordered his execution, but Zhu Yun clung to a palace railing, breaking it. General Xin Qingji intervened, and Zhu Yun was spared. He retired, taught students, and died at home in his seventies, requesting simple burial.

Mei Fu studied in Chang'an, mastering the Book of Documents and Guliang's Spring and Autumn Annals. He repeatedly submitted memorials criticizing the growing power of the Wang family and urging Emperor Cheng to recruit talented advisors. He warned that killing honest officials like Wang Zhang silenced criticism and endangered the state. He advocated establishing Confucius' descendants as heirs to the Shang dynasty's sacrifices. His advice was largely ignored. During Wang Mang's reign, Mei Fu abandoned his family and disappeared, later seen working as a market servant in Wu County under a false name.

Yun Chang studied under Wu Zhang, a famous scholar of the Book of Documents. When Wu Zhang was executed for conspiring against Wang Mang, his disciples denied their connection. Yun Chang alone admitted being Wu Zhang's student, collected his body, and arranged proper burial. He later served as an official under Wang Mang but resigned due to illness and died at home.

The commentary praises Yang Wangsun's noble aspirations, Hu Jian's decisive bravery, and Mei Fu's principled words. Yun Chang's integrity was revealed through burying his teacher, and he wisely withdrew from service during troubled times.
