Zhang Liang, courtesy name Zifang, was of Korean ancestry. His grandfather and father served as prime ministers to five generations of Han kings. After Qin conquered Han, Zhang Liang, though young and never an official, used his family wealth to hire an assassin to avenge Han. He ambushed the First Emperor at Bolangsha but struck the wrong carriage. Forced to flee, he changed his name and hid in Xiapi.

On a bridge in Xiapi, an old man deliberately dropped his shoe and ordered Zhang Liang to retrieve it. Zhang Liang complied despite his initial anger. The old man later gave him a book, the "Tai Gong's Art of War," and predicted he would become a teacher to a king. Zhang Liang studied it diligently.

During the uprisings, Zhang Liang gathered followers and joined Pei Gong (Liu Bang). He frequently advised Pei Gong using the Art of War, and Pei Gong often adopted his strategies. Zhang Liang believed Pei Gong was a heaven-sent talent and remained with him. He later persuaded Xiang Liang to establish Han Cheng as King of Han, and Zhang Liang became Han's Minister of Education.

Zhang Liang advised Pei Gong against attacking Qin's strong forces directly, suggesting instead to bribe the Qin general and attack when the troops were lax. This led to a great victory and Qin's surrender. When Pei Gong was tempted by the luxuries of Qin's palace, Zhang Liang urged him to leave, warning that indulgence would harm his cause. Pei Gong heeded this and returned to Bashang.

During the Hongmen Banquet crisis, Zhang Liang refused to abandon Pei Gong and helped reconcile with Xiang Yu through Xiang Bo. Later, when Pei Gong became King of Han, Zhang Liang advised burning the plank road to show no intention of returning east, stabilizing Xiang Yu. He also recommended using Han Xin, Peng Yue, and King Bu of Jiujiang to defeat Chu.

Zhang Liang opposed enfeoffing descendants of the six kingdoms, arguing it would undermine support for the Han king. He convinced the king to destroy the seals prepared for this purpose. He also advised granting titles to Han Xin and others to secure their loyalty.

After the Han dynasty was established, Zhang Liang was granted the title of Marquis of Liu. He declined a larger fief, citing his lack of military exploits. He advised the emperor to first enfeoff Yong Chi, a man the emperor resented, to reassure other generals and prevent rebellion. He also supported Liu Jing's proposal to make Guanzhong the capital, arguing its strategic advantages.

When the emperor wanted to depose the crown prince, Zhang Liang initially refused to intervene. Pressed by Empress Lü, he suggested recruiting four reclusive sages to support the prince. The emperor, seeing the prince had their support, abandoned the plan. Zhang Liang later withdrew from worldly affairs, studying Taoism and seeking immortality. He died six years after Emperor Gao, receiving the posthumous title Marquis Wencheng.

Chen Ping, from a poor family, was known for his height and good looks. He married a wealthy woman after a local elder recognized his potential. During village festivals, he divided meat evenly, saying he would govern the world similarly.

Chen Ping served Wei and then Chu, but fled both when his advice was ignored or he feared execution. He joined Liu Bang, who appointed him a captain despite objections from other generals. Chen Ping explained his past changes of allegiance by saying his former masters could not use him properly. He proposed using gold to sow discord among Xiang Yu's advisors, leading to Xiang Yu's suspicion of his strategist Fan Zeng.

Chen Ping advised the emperor to capture Han Xin by feigning a visit to Yunmeng, rather than attacking him directly. He also helped the emperor escape a siege at Pingcheng using a secret plan. He served as a strategist for six major campaigns, each time increasing his fief.

After Emperor Gao's death, Chen Ping pretended to indulge in wine and women to avoid suspicion from Empress Lü. He outwardly complied with her appointing Lü family members as kings, but later conspired with Zhou Bo to eliminate the Lü clan and establish Emperor Wen. As prime minister, he famously told the emperor that a prime minister's role is to oversee officials, not handle specific tasks.

Zhou Bo, a simple man from Pei, began as a funeral flutist. He followed Liu Bang from the start, distinguishing himself in battle by often being the first to scale city walls. He served as a general under Emperor Gao, pacifying many regions and capturing numerous enemy officials.

After Emperor Gao's death, Zhou Bo served as Grand Commandant. When Empress Lü died, he and Chen Ping plotted to eliminate the Lü clan. Zhou Bo, as commander of the Northern Army, rallied the soldiers by showing them his seal and declaring loyalty to the Liu family. They killed the Lü kings and established Emperor Wen.

Zhou Bo became Right Chancellor but feared his power and resigned. Later accused of rebellion, he was imprisoned and bribed a jailer for advice. He was released after his wife, the emperor's daughter, testified for him. He remarked on the harshness of jailers. His son Zhou Yafu succeeded him.

Zhou Yafu, known for his strict military discipline, refused to let Emperor Wen enter his camp without proper protocol, earning the emperor's praise as a "true general." During the Rebellion of the Seven States, he advised letting Liang bear the enemy's attack while cutting off their supply routes. He ignored the emperor's order to save Liang, focusing on his strategy, which ultimately defeated Wu and Chu.

As prime minister, Yafu opposed enfeoffing the queen's brother and Xiongnu defectors, arguing it violated Emperor Gao's rules. The emperor overruled him. Later, Yafu's son bought imperial burial items, and Yafu was accused of rebellion. He starved himself in prison, fulfilling a prophecy that he would die of hunger.

The historian comments that Zhang Liang, despite his wisdom, looked like a woman. Chen Ping used clever schemes to survive the Lü clan's rule. Zhou Bo, though crude, stabilized the Han dynasty, fulfilling Emperor Gao's prediction that he would secure the Liu family.
