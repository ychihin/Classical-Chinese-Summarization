The text describes several officials from the Han dynasty, beginning with Wan Shijun (Shi Fen), a man from Zhao who served Emperor Gaozu at age fifteen due to his respectful demeanor. He rose through ranks, becoming Prince Taifu and later Jiuqing under Emperor Jing, who named him Lord Wan Shi for his family’s collective high salaries. Wan Shijun was known for extreme caution and filial piety, retiring in old age but maintaining strict rituals, such as bowing at palace gates and fasting when descendants erred. His sons, especially Shi Jian and Shi Qing, emulated his virtues. Shi Jian, a filial son, washed his father’s clothes secretly and served as Lang Zhongling, while Shi Qing became prime minister under Emperor Wu, though he was passive and avoided policy advice, leading to criticism during crises like refugee migrations. Shi Qing was eventually dismissed but died later. His descendants’ filial piety declined over time.

Wei Wan, a charioteer from Dai, rose to Zhonglang General through merit and caution. He served Emperor Jing, who valued his honesty, appointing him as prince’s tutor and later prime minister. Wei Wan avoided innovation, focusing on routine, and was dismissed under Emperor Wu for failing to redress grievances. Zhi Busuo, a Lang official under Emperor Wen, was known for not defending himself against slander, such as false accusations of incest. He became Marquis of Sai for quelling the Wu-Chu rebellion but was dismissed with Wei Wan. Zhou Ren, a physician from Rencheng, served Emperor Jing as a doctor, known for secrecy and humility, refusing bribes and rewards. He retired with a pension. Zhang Shi (Zhang Ou), a son of a marquis, served as imperial censor under Emperor Wu, practicing leniency and weeping for condemned prisoners, retiring with honors. The text concludes with a praise emphasizing silent virtue and criticizing excessive displays like Shi Jian’s laundry or Zhou Ren’s torn clothes.
