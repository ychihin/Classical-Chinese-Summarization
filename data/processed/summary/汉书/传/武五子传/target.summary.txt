# Summary of Historical Text on Emperor Xiaowu's Sons and the Witchcraft Case

Emperor Xiaowu had six sons from various consorts. Prince Ru, born to Queen Wei, was established as crown prince at age seven in the first year of Yuanshou. Emperor Wu was delighted and built a Plum Temple, ordering Dongfang Shuo and Meigao to write congratulations. The prince studied classical texts under Duke Xiaqiu Jiang, was crowned at twenty, and resided in the Tai Palace, where Emperor Wu built Bowang Garden for him to interact with guests.

In the fourth year of Yuanding, the prince married Shi Liangdi and fathered Liu Jin. In Emperor Wu's later years, Jiang Chong gained favor and held grudges against the prince and the Wei family. When witchcraft accusations arose, Jiang Chong exploited them for revenge. Emperor Wu, elderly and suspicious, ordered severe investigation. Prime Minister Gongsun He, his son, princesses, and the queen's brother's son were all implicated and killed.

Jiang Chong investigated the witchcraft case, claiming poisonous air in the palace. He entered the palace, demolished the throne, and dug up ground. Assisted by others, he went to the prince's palace and found a small figure carved from paulownia wood. Emperor Wu was ill at Ganquan Palace, leaving only the queen and prince in the palace.

The prince consulted his young master Shi De, who warned him about the fate of Prince Fusu of Qin and advised pretending to issue an imperial edict to arrest Jiang Chong. The prince agreed. On Renwu day in the seventh month of the second year of Zhenghe, the prince sent retainers to arrest Jiang Chong and others. One official was killed, another escaped to Ganquan Palace. The prince entered Weiyang Palace, reported to the queen, mobilized weapons and guards, declared Jiang Chong a rebel, killed him, and burned a shaman. He then deployed guests as generals against Prime Minister Liu Quli's army. Chang'an fell into chaos, and the prince's army was defeated. He fled but was not caught.

Emperor Wu was furious. A Huguan elder named Mao wrote a letter arguing that the prince had no evil intentions, comparing the situation to ancient examples of slander causing family discord. He urged the emperor to understand the prince's actions and withdraw troops. Emperor Wu was moved and awakened.

The prince fled east to Hu County, hiding in a commoner's home. The family was poor, selling shoes to support him. When discovered by local officials, the prince hanged himself. His two grandsons were also killed. Emperor Wu, saddened, later learned the witchcraft accusations were mostly untrue. He executed Jiang Chong's family, built the Palace of Thoughts and a tower in Hu County, and mourned the prince's death.

The prince had three sons and one daughter, all killed when he failed. Empress Wei and Shi Liangdi were buried south of Chang'an. One grandson survived and later became Emperor Xiaoxuan. Upon ascending, he issued edicts regarding proper burial and memorial arrangements for his father, the former crown prince, establishing cemeteries and households for worship.

King Huai of Qi, Liu Hong, died without son after eight years. King Liu Dan of Yanla was eloquent, studied classics, and attracted knights. After Prince Wei's defeat and Liu Hong's death, Liu Dan believed he should be crown prince. Emperor Wu became angry, imprisoned his envoy, and later cut off his fiefdom. Emperor Wu disliked Liu Dan and made his youngest son prince instead.

When Emperor Wu died and the prince succeeded as Emperor Xiaozhao, Liu Dan refused to mourn, suspecting changes in the capital. He sent inquiries and later requested temples be built for Emperor Wu. General Huo Guang rewarded him, but Liu Dan angrily claimed he should be emperor. He conspired with others, claiming the young emperor was not Emperor Wu's true son. The plot was discovered, and Liu Dan's co-conspirators were executed. Later, Liu Dan's sister and others conspired with him against Huo Guang. The fourteen-year-old Emperor Zhao trusted Huo Guang and distrusted Liu Dan. The conspiracy was exposed, and Liu Dan hanged himself after receiving an imperial seal condemning his actions.

King Li of Guangling, Liu Xu, was strong and liked entertainment. He coveted the throne when Emperor Zhao was young and childless. He employed witches to pray for his ascension. When Emperor Zhao died and King Changyi succeeded, Liu Xu had witches curse him. After King Changyi was deposed and Emperor Xuan ascended, Liu Xu continued his schemes. His treason was discovered, and he hanged himself after a final banquet.

King Ai of Changyi, Liu He, was summoned to preside over Emperor Zhao's funeral. He traveled hastily, causing horses to die. During his twenty-seven days as emperor, he behaved lewdly. General Huo Guang deposed him and returned him to his hometown. Later, Emperor Xuan granted him the title of Marquis of Haihun. After his death, his title was terminated.

The text concludes with a reflection on the witchcraft case's harm, noting it was not solely Jiang Chong's fault but also divine will, comparing the situation to the Qin dynasty's downfall and emphasizing that war, like fire, burns itself if not stopped.
