Kuai Tong, a native of Fanyang, lobbied Xu Gong, the magistrate of Fanyang, during the Chu-Han conflict. He warned Xu Gong that his harsh rule had created many enemies, and with Qin laws no longer enforceable, he was in danger. Kuai Tong then proposed a plan to Wu Chen, Wu Xinjun: rather than attacking cities, he should welcome Xu Gong with honor to encourage other cities to surrender. Wu Chen followed this advice, and over thirty cities surrendered without battle. Later, Kuai Tong persuaded Han Xin to attack Qi despite Li Shiqi's diplomatic mission, arguing that military conquest would earn greater merit. Han Xin agreed, crossed the Yellow River, and defeated Qi, leading to Li Shiqi's execution. Kuai Tong later urged Han Xin to betray Liu Bang and create a tripartite balance of power, warning that Han Xin's achievements made him a threat to the Han king. Han Xin hesitated, citing Liu Bang's kindness, and declined. Kuai Tong, fearing retribution, feigned madness. After Han Xin's execution, Liu Bang summoned Kuai Tong, who defended himself by saying a dog barks at those not its master. Liu Bang pardoned him. Kuai Tong later served as a guest to Cao Shen, the prime minister of Qi, and recommended the reclusive scholars Shu Guo and Liang Shijun. He also wrote a book on lobbying strategies.

Wu Bei, a descendant of Wu Zixu, served as a lieutenant in Huainan under King Liu An. When the king plotted rebellion, Wu Bei repeatedly admonished him, arguing that the Han dynasty was stable and prosperous, with orderly governance, thriving trade, and subdued enemies. The king dismissed this, citing the Qin collapse as precedent. Wu Bei countered that Qin's tyranny had caused widespread suffering, while the Han emperor ruled with benevolence and had popular support. He warned that rebellion would fail, as the king's forces were weak and the times were peaceful. The king ignored him and proceeded with plans, but the conspiracy was discovered. Wu Bei surrendered and confessed, but was executed despite his earlier praise of Han, as Zhang Tang argued his initial role in planning the rebellion was unforgivable.

Jiang Chong, originally named Jiang Qi, fled to Chang'an after a feud with Prince Liu Dan of Zhao. He reported the prince's incest and adultery, leading to Liu Dan's deposition. Jiang Chong gained Emperor Wu's favor through his appearance and advice on foreign policy. He was appointed to supervise the capital, enforcing laws against luxury and arresting offenders. He detained the prince's envoy for using the imperial road, earning the emperor's praise. Later, fearing the prince would harm him after the emperor's death, Jiang Chong fabricated a witchcraft scandal, accusing the prince of cursing the emperor. He tortured confessions, killing tens of thousands. The prince, unable to defend himself, arrested and executed Jiang Chong. The prince's rebellion failed, and Emperor Wu later annihilated Jiang Chong's clan.

Xifu Gong, a scholar from Heyang, gained fame through his friend Fu Yan, the queen's father. With Sun Chong, he fabricated a plot against King Shuping Liu Yun, accusing him of witchcraft. The king and his associates were executed, and Xifu Gong was made Marquis of Yiling. He became a close advisor to Emperor Ai, criticizing officials and proposing policies, including expanding irrigation canals. He suggested a deceptive strategy to weaken the Xiongnu by forging letters, but General Gongsun Lu opposed it. Xifu Gong also urged military preparations based on astrological signs, but Prime Minister Wang Jia warned against flattery and deceit. The emperor initially supported Xifu Gong, but after a solar eclipse, Dong Xian undermined him. Xifu Gong was dismissed and later accused of cursing the emperor. He died in prison, and his family was exiled. After Emperor Ai's death, Sun Chong and others were also exiled.

The text concludes with a lament on the dangers of eloquent but harmful advisors, citing historical examples from the Spring and Autumn period to the Han dynasty, where slander and deceit led to disasters, including the deaths of rulers and the ruin of states.
