Zhai Fangjin, courtesy name Ziwei, was from Shangcai, Runan, born into a humble family. After his father died when he was twelve or thirteen, he worked as a minor historian but was often scolded. Seeking guidance, he consulted Cai's father in Runan, who noted his noble bone structure and advised him to study Confucian classics. Inspired, he feigned illness, left his job, and traveled to Chang'an with his stepmother, who supported him by knitting shoes. He studied the "Spring and Autumn Annals" under a doctor, and over a decade became proficient in Confucian classics, gaining many disciples and acclaim.

He passed the imperial exams, became a doctor during the Heping period, and later served as governor of Shuofang, known for orderly governance. He was promoted to Prime Minister Sizhi. While accompanying Emperor Cheng to Ganquan, he violated road rules, leading to his carriage being confiscated by Sili Xiaowei Chen Qing. Later, Fangjin impeached Chen Qing for disrespect, resulting in Chen's dismissal. He also impeached Juanxun, another Sili Xiaowei, for arrogance and disrespect, leading to Juanxun's demotion. Within a year, Fangjin dismissed two high officials, instilling fear in the court.

As Jingzhaoyin, he cracked down on powerful families, but at Hu Chang's advice, he moderated his strictness. After three years, he became imperial censor, then prime minister and Marquis of Gaoling. He was impartial and strict, often impeaching ministers like Chen Xian and Feng Xin for corruption, leading to their dismissal. He also targeted Chunyu Chang's associates, removing over twenty officials. Despite his harshness, Emperor Cheng trusted him.

Fangjin studied "Guliang Zhuan" but favored "Zuo Shi Zhuan" and astronomy. After nine years as prime minister, Mars entered the heart constellation, and his subordinate Li Xun warned of impending disaster. Fangjin grew anxious. The emperor later criticized him for poor governance and tax policies, but spared him. Fangjin committed suicide that day. The emperor honored him with posthumous titles and gifts.

His son Zhai Xuan was a gentleman, while another son, Zhai Yi, became a governor. During Wang Mang's regency, Zhai Yi rebelled, declaring Liu Xin emperor and amassing over 100,000 troops. Wang Mang crushed the rebellion, executing Zhai Yi and his family, and desecrating Fangjin's grave. The rebellion's failure led to Wang Mang's usurpation. Historian Ban Biao noted Fangjin's rise from poverty to prominence, but lamented Zhai Yi's doomed uprising.
