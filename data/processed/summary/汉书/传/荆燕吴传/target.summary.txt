The text summarizes the histories of three Han dynasty princes: Liu Jia (King of Jing), Liu Ze (King of Yan), and Liu Bi (King of Wu). Liu Jia, a cousin of Emperor Gaozu, served as a general, leading campaigns against Chu forces, burning supplies, and later capturing Jiujiang. He was made King of Jing but was killed in battle against the rebel Yingbu. Liu Ze, a brother of Gaozu, was initially a marquis. Through a scheme by Tian Sheng, he was made King of Langye after persuading Empress Lu to enfeoff the Lü clan. After Empress Lu’s death, he joined others in supporting Emperor Xiaowen and was renamed King of Yan. His descendants included a son, Kang Wang, and a grandson, Dingguo, who committed adultery with his father’s concubine and sisters, leading to his suicide and the abolition of his state. Liu Bi, son of Liu Zhong, was made King of Wu after defeating Yingbu. He grew wealthy from copper and salt, and after his son was killed by the crown prince, he feigned illness and plotted rebellion. During Emperor Jing’s reign, Chao Cuo advocated reducing the princes’ territories, prompting Liu Bi to lead a rebellion of seven states (Wu, Chu, Zhao, Jiaoxi, Jiaodong, Jinan, and Zichuan) in 154 BCE. Liu Bi issued a call to arms, offering rewards for killing Han officials. Emperor Jing executed Chao Cuo to appease the rebels, but the rebellion continued. Zhou Yafu, a Han general, cut off Wu’s supply lines and defeated them. Liu Bi fled to Dongyue, where he was killed. The other rebel princes were also defeated or committed suicide. The text concludes that Liu Jia’s kingship was due to the unstable times, Liu Ze’s was based on a risky scheme, and Liu Bi’s rebellion stemmed from his wealth and personal grievance, leading to disaster.
