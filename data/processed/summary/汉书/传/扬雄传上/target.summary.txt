Yang Xiong, courtesy name Ziyun, was from Chengdu, Shu County. His ancestors were descendants of Zhou Boqiao, but as a side branch, they took Yang of Jin as their surname. After the Zhou Dynasty declined, some Yang family members became marquises. During the power struggles of the six ministers of Jin, Yang Hou fled to Chuwu Mountain. Later, during the Chu-Han conflict, the Yang family moved to Bajiang Prefecture. Yang Jiguan served as prefect of Lujiang, and during the Han Dynasty, his ancestor Yang Ji settled in Pi, south of Mount Laoshan, where the family farmed and raised silkworms for generations. From Ji to Xiong, there was only one son per generation for five generations, so Yang Xiong had no other Yang clan in Shu.

Yang Xiong was eager to learn as a child, not studying chapters and sentences but focusing on word explanations. He was well-read, easy-going, and tolerant, with a stutter that made him speak slowly. He was contemplative, quiet, and without strong desires, not pursuing wealth or fame. His family property was meager, but he remained content. He admired Sima Xiangru's poetry and imitated him. He also felt sorrow for Qu Yuan's fate, believing a gentleman should adapt to circumstances rather than drown himself. He wrote "Reverse Lisao" to refute Qu Yuan's actions, excerpting from "Li Sao" to express his views.

During Emperor Cheng's reign, a retainer recommended Yang Xiong's writing. The emperor summoned him to wait for an imperial edict. In the first month, Yang Xiong presented "Ode to Sweet Springs" as a satirical remonstrance. The ode described the grand procession to Ganquan Palace, its magnificent architecture, and the emperor's prayers for blessings. Yang Xiong praised the palace's splendor but subtly criticized its extravagance, comparing it to the imperial Purple Palace and suggesting it was built by ghosts and gods. He also noted that Zhaoyi, the emperor's favored consort, accompanied him, which he tactfully advised against.

In March, the emperor went to Fenyin for sacrifices, visiting various historical sites. Yang Xiong offered "Hedong Fu" to satirize, praising the emperor's journey and reflecting on ancient rulers. He emphasized the importance of virtue and following the examples of the Three Emperors and Five Emperors.

In December, Yang Xiong accompanied the emperor on a hunt. He believed that ancient rulers managed resources wisely, ensuring prosperity without harming the people. He criticized Emperor Wu's expansion of Shanglin Garden and the construction of lavish palaces, arguing they were excessive. He presented "Xiao Xie Fu" to satirize, describing the grand hunt and its impact on the land and people. He urged the emperor to focus on virtue, share resources with the people, and avoid extravagance. The ode concluded with a call for moral governance and a return to simplicity.
