Dou Ying, courtesy name Wangsun, was the son of Queen Xiaowen's cousin. He lived in Guanjin and enjoyed entertaining guests. During Emperor Xiaowen's reign, he served as prime minister of Wu State but was dismissed due to illness. Under Emperor Xiaojing, he became Zhan Shi. When Emperor Xiaojing offered the throne to his brother King Liang Xiao, Dou Ying objected, citing Han law that the throne must pass from father to son. This angered Queen Mother Dou, leading to Dou Ying's resignation and exclusion from court. In the third year of Emperor Xiaojing's reign, during the Wu-Chu rebellion, Dou Ying was appointed general and granted gold, which he distributed to his officers. After suppressing the rebellion, he was made Marquis of Wei. He later served as the prince's teacher but argued for the deposed prince, leading to his retirement. Persuaded by Zhisui, he resumed his duties. Despite Queen Mother Dou's recommendations, Emperor Xiaojing refused to make Dou Ying prime minister, citing his pride and rashness.

Tian Fu, half-brother of Emperor Xiaojing and Queen Wang, rose in prominence after Emperor Wu's ascension, becoming Marquis of Wu'an. He initially humbled himself before Dou Ying but later gained power. Tian Fu recommended Dou Ying as prime minister while becoming Taiwei himself. Both favored Confucianism, promoting Zhao Wan and Wang Zang, and planned to establish a Mingtang. However, Queen Mother Dou, a Huang-Lao adherent, opposed them, leading to their dismissal. Tian Fu remained influential due to his ties to the Queen Mother, while Dou Ying's power waned. After Queen Mother Dou's death, Tian Fu became prime minister, growing arrogant. He built lavish residences, acquired fertile lands, and received countless treasures. Dou Ying, by contrast, became alienated and powerless, finding solace only in his friendship with Guan Fu.

Guan Fu, courtesy name Zhongru, was born in Yingyin. His father, Zhang Meng, served Guan Ying and took the surname Guan. During the Wu-Chu rebellion, Guan Fu fought fiercely to avenge his father, earning fame despite severe injuries. He later served as a general but lost his position due to legal troubles. Known for his uprightness and disdain for flattery, Guan Fu respected the poor and insulted the powerful. He associated with heroes and rogues, amassing wealth and a large following. His family's arrogance in Yingchuan led to a local song warning of their downfall. After losing power, Guan Fu bonded with Dou Ying, each seeking to bolster the other's status. Their friendship deepened, though Guan Fu's bluntness often caused conflict.

Tian Fu once agreed to visit Dou Ying with Guan Fu but forgot, causing Guan Fu to confront him. At a banquet, Guan Fu insulted Tian Fu, leading to a rift. Tian Fu later demanded land from Dou Ying, who refused, deepening their enmity. In the fourth year of Yuanguang, Tian Fu reported Guan Fu's family's arrogance, and Guan Fu retaliated by exposing Tian Fu's misdeeds. At a wedding feast for Tian Fu, Guan Fu insulted guests, leading to his arrest. Tian Fu accused him of disrespect, and Dou Ying tried to save him, arguing his drunken mistake was minor. In court, Tian Fu slandered Guan Fu as treasonous. The emperor asked ministers to judge, but opinions were divided. Queen Mother Dou pressured the emperor, leading to Guan Fu's execution and Dou Ying's imprisonment. Dou Ying was later executed for allegedly forging an imperial edict. Tian Fu died soon after, haunted by ghosts of Dou Ying and Guan Fu. His son's title was later revoked.

Han Anguo, courtesy name Changru, was born in Cheng'an County, Liang State. He studied Han Feizi and served King Xiao of Liang during the Wu-Chu rebellion, helping repel the Wu army. After the rebellion, his reputation grew. When Emperor Jing was displeased with King Liang's extravagance, Han Anguo defended him to the eldest princess, easing tensions. He later became internal history of Liang, resolving conflicts by persuading King Xiao to surrender conspirators. After King Xiao's death, Han Anguo lost his position but regained favor under Emperor Wu through Tian Fu's patronage. He became a censor and advocated peace with the Xiongnu, opposing Wang Hui's war plans. Despite initial success, the Mayi ambush failed, and Wang Hui committed suicide. Han Anguo later served as a general but was demoted due to military setbacks. He died of illness, frustrated and unhappy. His colleague Husui, a virtuous official, also died before becoming prime minister.

In summary, Dou Ying and Tian Fu rose through family ties, while Guan Fu gained fame through valor. Their conflicts, fueled by pride and arrogance, led to their downfalls. Han Anguo's wisdom and loyalty were overshadowed by fate, and Wang Hui's war advocacy ended in tragedy.
