Wang Shang, styled Ziwei, originally from Liwu County in Zhuo Commandery, later moved to Duling. His father Wang Wu and uncle were granted marquisates as relatives of Emperor Xuan, with Wang Wu becoming Marquis of Lechang. Wang Shang succeeded his father, distributed his inheritance to half-brothers, and was praised for his seriousness and loyalty. He rose to become General of the Left and Guanglu大夫 during Emperor Yuan's reign, supporting the crown prince against the favored King Dingtao.

Under Emperor Cheng, Wang Shang served as General of the Left. When false rumors of a flood caused panic in the capital, General Wang Feng proposed evacuating to the city walls. Wang Shang argued that floods had never breached a virtuous capital, and the panic would worsen. The emperor agreed, and calm was restored. Wang Shang replaced Kuang Heng as Chancellor, earning the emperor's trust. During a visit by the Xiongnu Chanyu, Wang Shang's imposing stature so frightened the Chanyu that he trembled, prompting the emperor to praise him as a worthy Chancellor.

Wang Shang clashed with Wang Feng over the punishment of Yang Wei, a corrupt official. Wang Feng urged leniency, but Wang Shang insisted on dismissal, deepening Wang Feng's resentment. Wang Feng secretly sought Wang Shang's faults and exposed a family scandal: Wang Shang had initially refused to send his ill daughter to the harem at the Empress Dowager's request, but later used a favored concubine's influence to do so after being threatened. A solar eclipse occurred, and Zhang Kuang blamed Wang Shang for moral failings, citing his alleged adultery, murder, and deceit. Despite Wang Feng's demands for execution, the emperor spared Wang Shang, stripping him of his title. Wang Shang died three days later from vomiting blood.

Shi Dan, styled Junzhong, from Lu, later moved to Duling. His grandfather's sister was a concubine of Prince Wei, mother of Emperor Xuan's father. Shi Dan served as a crown prince's attendant for over a decade. When Emperor Yuan fell ill and favored King Dingtao over the prince, Shi Dan wept before the emperor, arguing the prince was established and respected. The emperor relented, securing the prince's succession. After Emperor Cheng's accession, Shi Dan became General of the Left and Marquis of Wuyang. He was outwardly suave but cautious, trusted by the emperor. He lived extravagantly with many concubines and servants, serving as general for sixteen years before retiring due to illness and dying shortly after.

Fu Xi, styled Zhiyou, from Wen County, was cousin to Emperor Ai's grandmother. He served as a crown prince's attendant and later as General of the Right. When Wang Mang resigned, Fu Xi declined power, citing illness. Empress Dowager Fu wanted to claim equal status with Emperor Cheng's mother, but Fu Xi, with Kong Guang and Shi Dan, opposed this. The emperor dismissed Shi Dan and then Fu Xi, accusing him of failing to correct errors and fostering treachery. Fu Xi was sent to his fiefdom, later recalled by Wang Mang, but died of old age. His son inherited the title, which ended after Wang Mang's fall.

The commentary notes that from Emperor Xuan to Emperor Ai, powerful families like the Xu, Shi, Wang, Ding, and Fu clans held high positions but lacked talent. Wang Shang was deposed for his integrity, dying blameless. Shi Dan's loyalty secured the crown prince's position, earning rewards. Fu Xi's steadfastness was recognized, but during the transition between Emperor Ai and Emperor Ping, fortune and misfortune shifted rapidly.
