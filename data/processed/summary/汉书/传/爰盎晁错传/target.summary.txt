Yuan Ang, also known as Zisi, was a native of Chu who later moved to Anling. He served as a retainer to Lü Lu during Empress Lü's reign and was recommended as a doctor by his brother Yuan Sha under Emperor Wen of Han. Yuan Ang famously criticized Prime Minister Zhou Bo, calling him a "hero" rather than an "important minister," arguing that Zhou Bo had failed to act against the Lü clan during their usurpation and only succeeded after Empress Lü's death. Despite Zhou Bo's initial anger, Yuan Ang later defended him when Zhou Bo was falsely accused of treason, leading to their friendship.

Yuan Ang advised Emperor Wen against indulging Liu Chang, the King of Huainan, whose arrogance led to rebellion. When the king died in exile, Yuan Ang consoled the grieving emperor by listing his three great virtues: filial piety during his mother's illness, courage in facing danger when coming to the capital, and humility in repeatedly declining the throne. Yuan Ang also clashed with the eunuch Zhao Tan, insisting he should not ride in the emperor's carriage, and prevented the emperor from reckless chariot racing. He corrected the seating order between the queen and Consort Shen, warning against favoritism by citing the tragic fate of "human pigs."

Yuan Ang served as governor of Longxi, prime minister of Qi, and later of Wu, following his nephew Yuan Zhong's advice to avoid conflict with the rebellious King of Wu by drinking daily and not interfering. He later confronted Prime Minister Shen Tujia, criticizing him for being less capable than Chen Ping and Zhou Bo and for stifling open discourse. Shen Tujia eventually apologized and treated him as a guest.

Yuan Ang had a long-standing feud with Chao Cuo. When the Rebellion of the Seven States broke out, Yuan Ang advised Emperor Jing to execute Chao Cuo to appease the rebels. After Chao Cuo's death, Yuan Ang was sent as an envoy to Wu but was surrounded by Wu forces. A former subordinate, whom Yuan Ang had once forgiven for an affair with his maid, helped him escape. Yuan Ang later became Prime Minister of Chu but resigned due to illness. He was assassinated by agents of the King of Liang after opposing the king's succession to the throne.

Chao Cuo, a native of Yingchuan, studied Legalist philosophy under Zhang Hui and became a doctor under Emperor Wen. He was sent to study the *Book of Documents* from Fu Sheng and later served as a tutor to the crown prince, earning the nickname "think tank." Chao Cuo wrote extensively on military strategy, advocating for selecting good generals, training soldiers, and using superior weapons. He proposed using surrendered barbarians and border settlers to defend against the Xiongnu, emphasizing the importance of terrain and equipment. He also criticized the Qin dynasty's harsh policies and recommended settling families in border fortresses with proper housing, tools, and incentives.

Under Emperor Jing, Chao Cuo rose to power as Imperial Censor and pushed for reducing the fiefs of feudal princes, sparking widespread resentment. His father warned him of the danger, but Chao Cuo insisted it was necessary for the state's stability. When the Seven States rebelled, Chao Cuo was executed on the advice of Yuan Ang and others, despite his loyalty. After his death, Deng Gong defended him, noting that the rebellion was premeditated and not truly about Chao Cuo. Emperor Jing later regretted the execution.

The historian's commentary notes that Yuan Ang, though not deeply learned, was perceptive and benevolent, but his scheme to kill Chao Cuo ultimately led to his own downfall. Chao Cuo was farsighted in statecraft but blind to his personal peril, unlike Zhao Kuo's mother who saved her family from destruction. Despite his unjust death, Chao Cuo's loyalty was mourned, and his policy writings were preserved.
