# Summary of Historical Records on the Sons of Emperor Gaozu of Han

Emperor Gaozu of Han had eight sons from various consorts. Empress Lu bore Emperor Xiaohui; Lady Cao bore Liu Fei, King of Qi; Bo Ji bore Emperor Xiaowen; Lady Qi bore Liu Ruyi, King of Zhao; and other concubines bore Liu You, Liu Yue, and Liu Jian. Liu Fei, King Hui of Qi, was granted over seventy cities in the sixth year of Gaozu's reign. During Emperor Hui's reign, King Qi visited the capital. Emperor Hui honored him as eldest brother at a banquet, angering Empress Dowager Lu, who attempted to poison King Qi. After discovering the plot, King Qi dedicated Chengyang County to increase Princess Lu Yuan's fiefdom, appeasing the Empress Dowager and securing his safe return. Liu Fei died thirteen years later, succeeded by his son Liu Xiang.

Liu Ruyi, King of Zhao, was killed by Empress Dowager Lu after Gaozu's death, leaving no heirs. Liu You became King of Zhao but was imprisoned and starved to death after his Lu-family queen falsely accused him of plotting rebellion. After Empress Dowager Lu's death, Emperor Wen established Liu Sui as King of Zhao. Liu Sui later joined the Wu-Chu rebellion, was besieged for seven months, and committed suicide after the city fell.

Liu Hui was moved to Zhao against his wishes. His Lu-family queen poisoned his beloved concubine, causing him to compose a sorrowful song and commit suicide. Liu Jian, King of Yan, died, and his son was killed, extinguishing his line.

King Qi Yao's nine sons were established as kings. During Empress Dowager Lu's regency, Lu-family members were granted kingdoms from Qi's territory. Liu Zhang, a grandson of King Qi, served at court and openly defied the Lu family during a banquet, killing a drunken Lu relative under military drinking rules. After Empress Dowager Lu's death, the Lu family plotted rebellion. Liu Zhang secretly informed his brother King Qi, who raised troops. The Han generals killed the Lu family, and the ministers chose to make King Dai (Emperor Wen) emperor instead of King Qi, fearing the Qi queen's mother's family was too powerful.

Emperor Wen restored Qi's lost territories and granted titles to Liu Zhang and Liu Xingju. Liu Xingju later rebelled and committed suicide. Emperor Wen divided Qi into six kingdoms for King Qi Hui's sons. During Emperor Jing's reign, four of these kings joined the Wu-Chu rebellion. King Xiao of Qi initially hesitated, was besieged, but held out until Han forces arrived. After learning the king had negotiated with rebels, he committed suicide. Emperor Jing pardoned him posthumously.

Later, King Qi Li committed adultery with his sister. When Emperor Wu sent an investigator, the king committed suicide, leaving no heirs, and Qi was abolished. The remaining Qi territories eventually passed to the Han dynasty. Over time, the power of princes was reduced after the Seven Kingdoms rebellion, with laws restricting their authority until they could only collect taxes from their fiefdoms.
