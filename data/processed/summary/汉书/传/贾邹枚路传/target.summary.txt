Jia Shan, from Yingchuan, studied under his grandfather Jia Qu and was known for his broad knowledge across various schools of thought, though he did not specialize in Confucianism. He served as a riding attendant under Marquis Yingyin. During Emperor Xiaowen's reign, Jia Shan used the Qin Dynasty as a cautionary example to discuss governance, titling his work "Zhi Yan." He criticized Qin's tyranny, excessive construction of palaces like Epang, extravagant roads, and the lavish Lishan Mausoleum, which drained resources and oppressed the people, leading to widespread rebellion. He emphasized the importance of loyal ministers and open communication, warning that rulers who suppress criticism and act tyrannically risk losing their realm. Jia Shan urged the emperor to adopt benevolent policies, listen to advice, and avoid Qin's mistakes.

Jia Shan also highlighted the Qin Dynasty's failure to maintain stability despite its vast power, contrasting it with the Zhou Dynasty's more moderate rule. He noted that Qin's harsh laws and exploitation led to its downfall, while wise rulers like King Wen of Zhou attracted talented ministers through benevolence. He advised the emperor to cultivate virtue, reduce punishments, and encourage honest counsel. Later, Jia Shan opposed Emperor Wen's decision to allow private coinage, arguing it would undermine imperial authority. Despite his urgent remonstrations, Emperor Wen did not punish him, aiming to keep open the path for advice.

Zou Yang, a native of Qi, served under King Wu Liu Bi of Wu. When the king harbored rebellious intentions after his son's death, Zou Yang wrote a letter advising against rebellion, using Qin's fall as a warning. He argued that unity and loyalty to the Han court were essential, warning that rebellion would lead to destruction. When King Wu ignored him, Zou Yang joined King Xiao of Liang. However, he was slandered and imprisoned. From prison, he wrote to King Liang, citing historical examples of loyal ministers who were misunderstood, such as Jing Ke and Bian He, and urged the king to trust his sincerity. King Liang eventually released him and made him a guest.

Mei Cheng, from Huaiyin, also served King Wu and advised him against rebellion, warning that the Han Dynasty's strength far exceeded Wu's. He used metaphors like a thread holding a heavy weight to illustrate the danger of rebellion. When King Wu persisted, Mei Cheng left for Liang. After Emperor Jing ascended, Chao Cuo's policies provoked the Seven Kingdoms' rebellion. Mei Cheng again advised King Wu to withdraw, but the king refused and was eventually killed. Mei Cheng later served King Xiao of Liang and became famous for his writings. Emperor Wu later summoned him, but he died en route.

Lu Wenshu, from Julu, studied law and the Spring and Autumn Annals. During Emperor Xuan's reign, he wrote a memorial advocating benevolence and lighter punishments, criticizing the harsh legal system inherited from Qin. He argued that severe torture led to false confessions and injustice, undermining peace. He urged the emperor to follow the virtuous policies of King Wen and King Wu of Zhou, reduce penalties, and open channels for advice. Emperor Xuan appreciated his petition and promoted him. Lu Wenshu later served as prefect of Linhuai, where he performed excellently until his death. His descendants became high-ranking officials.
