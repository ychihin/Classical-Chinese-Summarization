Zhang Er, a native of Daliang, was once a retainer of Wei nobleman Wuji. After losing his household registration, he wandered to Waihuang, where a wealthy family's daughter left her husband on the advice of her father's guest and married Zhang Er with a substantial dowry. This allowed him to befriend many guests and become the magistrate of Waihuang County. Chen Yu, also from Daliang, was a Confucian scholar who married into a wealthy family in Zhao. He regarded Zhang Er as a father figure, and they formed a life-and-death friendship. When Emperor Gaozu was a commoner, he had ties with Zhang Er.

After Qin destroyed Wei, a reward was offered for Zhang Er and Chen Yu, so they changed their names and became gatekeepers in Chendi. When a minor official whipped Chen Yu for a mistake, Zhang Er stopped him from retaliating, advising patience. When Chen She revolted, Zhang Er and Chen Yu joined him. They advised Chen She not to declare himself king prematurely but to establish descendants of the six states to win support. Chen She ignored them and made himself king. Later, they persuaded Chen She to let them lead troops to capture Zhao territory. They recruited soldiers and won over local heroes, capturing many cities. Wu Chen declared himself King of Zhao, with Chen Yu as general and Zhang Er as prime minister. Chen She initially opposed but later confirmed Wu Chen's title under advice.

Wu Chen was captured by Yan forces, but a clever servant convinced the Yan general that Zhang Er and Chen Yu secretly wanted Wu Chen dead to divide Zhao, so Yan released him. Li Liang, a Zhao general, rebelled after feeling insulted by Wu Chen's sister, killed Wu Chen, and attacked Handan. Zhang Er and Chen Yu escaped, gathered troops, and established Zhao Xie, a descendant of Zhao royalty, as king. Li Liang was defeated by Chen Yu and fled to Qin general Zhang Han, who razed Handan.

Zhang Er and Zhao Xie were besieged in Julu by Qin forces. Chen Yu, stationed nearby, refused to send troops, believing his forces too weak. Zhang Er sent envoys to rebuke him, but Chen Yu only sent 5,000 men, who were annihilated. After Xiang Yu defeated the Qin army, Zhang Er blamed Chen Yu for the deaths of the envoys, leading to a bitter rift. Chen Yu offered his seal to Zhang Er, who accepted it after persuasion, taking command of Chen Yu's army. Chen Yu left in anger, and the two became enemies.

After Xiang Yu divided the realm, Zhang Er was made King of Changshan, while Chen Yu received only three counties. Chen Yu, resentful, allied with Tian Rong of Qi to attack Zhang Er, who fled to Han. The Han king treated him well. Chen Yu later demanded Zhang Er's death before aiding Han, but the Han king sent a fake head. After Han's defeat, Chen Yu learned the truth and abandoned Han. Han later sent Zhang Er and Han Xin to defeat and kill Chen Yu and Zhao Xie. Zhang Er was made King of Zhao and died in the fifth year of Han. His son Zhang Ao succeeded him and married Princess Lu Yuan.

Emperor Gaozu treated Zhang Ao rudely, angering his ministers Guan Gao and Zhao Wu, who plotted to kill Gaozu. Zhang Ao refused to participate. The plot failed, and Guan Gao took full blame, enduring torture to prove Zhang Ao's innocence. Gaozu eventually pardoned Zhang Ao and admired Guan Gao's loyalty, but Guan Gao committed suicide. Zhang Ao remained respectful to Princess Lu Yuan and was made Marquis of Xuanping. His descendants held various titles, though some were later stripped due to crimes.

The historian comments that Zhang Er and Chen Yu were initially life-and-death friends but turned against each other over power, illustrating the shame of fair-weather friendships.
