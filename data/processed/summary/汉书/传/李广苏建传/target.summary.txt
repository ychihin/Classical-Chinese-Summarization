Li Guang, a native of Longxi County, was a descendant of a Qin general and came from a family skilled in archery. He began his military career during Emperor Wen's reign, fighting the Xiongnu at Xiaoguan, where his archery skills earned him a position as a Langguan. Emperor Wen lamented that Li Guang's talents would have earned him a marquisate in the era of Emperor Gaozu. Under Emperor Jing, he served as a Cavalry General and fought in the campaign against the rebellious seven kingdoms of Wu and Chu, but was denied a reward due to accepting a seal from the King of Liang. He later served as the Prefect of Shanggu and other border commanderies, frequently battling the Xiongnu.

During a campaign in Shangjun, Li Guang, with only a hundred cavalry, encountered thousands of Xiongnu. He cleverly halted his men and dismounted, convincing the enemy they were a decoy, which prevented an attack. After nightfall, the Xiongnu withdrew, suspecting an ambush. Li Guang later served as a palace guard under Emperor Wu, alongside Cheng Bushi. While Cheng Bushi's army was strictly disciplined, Li Guang's was more relaxed, yet his soldiers were loyal and willing to die for him. The Xiongnu feared Li Guang's strategies, and soldiers preferred serving under him.

Li Guang was captured by the Xiongnu in a later campaign but escaped by feigning death, stealing a horse, and shooting his pursuers. He was sentenced to death for his losses but redeemed himself with money, becoming a commoner. He later served as the Right Governor of Beiping, where the Xiongnu avoided him for years. During a hunt, he famously shot an arrow into a stone, mistaking it for a tiger. Despite his reputation, Li Guang never received a marquisate, a fact he lamented to Wang Shuo, who attributed it to Li Guang's past deception and killing of surrendered Qiang rebels.

In his final campaign, Li Guang served as a former general under Wei Qing. Ordered to take a circuitous eastern route, he lost his way and failed to join the main force. After Wei Qing's interrogation, Li Guang, feeling humiliated and over sixty, committed suicide. His death caused widespread mourning. His son Li Gan later wounded Wei Qing in revenge and was killed by Huo Qubing, who claimed it was a deer attack. Li Guang's grandson Li Ling led a campaign against the Xiongnu with 5,000 infantry, fighting bravely but ultimately surrendering after running out of arrows and reinforcements. The emperor, misled by reports that Li Ling was training the Xiongnu, executed his family. Li Ling remained with the Xiongnu, marrying a Chanyu's daughter and dying there years later.

Su Wu was sent as an envoy to the Xiongnu in 100 BCE. When a conspiracy involving his deputy Zhang Sheng was exposed, Su Wu attempted suicide to avoid disgrace. He survived and was imprisoned, surviving on snow and felt. Exiled to the North Sea to herd rams, he steadfastly refused to surrender, even when his former friend Li Ling tried to persuade him. After nineteen years, the Han Dynasty learned he was alive through a clever ruse involving a wild goose with a message. Su Wu returned to Chang'an, honored for his loyalty, though his son was later executed for conspiracy. He died in his eighties, and his portrait was later placed in the Qilin Pavilion among eleven notable ministers of Emperor Xuan's reign. The text praises Li Guang's integrity and Su Wu's unwavering loyalty, noting the tragic fate of the Li family and the exemplary virtue of Su Wu.
