Xiao He, from Peidi, began his career as a meticulous official under Pei Lord, supporting the future Emperor Gaozu (then a commoner and later a pavilion chief) in official matters. When Gaozu served in Xianyang, Xiao He contributed 500 yuan for his farewell, exceeding the 300 yuan given by others. As an official in Sishui County, he excelled in inspections, but declined a court recruitment to remain. When Gaozu rose as Duke of Pei, Xiao He became his prime minister and governor. Upon entering Xianyang, while others seized gold and silk, Xiao He collected Qin’s legal and administrative books, enabling Gaozu to understand the empire’s geography, households, and people’s grievances. As prime minister, he governed Ba and Shu, pacifying the people and supplying the army.

In the second year of the Han Dynasty, Xiao He stayed in Guanzhong, governing Yueyang, establishing laws, temples, and counties, reporting to the emperor when possible. He recruited soldiers and supplied grain, earning the emperor’s trust. During the confrontation with Xiang Yu, the emperor’s frequent consolations led Bao Sheng to advise Xiao He to send his capable relatives to the army to allay suspicion, which he did, pleasing the emperor. After Xiang Yu’s death, Liu Bang ascended the throne and awarded Xiao He first among the marquises, granting him 8,000 households. When ministers protested, the emperor compared their merit to hunting dogs and Xiao He’s to a hunter, silencing them. Later, Eqiu argued Xiao He’s contributions—preserving Guanzhong and supplying troops—were eternal, securing him the top rank. Xiao He was granted special court privileges.

During Chen Xi and Han Xin’s rebellions, Xiao He helped Empress Lu kill Han Xin. The emperor granted him 5,000 households and a guard, but Zhao Ping warned this was due to suspicion, advising Xiao He to refuse the reward and fund the army. He complied, pleasing the emperor. When Yingbu rebelled, a retainer warned Xiao He’s popularity could lead to his extermination, urging him to damage his reputation by buying land cheaply. He did so, and the emperor was happy. Later, the emperor imprisoned Xiao He for requesting Linyuan for the people, but a courtier argued his loyalty, leading to his release. Xiao He served Emperor Hui, recommending Cao Shen as his successor. He died in the second year of Xiaohui’s reign, posthumously named Wen Zhonghou. His descendants inherited his title, with later emperors restoring it after extinctions.

Cao Shen, also from Peidi, served as a jailer in Qin and followed Gaozu as Zhongjuan. He achieved numerous military victories, including defeating Qin forces, capturing cities, and killing generals. After Xiang Yu made Peigong King of Han, Cao Shen became Marquis of Jiancheng. He pacified the Three Qin Dynasties, captured Xianyang, and fought in key battles. As a general and lieutenant, he attacked princes, and after King Xiang’s defeat, he served as false prime minister, defeating Wei and capturing King Bao. He followed Han Xin to attack Zhao and Qi, capturing cities and generals. After the Han emperor’s ascension, Cao Shen became prime minister of Qi, attacking Chen Xi and Yingbu. His achievements included conquering two countries and 122 counties, capturing two kings and three prime ministers.

As prime minister of Qi, Cao Shen adopted Huang-Lao principles, governing with tranquility and earning praise. Upon hearing of Xiao He’s death, he prepared to become prime minister of the court, advising his successor to prioritize prison litigation and market trade. Despite past conflicts, Xiao He recommended Cao Shen. As prime minister, Cao Shen adhered to Xiao He’s laws, selecting loyal elders and excluding those seeking fame. He drank daily, avoiding advice, and covered minor mistakes. When Emperor Hui criticized his inaction, Cao Shen argued that Emperor Gao and Xiao He had established clear laws, and he merely maintained them, which the emperor accepted. He died after three years as prime minister, posthumously named Yihou. His descendants inherited his title, with later generations continuing until Wang Mang’s era.

The praise notes that both Xiao He and Cao Shen, born in Qin, rose with Han, stabilizing the state through their complementary roles—Xiao He guarding Guanzhong and Cao Shen leading expeditions. Their reputations endured, benefiting future generations.
