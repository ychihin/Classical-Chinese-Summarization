Since the Han Dynasty's founding, sycophants and favored ministers who gained imperial favor through flattery appeared regularly. During Emperor Gaozu's reign, there was Ji Ru; under Emperor Xiaohui, there was Hong Ru. Neither possessed outstanding talents, relying solely on flattery to gain wealth and favor. They accompanied the emperor closely, influencing even ministers who used them to communicate with the throne. Their impact was so great that Emperor Hui's officials adopted their customs, decorating hats with delicate feathers, belts with seashells, and applying makeup—a trend attributed to figures like Ji Ru and Hong Ru. Both became wealthy and noble in their lifetimes, later moving to Anling.

Subsequent favored figures included Deng Tong, eunuch Zhao Tan, and Beigong Bozi under Emperor Wen; Han Yan and eunuch Li Yannian under Emperor Wu; eunuchs Hong Gong and Shi Xian under Emperor Yuan; Zhang Fang and Chun Yuchang under Emperor Cheng; and Dong Xian under Emperor Ai. Emperors Jing, Zhao, and Xuan had no notable favorites, though Zhou Ren under Emperor Jing, Jin Shang under Emperor Zhao, and Peng Zu under Emperor Xuan received some favor without causing harm.

Deng Tong, from Nan'an in Shu, was known as Huangtoulang for wearing a yellow hat while rowing. Emperor Wen once dreamed of ascending to heaven but was unable until a yellow-headed man pushed him upward. After waking, the emperor sought the man and found Deng Tong, whose clothing matched the dream. Delighted, the emperor favored him greatly, rewarding him with tens of thousands in cash and high office. Deng Tong was cautious and solemn, avoiding outsiders even during holidays. A fortune-teller predicted he would die poor and hungry, but the emperor, confident in his power, gave him Tongshan Mountain in Shu to mint coins, making Deng Tong immensely wealthy. When Emperor Wen developed abscesses, Deng Tong sucked them with his mouth. The emperor asked who loved him most, and Deng Tong replied the prince. The prince, uncomfortable with sucking the sores, later resented Deng Tong. After Emperor Wen's death, Emperor Jing dismissed Deng Tong. Accused of minting coins outside, Deng Tong was convicted, his property confiscated, and he died penniless in another's home.

Zhao Tan gained favor through astrology, and Beigong Bozi through elder-like generosity, but neither matched Deng Tong. Han Yan, grandson of Gonggaohou Qidang, studied with Emperor Wu during his youth. After Emperor Wu became emperor, Han Yan's skills in horseback riding and archery, along with his intelligence, earned him favor comparable to Deng Tong. He often slept and rose with the emperor. Once, while hunting, King Jiangdu mistook Han Yan's entourage for the emperor and knelt, but Han Yan passed without noticing. The king complained to the empress dowager, who hated Han Yan thereafter. When adultery rumors reached her, she had Han Yan killed despite the emperor's pleas. Han Yan's brother, Han Shuo, also favored, was later killed during the witchcraft disaster.

Li Yannian, from Zhongshan, was a musician who suffered corruption and managed the emperor's dogs. His sister, favored as Lady Li, gave birth to the King of Changyi. Li Yannian's musical talents earned him high rank, equal to Han Yan. After Lady Li's death, his brother Li Ji's promiscuity led to their execution. Thereafter, favored ministers were mostly relatives. Wei Qing and Huo Qubing, though favored, rose through genuine military merit.

Shi Xian, from Jinan, and Hong Gong, from Pei, both suffered corruption and served as eunuchs. Under Emperor Xuan, Hong Gong became Zhongshu Ling, skilled in laws and reports. After Hong Gong's death, Shi Xian succeeded him under Emperor Yuan. When Emperor Yuan fell ill, he entrusted all government affairs to Shi Xian, who became all-powerful. Shi Xian was cunning, using strict laws to attack opponents. He persecuted Xiao Wangzhi, Zhou Kan, and Liu Gengsheng, leading to Xiao's suicide and the others' imprisonment. Later, Zhang Meng, Jing Fang, Chen Xian, and others who exposed Shi Xian's faults were convicted. Su Jian, who reported Shi Xian's private letters, was executed. Officials feared Shi Xian, who formed a clique with Lao Liang and Wulu Chongzong. A folk song mocked their power. Shi Xian tried to attach himself to Feng Fengshi but recommended Feng Kui, who then exposed Shi Xian's monopoly. The emperor dismissed Feng Kui. When the censor position opened, ministers recommended Feng Kui's brother, but Shi Xian blocked it. Shi Xian often feigned self-criticism to maintain trust. After Emperor Yuan's death, Emperor Cheng demoted Shi Xian, who died on the road. His followers were dismissed.

Chun Yuchang, from Yuancheng, served Wang Feng diligently, becoming his nephew. Before Wang Feng's death, he recommended Chun Yuchang to the empress dowager and emperor. Chun Yuchang helped make Feiyan empress, earning a marquis title. He became wealthy, marrying many concubines and having an affair with Xu Wei, sister of deposed Empress Xu. Empress Xu bribed Chun Yuchang to regain favor, but he deceived her. Wang Mang, fearing Chun Yuchang's rise, exposed his crimes to Wang Gen and the empress dowager. Chun Yuchang was dismissed and exiled, dying in prison. His family was exiled, and many officials lost their posts.

Dong Xian, from Yunyang, served as prince's minister under Emperor Ai. His handsome appearance won favor, and he became Huang Menlang. He rode with the emperor and slept with him. Once, when Dong Xian slept on Emperor Ai's sleeve, the emperor cut it off rather than wake him. Dong Xian's family received immense rewards, and he became Grand Sima at age twenty-two. After Emperor Ai's death, the empress dowager summoned Wang Mang, who impeached Dong Xian for failing to care for the emperor. Dong Xian and his wife committed suicide. Their property was confiscated, and his family exiled. The text concludes that these favored ministers, lacking virtue, met bad ends, and their rise contributed to the Western Han's decline.
