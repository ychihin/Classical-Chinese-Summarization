The ancient system of kings established hierarchical distinctions from emperor to slave, with graded official titles, salaries, and life-and-death rites, ensuring that the lowly did not surpass the noble, thus stabilizing society. People were taught to cultivate land and raise livestock according to seasonal and geographical conditions, with strict rules against cutting trees or fishing before the first lunar month, and prohibitions on harvesting immature plants, animals, or eggs to allow natural reproduction and abundant reserves. Scholars, farmers, artisans, and merchants each specialized in their occupations, exchanging goods to meet needs without excessive taxation, as noted in the "Book of Changes" and "Guanzi," which emphasized that people content with their professions and customs would have few desires and ample property, guided by morality and ritual.

The decline began when the Zhou royal family weakened and etiquette collapsed, exemplified by Duke Zhuang of Lu violating Zhou rites by carving and painting his father's temple, and other officials like Zang Wenzhong and the three Huan families of Lu exceeding their ranks in music and sacrifices. This spread to commoners, who abandoned farming for commerce, leading to food shortages and luxury excess. After the reigns of Duke Huan of Qi and Duke Wen of Jin, ritual and righteousness were destroyed, superiors and subordinates offended each other, and deceitful, treacherous, and murderous individuals gained wealth and status. The rich decorated homes with silk and fed dogs and horses grain, while the poor wore coarse cloth and ate beans, yet the poor did not resent their status. Those who followed justice faced hunger, while the wicked prospered, all stemming from the destruction of the legal system.

In response, King Gou Jian of Yue, besieged on Kuaiji Mountain, appointed Fan Li and Ji Ran to govern. Ji Ran advised buying ships during droughts and vehicles during floods to prepare for opposite conditions, understanding market cycles. Yue followed this policy, became wealthy, and avenged Wu. Fan Li later applied these principles to enrich his family, changing his name to Zhu Gong in Taoyi, a central trading hub. He hoarded goods, adapted to circumstances, and lent money wisely, amassing a fortune three times over 19 years, distributing wealth to the poor, and leaving a legacy that made "Duke Tao Zhu" synonymous with riches.

Other wealthy figures included Zi Gong, a disciple of Confucius who traded between Cao and Wei, becoming richer than Yan Yuan, though Confucius praised Yan's wisdom. Bai Gui of Zhou, during Wei Wenhou's time, observed market price changes, advocating buying when others sold and vice versa. He lived frugally, shared hardships with servants, and seized opportunities swiftly, comparing his methods to Yi Yin, Sun Tzu, and Shang Yang. He taught only those who were intelligent, brave, benevolent, and strong. Yidun made a fortune from salt, Guo Zong from iron, and Wu Shiying from livestock, who bribed the Rong King and became so wealthy that Qin Shihuang honored him. The widow Qing of Ba preserved her family's cinnabar mine, and Qin Shihuang built a platform for her as a chaste woman.

Under Qin and Han systems, a prince with a thousand households earned 200,000 yuan annually from rents, while commoners with 10,000 yuan could earn 2,000 yuan in interest. Wealthy families raised livestock, planted trees, or cultivated crops like jujubes, chestnuts, oranges, lacquer, mulberries, bamboo, or ginger, matching the income of a thousand-household prince. The proverb said that for a poor man to get rich, commerce was better than farming or crafts. In a bustling city, annual sales of wine, vinegar, grain, livestock, timber, and other goods could yield profits comparable to a prince's income.

The Zhuo family of Shu, exiled from Zhao, chose fertile land at Marriage Mountain, mined iron, and traded with Yunnan and Shu, amassing wealth with 800 servants. Cheng Zheng, a captive, also prospered in iron. Later, Luo Pei of Chengdu, trusted by the Shi family, made millions in Ba, Shu, and the capital, bribing nobles to monopolize well salt. The Kong family of Wan, exiled to Nanyang, combined iron smelting with farming and trade, earning a reputation for generosity. The Bing family of Lu, frugal and meticulous, made millions from iron, influencing many to abandon literature for commerce. Dao Xian of Qi valued cunning slaves, using them to trade fish and salt, accumulating tens of millions. After his decline, Wei of Linzi had 50 million. Shishi of Zhou, with hundreds of carts, traded across counties, making 100 million, followed by Zhang Changshu and Xue Zizhong under Wang Mang.

The Ren family of Xuanqu stored grain during Qin's fall, profiting when war drove prices to 10,000 yuan per stone. They focused on farmland and livestock, eating only what they produced, and became a model village, respected by the emperor. During the Seven Kingdoms' rebellion, Wuyan family lent money at tenfold interest, earning a fortune in Guanzhong. Other wealthy families included the Tian clan, Fan Jia, Zhi Gang, and Wang Junfang, with Fan Jia having 50 million. Countless others gained wealth through patents, bribery, or illegal means like tomb robbing and gambling, corrupting society and leading to chaos.
