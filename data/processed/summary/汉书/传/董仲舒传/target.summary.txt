Dong Zhongshu, a scholar from Guangchuan, studied the "Spring and Autumn" Annals in his youth and became a court academician during Emperor Jing's reign. He lectured from behind a curtain, with senior disciples teaching junior ones, and some students never saw him. He was so devoted to his studies that he did not look at his garden for three years. His dignified conduct earned him the respect of all scholars.

When Emperor Wu ascended the throne, he sought virtuous scholars, and Dong Zhongshu responded to the emperor's policy questions. The emperor asked about the principles of governance, the rise and fall of dynasties, and how to achieve harmony between heaven and humanity. He inquired why the virtuous ways of ancient sage kings declined, why disasters occurred, and how to reform governance to bring peace and prosperity.

Dong Zhongshu's countermeasure explained that heaven sends disasters to warn rulers of moral failings. He emphasized that diligent study and virtuous conduct are essential for good governance. He argued that ritual and music are tools for education, and that a ruler must first rectify his own heart to rectify the court and the people. He criticized the Qin dynasty for abandoning moral education and relying solely on punishment, leading to its rapid downfall. He urged the Han dynasty to reform its institutions, establish a national university, and promote Confucian education to cultivate virtuous officials.

The emperor, impressed, asked further about the differences between ancient rulers like Yao and Shun, who governed with ease, and King Wen of Zhou, who was constantly busy. Dong Zhongshu explained that the times differed: Yao and Shun inherited a peaceful world, while King Wen faced chaos. He stressed that the essence of governance—benevolence, righteousness, and ritual—remains constant, but methods must adapt to circumstances. He advocated for selecting officials based on merit, not seniority, and for establishing schools to train talent.

In a third response, Dong Zhongshu elaborated on the harmony between heaven and humanity. He argued that heaven's will is reflected in natural phenomena, and rulers must align their actions with it. He criticized the Qin for its harsh laws and urged the Han to adopt the Xia dynasty's emphasis on loyalty to remedy the Zhou's excessive refinement. He proposed banning non-Confucian teachings to unify thought and law.

After his countermeasures, Dong Zhongshu served as prime minister to the King of Jiangdu, advising him with propriety. He later became a palace attendant but was nearly executed after a rival stole a draft of his memorial on disasters. Pardoned, he remained honest and upright. He later served the King of Jiaoxi, but fearing danger, he retired to write. His works, including over 100,000 words on the "Spring and Autumn," clarified Confucian principles. He died at home, and his descendants became high officials.

Liu Xiang praised Dong Zhongshu as a king's assistant, comparable to Yi Yin and Lu Wang, surpassing Guan Zhong and Yan Ying. However, Liu Xiang's son Liu Xin argued that Dong, while a leader among Confucians after the Qin's destruction of texts, could not match Yi Yin or Lu Wang, though he was greater than Guan Zhong and Yan Ying. Liu Xin's view was later deemed accurate.
