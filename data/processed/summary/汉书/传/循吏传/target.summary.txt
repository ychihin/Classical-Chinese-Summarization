During the early Han Dynasty, the harsh policies of the Qin Dynasty were reversed, allowing the people to recover and live simply, with relaxed laws. Prime Ministers Xiao He and Cao Cao set an example with their generous and quiet governance, earning praise from the people. Emperor Hui ruled without intervention, and Empress Lu remained secluded, yet the realm was peaceful, with abundant food and clothing. Under Emperors Wen and Jing, customs improved, and officials like Wu Gong and Weng Weng governed with integrity, inspiring obedience without force.

Emperor Wu's reign saw external conflicts and internal legal reforms, exhausting the people and causing unrest. Few officials were noted for good governance, except Confucian scholars Dong Zhongshu, Gongsun Hong, and Ni Kuan, who used Confucian principles in administration and were valued by the emperor. Emperor Zhao ascended young, with Huo Guang as regent, who maintained stability after years of war. During the Shiyuan and Yuanfeng years, the Xiongnu surrendered, and the people grew prosperous, leading to talent recommendations, care for the people, and discussions on salt and iron taxes.

Emperor Xuan, who rose from humble beginnings, understood the people's hardships. After Huo Guang's death, he personally managed court affairs, holding court every five days and interviewing officials to ensure their actions matched their words. He believed that stable county governors were key to peace, rewarding effective ones with promotions and titles. This led to a flourishing of virtuous officials, known as the ZTE era. Some, like Zhao Guanghan and Han Yanshou, were diligent but faced punishment, while others like Wang Cheng and Huang Ba were honored in life and remembered after death.

Wen Weng, a native of Shu County, was known for his love of learning and governance. As prefect of Shu, he selected talented officials to study in Chang'an, reducing expenses and providing gifts to scholars. He established schools in Chengdu, exempting students from taxes and appointing them to official positions. His efforts transformed Shu's culture, making it comparable to Qi and Lu. Emperor Wu later ordered all counties to establish schools, following Wen Weng's example. After his death, the people built a temple for him, and his influence persists in the Bashu region.

Wang Cheng, whose origins are unknown, served as a minister and attracted over 80,000 refugees, earning Emperor Xuan's praise and a marquis title. However, after his death, it was reported that he had exaggerated his achievements, leading to a trend of false fame among officials.

Huang Ba, from Yangxia County, studied law and became an official through donations. He was known for his integrity, gentle demeanor, and ability to unite people. As prefect of Yingchuan, he implemented policies to support the poor, promote agriculture, and reduce crime. His governance was so effective that the county's household registration increased, and he was praised by Emperor Xuan. However, as prime minister, his performance declined, and he was criticized for overstepping his duties. Despite this, he is remembered as one of the best local officials in Han history. He died in office, and his descendants continued to serve as officials until Wang Mang's era.

Zhu Yi, from Shu County, was known for his honesty and fairness as a minor official. He rose to become a minister of agriculture, living frugally and sharing his salary with his hometown elders. He died in 61 BC, and Emperor Xuan praised his integrity, granting gold for sacrifices. He was buried in Tongxiang, where the people built a temple for him.

Gong Sui, from Pingyang County, was a loyal advisor to King Liu He of Changyi, often admonishing him for his unruly behavior. After Liu He was deposed, Gong Sui was sentenced to hard labor but later appointed governor of Bohai County. He quelled a rebellion by offering amnesty to farmers and promoting agriculture, leading to prosperity. He died in office, highly regarded by Emperor Xuan.

Zhao Xinchen, from Shouchun County, was a diligent official who improved agriculture through irrigation, opening ditches and building water gates. He prohibited extravagance and promoted thrift, leading to increased household numbers and reduced crime. He served as governor of Henan and later as a minister, advising against wasteful palace projects. He died in office, and a temple was built for him in Nanyang.
