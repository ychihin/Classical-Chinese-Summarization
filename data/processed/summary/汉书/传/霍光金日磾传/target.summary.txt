# Summary of Huo Guang and Jin Rixi

Huo Guang, courtesy name Zimeng, was the younger half-brother of the renowned general Huo Qubing. Their father Zhongru served as a county official in the Pingyang Hou family and had an affair with the maid Wei Shaoer, fathering Huo Qubing. After completing his duties, Zhongru returned home, married, and fathered Huo Guang, severing all ties with Wei Shaoer. When Huo Qubing later learned of his father's identity while serving as a hussar general attacking the Xiongnu, he visited Zhongru in Hedong, purchased lands and slaves for him, and took ten-year-old Huo Guang to Chang'an. Huo Guang was appointed as Lang and later promoted to various positions. After Huo Qubing's death, Huo Guang served as Guanglu and Commander-in-Chief, accompanying Emperor Wu for over twenty years with cautious conduct, earning the emperor's complete trust.

As Emperor Wu aged and Prince Wei was defeated, the emperor chose his young son as heir and entrusted Huo Guang with assisting him. He gave Huo Guang a painting of Duke Zhou carrying King Cheng, symbolizing his expectations. When Emperor Wu fell ill at Wuzha Palace, he appointed Huo Guang as Grand Sima General, along with Jin Rixi, Shangguan Jie, and Sang Hongyang as co-regents. After Emperor Wu's death, the eight-year-old Emperor Zhao ascended the throne, with Huo Guang making all political decisions. Huo Guang was calm, thoughtful, and meticulous in his conduct, maintaining consistent positions when entering and leaving the palace gates.

Huo Guang's daughter married Shangguan Jie's son Shangguan An, whose daughter became Emperor Zhao's empress. However, conflicts arose when Huo Guang refused to grant titles to Ding Wairen, a favorite of the Grand Princess Eyi Gaizhu. Shangguan Jie, his son, Sang Hongyang, and the Grand Princess conspired with King Liu Dan of Yan, sending a false letter accusing Huo Guang of arrogance and improper conduct. Emperor Zhao, only fourteen, saw through the deception, noting that the letter mentioned events that occurred only ten days prior, which King Yan could not have known. The conspirators then plotted to assassinate Huo Guang, but their scheme was discovered. Huo Guang executed Shangguan Jie, Shangguan An, Sang Hongyang, Ding Wairen, and their families, while King Yan and the Grand Princess committed suicide.

After Emperor Zhao died without an heir, the ministers initially considered King Liu Xu of Guangling, but Huo Guang opposed this due to the king's immoral character. Following a suggestion from a Lang official citing historical precedents, Huo Guang arranged for the installation of King Liu He of Changyi. However, Liu He soon began behaving lewdly and improperly. Huo Guang, troubled by this, consulted with Tian Yannian, who cited the precedent of Yi Yin deposing Taijia. Huo Guang gathered the ministers, and after Tian Yannian threatened those who hesitated with execution, they agreed to depose the king. Huo Guang reported to the Empress Dowager, who issued an edict. The ministers jointly impeached King Changyi for numerous offenses during his twenty-seven-day reign, including failing to observe mourning, engaging in debauchery, and issuing over 1,127 orders for various items. The Empress Dowager approved the deposition, and Huo Guang personally removed the imperial seal from King Changyi, who was sent back to his former residence.

Huo Guang then arranged for the installation of Emperor Wu's great-grandson, who was living among the people, as Emperor Xuan. For his services, Huo Guang received additional fiefdoms totaling 20,000 households, along with gold, money, silk, slaves, and horses. His family members held numerous important positions throughout the court. Huo Guang remained in power for twenty years until his death, after which he was buried with great honors at Maoling and given the title Xuanchenghou.

After Huo Guang's death, his wife Xian and their sons Huo Yu, Huo Yun, and Huo Shan became increasingly arrogant and extravagant. Xian had previously poisoned Empress Xu to install her daughter as empress, a secret that Huo Guang had concealed. As Emperor Xuan gradually reduced the Huo family's power, they grew fearful and plotted rebellion, planning to depose the emperor and install Huo Yu. The conspiracy was discovered, leading to Huo Yun, Huo Shan, and Fan Mingyou committing suicide, while Xian, Huo Yu, and others were executed. The Huo clan was exterminated, and Empress Huo was deposed.

Jin Rixi, originally a Xiongnu prince, was captured as a slave after his father King Xiutu was killed. He served as a horse keeper but caught Emperor Wu's attention through his dignified bearing and honest answers. He became a trusted attendant, known for his loyalty and filial piety. He killed his own son for improper behavior with palace servants and later helped thwart a rebellion by Mang Heluo. When Emperor Wu appointed regents, Jin Rixi served as Huo Guang's deputy, refusing a marquisate due to Emperor Zhao's youth. His descendants continued serving the Han court for seven generations, maintaining a reputation for loyalty and filial piety.
