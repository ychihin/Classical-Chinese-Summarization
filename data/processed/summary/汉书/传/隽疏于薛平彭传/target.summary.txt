The text presents biographies of several Han Dynasty officials, highlighting their careers, virtues, and interactions with authority.

Jun Buyi, a native of Bohai, was known for his strict adherence to etiquette and expertise in the "Spring and Autumn." During the late years of Emperor Wu, he was summoned by the imperial envoy Baoshengzhi, who was impressed by his virtue. Jun Buyi arrived in full ceremonial attire, refusing to remove his sword, which he deemed a gentleman's essential tool. Baoshengzhi received him with respect, and Jun Buyi advised him to balance firmness with leniency in governance. Baoshengzhi recommended him to the court, and he became Governor of Qingzhou. He later thwarted a rebellion by Liu Ze, a grandson of the King of Qi, and was promoted to Jing Zhaoyin. His mother’s influence ensured he was dignified but not cruel in office. In the fifth year of Emperor Zhao’s reign, a man claiming to be Prince Wei appeared. Jun Buyi immediately arrested him, citing the "Spring and Autumn" to justify his action, earning praise from Emperor Zhao and General Huo Guang. He refused Huo Guang’s offer of marriage and later retired due to illness, dying at home. The imposter was later executed.

Shu Guang and his nephew Shu Shou served as tutors to the crown prince. Shu Guang, a native of Lanling, was learned in the "Spring and Autumn." He advised Emperor Xuan against appointing the prince’s maternal relative Xu Shun as supervisor, arguing that the prince’s teachers should be chosen from the world’s best. After five years, when the prince was proficient in the "Analects" and "Classic of Filial Piety," Shu Guang urged his nephew to retire with him, citing the principle of knowing when to stop. They resigned, receiving generous gifts of gold. Shu Guang used the gold to host feasts for his clan, refusing to buy land for his descendants, fearing wealth would lead to laziness or faults. He believed the emperor’s gift was meant for sharing with his community.

Yu Dingguo, from Donghai, learned law from his father Yu Gong, a fair judge. Yu Gong had once protested the wrongful execution of a filial widow, which led to a three-year drought until her innocence was acknowledged. Yu Dingguo became a court official, studying the "Spring and Autumn" with humility. As Tingwei, he was known for leniency and fairness, earning praise that no one felt wronged under his jurisdiction. He served as imperial censor and later replaced Huang Ba as prime minister, being respected by Emperor Yuan. However, disasters in Guandong led to imperial reprimands. Yu Dingguo apologized and eventually retired, dying at over seventy. His son Yu Yong and grandson Yu Tian succeeded him, though Yu Tian lacked his ancestors’ virtues.

Xue Guangde, from Pei, taught the "Book of Songs" and served as imperial censor. He was known for his outspoken advice, once urging Emperor Yuan to return from a hunt due to poverty in Guandong. Another time, he threatened suicide to prevent the emperor from crossing a river by boat, leading to a safer bridge crossing. After ten months, he resigned due to disasters and was dismissed, returning home with honor.

Ping Dang, from Pingling, was proficient in classics and served as prime minister under Emperor Ai. He argued for maintaining the Supreme Emperor’s mausoleum and temple gardens, citing filial piety. He also recommended relief for refugees and opposed granting a marquisate to Chunyu Chang, leading to his demotion. Later, he was recalled but fell ill, refusing to accept a marquisate under duress, and died after a month.

Peng Xuan, from Huaiyang, studied the "Book of Changes" and served as a general. Emperor Ai dismissed him due to his kingdom origins, but he was later recalled. After Emperor Ai’s death, Wang Mang took power, and Peng Xuan resigned, citing old age. Wang Mang, resenting his resignation, denied him customary gifts. Peng Xuan died in his fief, and his title was later abolished.

The text concludes with praise for these officials: Jun Buyi for his wisdom and achievements, Shu Guang for timely retirement, Yu Dingguo for his fairness, and Xue Guangde, Ping Dang, and Peng Xuan for their integrity, contrasting them with those who cling to power.
