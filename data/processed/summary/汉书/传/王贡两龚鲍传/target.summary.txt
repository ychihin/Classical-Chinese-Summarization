**Summary of Historical Records on Hermits, Officials, and Moral Integrity**

King Wu of Zhou overthrew King Zhou of Shang, moving the Nine Tripods to Luoyi. Boyi and Shuqi, deeming King Wu unfaithful and unfilial, starved to death on Shouyang Mountain rather than eat Zhou grain, earning praise for their moral purity. Confucius lauded their unwavering resolve, and Mencius declared that hearing of Boyi’s conduct could reform the greedy and cowardly, inspiring generations. After the Han rose, four recluses—Yuan Gong, Qili Ji, Xia Huang Gong, and Mr. Jiaoli—fled to Shangluo Mountain during Qin rule. Emperor Gaozu summoned them in vain, but Empress Lu, following Zhang Liang’s strategy, had the crown prince humbly invite them with gifts. Their presence stabilized the prince’s position, as recorded in the *Biography of the Marquis of Liu*. Later, Zheng Zizhen of Gu Valley and Yan Junping of Shu cultivated self-respect, refusing improper attire or food. Zheng Zizhen declined recruitment by General Wang Feng. Yan Junping, a diviner in Chengdu, used his trade to guide people toward filial piety, obedience, and loyalty, earning a modest living before teaching Laozi. He authored over 100,000 words on Daoist philosophy. Yang Xiong, who studied under him, praised his conduct to officials, noting that Yan could be seen but not employed. Yan lived past ninety, revered by the people of Shu. Yang Xiong later wrote that a gentleman’s virtue establishes his reputation, contrasting the wealth of princes with the enduring fame of recluses like Zheng Zizhen and Yan Junping, whom he deemed national treasures.

Wang Ji (Ziyang) of Langxie excelled in classics, serving as a county official and later as lieutenant of Changyi. He admonished King Changyi for excessive hunting, citing ancient customs and the *Book of Songs* to urge moderation, warning that reckless travel harmed health and governance. He advocated study, ritual, and self-cultivation over indulgence. The king, though flawed, rewarded Wang Ji for his loyalty but continued his ways. After Emperor Zhao’s death, General Huo Guang summoned King Changyi to the capital. Wang Ji advised the king to mourn silently and defer to Huo Guang, comparing him to Zhou Gong and Yi Yin. The king was deposed after 20 days for lewdness; most of his officials were executed, but Wang Ji and Gong Sui were spared due to their admonitions, though sentenced to shave their heads and build cities. Wang Ji later served as governor of Yizhou and a court doctor. Under Emperor Xuan, he criticized the court’s focus on trivial matters over foundational governance, urging ritual education, selection of virtuous aides, and rejection of hereditary offices. He proposed reducing extravagance, abolishing coinage, and promoting agriculture. The emperor deemed his ideas impractical, so Wang Ji resigned, returning to Langxie. His reputation for integrity was marred by a minor incident: he divorced his wife for picking dates from a neighbor’s tree, though neighbors persuaded him to take her back. Wang Ji taught the Five Classics and died en route to a summons from Emperor Yuan. His son Wang Jun, also virtuous, served as an admonisher and governor, known for his capability alongside Zhao Guanghan and Zhang Chang. Wang Jun, after his wife’s death, refused remarriage, citing lack of virtue. His son Wang Chong became a censor but was demoted for pleading for a relative; later, under Wang Mang, he became Grand Sikong and Marquis of Fuping, but died by poisoning. The Wang family maintained honesty across generations, though their love for fine carriages and clothes puzzled contemporaries, leading to the saying “Wang Yang could turn gold.”

Gong Yu (Shaoweng) of Langxie, known for his classic learning, served as a doctor and governor of Liangzhou before resigning. Recruited by Emperor Yuan, he advised frugality, citing ancient standards: few concubines, simple palaces, and light taxes. He criticized the extravagance of Emperor Wu’s era, noting that officials and nobles overstepped ranks, leading to waste and poverty. He urged reducing palace horses, craftsmen, and concubines, and returning gardens to the poor. Emperor Yuan adopted some suggestions, cutting horse feed and distributing land. Gong Yu later became a censor, submitting dozens of memorials. He argued that taxes on three-year-olds caused infanticide, proposing taxes start at age seven. He condemned coinage for diverting labor from farming, causing hunger, and advocated returning to barter. He also criticized atonement laws for encouraging corruption, urging strict punishment for dishonest officials. Emperor Yuan implemented the seven-year tax rule and reduced palace guards, though other reforms stalled. Gong Yu died shortly after becoming censor; the emperor granted his son a post. His proposals on temple abolition were later enacted but criticized by scholars.

Gong Sheng (Junbin) and Gong She (Junqian) of Chu, known for integrity, were called the “Two Gongs.” Gong Sheng served as an admonisher under Emperor Ai, criticizing poverty, crime, and official corruption. He accused Dong Xian of disrupting court order, leading to conflict with colleagues. After a dispute with Xiahou Chang over ritual, he was demoted and resigned. Recruited again, he refused to serve under Wang Mang, who sent envoys with lavish gifts. Gong Sheng, bedridden, refused the seal, saying he could not serve two dynasties. He starved himself to death at 79, instructing simple burial. Gong She, also a classicist, served briefly as governor of Taishan but resigned due to illness. He died at 68 during Wang Mang’s regency. Bao Xuan (Zidu) of Bohai, a diligent scholar, served as an admonisher under Emperor Ai. He wrote vehemently against the favoritism shown to Dong Xian and the Fu and Ding clans, warning that corruption, heavy taxes, and harsh laws caused seven reasons for displacement and seven causes of death. He urged the recall of virtuous officials like He Wu and Kong Guang. Emperor Ai initially tolerated him, but after Bao Xuan humiliated a prime minister’s subordinate, he was imprisoned. Over a thousand students protested, leading to his sentence being reduced to shaving and iron chains. He later moved to Shangdang, where Wang Mang had him executed for associating with a wanted man. Other figures like Xin Xing, Ji Kui, and Xue Fang displayed varying degrees of integrity, with some refusing Wang Mang’s summons. The historian concludes that while hermits and officials each have merits, true virtue lies in unwavering adherence to the good path, as exemplified by Gong Sheng and Xue Fang.
