Dongfang Shuo, courtesy name Manqian, was born in Yanci County, Pingyuan County. After Emperor Wu of Han ascended the throne, he sought scholars of integrity and literature. Dongfang Shuo wrote a letter to the emperor, boasting of his abilities: he studied writing at thirteen, fencing at fifteen, and by sixteen had memorized 220,000 words of the *Book of Songs* and *Shangshu*. At nineteen, he memorized another 220,000 words of Sun Wu's *Art of War*. He described himself as tall, brave, agile, honest, and trustworthy, claiming he could serve as a minister. Emperor Wu, impressed by his audacity, ordered him to stay at the Gongche Mansion, though his salary was meager and he received no audience.

Dongfang Shuo once tricked the dwarfs guarding the royal horse pen, claiming the emperor planned to kill them for being useless. When the dwarfs tearfully begged the emperor, he summoned Dongfang Shuo, who argued that the dwarfs were short and received the same salary as him, a tall man, leading to unfairness. Emperor Wu laughed and promoted him to a closer position. Later, Dongfang Shuo correctly guessed a gecko under a basin using the *I Ching*, earning silk rewards. When a courtier challenged him, he guessed a parasitic growth on a tree, proving his skill. He also cleverly deflected accusations of slander with riddles, impressing the emperor, who appointed him Chang Shilang.

On a dog day, Dongfang Shuo cut meat from the emperor's reward without waiting for orders, taking it home. When questioned, he humorously blamed himself, praising his own actions, which amused Emperor Wu, who rewarded him with wine and meat. Emperor Wu often hunted incognito, causing distress to farmers. He planned to build Shanglin Garden, but Dongfang Shuo admonished him, citing three reasons: it would ruin fertile land, destroy homes and tombs, and waste resources. He warned against luxury, referencing historical examples like King Zhou of Yin and Qin Shihuang. Despite this, Emperor Wu built the garden but promoted Dongfang Shuo to Taizhong doctor.

When Lord Zhaoping, son of Princess Longlu, killed a nanny and faced execution, Emperor Wu mourned but upheld the law. Dongfang Shuo congratulated him, praising his impartiality. The emperor initially scolded him but later accepted his explanation that wine could relieve sorrow. Dongfang Shuo was demoted for urinating in the palace but reinstated after this exchange. He also advised against allowing Dong Yan, a favorite of Princess Guantao, into the palace, citing three crimes. Emperor Wu heeded his advice, and Dong Yan's influence waned.

Dongfang Shuo urged Emperor Wu to emulate Emperor Wen's frugality to reform society, criticizing the current extravagance. When asked to compare himself to other scholars, he humorously belittled them. He wrote "Replying to Guests" and "On Mr. Feiyou," defending his low status and arguing that times had changed. In "On Mr. Feiyou," he discussed the dangers of outspoken advice and the importance of wise rulers. His works were recorded by Liu Xiang, though many rumors about him were false. Historians note his wit and humor, comparing him to a comedic figure, but his advice was often insightful.
