Emperor Xiaojing had fourteen sons by various consorts. Among them, Liu De, the King of Hejian, was a notable scholar who loved ancient texts. He diligently collected books from the people, offering generous compensation for rare copies, amassing a collection as vast as the imperial library. Unlike King Huainan of An, whose books were often useless, Liu De’s collection consisted of pre-Qin classics like the *Zhou Guan*, *Shangshu*, *Rites*, *Mencius*, and *Laozi*, all attributed to Confucius’s seventy disciples. He established doctors for the *Mao Poetry* and *Zuo’s Spring and Autumn Annals*, practiced rituals and music, and adhered to Confucian teachings, attracting many scholars from Shandong. During Emperor Wu’s reign, he presented elegant music and answered over thirty questions from the court with clarity and reason. After reigning for twenty-six years, he died, praised by officials for his uprightness, kindness, and frugality. His descendants ruled Hejian for generations, though later kings like Liu Yuan faced scandal and deposition for cruelty and misconduct, leading to the kingdom’s abolition and restoration under Emperor Cheng.

Other sons of Emperor Xiaojing had varied fates. Liu Rong, the Min King of Linjiang, was once crown prince but was deposed and later committed suicide after being accused of building a palace on temple grounds. Liu Yu, the King of Lu, was known for building palaces and raising dogs and horses, but he had a stutter and disliked literature. His descendant, Liu Gong, destroyed Confucius’s old house to expand his palace but stopped upon hearing music, discovering ancient scriptures in the walls. Liu Fei, the King of Yi in Jiangdu, was a talented youth who fought against Wu during the rebellion, but later became arrogant and extravagant. His son, Liu Jian, was notoriously depraved, committing adultery, murder, and even plotting rebellion with weapons and seals. He was eventually forced to commit suicide, and his kingdom was abolished. Liu Duan, the King of Jiaoxi, was cruel and physically weak, often killing officials who tried to govern lawfully, leading to his kingdom’s reduction and eventual abolition.

Liu Pengzu, the King of Zhao, was deceitful and flattering, using sophistry to frame officials and amassing wealth through trade. He died after sixty years of rule, but his son Liu Dan was executed for promiscuity and murder. Liu Sheng, the King of Zhongshan, was a drunkard with over 120 children, but he famously wept before Emperor Wu, complaining of officials’ encroachments on princes’ rights. This led to Emperor Wu’s policy of dividing princes’ lands among their sons, weakening their power. Liu Fa, the King of Changsha, was born from a concubine’s deception and ruled a poor, humid kingdom. His descendants faced scandals, including a king who burned houses and killed people. Liu Yue, the King of Guangchuan, died early, and his son Liu Qi was accused of incest and false accusations, leading to his kingdom’s temporary abolition. Liu Qu, a later king, was a scholar who studied the *Yi* and *Analects*, but he became tyrannical, torturing and killing concubines under the influence of his queen, Zhaoxin. He was deposed and committed suicide.

Other kings included Liu Shun of Changshan, who was arrogant but often forgiven by his father. His son Liu Bo was deposed for neglecting his father’s funeral, and the kingdom was divided among his brothers. Liu Ji of Jiaodong was cautious during the Huainan rebellion, but his sons were made kings of smaller states. The text concludes with a reflection on the dangers of luxury and power, noting that most princes became arrogant and immoral, except for the noble King Xian of Hejian.
