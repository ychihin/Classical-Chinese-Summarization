He Wu, styled Jungong, was from Pixian County, Shu County. During the reign of Emperor Xuan, peace prevailed, and He Wu, at fourteen or fifteen, learned to sing poems composed by Wang Li in Chengdu. Summoned by the emperor, he later studied the *Book of Changes*, became a doctor, and served as county magistrate of E County, though he was dismissed for legal violations. His five brothers all held county offices, earning local respect. He Wu once handled a tax dispute involving his brother He Xian with integrity, reporting the tax collector Qiu Shang to the prefect, who conscripted Qiu Shang, earning admiration.

He Wu later became a censor and governor of Yangzhou. He dealt with officials like Dai Sheng, a great Confucian, with fairness, not publicizing Dai Sheng's slander and ensuring his son received a just trial. As governor, He Wu prioritized visiting academies to examine students and assess political gains before meeting county officials. He maintained strict propriety, refusing private requests, as when he resisted pressure from He Xian and Yang Fuzhong to summon a relative. After five years, he became Prime Minister Sizhi, then prefect of Qinghe, but was dismissed due to disasters. Later, he served as admonishing doctor, governor of Yanzhou, Sili Xiaowei, and Jing Zhaoyin. He was demoted for a recommendation error but rose to become Grand Sikong and Marquis of Fanxiang.

He Wu was kind and loved recommending scholars, treating people like Gong Sheng and Tang Lin favorably. He disliked cliques, cross-examining Confucians and civil servants. He proposed reforms, such as changing internal history to lieutenant, and worked with Fang Jin to abolish governorships for prefectures, though the old system was later restored. His achievements rivaled Xue Xuan's, but his talent was lesser, while his classical knowledge and integrity were greater. After Emperor Cheng's death, He Wu was accused of unfilial conduct and dismissed by Emperor Ai. Later, Bao Xuan and Wang Jia defended him, and he was recruited as imperial censor, then former general.

When Emperor Ai died, the Queen Mother appointed Wang Mang as Grand Sima. He Wu and Gongsun Lu, fearing relatives' control, recommended each other, but the Queen Mother chose Wang Mang. Wang Mang had them dismissed for mutual recommendation. As Wang Mang's power grew, He Wu was framed and committed suicide. Many thought him wronged, so Wang Mang made his son a marquis, but the title was later abolished.

Wang Jia, styled Gongzhong, from Huling, passed examinations and became a doctor, but was dismissed for a gate duty error. He later served as Nanling county magistrate and Changling county captain. During the Hongjia period, he was promoted to Taizhong doctor, then governor of Jiujiang and Henan, gaining fame. He became Da Honglu, Jing Zhaoyin, and imperial censor, then prime minister and Marquis of Xinfu. Wang Jia was upright and prestigious. He advised Emperor Ai to carefully select talents, forgive minor faults, and encourage officials, citing historical examples like Wei Shang and Han Anguo. He recommended Confucian scholars and talented officials, all of whom became famous.

When Dong Xian was favored, Wang Jia opposed granting him a title without proper discussion, warning against losing public trust. The emperor initially relented but later granted the title, reprimanding Wang Jia. Wang Jia continued to admonish, criticizing Dong Xian's extravagance and the emperor's favoritism, which harmed the state. He urged restraint, citing past examples like Emperor Xiaowen's frugality. The emperor grew displeased but doted on Dong Xian more. Wang Jia also recommended Liang Xiang and others, but the emperor, angered by Wang Jia's defense of them, had him questioned. Wang Jia was impeached for confusing the country and died in prison after refusing food. After his death, the emperor regretted it and later honored him.

Shi Dan, styled Zhonggong, from Langye, studied the *Book of Songs* under Kuang Heng. He became a doctor, then Taifu of King Shuping. Recommended by Fang Jin and Kong Guang, he served as Dr. Guanglu and Prime Minister Sizhi, later becoming Shaofu, Guangluxun, and tutor to the crown prince. When Emperor Ai ascended, Shi Dan became Grand Sima and Marquis of Gaole, then Grand Sikong. He advised the emperor to be cautious, citing disasters from hasty changes. He opposed honoring Empress Dowager Fu and Empress Ding with imperial titles, arguing it violated ritual propriety. This led to his disfavor.

Shi Dan was later accused of leaking a sealed memorial and dismissed for deceit. Despite Tang Lin's plea, he was reduced to Marquis of Guannei. After Zhu Bo's rise, Shi Dan was further demoted for opposing the honorary titles. When Emperor Ping came to power, Wang Mang restored Shi Dan, granting him the title of Marquis of Yiyang. He died shortly after, and his title ended with Wang Mang's fall.

The commentary notes that He Wu, Wang Jia, and Shi Dan's warnings proved true. Wang Mang's rise and Dong Xian's favor led to He Wu and Wang Jia's downfalls, while Shi Dan faced alternating rewards and punishments. It reflects the difficulty of receiving titles in turbulent times.
