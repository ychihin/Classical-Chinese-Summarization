King Li of Huainan, the youngest son of Emperor Gao of Han, was born to a former beauty of King Zhao Zhang Ao. His mother became pregnant after being favored by Emperor Gao during a visit to Zhao. When King Zhao was implicated in a rebellion, she was imprisoned and revealed her pregnancy, but Emperor Gao ignored her pleas. After giving birth to King Li, she committed suicide out of resentment. Emperor Gao regretted this and had Empress Lu raise the child. In the eleventh year of his reign, Emperor Gao made Liu Chang the King of Huainan after suppressing a rebellion.

King Li grew up relying on Empress Lu and was favored during the reigns of Emperor Xiaohui and Empress Lu, but he resented Marquis Piyang for not saving his mother. After Emperor Xiaowen ascended, King Li became arrogant and broke laws repeatedly. In the third year of Emperor Wen's reign, he killed Marquis Piyang in revenge, claiming three crimes: failing to save his mother, not preventing Empress Lu's murder of King Zhao Ruyi, and not stopping Empress Lu from ennobling the Lu family. Emperor Wen pardoned him out of pity, but King Li grew more defiant, making his own laws and acting like an emperor.

Emperor Wen's uncle Bo Zhao admonished King Li in a letter, listing eight dangerous behaviors: abandoning his state, disobeying orders, prioritizing his mother's grave over his father's, and more. He warned that disaster would follow if King Li did not reform. King Li was displeased and ignored the advice. In the sixth year, he conspired with seventy men, including Chai Qi, to rebel, contacting Minyue and Xiongnu. The plot was discovered, and King Li was summoned to Chang'an. After interrogation, officials reported his crimes: abandoning Han laws, appointing officials without authorization, harboring fugitives, and planning rebellion. They demanded his execution, but Emperor Wen commuted the sentence to exile in Shu County.

King Li refused food and died on the way. Emperor Wen mourned and executed the officials who failed to provide food. He later enfeoffed King Li's four sons as marquises. In the sixteenth year, he made three of them kings in Huainan. During the Rebellion of the Seven States, King Huainan Liu An initially considered joining Wu but was restrained by his prime minister. Liu An, known for his literary pursuits, compiled the "Inner Books" and other works. He plotted rebellion after a comet appeared, believing the emperor had no heir. His daughter Liu Ling spied in Chang'an. The plot was exposed when his grandson Liu Jian informed on him. King Huainan committed suicide, and his kingdom was abolished as Jiujiang County.

King Hengshan Liu Ci, brother of King Huainan, also plotted rebellion. He feuded with his son, Prince Liu Shuang, and favored his younger son Liu Xiao. After Queen Chengshu died, Queen Xu Lai and concubine Jue Ji slandered the prince. The prince was whipped and later accused his father of rebellion. King Hengshan committed suicide when investigated. His kingdom was also abolished. The historian comments that both kings, despite being imperial relatives, perished due to their rebellious nature and the corrupting influence of their ministers, noting that people of Jingchu have historically been prone to insurrection.
