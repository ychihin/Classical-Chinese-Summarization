Xue Xuan, a native of Tan County, Shuhai, rose from minor official posts to become a respected administrator. Known for his integrity and legal expertise, he served as Censor Zhongcheng under Emperor Cheng, advocating for simpler governance and criticizing harsh laws that disrupted social harmony. His memorials urged leniency and moral cultivation, which the emperor approved. As governor of Linhuai and later Chenliu, he quelled bandits and earned widespread respect. In Zuo Fengyi, he skillfully handled corrupt officials like Yang Zhan and Xie You, using subtle pressure to force their resignations, while also reassigning ineffective magistrates to improve governance. Xue Xuan’s fair and compassionate rule, including his handling of a jailer’s suicide, won him praise. He was promoted to Shaofu and later, on Gu Yong’s recommendation, became Imperial Censor and then Prime Minister, enfeoffed as Marquis of Gaoyang. However, his tenure was marred by criticism for being overly meticulous and lacking depth in classical learning. After failing to quell uprisings in Guanghan and mishandling tax collection during a funeral, he was dismissed by the emperor for incompetence. Later reinstated as a special advisor, he was again dismissed for associating with the disgraced Chunyu Chang. His later years were troubled by family discord with his brother Xue Xiu over mourning rites, and his son Xue Kuang’s violent attack on Shen Xian, who had slandered Xue Xuan. Despite legal debates, Xue Kuang was exiled, and Xue Xuan died at home as a commoner.

Zhu Bo, from Duling, began as a poor county official, known for his boldness and friendship with elite sons like Chen Xian. He famously disguised himself to save Chen Xian from prison, enduring punishment himself, which launched his reputation. Under Wang Feng, he became magistrate of several counties, excelling in governance. As governor of Jizhou, he astutely handled a flood of complaints by issuing swift verdicts, astonishing officials. In Langye, he aggressively reformed lax customs, dismissing sick officials and enforcing strict dress codes, while using intimidation and rewards to control subordinates. His tenure in Zuo Fengyi was marked by cunning and harshness, such as rehabilitating a scarred thief as an informant and exposing corrupt officials through psychological tactics. Promoted to Da Si Nong and later Tingwei, he demonstrated legal acumen by correctly predicting case outcomes. He became Grand Sikong under Emperor Ai, advocating for restoring the Censor’s office over the Grand Sikong post, arguing it better suited the Han system. He also reversed the abolition of provincial governors, favoring their supervisory role. Frugal and workaholic, Zhu Bo shunned luxury but cultivated a network of guests, aiding their ambitions. However, he became entangled in court intrigues, allying with Fu Yan to please Empress Dowager Dingtao, leading to the dismissal of rivals like Kong Guang. As Prime Minister, he was pressured by the empress dowager to impeach Fu Xi, but the emperor discovered his collusion. Interrogated, Zhu Bo was charged with treason for manipulating affairs with relatives, and he committed suicide, his fief revoked. His rise and fall exemplified ambition without moral restraint, as noted in historical critiques.
