Wei Xian, courtesy name Changru, was from Zou in the State of Lu. His ancestor, Wei Meng, served as a tutor to King Yuan of Chu and his descendants. Because Liu Wu was dissolute, Wei Meng wrote poems to admonish him, then resigned and moved his family to Zoudi, where he wrote another remonstrative poem. This poem recounts the history of their lineage from the Shang Dynasty through the Zhou, the decline of Chu, and the rise of the Han Dynasty. It criticizes the current king for neglecting state affairs, indulging in pleasure, and surrounding himself with flatterers, warning him to learn from past examples. Wei Meng later died in Zoudi. It was passed down for five generations to Wei Xian.

Wei Xian was a simple, pure-hearted man devoted to learning. He was proficient in the "Li" and "Shangshu," taught the "Poetry," and was called the great Confucian of Zou Lu. He was recruited as a doctor, taught Emperor Zhao the "Poetry," and was promoted to Guanglu doctor Zhan Shi and later Dahonglu. After Emperor Zhao died without an heir, Wei Xian was made Marquis of Guannei for his role in supporting Emperor Xuan. He became Shaofu of Changxin Palace and was highly respected. In the third year of Benshi, he became prime minister and was granted the title of Marquis of Fuyang. Over seventy, he served for five years before resigning due to illness, receiving gold and a mansion. His resignation began the practice of prime ministers retiring. He died at eighty-two and was given the title of Jiehou.

Wei Xian had four sons. The eldest, Wei Fangshan, died early. The second, Wei Hong, became a prefect. The third, Wei Shun, guarded the ancestral cemetery. The youngest, Wei Xuancheng, became prime minister due to his knowledge of the classics. A proverb in Zoulu stated it was better to leave a son a classic than a box of gold. Wei Xuancheng was appointed as a Langguan due to his father's favor. He was eager to learn, humble, and respectful to all, earning a wide reputation. He was promoted to admonishing doctor and then captain of Dahe County.

When Wei Xian was ill, he was angry that Wei Hong, who was supposed to be the heir, was imprisoned for a crime. Wei Xian's disciple and clan members, pretending it was his order, established Wei Xuancheng as the successor. Xuancheng knew this was not his father's wish, so he feigned insanity to avoid inheriting the title. The court investigated, and many believed he was trying to give the title to his brother. The prime minister and censor impeached him for feigning illness, but the emperor did not punish him and instead called him to serve. Xuancheng accepted the title. Emperor Xuan made him governor of Henan and his brother Wei Hong a captain. Xuancheng later became a guard in Weiyang Palace and was promoted to Taichang. Due to a mistake during a rainstorm when he rode instead of using a four-horse cart to the ancestral temple, he was demoted to the rank of Guannei Marquis. He was deeply saddened and wrote a poem blaming himself, lamenting his loss of title and vowing to be more careful.

Later, Emperor Xuan wanted someone to influence King Xian of Huaiyang, so he appointed Wei Xuancheng as the lieutenant of Huaiyang. After Emperor Yuan came to the throne, Xuancheng became Shaofu, Prince Taifu, and Yushi Dafu. During the Yongguang period, he replaced Yu Dingguo as prime minister, returning to the same position his father held. He wrote another poem admonishing his descendants to be careful in their duties and dress. He served as prime minister for seven years, surpassing his father in literary talent but not in ethical stability. He died in the third year of Jianzhao and was named Gonghou. He requested to be buried with his father, which was granted. His descendants inherited his title.
