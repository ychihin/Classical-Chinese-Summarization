# Summary of Historical Records: Kuang Heng, Zhang Yu, Kong Guang, and Ma Gong

Kuang Heng, courtesy name Zhigui, was born in Haicheng County. His family had farmed for generations, and despite poverty, he loved studying and worked as a hired laborer to fund his education. His energy exceeded ordinary people, and many Confucian scholars praised him. He was awarded the title of "Jia Ke" but failed to pass its regulations, so he was appointed as Taichang's storyteller and later as Pingyuan County Literature. Scholars recommended him as unparalleled in classical studies, but Emperor Xuan did not favor Confucian scholars and sent him back. However, the crown prince secretly appreciated him.

When Emperor Yuan ascended the throne, Shi Gao appointed Kuang Heng as historian of Cao and recommended him to the emperor, who made him a doctor. During disasters like solar eclipses and earthquakes, Kuang Heng submitted a memorial stating that different times required different governance. He observed that amnesty did not reduce crime and argued that education should promote etiquette and humility. He criticized corrupt customs, greed, and luxury, urging the emperor to set an example through moral leadership. He cited classical texts to support his arguments and recommended reducing palace expenses, eliminating extravagance, promoting virtuous officials, and reforming education. The emperor was pleased and promoted him to Guanglu Doctor and Prince Shaofu.

Kuang Heng frequently advised the emperor on proper conduct, emphasizing that governing a country begins with governing one's family. He stressed the importance of distinguishing between the queen and concubines and establishing proper hierarchies. After Emperor Cheng ascended the throne, Kuang Heng warned about choosing a spouse according to Confucian etiquette. He later submitted a memorial to renovate suburban temples and cancel extravagant sacrifices. During Emperor Yuan's reign, Kuang Heng feared Zhongshu Ling Shi Xian and did not oppose him. When Emperor Cheng first ruled, Kuang Heng and Zhen Tan impeached Shi Xian, but were themselves impeached for not reporting Shi Xian's crimes earlier. The emperor did not punish Kuang Heng, but he felt ashamed and asked to resign. The emperor refused and encouraged him.

Kuang Heng's son Kuang Chang killed someone while drunk and was imprisoned. Kuang Heng's brother conspired to rescue him, and when discovered, Kuang Heng pleaded guilty. The emperor restored his position, but Kuang Heng was later impeached for land theft. His fiefdom in Le'an Township had mistakenly used incorrect boundaries for over ten years, gaining 400 extra hectares. Kuang Heng did not correct this and collected rent from the additional land. He was impeached, and although the emperor did not punish him severely, he was removed from the post of prime minister and demoted to a commoner. He died at home. His son Kuang Xian was also proficient in classics and held the position of Jiu Qing.

Zhang Yu, courtesy name Ziwen, was born in Ruan County. As a child, he liked watching fortune tellers and became skilled at divination. He studied the Book of Changes and the Analects of Confucius in Chang'an, then gathered disciples to teach. During Emperor Xuan's reign, Xiao Wangzhi praised his knowledge, but the emperor did not approve his appointment. Later, he became a doctor and taught the prince the Analects of Confucius. When Emperor Cheng ascended, Zhang Yu was appointed Guanglu Dafu and served as Shizhong. He felt uneasy serving alongside the authoritarian Wang Feng and repeatedly asked to resign, but the emperor refused and rewarded him generously. Zhang Yu eventually became prime minister and was granted the title of Marquis of Anchang. After six years, he resigned due to old age and illness.

Zhang Yu was cautious and kind, engaging in business and farming. He bought 400 hectares of fertile farmland and lived luxuriously, enjoying music. He treated his students differently: with Dai Chong, he shared wine and music in the back hall, while with Peng Xuan, he had simple meals in the main hall. When old, he built a tomb at Fei Niu Pavilion in Pingling, which was a royal ancestral worship site. Despite Wang Gen's objections, the emperor granted his request. Wang Gen slandered him, but the emperor respected Zhang Yu even more. When the emperor visited him during illness, Zhang Yu requested that his daughter be closer, and the emperor transferred her husband. The emperor also appointed Zhang Yu's youngest son as Huang Menlang. Zhang Yu served as the emperor's teacher even after resigning, and the emperor consulted him on major decisions. During solar eclipses and earthquakes, when officials blamed the Wang family, Zhang Yu advised the emperor not to trust such claims, dispelling doubts about Wang. He died in the second year of Jianping and was given the title of Marquis of Jie.

Kong Guang, courtesy name Zixia, was the fourteenth generation grandson of Confucius. His father Kong Ba was a doctor and teacher to the crown prince. Kong Guang was proficient in classics and was elected as Yilang before age twenty. Kuang Heng recommended him as an admonishing doctor, but he was demoted for disagreeing with the emperor. He later became a doctor and successfully completed missions, gaining fame. He was promoted to Guanglu Dafu and served as Shangshu for over ten years, maintaining laws and revising systems. He was cautious, never leaking court affairs, and avoided forcefully remonstrating. He recommended officials secretly and did not form factions. He became imperial censor and later prime minister, granted the title of Marquis of Boshan.

During the succession debate, Kong Guang argued for King Zhongshan based on blood relations, but the emperor chose King Dingtao. Kong Guang was demoted to Tingwei. Later, he argued that Chunyu Chang's concubines who left before his crime should not be implicated, and the emperor agreed. After Emperor Cheng's sudden death, Kong Guang was appointed prime minister. When Emperor Ai ruled, Kong Guang advised against Empress Dowager Fu living close to the emperor, but was overruled. He later opposed retaining the treacherous Fu Qian, but the emperor kept him. Kong Guang was eventually dismissed for failing to advise properly during disasters. After several prime ministers failed, the emperor recalled Kong Guang during a solar eclipse. Kong Guang explained that eclipses occurred when ministers became too powerful and advised the emperor to reform governance. The emperor reappointed him as prime minister.

Under Empress Dowager and Wang Mang's rule, Kong Guang was used to draft documents attacking political opponents. Fearing Wang Mang's growing power, Kong Guang tried to resign. Wang Mang appointed him as the emperor's Taifu, and later Taishi. Kong Guang often claimed illness to avoid attending court with Wang Mang. He served as imperial censor and prime minister twice, and as chief situ, Taifu, and Taishi, holding high positions for seventeen years. He died at age seventy in the fifth year of Yuanshi, receiving an elaborate funeral with thousands of mourners. His title was Jian Liehou. His descendants inherited his title, and Wang Mang later appointed his nephew as Grand Sima.

Ma Gong, courtesy name Youqing, studied Yan's Spring and Autumn Annals and served as a minister. He was promoted to Ting Weiping and served as governor of several provinces, gaining praise. He replaced Kong Guang as chief disciple and was granted the title of Fudehou. After Kong Guang's death, Ma Gong succeeded him as Grand Master and Situ. During the Yuanshi period, Wang Mang investigated those who discussed Empress Dowager Fu's funeral. Because Ma Gong was Wang Mang's friend, he was not investigated, but he felt ashamed and resigned. Wang Mang allowed him to return home. After Wang Mang usurped the throne, Ma Gong became the crown prince's counselor and died in office.
