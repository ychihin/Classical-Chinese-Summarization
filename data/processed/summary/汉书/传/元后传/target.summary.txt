# Summary of the Historical Text on Queen Xiaoyuan and the Wang Family

Queen Xiaoyuan was the aunt of Wang Mang, who claimed descent from the Yellow Emperor. According to Wang Mang's autobiography "Ziben," the lineage traced from the Yellow Emperor through Yu Shun to various descendants who were granted titles during the Zhou Dynasty. The family eventually took the surname Wang after Tian An, a descendant of Qi royalty, lost his feudal state during the Han Dynasty and was called "Wang Family" by the people of Qi.

Wang Mang's ancestor Wang He (Wengru) served as Emperor Wu's embroidered censor. Unlike other harsh officials who killed tens of thousands, Wengru showed leniency and was dismissed for not following the emperor's wishes. He saved over ten thousand people and predicted his descendants would prosper. After resigning, he moved to Yuancheng in Wei County, where a divination predicted that a virtuous woman would appear in 645 years, and later that the Wang family would have a noble daughter who would flourish.

Wengru's son Wang Jin fathered Wang Zhengjun, who became Empress of Emperor Yuan. Before her birth, her mother dreamed of the moon falling into her belly. Zhengjun was gentle and virtuous. After two betrothals ended with her fiancés dying, a fortune-teller declared her extremely valuable. She entered the palace at eighteen and became pregnant after being summoned by the crown prince, giving birth to Emperor Cheng.

When Emperor Yuan considered deposing the prince due to his fondness for King Dingtao Gong, Wang Feng (the queen's brother) worried alongside the queen. Emperor Yuan ultimately did not depose the prince due to the queen's caution and the late emperor's regard for him. After Emperor Yuan's death, Emperor Cheng ascended, honoring his mother as Empress Dowager and appointing Wang Feng as Grand General. The Wang family's prosperity began, with five brothers granted marquisates on the same day, known as the Five Marquises.

Wang Feng held immense power, with Emperor Cheng afraid to act independently. When Emperor Cheng wanted to appoint Liu Xin as a regular attendant, he was told to ask Wang Feng first, who refused. Emperor Cheng had no choice but to give up. Wang Feng dominated all state affairs, and ministers dared not look directly at him.

After Wang Feng's death, Wang Yin succeeded him as Grand Sima, followed by Wang Shang and Wang Gen. The Wang family's extravagance was legendary, with concubines, slaves, and lavish palaces. The common people sang songs comparing their palaces to the emperor's White Tiger Palace. Despite their excesses, they cultivated wise men to appear refined.

Emperor Cheng had no heirs for over twenty years. King Dingtao's grandmother bribed Wang Gen to make him crown prince. After Emperor Cheng's death, Emperor Ai ascended but was unhappy with the Wang family's power. He eventually forced Wang Mang and others to return to their fiefdoms. When Emperor Ai died without an heir, the Empress Dowager appointed Wang Mang as Grand Sima, and they installed Emperor Ping, who was only nine years old.

Wang Mang gradually consolidated power, eventually becoming regent and later usurping the throne to establish the Xin dynasty. The Empress Dowager refused to give him the imperial seal, angrily denouncing him for betraying the Han dynasty. She threw the seal on the ground, predicting the Wang family would be exterminated. Wang Mang changed her title to "New Empress Dowager Wenmu," severing her connection to Han. She died at eighty-four, and ten years later, Han soldiers killed Wang Mang.

The historian Ban Biao noted that since the Three Dynasties, few dynasties fell due to favored women. The Wang family's rise came through four generations of empresses, with five generals and ten marquises, culminating in Wang Mang's usurpation. The Yuan Empress Dowager's loyalty, holding the imperial seal and refusing to surrender it, was truly pitiful.
