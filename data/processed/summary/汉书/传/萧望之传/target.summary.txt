Xiao Wangzhi, courtesy name Changqian, was a scholar from Lanling who later moved to Duling. He studied the "Qi Shi" under Houcang for nearly ten years, and also studied under Dr. Bai Qi and Xia Housheng, mastering "The Analects of Confucius" and "Etiquette." His reputation grew among Confucian scholars in the capital.

During the reign of General Huo Guang, Xiao Wangzhi was recommended for office but refused to submit to the humiliating security procedures of being stripped and searched before meeting Huo Guang. He advised Huo Guang to treat scholars with respect, but Huo Guang did not appoint him. Xiao Wangzith later became a Langguan after passing an exam, serving as Xiaoyuan East Gate Marquis. When his younger brother committed a crime, he was dismissed and returned to his hometown as a county official.

After Huo Guang's death, Xiao Wangzhi was appointed as Minister of Ceremonies of Daxing. He warned Emperor Xuan about the dangers of ministers holding too much power, citing historical precedents. Emperor Xuan appointed him as a visitor, and he quickly rose through the ranks to become an official of two thousand stone level. He was later appointed as the governor of Pingyuan but preferred to serve in the court. He was eventually recalled to manage the Shaofu and later became Zuo Fengyi.

During the Xiqiang rebellion, Xiao Wangzhi opposed Zhang Chang's proposal to allow criminals to atone for their sins by donating food, arguing it would lead to inequality and moral decay. His views were supported by Prime Minister Wei Xiang and Yushi doctor Bingji, and the proposal was not implemented.

Xiao Wangzith served as Zuo Fengyi for three years and was promoted to Dahonglu. He advised against marrying a princess to Wusun, citing the unreliability of the alliance, and the emperor eventually recalled the princess. During the Wufeng period, he advised against attacking the Huns during their civil strife, advocating for a policy of benevolence and assistance instead.

Xiao Wangzhi later became the imperial censor but fell out of favor with Emperor Xuan due to conflicts with the prime minister and accusations of arrogance and misconduct. He was demoted to Crown Prince Tutor. After Emperor Yuan ascended the throne, Xiao Wangzhi served as a trusted advisor but clashed with eunuchs Hong Gong and Shi Xian. He was falsely accused of conspiring to depose the general and alienate the emperor's relatives, leading to his imprisonment. He committed suicide by drinking poison, deeply saddening Emperor Yuan.

Xiao Wangzhi had eight sons, including Xiao Yu, Xiao Xian, and Xiao You, who became high officials. Xiao Yu was known for his bravery and integrity, serving in various positions despite frequent dismissals. Xiao Xian and Xiao You also held significant posts, with the Xiao family producing six or seven officials of two thousand stone level. The historian praised Xiao Wangzhi as a leading Confucian scholar and capable minister, lamenting his tragic death at the hands of sycophants and eunuchs.
