In ancient times, the emperor established a country with a clear hierarchy from officials to common people, ensuring order and obedience. Confucius noted that when the world is at peace, political power does not rest with officials, who must obey the law and face punishment for neglect or violation. However, with the decline of the Zhou royal family, princes began to control rituals and military campaigns. After Duke Huan of Qi and Duke Wen of Jin, officials gained exclusive power, leading to the Warring States Period, where princes abandoned propriety for force. Figures like Lord Xinling, Lord Pingyuan, Lord Mengchang, and Lord Chunshen relied on noble authority to become knight-errants, attracting followers who valued personal bonds over official duties. This trend continued into the Han Dynasty, where bans were loose, and powerful figures like King Liu Bi of Wu and King Liu An of Huainan hosted thousands of guests. Commoners like Guo Xie gained influence, often resorting to violence and crime, yet were admired for their generosity and charm. Despite their crimes, they were seen as sinners against orthodox moral standards, and the emperor eventually ordered Guo Xie's execution for forming a secret faction and abusing power.

Guo Xie, a native of Zhi County, was the grandson of a good minister. His father was a chivalrous man killed during Emperor Xiaowen's reign. Guo Xie was calm and brave but vicious in his youth, committing murder, robbery, and grave robbing. As he matured, he reflected on his mistakes, practiced self-discipline, and responded to grudges with kindness, though his sinister nature persisted. His sister's son was killed for forcing someone to drink, and Guo Xie, deeming the victim at fault, let the murderer go, earning respect. Another incident involved a man who disrespected Guo Xie; instead of retaliating, Guo Xie secretly exempted him from duty, leading to the man's apology and increased admiration for Guo Xie. In Luoyang, Guo Xie mediated a dispute where local celebrities failed, and he ensured the credit went to them. Despite his humble and frugal lifestyle, Guo Xie's influence grew, and he was eventually forced to move to Maoling due to imperial suspicion. After his move, conflicts arose, leading to murders and his eventual arrest. Although many crimes were pardoned, Guo Xie was executed for treason after a Confucian scholar was assassinated by his followers.

After Guo Xie, many knights emerged but none were notable. Figures like Fan Zhongzi, Wangsun Fang, and others were courteous and humble, while others like the Yao and Du families were bandits. Wan Zhang, a knight from Chang'an, became a secret friend of Jing Zhaoyin but was embarrassed by the attention he received. He later refused gifts from the disgraced Shi Xian, earning praise for his conduct. During the Heping period, Wang Zun captured and killed many heroes, including Wan Zhang. Lou Hu, from Qi State, initially practiced medicine but later studied classics and became a minor official. He gained fame by serving the five Wang brothers simultaneously, known for his eloquence and integrity. After his mother's death, thousands mourned. Lou Hu later served as an envoy, distributing gifts to relatives, and was promoted to prefect of Tianshui. He was dismissed but later reinstated, and during Wang Mang's regency, he captured a fugitive, earning a marquisate. However, he was eventually dismissed again and lived in obscurity, though he maintained his principles by supporting an old friend, Lu Gong, for life.

Chen Zun, from Duling County, was known for his indulgence and extravagance, contrasting with his friend Zhang Song's frugality. Despite his reckless behavior, Chen Zun was valued for his talent and became a magistrate. He was later dismissed for misconduct but continued to host lavish parties. During Wang Mang's reign, he served as governor of Henan County but was dismissed again. Chen Zun was known for his eloquence and charm, and his letters were treasured. He died in Shuofang after being captured by bandits. Yuan She, from Maoling, gained fame by returning funeral donations and mourning his father for three years. He became a magistrate but was dismissed after seeking revenge for his uncle's murder. Yuan She was known for his generosity and ability to mobilize people, often helping the poor and organizing funerals. However, he was also involved in violence, killing a man who slandered him. During Wang Mang's reign, Yuan She was appointed as an official but was eventually executed after ordering the assassination of a chief registrar. Since the reigns of Emperor Ai and Emperor Ping, many heroes emerged in various counties, but their numbers were too many to count.
