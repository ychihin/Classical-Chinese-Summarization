The Wusun Kingdom, governed by Da Kunmi from Chigu City, was located 8,900 miles from Chang'an. It had 120,000 households, a population of 630,000, and an army of 188,800. The kingdom had a structured government with officials including a prime minister, generals, marquises, and eunuchs. The land was grassy, rainy, and cold, with residents practicing nomadic herding like the Xiongnu. The country was rich in horses, with wealthy individuals owning up to 4,000-5,000. The people were strong-willed but greedy and unfaithful. Wusun originally submitted to the Xiongnu but later became powerful and resisted them. It bordered the Xiongnu, Kangju, Dawan, and various Chengguo Kingdoms. The region was originally inhabited by the Sai people, then conquered by the Dayuezhi, and later by Wusun Kunmo, who incorporated both Sai and Dayuezhi populations.

Zhang Qian, sent by Emperor Wu of Han, attempted to persuade Wusun to return to their homeland and ally with Han against the Xiongnu. Kunmo initially treated Zhang Qian with Xiongnu-like arrogance but eventually showed respect. Wusun was divided among Kunmo's sons, with internal conflicts over succession. Despite Zhang Qian's efforts, Wusun was reluctant to move east due to distance and longstanding ties with the Xiongnu. However, after envoys saw Han's wealth, Wusun grew more respectful. The Xiongnu, angered by Wusun's contact with Han, threatened attack, leading Wusun to propose marriage to a Han princess. The Han agreed, and during the Yuanfeng period, Princess Xijun was sent to marry Kunmo. The Xiongnu also sent a bride, and Kunmo took both as wives. The princess, homesick and unable to communicate with the elderly Kunmo, composed a sorrowful song. Emperor Wu, pitying her, sent gifts regularly.

After Kunmo's death, his grandson Cen Zuo married the princess per Wusun custom, as Emperor Wu approved to maintain the alliance. Cen Zuo became king, and the princess bore a daughter. Upon her death, Princess Jieyou, granddaughter of a Han king, was sent to marry Cen Zuo. Cen Zuo's son by his Xiongnu wife was young, so the throne passed to his uncle's son, Weng Guimi, who married Jieyou and had three sons and two daughters. During Emperor Zhao's reign, Princess Jieyou sought Han aid against Xiongnu attacks. Emperor Xuan later sent a large force, including 150,000 Han cavalry and 50,000 Wusun troops, defeating the Xiongnu and capturing many people and livestock. Wusun took the spoils. Emperor Xuan rewarded Chang Hui, who had led the Wusun forces.

Wusun Kunmi proposed making his Han-descended grandson Yuan Guimi heir, with a double marriage to sever ties with the Xiongnu. Emperor Xuan agreed but faced opposition from officials like Xiao Wangzhi, who doubted Wusun's reliability. Despite this, the emperor proceeded, sending a young princess to Wusun. However, Weng Guimi died before her arrival, and the throne passed to Nimi, Cen Zuo's son. Chang Hui advised keeping the princess in Dunhuang, and after debate, Emperor Xuan recalled her. Princess Jieyou remained in Wusun for over 40 years, and the mad king, who succeeded, was cruel and unpopular. A plot to kill him failed, leading to conflict. The Han intervened, executing those involved. Later, Wujiutu, a son of Weng Guimi, killed the mad king and declared himself Kunmi. The Han sent forces but ultimately negotiated, dividing Wusun into two Kunmi with separate households. Princess Jieyou, now elderly, was allowed to return to Chang'an, where she died two years later.
