Zhang Tang, a native of Duling, demonstrated legal acumen early in life when, as a boy, he interrogated a rat for stealing meat, impressing his father, a county magistrate. After his father's death, Zhang Tang served as a county official in Chang'an. He befriended Zhou Yanghou Tiansheng, who later introduced him to influential figures. Recognized for his talent by Ning Cheng, Zhang Tang was recommended to the Prime Minister's Office and later appointed as an official in the prime minister's palace under Marquis Wu'an. He was promoted to censor after investigating Queen Chen's witchcraft case, and later became Taizhong doctor. Alongside Zhao Yu, he formulated strict laws, eventually becoming a court captain. Zhang Tang was known for his cunning, often colluding with wealthy merchants while pretending to respect Confucian scholars to please the emperor. He manipulated legal cases to align with the emperor's wishes, admitting faults to gain forgiveness and promoting subordinates to deflect blame. His private life was serious, and he cared for old friends and poor relatives, earning a good reputation despite his harsh methods.

Zhang Tang handled major rebellion cases, such as those of the Huainan, Hengshan, and Jiangdu kings, often arguing for severe punishments. He gained the emperor's favor by proposing financial policies like minting coins and monopolizing salt and iron, which strained the populace. His influence grew to the point where national affairs depended on him, leading to widespread blame from ministers and commoners. When the Xiongnu sought peace, Zhang Tang opposed the advice of Dr. Dishan, who advocated for marriage alliances. Dishan criticized Zhang Tang's deceitful and harsh methods, but the emperor sided with Zhang Tang, sending Dishan to a border fortress where he was killed by the Xiongnu. This incident intimidated other ministers.

Zhang Tang's downfall began when his former friend Tian Jia criticized his moral faults. As imperial censor for seven years, he faced hostility from Li Wen, a censor Zhongcheng. Zhang Tang's favorite official, Lu Yeju, plotted against Li Wen, but the scheme unraveled when King Zhao reported Zhang Tang's inappropriate behavior toward Lu Yeju. Lu Yeju's brother later exposed their conspiracy. Additionally, Zhang Tang clashed with Prime Minister Qing Zhai over a tomb robbery, refusing to apologize and attempting to impeach the prime minister. Three chief ministers, including Zhu Maichen, conspired against Zhang Tang, accusing him of hoarding supplies and dividing wealth with subordinates. The emperor, believing Zhang Tang treacherous, sent envoys to question him. Zhang Tang refused to explain and, under pressure from envoy Yu, committed suicide. His family was found to have minimal wealth, and his mother insisted on a simple burial. The emperor later regretted his actions, executed the three chief ministers, and promoted Zhang Tang's son, Zhang Anshi.

Zhang Anshi, known for his meticulousness and loyalty, served under Emperor Zhao and Huo Guang. He was appointed as a general and marquis, playing a key role in deposing the King of Changyi and enthroning Emperor Xuan. Despite his high position, Anshi remained humble and cautious, often feigning illness to avoid political entanglements. He managed his household frugally, with his wife spinning crops and servants skilled in industry. His brother Zhang He cared for Emperor Xuan as a child, and after Zhang He's death, the emperor posthumously honored him. Anshi's descendants, including Zhang Yanshou and Zhang Lin, maintained the family's reputation for humility and frugality. Zhang Fang, a later descendant, was favored by Emperor Cheng but faced criticism for his arrogance and misconduct, leading to his dismissal. The Zhang family remained prominent through the Han dynasty, with many serving as high officials. The text concludes by praising the Zhang family's enduring favor and virtue, contrasting Zhang Tang's harshness with Anshi's righteousness and humility.
