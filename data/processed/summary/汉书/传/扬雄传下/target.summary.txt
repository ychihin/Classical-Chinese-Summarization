The emperor, seeking to impress barbarian envoys with the empire's abundance of animals, ordered a massive hunt in the Nanshan region. Nets and traps were set to capture bears, porcupines, tigers, leopards, foxes, and elk, which were transported to the Changyang Shooting Bear Pavilion. The animals were enclosed in nets, and the barbarians were taught to fight them bare-handed. The emperor personally watched, while farmers were unable to harvest their crops due to the disruption.

Yang Xiong accompanied the emperor and later presented "Ode to Chang Yang," using a dialogue between Zimo Keqing and the master of Hanlin to satirize the hunt. Zimo criticized the hunt for harassing farmers and questioned the emperor's priorities, arguing that such displays of power were not the monarch's urgent duty. The master of Hanlin defended the hunt by recounting past military campaigns against the Qin and various barbarian tribes, framing the hunt as a necessary demonstration of strength to maintain peace and deter future invasions. He argued that the hunt was part of a broader strategy to uphold the emperor's legacy and ensure the prosperity of the people.

Yang Xiong also wrote "Explanation of Mockery" in response to critics who questioned his reclusive lifestyle and his work on "Tai Xuan." He defended his indifference to worldly success by contrasting the chaotic Warring States period, where scholars could rise and fall rapidly, with the stable Han Dynasty, where opportunities were limited. He argued that his focus on "Tai Xuan" was a deliberate choice to avoid the dangers of political ambition, comparing himself to historical figures who thrived or perished based on timing.

In "Resolving Difficulties," Yang Xiong addressed complaints that "Tai Xuan" was too obscure. He argued that profound truths are inherently difficult to communicate, comparing his work to the "Book of Changes" and classical texts that required deep understanding. He maintained that his writings were meant for discerning readers, not the masses, and that their value would be recognized over time.

Yang Xiong's later years were marked by political turmoil during Wang Mang's usurpation. Despite being implicated in a scandal involving Liu Xin's son, he was spared due to his lack of involvement. He lived in poverty, indifferent to fame, and died at seventy-one. His works, particularly "Fayan," gained popularity after his death, though "Tai Xuan" remained less understood. Critics compared his writing of scriptures to unauthorized claims of kingship, but Huan Tan predicted his works would endure, noting that future generations would appreciate their depth.
