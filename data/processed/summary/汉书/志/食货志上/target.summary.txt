Among the eight types of government officials in "Hong Fan," one oversees food and another property. Food refers to grain from farmers, while property includes cloth, silk, golden knives, and turtle shells, used to distribute wealth. These are the foundation of life, originating from Shennong's era, who taught farming and trade. Sufficient food and circulated property enrich the nation, forming political and religious culture. Huang Di adjusted for the people, Yao informed them of seasons, Shun appointed Houji to address starvation, and Dayu controlled floods, regulated fields, and encouraged trade. The Shang and Zhou dynasties prospered by stabilizing and enriching the people before educating them. The Book of Changes states that life is heaven and earth's great virtue, and a saint's most precious possession is the throne, held through benevolence and wealth accumulation.

Property is the basis for emperors to gather people, support the masses, and govern. Wise kings defined the people, built cities, houses, markets, and schools, dividing them into scholars, farmers, workers, and merchants. Officials were appointed by talent, and each group bore responsibility, ensuring no idleness. Land was measured in steps, acres, and wells, with eight families jointly owning a well, each receiving private and public land. People helped each other, fostering harmony. Land quality varied: good land yielded 100 acres, medium 200, poor 300. Farmers exchanged fields based on fallow periods. Taxes were one-tenth of public land and industry income, while labor provided military supplies. At age 20, fields were divided; at 60, returned. Grains were mixed to prevent disasters, and mulberry trees, vegetables, and livestock were cultivated. Houses in fields were huts; in communities, li. Neighborhoods, li, clans, parties, states, and townships were organized, with officials from corporal to qing. Education began at age eight, with university at 15. Outstanding students advanced, and princes recommended scholars to the emperor. Folk songs were collected to inform the ruler.

After the Zhou Dynasty declined, tyrants ignored boundaries, corvée was rampant, and public fields were neglected. Lu Xuangong's "first tax mu" was criticized. The Warring States period valued hypocrisy and violence over benevolence. Li Kui advised Wei Wenhou on land productivity, calculating that careful cultivation yielded extra grain. He noted that high grain prices hurt scholars, workers, and merchants, while low prices hurt farmers. A household of five with 100 acres produced 150 dan of millet, but after taxes, food, and expenses, they faced deficits, leading to poverty. Li Kui proposed buying grain at moderate prices during good harvests and selling during famines to stabilize prices, implemented in Wei, making it rich and strong. Qin Xiaogong appointed Shang Yang, who destroyed well fields and emphasized farming and warfare, dominating neighboring states. However, hierarchy was lost, the rich accumulated wealth, and the poor suffered. Qin Shihuang annexed the world, imposed heavy taxes, and conscripted labor, causing rebellion.

After the Han Dynasty, the Qin's shortcomings continued. Princes revolted, people lost jobs, and famine struck, with grain costing 5,000 yuan per stone and cannibalism. Emperor Gaozu ordered people to sell children and move to Shu and Han. After peace, the emperor restricted frugality, reduced land rents to one-fifteenth, and taxed based on official salaries. Emperor Wen practiced frugality and encouraged agriculture. Jia Yi advised that production must exceed consumption, warning of poverty and unrest. He urged returning people to agriculture to build savings. Emperor Wen was moved and began farming himself. Chao Cuo further advised that wise kings ensure people have savings, citing Yao, Yu, and Tang's success. He noted that poverty leads to evil, and farmers work hard but suffer from taxes and merchants' exploitation. He proposed valuing grain over gold and jade, and using grain for rewards and punishments. Emperor Wen allowed people to send grain to the border for titles, reducing taxes. Over 70 years, the country prospered, with full granaries and surplus property. However, wealth led to pride and land annexation. Dong Zhongshu advised planting more wheat and reforming taxes and labor, but after his death, prices rose and cannibalism returned.

Emperor Wu regretted his conquests and appointed Cheng Fu to promote agriculture. Zhao Guo introduced the Daitian method, with three furrows per acre, changing positions yearly. This method, using convenient tools, increased yields. Zhao Guo taught it widely, and it proved efficient. By Emperor Zhao's time, exiles returned, fields expanded, and savings grew. Emperor Xuan appointed virtuous officials, and grain prices fell to five yuan per stone. Geng Shouchang proposed purchasing grain from nearby counties to save on transport costs, building Changping warehouses to stabilize prices. Emperor Yuan faced floods and famine, with cannibalism in Langxie. Confucian officials advised dismissing salt, iron, and Changping offices, which the emperor did, but later restored salt and iron due to insufficient funds. Emperor Cheng's reign was peaceful but extravagant, with floods causing cannibalism. Emperor Ai considered land and slave restrictions, but they were not implemented. Wang Mang usurped the throne, despised Han systems, and provoked the Xiongnu, leading to war. He renamed land and slaves, prohibited sales, and imposed harsh punishments, causing chaos. Droughts and famines led to cannibalism, with grain prices soaring. Wang Mang opened granaries but officials stole supplies, and many died. He lamented his rule until his death.
