The text presents a comprehensive system of astronomical and calendrical calculations, beginning with a series of defined numerical constants and methods. The "method number" is derived from multiplying the original Huang Zhong Chujiu by itself. The "leap method" of nineteen is used as the number of years in a chapter. The "Tongfa" (1,539) is obtained by multiplying the leap number by the daily number, and the "Yuanfa" (4,617) is three times the Tongfa. Other key numbers include the "Zhang month" (235), "Moon Dharma" (2,392), and "Weekly days" (562,120). The text details the calculation of various astronomical cycles, including the synodic meeting, meeting months, and the cycles of the year star, Taibai Star, Zhenxing, Yinghuo, and the stars (Mercury). For each planet, the text provides specific numbers for its appearance, motion (direct, retrograde, and stationary), and the duration of its visibility and concealment, along with the degrees it travels.

The text then outlines methods for deducing the Sun-Moon Yuan Tong, calculating the winter solstice, and determining the positions of the five stars. It describes how to calculate the number of years since the original Shangyuan and the specific cycles of Jiazi, Jiachen, and Jiashen. Procedures are given for calculating the first day of the lunar month, the location of the leap remainder, and the twenty-four solar terms. The text also details the calculation of the stars' positions at morning and midnight, the lunar eclipse, and the complex numbers for the manifestation of the five stars. It explains how to determine the middle number of a star's appearance, the star and the moon, and the degree of the next day after entering the center.

The text provides a detailed breakdown of the five steps of calculation for the planets, using the number of days since their first appearance and their daily motion. It also describes the calculation of the age (Sui Xing) and its profits and losses, noting that the Sui star's misplacement can cause disasters. The text then lists the twelve celestial divisions (Xingji) from Dou Twelve Degrees to Dou Shidu, associating each with a solar term and the corresponding months in the Xia, Shang, and Zhou calendars. It provides the degrees for various constellations and explains the cycles of nine ends and three ends, which relate to the unification of the calendar.

The text includes a historical chronology, starting from the legendary emperors (Taiwu, Yandi, Huangdi, Shaowu, Zhuan Xu, Xue, Tang, Yu) and their associated virtues (wood, fire, earth, metal, water). It traces the lineage and reigns of the Xia, Shang, and Zhou dynasties, providing specific years and astronomical events. For example, it notes that the Xia dynasty had 432 years, the Shang dynasty 629 years, and the Zhou dynasty 867 years. The text also details the reigns of the Qin dynasty and the early Han emperors, from Gaozu to Emperor Guangwu, providing the number of years for each reign and the total years since the Shangyuan. It concludes with the reign of Emperor Guangwu, noting that he reigned for 33 years.
