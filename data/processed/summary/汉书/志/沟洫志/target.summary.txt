The "Book of Xia" records Yu's thirteen-year flood control efforts, during which he passed his home without entering. He traveled by various means—car, boat, sled, or sedan—to delineate Kyushu's boundaries, dredge rivers, and assess land for taxation. Despite his work, the Yellow River continued to devastate the Central Plains. Yu prioritized managing it, guiding its waters from Jishi Mountain through Longmen and other locations, but its turbulent flow proved difficult to tame. He eventually split the river into two to reduce its force. After Kyushu's rivers and lakes were stabilized, the benefits extended to the Xia, Shang, and Zhou dynasties. The Yellow River was later diverted southeast, intersecting with several rivers and countries.

In various regions, canals were built for irrigation and transport. In Chu, ditches connected Moshui and Yunmeng; in Wu, canals linked three rivers and five lakes; in Qi, the Zi and Jishui rivers were joined. In Shu, Li Bing dug two rivers in Chengdu to prevent flooding. These canals facilitated boat travel and irrigation, bringing prosperity. During Wei Wenhou's reign, Ximen Bao governed Ye County with a good reputation, but his successor Shi Qi criticized him for not using the Zhangshui River for irrigation. Shi Qi then diverted the Zhangshui to irrigate Ye County, making the area fertile. Later, the Qin state employed Zheng Guo, a Han spy, to build a canal from the Jingshui River. Though initially suspected, Zheng Guo convinced Qin of the canal's long-term benefits. The canal irrigated over 40,000 hectares of salty land, enriching Qin and enabling it to conquer other states; it was named the Zheng Guo Canal.

During the Han dynasty, the Yellow River burst multiple times. In Emperor Xiaowen's reign, it broke at Suizao, and later during Yuanguang, it breached at Huzi, flowing into Juyeze and the Huai River. Attempts to block it failed, and Tian Ju advised against further efforts, citing divine will. Zheng Dangshi proposed a canal from Chang'an to divert the Wei River, reducing transport time and irrigating farmland. The emperor approved, and the canal was completed in three years, improving water transport and irrigation. Subsequent proposals included diverting Fen and Yellow River waters in Hedong, but the canals became useless after the river changed course. Other projects, like the Baoxie Road canal, faced challenges due to rocky waters. Yan Xiong's plan to irrigate salty land with Luoshui water led to the Longshou Canal, but it yielded little benefit after a decade.

After the Huzi breach, the emperor personally oversaw its blockage, composing songs about the struggle. The breach was finally sealed, and two canals were built to restore the river's old course. This success spurred widespread canal construction across Shuofang, Xihe, Hexi, and other regions, each irrigating over 10,000 hectares. Smaller canals were countless. By the sixth year of Yuanding, Ni Kuan proposed six auxiliary canals for the Zheng Guo Canal area. The emperor emphasized agriculture and reduced land taxes. Sixteen years later, Bai Gong opened the Baiqu Canal, diverting Jingshui water to irrigate 4,500 hectares. The people praised both canals for their fertility. During the Han-Xiongnu wars, Yannian proposed a massive canal to divert the Yellow River, but the emperor hesitated, citing Dayu's legacy. After the Xuanfang breach, the river broke again at Guantao, forming the Tunshi River, which was left unblocked.

During Emperor Xuan's Dijie period, Guo Chang straightened a meandering section of the Yellow River, benefiting the people. In Yongguang, the river burst at Lingmingdukou, causing the Tunshi River to disappear. Under Emperor Cheng, Feng Kui warned of flood risks and proposed dredging the Tunshi River. Despite initial inaction, a major breach in Guantao flooded 32 counties. Wang Yanshi successfully blocked it, earning rewards. Later breaches required further efforts, with Yang Yan and others involved. Debates over river management continued, with some advocating for relocating people and opening canals, while others favored minimal intervention. During Wang Mang's reign, various experts proposed strategies, but none were implemented. The text concludes by emphasizing the Yellow River's significance and the need for careful study of water management.
