The uses of property, money, cloth, and silk were not recorded before the Xia and Yin dynasties. Jiang Taigong established currency circulation for the Zhou Dynasty: gold was one inch square and weighed one catty; coins were round with square holes, using baht as weight; cloth was two feet two inches wide and four feet long per piece. Currency was considered more precious than gold, sharper than a knife, smoother than spring water, more dispersed than cloth, and more able to gather than silk. After Jiang Taigong retreated to Qi, he implemented this system. Guan Zhong assisted Duke Huan of Qi by establishing standards for measuring weight, noting that grain prices fluctuated with harvests and that goods varied in value based on demand. He warned that without governance, hoarding merchants would exploit the people, raising prices a hundredfold. A country with ten thousand cars needed merchants worth ten thousand gold to buy them, as profits were hidden. By consuming what was produced, expenses could be met, but hunger arose when grain was hoarded. The monarch should levy low prices when grain was abundant and distribute at high prices when scarce, adjusting supply and demand to stabilize prices. A town of ten thousand households should have a collection of ten thousand bells and millions of strings of money. Farming required abundant equipment and seeds in spring and summer, preventing big merchants from robbing the people. Duke Huan used Qi to unite the princes and become an overlord.

More than a hundred years later, King Jing of Zhou worried that money was too light and planned to cast large coins. Duke Shan Mu advised against it, arguing that when disasters came, property and currency should be weighed to save the people. If money was light, heavy money should be created for use, with both types circulating together. Abolishing light money for heavy money would cause the people to lose wealth, leading to shortages and flight. King Jing ignored this and made large coins with "actual goods" engraved, which benefited the people. When Qin annexed the world, currency was divided into two classes: gold measured in yi, and copper coins shaped like Zhou money with "ban liang" inscribed, weighing half a liang. Pearls, jade, turtles, shells, silver, and tin were decorations, not currency, and changed value arbitrarily. After the Han rose, they found Qin coins heavy and ordered pod coins instead, with gold returning to the weekly system of kilograms. Those who pursued profits hoarded goods, causing prices to skyrocket—rice reached ten thousand gold per stone, and a horse was worth one hundred gold. Emperor Gaozu ordered merchants not to wear silk or ride carriages and increased taxes to frustrate them. During Emperor Xiaohui and Queen Gao's reign, laws on merchants were relaxed, but their descendants could not become officials.

In the fifth year of Emperor Xiaowen's reign, four baht coins were minted with "banliang" on the front, and the law on stealing money was abolished, allowing people to mint coins freely. Jia Yi remonstrated, arguing that coining money with copper and tin led to doping for profit, and even daily tattooing could not stop it. He noted that coins varied by county—some light, some heavy—causing confusion. People abandoned farming to mine copper, increasing illegal money without increasing grain. He proposed collecting copper to prevent scattering, which would bring seven blessings: reducing tattooing crimes, eliminating fake money, returning miners to farming, balancing money and goods, distinguishing noble from humble, enriching the government while impoverishing merchants, and competing with the Xiongnu. The emperor did not listen. Wu State minted coins in Shanhuo, becoming richer than the emperor, and later rebelled. Deng Tong, a great official, minted coins and became wealthier than kings. Their money spread worldwide.

During Emperor Wu's reign, wars with the Xiongnu and campaigns in the southwest and Guangdong drained resources. The government used money to buy titles and exempt people from punishment, leading to official chaos. Gongsun Hong, as prime minister, used moral principles to restrain ministers, while Zhang Tang used harsh laws. The Yellow River flooded, costing billions. The emperor discussed changing coins to supplement expenses, using white deer skin for leather coins worth 400,000 yuan and silver-tin platinum coins. The government destroyed half-liang coins and recast three baht coins, punishing secret minting with death. Shu Guo Xianyang and Kong Wei were appointed as great agricultural ministers to manage salt and iron, while Sang Hongyang, a merchant's son, became favored. The government set up salt and iron officials, taxed merchants, and confiscated hidden property. The rich went bankrupt, and the government grew richer through salt and iron. The emperor built Kunming Pool and Bailiang Fortress, expanding palaces. The government used confiscated land and slaves for farming, and the grain transported to the capital reached four million shi.

After Wang Mang became regent, he changed the Han system, creating various coins like big coins, deed-knife coins, and wrong-knife coins. He later abolished these and introduced treasure goods, including gold, silver, turtle, shell, money, and cloth coins. He established six types of coins, each with different values, and implemented credit and five equalizations to control prices. He set up officials in Chang'an and five capitals to manage trade, taxing craftsmen and merchants. He also regulated wine production, with the government making wine and taking seven-tenths of the profit. Despite these measures, the people suffered, and many broke the law. Wang Mang executed those who made private coins and moved critics to remote areas. The corvee was heavy, and droughts and locusts caused trouble. Officials collected taxes privately, and the rich could not protect themselves, leading to widespread banditry. The Han army killed Wang Mang four years after the conquest of Zhutu Xiyong. Two years later, Shizu restored the five-baht coin and eliminated harsh laws. The text praises ancient methods of regulating commodities and prices, noting that during Emperor Xiaowu's time, expenses were met without increasing taxes, but under Wang Mang, the system failed, and the people suffered.
