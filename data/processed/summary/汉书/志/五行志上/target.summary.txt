The text begins by referencing the "Yi," stating that sages observe heavenly signs and imitate patterns from the Yellow River and Luoshui River. Liu Xin believed Fu Xi received the "River Map" and created the Bagua, while Dayu received the "Luo Shu" and formed the "Hong Fan." After the Zhou dynasty defeated the Yin, King Wu consulted Jizi, who explained that God gave Dayu nine chapters of "Hongfan," which established the order of heaven, earth, and people. The text lists nine categories, including the Five Elements, Five Affairs, Eight Policies, and others, totaling sixty-five words that constitute the "Luo Shu." Liu Xin viewed the "He Tu" and "Luo Shu" as complementary, with the Bagua and Jiu Zhang as their expressions. The text traces the study of these principles through King Wen, Confucius, and later scholars like Dong Zhongshu, Liu Xiang, and Liu Xin, culminating in the "Five Elements Chronicle," which records twelve dynasties up to Wang Mang's usurpation.

The first element, Wood, is associated with the east and represents the ability to be bent or straight. The text explains that improper hunting, feasting, and military campaigns disrupt wood's nature, leading to strange phenomena like wood ice. Liu Xin attributed wood ice to blocked yang and yin qi, while Liu Xiang linked it to excessive yin threatening noble ministers. Examples from the "Spring and Autumn" include rain and wood ice in the sixteenth year of Duke Cheng of Lu, interpreted as a sign of noblemen's troubles or military conflict.

Fire, the second element, represents the south and brilliance. Proper governance, including selecting wise officials and distinguishing virtue, allows fire to maintain its nature. Neglecting morality and allowing slander leads to fires that damage ancestral temples and palaces. The text cites numerous fires from the "Spring and Autumn," such as the palace granary fire in the fourteenth year of Duke Huan, interpreted by Dong Zhongshu as a warning against laziness and by Liu Xiang as a sign of the wife's rebellious heart. Other fires include those in Qi, the West Palace, Luoyang's Xuanxie, and the newly completed palace in Lu, each linked to political misdeeds like usurpation, improper marriages, or neglect of rituals.

Earth, the third element, is central and foundational for growth. Proper governance involves modest palaces, regulated marriages, and respect for relatives. Extravagance and promiscuity cause earth to lose its nature, leading to poor harvests. The text cites the death of wheat seedlings in the twenty-eighth year of Duke Yan of Lu, attributed by Dong Zhongshu to the wife's obscenity and by Liu Xiang to the construction of pavilions and immoral behavior.

Metal, the fourth element, represents the west and is associated with military campaigns. Proper use involves timely warfare and respect for life. Arrogance and disregard for the people cause metal to lose its properties, leading to strange events like speaking stones. The text references stones speaking in Jin during the eighth year of Duke Zhao, interpreted by Shi Kuang as a sign of exhausted people's power due to excessive palace construction. A similar event in the Han dynasty involved a large stone crying in Tianshui, linked to rebellions and the construction of Changling mausoleum.

Water, the fifth element, represents the north and is associated with ancestral rituals and proper governance. Neglecting sacrifices and issuing contrary orders cause water to lose its nature, leading to floods and continuous rains. The text lists numerous floods from the "Spring and Autumn" and Han dynasty, such as those in the first year of Duke Huan of Lu, attributed to the killing of Duke Yin and subsequent conflicts. Other floods are linked to scandals, improper marriages, and military campaigns. In the Han dynasty, floods occurred during the reigns of Gaohou, Emperor Wen, Emperor Xuan, and Emperor Cheng, often tied to political turmoil, such as the Lu family's power, Xiongnu invasions, or the abolition of ancestral temples.
