The text begins by stating that the unification of laws, measures, and weights was established to create consistency between near and far regions, fostering trust among the people. It traces the origins of numerical systems from Fuxi's Eight Diagrams, which were later perfected during the reigns of Huangdi, Yao, and Shun. During the Three Dynasties, the system of weights and measures became clearer through the study of ancient affairs. However, during the decline of the Zhou Dynasty, control was lost, prompting Confucius to emphasize the importance of careful governance over weights, measures, and the appointment of officials to restore order in the world.

After the Han Dynasty was established, Zhang Cang, the Marquis of Beiping, first determined the laws and calendar, with further corrections made by the Leguan of Emperor Xiaowu. During the Yuanshi period, Wang Mang sought fame by recruiting over a hundred experts on bell rules, led by Xi and Liu Xin, to compile a detailed report. They deleted inaccurate words, selected correct meanings, and organized the information into chapters covering perfect numbers, harmonious music, length standards, measuring instruments, and weighing tools. These were combined and verified against ancient and modern practices, confirming their consistency.

The text explains that numbers—one, ten, one hundred, one thousand, and ten thousand—are used to calculate and count things, aligning with human life. The law originated from Huang Zhong, produced by multiplying three from the beginning twelve times, resulting in 170,147, representing the five elements. The calculation method uses bamboo pieces, one minute in diameter and six inches long, with 271 pieces forming a hexagon. The diameter corresponds to one-tenth of the yellow bell in the twelve rhythms, and the length matches the forest bells. The number is based on the Dayan number fifty from the "Book of Changes," with forty-nine forming the Yang Liu Yao, creating the six Yao cycles.

The text delves into the five tones—Gong, Shang, Jiao, Zheng, and Yu—and their association with musical instruments made from earth, gourds, leather, bamboo, silk, stone, metal, and wood. Each tone is linked to elements, constants, and human attributes: Jiao corresponds to wood and benevolence, Shang to gold and righteousness, Zheng to fire and etiquette, Yu to water and wisdom, and Gong to earth and faith. The foundation of the five tones comes from the rhythm of Huang Zhong, with nine inches used as the palace tune, adjusted to determine the other tones.

The twelve laws are divided into six Yang (laws) and six Yin (Lu), each associated with specific months and positions. The text describes the creation of these laws by the Yellow Emperor, who sent Ling Lun to cut bamboo from Kunlun Mountain, producing twelve tubes that matched the calls of the phoenix. The laws are named and explained in terms of their seasonal and elemental significance, such as Huang Zhong representing the middle color and the beginning of all things in November.

The three unifications—heaven, earth, and man—are discussed, with Huang Zhong representing heaven, Lin Zhong representing earth, and Tai Ku representing man. The text explains how the numbers of heaven and earth form the basis for the twelve laws, with calculations involving the multiplication of three to produce various numbers. The process of yin and yang complementing each other is detailed, starting from Huang Zhong and forming pairs through the twelve laws.

The text then covers the instruments for measuring length, volume, and weight. Length is measured in minutes, inches, feet, and yards, originating from the length of Huang Zhong. Volume is measured using bamboo tubes filled with millet, with units including the 龠, he, liter, dou, and dendrobium. Weight is measured in baht, liang, jin, jun, and stone, with the weight of a Huang Zhong tube serving as the basis. The five principles—compass, moment, accuracy, rope, and weight—are described as tools for measuring objects, each associated with directions, seasons, and elements.

The text discusses the importance of calendar reckoning, tracing its origins to Zhuanxu and Yao, who appointed officials to observe celestial movements. The Han Dynasty initially used the Qin calendar but later adopted the "Taichu Calendar" under Emperor Wu, created by scholars like Deng Ping and Luo Xiahong. The calendar was verified through observations and comparisons, with the "Taichu Calendar" proving most accurate. The text also recounts the controversy with King Zhang Shou, who criticized the calendar but was ultimately discredited.

Finally, the text links the calendar to the "Spring and Autumn Annals," emphasizing the importance of recording celestial events and maintaining proper rituals. It explains the three unifications and their connection to the five elements, with calculations involving the numbers of heaven and earth to determine the cycles of the sun, moon, and five stars. The text concludes by stating that the principles of the "Book of Changes" and "Spring and Autumn" are the laws of heaven and man, used to understand the world and govern accordingly.
