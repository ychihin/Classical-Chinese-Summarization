Humans are considered the most spiritual of living beings, possessing the five constant natures of benevolence, righteousness, propriety, wisdom, and faith, and imitating the shapes of heaven and earth. However, lacking natural defenses like fur or speed, humans must rely on wisdom and cooperation to survive. Without benevolence, they cannot form groups, and without groups, they cannot overcome external threats. Ancient saints established morality, rituals, and laws to maintain order, with kingship rooted in benevolence and humility. Rituals and punishments were created to imitate divine order, with the five rites and five punishments derived from heavenly principles. Military organization evolved from ancient times, with systems of land division and military taxes, such as the well-field system, supporting armies of varying sizes, from the House of Hundred Chariots to the Lord of Ten Thousand Chariots. Military training occurred during seasonal hunts, and feudal states were organized under officers, with regular reviews of troops.

After the Zhou Dynasty declined, Duke Huan of Qi and Duke Wen of Jin rose as hegemons, but their methods deviated from the former kings' legal system. Later, wars multiplied, and Confucius lamented the lack of proper training and righteousness. During the Warring States period, strategists like Sun Wu, Sun Bin, Wu Qi, and Shang Yang emerged, focusing on cunning and power. Sun Qing criticized these approaches, arguing that true invincibility comes from benevolence and righteousness, not mere tactics. He contrasted the armies of Qi, Wei, and Qin, noting that while Qin's elite soldiers dominated, they lacked moral integrity. The ultimate ideal was the benevolent army of King Tang and King Wu, which did not need to fight to prevail. The Han Dynasty, under Gaozu, combined civil and military governance, but later emperors struggled to maintain proper military and legal systems.

The text discusses the evolution of criminal law from ancient times. The Zhou Dynasty established three categories of laws—light, medium, and heavy—with specific punishments for different crimes. After the Zhou decline, King Mu expanded the penal code to 3,000 items. During the Spring and Autumn period, Zichan of Zheng cast criminal laws on a tripod, drawing criticism from Jin's Shuxiang, who argued that public knowledge of laws would lead to disputes and decline. Confucius emphasized moral guidance over legal coercion. The Warring States period saw harsh laws under Shen Buhai and Shang Yang, culminating in Qin Shihuang's oppressive regime, which led to widespread rebellion. The Han Dynasty initially simplified laws under Gaozu, but later emperors like Xiaowen and Xiaojing reformed punishments, abolishing corporal punishment and reducing flogging. Despite these efforts, legal complexity grew, and by Emperor Wu's reign, laws became increasingly strict, with over 13,000 capital offenses. Emperor Xuan and Emperor Yuan attempted to simplify laws, but reforms were incomplete.

The text further examines the Han Dynasty's legal reforms and their shortcomings. Emperor Xiaowen abolished the law of arresting innocent relatives, but later reinstated it for certain crimes. The Zhou system of five hearings, eight discussions, and three pardons aimed for fairness, but Han officials often failed to implement these principles. Emperors issued edicts to protect the elderly, young, and disabled from harsh punishments. Despite these efforts, the number of lawsuits and executions remained high, with over 10,000 deaths annually. The root causes included lack of moral education, unclear laws, poverty, and corruption. Confucius noted that ancient judges sought to save lives, while modern ones sought to kill. The text concludes that the Han Dynasty's legal system, though improved, still fell short of ancient ideals. Xunzi argued that punishments should match crimes, and that heavy penalties are necessary in peaceful times to deter evil. The author suggests restoring ancient corporal punishments and simplifying laws to achieve harmony, as seen in the prosperous reigns of Cheng and Kang.
