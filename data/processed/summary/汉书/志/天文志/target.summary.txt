The text describes a comprehensive system of Chinese astronomy, detailing 118 star officials governing 783 stars, which serve as celestial signs for states, officials, and events. Celestial phenomena—including the movements of the five planets (Jupiter, Mars, Saturn, Venus, Mercury), comets, meteors, solar and lunar eclipses, halos, and atmospheric changes—are manifestations of yin and yang, reflecting earthly governance. A wise ruler responds to these signs by correcting policies to avert disasters.

The celestial sphere is divided into palaces. The Purple Palace (central) contains the Tianji star (residence of Taiyi, the Heavenly Emperor), surrounded by stars representing the emperor’s sons, concubines, and vassals. The Big Dipper governs the seasons and calendar, with its handle indicating directions. Other asterisms include the Wenchang Palace (officials), the Three Energy Stars (harmony of ruler and ministers), and stars associated with prisons, warfare, and agriculture. The Eastern Palace (Canglong) includes the Heart (emperor’s hall) and Room (heavenly mansion), with stars predicting disease, war, and harvests. The Southern Palace (Red Bird) features the Hengxing (Taiwei enclosure, the emperor’s court) and Quanxing (Xuanyuan, the heroine), with stars for officials, generals, and law enforcement. The Western Palace (White Tiger) includes the Five Chariots and stars for barbarians, hunting, and border affairs. The Northern Palace (Xuanwu) contains the Yulin Heavenly Army and stars for floods, war, and funerals.

Each planet governs specific seasons, elements, and human virtues. Jupiter (wood, spring, benevolence) indicates prosperity or disaster based on its motion. Mars (fire, summer, ritual) signals chaos, war, and famine. Venus (metal, autumn, righteousness) is the star of war, with detailed rules for its appearance, color, and movement predicting military outcomes. Mercury (water, winter, wisdom) governs floods and military ambushes. Saturn (earth, late summer, faith) is the most auspicious, with its position indicating land gains or losses.

Conjunctions and interactions among the five planets predict major events: Jupiter-Saturn conjunctions indicate civil strife; Mars-Venus conjunctions bring mourning; and five-star gatherings signal a new ruler. Lunar eclipses and occultations of stars by the moon foretell the destruction of states or the death of kings. The text also describes comets, meteors, and other celestial objects (e.g., Tengu, Geze, Chiyou banners) as omens of war, rebellion, or natural disasters.

The sun’s path (ecliptic) and the moon’s nine orbits determine seasons and weather. Solar and lunar eclipses are linked to the ruler’s virtue and punishment. Clouds and vapors (qi) are used for divination, with colors and shapes indicating military outcomes, harvests, and regional events. The text includes historical examples from the Han dynasty, such as the five-star gathering at Dongjing (marking Emperor Gaozu’s rise), comets during the reign of Qin Shihuang, and planetary movements predicting rebellions (e.g., the Seven States Rebellion, the Huo Guang conspiracy). These examples illustrate how celestial phenomena were interpreted as direct responses to political events, from the decline of the Zhou dynasty to the usurpation of Wang Mang.
