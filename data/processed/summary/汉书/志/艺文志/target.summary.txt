Since the death of Confucius, the subtle words of the classics have been discontinued, and after the passing of his Seventy Disciples, divergent interpretations emerged. The "Spring and Autumn Annals" was divided into five schools: Zuo Shi, Gong Yang, Gu Liang, Zou Shi, and Jia Shi. During the Warring States period, debates over authenticity proliferated, and various schools of thought became confused. Qin Shihuang, fearing this chaos, burned books to deceive the people. After the Han Dynasty was established, the shortcomings of the Qin were eliminated, and a large-scale collection of books was initiated, opening channels for donations. By the time of Emperor Xiaowu, texts were incomplete, bamboo slips had fallen apart, and rituals were destroyed. The emperor lamented this and established a repository for books, appointing officials to copy them, enriching the secret palace with the teachings of scholars. Under Emperor Cheng, the loss of books was severe, so emissaries were sent to collect scattered texts. Liu Xiang was ordered to collate classics and poems, while others studied military books, astronomy, and medicine. After each proofreading, Liu Xiang cataloged the books, summarized their main ideas, and presented them to the emperor. Upon Liu Xiang's death, Emperor Ai sent his son Liu Xin to complete the work, who then compiled the "Seven Strategies," including summaries of the Six Arts, various schools, poetry, military strategy, numerology, and prescriptions.

The "Book of Changes" has twelve chapters, with interpretations by Shi, Meng, and Liangqiu, among others. Thirteen schools of interpretation exist, totaling 294 chapters. The "Yi" states that Fuxi created the trigrams by observing heaven, earth, and all things. During the Yin and Zhou dynasties, King Wen, as a prince, followed heaven's will and wrote the judgments. Confucius added ten chapters, such as "Tuan" and "Xiang." The principles of "Yi" are profound, spanning three sages and three ancient eras. When Qin Shihuang burned books, the "Yi" survived due to its divinatory nature. After the Han, Tian He taught it, and by Emperor Xuan and Yuan, schools like Shi, Meng, and Liangqiu were officially taught, while Fei's version matched the ancient text.

The "Shangshu" has forty-six volumes of ancient classics and fifty-seven articles. It was compiled by Confucius, starting from Yao and ending with Qin, totaling a hundred chapters. During the Qin burning, Fu Sheng hid it in a wall, and only twenty-nine chapters survived. By Emperor Xuan, Ouyang and Xiahou schools were established. The "Guwen Shangshu" was found in the wall of Confucius's residence, with sixteen more chapters than the original. Kong Anguo dedicated it to the emperor, but it was not included in the official curriculum due to witchcraft incidents. Liu Xiang used the ancient text to correct the Ouyang and Xiahou versions, finding missing slips and over 700 variant characters.

The "Book of Songs" has twenty-eight volumes from Lu, Qi, and Han schools, with Mao's version adding thirty volumes. Confucius selected 305 poems from the Zhou dynasty, which survived the Qin burning due to their oral transmission. After the Han, Duke Shen of Lu, Yuan Gu of Qi, and Han Sheng of Yan provided explanations, but only Lu's version was closest to the original meaning. Mao Gong's teachings, claimed to be from Zixia, were favored by King Xian of Hejian but not officially taught.

The "Ancient Book of Rites" has fifty-six volumes, with seventeen chapters in the classic. Schools include Hou and Taiwu. Confucius's disciples recorded 131 chapters of records. The Zhou dynasty's rituals were detailed, but after its decline, princes abandoned them. In the Han, Gaotangsheng taught seventeen chapters of "Shili," and Houcang became prominent. The "Ancient Book of Rites" from the Kong family had thirty-nine more articles than the seventeen, covering systems of emperors and officials.

Music, with six schools and 165 articles, was used by ancient emperors to uphold morality. After the Zhou decline, both ritual and music were destroyed. In the Han, the Zhi family preserved some rhythms but not their meaning. King Xian of Hejian collected music discussions and wrote "Yue Ji," presenting the Eight-Yen Dance. Wang Yu taught it and presented twenty-four volumes, but Liu Xiang's version differed, and this knowledge declined.

The "Spring and Autumn Annals" has twelve chapters, with two families: Gongyang and Haosui. Zuo Qiuming, the Taishi of Lu, wrote thirty volumes of commentary. Other commentaries include Gongyang, Guliang, Zou, and Jia, though the latter two had no books. The "Spring and Autumn" was used to record kings' actions, with left historians recording words and right historians recording deeds. After the Zhou decline, Confucius used Lu's records to compile the annals, and Zuo Qiuming explained the facts to preserve the true meaning.

The "Analects of Confucius" has twenty-one ancient articles from Confucius's wall, twenty-two from Qi, and twenty from Lu. It records Confucius's answers to disciples and their discussions. After his death, disciples compiled it. In the Han, Qi and Lu versions were taught, with notable scholars like Wang Ji and Zhang Yu.

The "Classic of Filial Piety" has one article, with twenty-two chapters in the ancient version and eighteen in the modern. It was taught by families like Changsun and Jiang. Confucius explained filial piety to Zengzi, and it was considered a universal duty.

Philology includes fifteen chapters of "Shi Zhou" and works like "Cangjie" by Li Si. The six scripts—ancient, odd, seal, clerical, Miao, and insect—were used to understand characters. After the Han, Xiao He required officials to recite 9,000 words. Yang Xiong and Du Lin expanded these works, totaling 102 chapters.

The Six Arts have 103 schools and 3,123 articles. They embody benevolence, righteousness, propriety, wisdom, and faith, with "Yi" as their origin. Ancient scholars mastered one skill in three years, but later generations fragmented the classics, with five-character explanations reaching 20,000 to 30,000 words, leading to confusion.

Confucian schools, with 53 works and 836 chapters, originated from officials of enlightenment. They study the Six Classics and follow Yao, Shun, and Confucius. However, later scholars lost the subtlety, fragmenting the Five Classics.

Taoist schools, with 37 works and 993 articles, originated from historians. They record successes and failures, emphasizing purity and humility. Abusers abandon etiquette and benevolence.

Yin-Yang schools, with 21 works and 369 articles, originated from astronomers. They respect heaven and calculate movements, but strict practitioners become constrained by taboos.

Legalist schools, with 10 works and 217 articles, originated from judges. They reward merit and punish guilt, but mean people abandon benevolence.

Famous schools, with 7 works and 36 articles, originated from ritual officials. They correct names, but those who reveal secrets cause chaos.

Mohist schools, with 6 works and 86 articles, originated from temple officials. They advocate frugality and fraternity, but fools oppose etiquette.

Strategist schools, with 12 works and 107 articles, originated from officials receiving guests. They weigh circumstances, but evil people focus on deception.

Miscellaneous schools, with 20 works and 403 articles, originated from officials discussing affairs. They integrate Confucianism, Mohism, names, and laws, but indulgent people become boundless.

Agricultural schools, with 9 works and 114 articles, originated from agriculture officials. They emphasize food and clothing, but despicable people disrupt order.

Novelist schools, with 15 works and 1,380 articles, originated from petty officials collecting folklore. They contain small sayings, but gentlemen do not pursue them.

In total, there are 189 works by various scholars, with 4,324 articles. These nine schools arose during the decline of kings, each advocating their doctrines. Though different, they complement each other like water and fire. Studying the Six Arts and these schools can reveal strategies.

Poetry and Fu include 106 works with 1,318 articles. Ancient officials used poetry to express aspirations. After the Spring and Autumn period, scholars like Qu Yuan and Sun Qing wrote poems to satirize. Later, writers like Sima Xiangru emphasized ornate words, drowning satire.

Military works include 53 books with 790 articles and 43 volumes of pictures. They originated from Sima officials. After the Han, Zhang Liang compiled 182 art of war, selecting 35. Later, four types were made.

Astronomy includes 21 works with 445 volumes. It arranges constellations and calculates stars for good and bad omens.

Calendar includes 18 works with 606 volumes. It determines seasons and solar terms, aiding sage kings in knowing destiny.

Five Elements include 31 works with 652 volumes. They correspond to benevolence, righteousness, propriety, wisdom, and faith, but small scholars use them for superstition.

Divination includes 15 works with 401 volumes. Turtles and yarrow were used by saints to predict good and bad, but in decline, divination became frequent.

Miscellaneous divination includes 18 works with 313 volumes. It records appearances and observes signs, based on dreams and demons.

Physiognomy includes 6 works with 122 volumes. It identifies land, houses, and people, but form and qi are subtle.

Numerology includes 190 works with 2,528 volumes. It covers astronomy, calendar, and divination, but books are incomplete.

Medicine includes 216 volumes. It explores blood and meridians to cure diseases, but clumsy people lose proportion.

Prescriptions include 11 types with 274 volumes. They use herbs to treat conditions, but improper medication causes harm.

Sexual arts include 8 works with 186 volumes. They regulate desire for health, but confused people lose restraint.

Immortal arts include 10 works with 205 volumes. They pursue immortality, but absurd articles increase.

In total, there are 38 types of six strategies, 596 kinds, and 13,269 volumes. Compared to "Dagger Strategy," there are three more types and a divination chapter, but ten military strategist types are reduced.
