The text begins by describing the Yellow Emperor's invention of boats and carts to traverse impassable roads, allowing him to travel the world, plan a vast territory, divide fields into states, and establish a country with a hundred miles of land and ten thousand regions. It then references the *Book of Changes* and *Shang Shu*, noting that during Yao's time, a flood submerged mountains and hills, isolating the world into twelve states. Dayu was sent to control the floods, and afterward, the regional system was changed to Kyushu, divided into five services, with tribute determined by land quality.

The text details the geography, soil, fields, tribute, and waterways of each of the nine provinces: Jizhou, Yanzhou, Qingzhou, Xuzhou, Yangzhou, Jingzhou, Yuzhou, Liangzhou, and Yongzhou. For each province, it describes the soil type, land grade, tribute items, and river routes for transportation. For example, Jizhou has white soil and first-grade tribute, while Yangzhou has moist mud soil and ninth-grade fields. The text also mentions the opening of mountains and dredging of rivers, such as the Yellow River being dredged from Jishi Mountain to Longmen Mountain.

After the pacification of Kyushu, the land became habitable, and the nine rivers were dredged. Taxes were levied based on land quality, and the area around the royal city was divided into five zones: Dianfu, Houfu, Suifu, Yaofu, and Huangfu, each with specific tribute requirements. The text then describes the succession of dynasties: Yao gave Dayu a black jade, Dayu became emperor as the Xiahou clan, the Shang inherited the Xia system, and the Zhou modified it, merging Xuzhou and Liangzhou into Yongzhou and Qingzhou and dividing Jizhou into Youzhou and Bingzhou.

The *Book of Rites of Zhou* describes the official Fang, who governed the land of the world and all countries in Kyushu. The text then lists the characteristics of each province, including mountains, lakes, rivers, irrigation canals, products, population ratios, and suitable livestock and grains. For instance, Yangzhou has Kuaiji Mountain, Juqu Lake, and Sanjiang River, with a population ratio of two men to five women, and is suitable for raising birds and animals and growing rice.

The Zhou Dynasty had five levels of nobility but only three levels of land enfeoffment: dukes and marquises with a hundred miles, counts with seventy miles, and sons and barons with fifty miles. There were about 1,800 small countries. After the Zhou Dynasty declined, the states were annexed and eliminated, leaving only dozens by the Spring and Autumn Period, with five hegemons. During the Warring States Period, the world was divided into seven countries, which were later unified by Qin. Qin, believing the Zhou's decline was due to enfeoffment, divided the world into counties and commanderies, eliminating the descendants of previous sages.

The Han Dynasty followed the Qin system but later expanded its territory under Emperor Wu, establishing Jiaoxu Prefecture in the south and Shuofang Prefecture in the north. The text then provides a detailed list of commanderies and counties, including their populations, jurisdictions, and historical notes. For example, Jingzhaoyin was the internal history of Qin, later renamed, with 195,702 households and 682,468 people, governing twelve counties including Chang'an and Xinfeng. Similarly, Zuo Fengyi had 235,101 households and 917,822 people, governing twenty-four counties such as Gaoling and Luyang.

The text continues with similar descriptions for other commanderies like You Fufeng, Hongnong, Hedong, Taiyuan, Shangdang, Hanoi, Henan, Dongjun, Chenliu, Yingchuan, Runan, Nanyang, Nanjun, Jiangxia, Lujiang, Jiujiang, Shanyang, Jiyin, Peijun, Wei, Julu, Changshan, Qinghe, Zhuo, Bohai, Pingyuan, Qiancheng, Jinan, Taishan, Qijun, Beihai, Donglai, Langxie, Donghai, Linhuai, Kuaiji, Danyang, Yuzhang, Guiyang, Wuling, Lingling, Hanzhong, Guanghan, Shu, Qianwei, Yuejiao, Yizhou, Yangke, and Bajun. Each entry includes household counts, populations, and lists of counties with historical notes, such as the establishment of counties during the reigns of various emperors and name changes during Wang Mang's interregnum.
