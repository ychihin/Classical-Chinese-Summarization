The text describes the evolution of religious and sacrificial practices during the Han Dynasty, focusing on the reigns of Emperors Wu, Zhao, Xuan, Yuan, Cheng, Ai, and Ping, as well as the usurper Wang Mang. It begins with the belief in ghosts and gods in Guangdong, where King Shuou’s reverence for them allegedly extended his life to 160 years, while neglect shortened his descendants’ lives. This led to the construction of temples and divination using chicken bones. Emperor Wu, influenced by alchemists like Gongsun Qing, built pavilions and towers in Chang’an and Ganquan Palace to invite immortals, including the Feilian, Gui, Yishou, and Longshou Pavilions, and the Tongtian Tower. He conducted sacrifices, such as those to Taiyi and Houtu, and expanded palace rooms. After a drought, Gongsun Qing cited the Yellow Emperor’s precedent, leading to a decree for worldwide worship of the spiritual star. Emperor Wu performed numerous rituals, including suburban sacrifices at Yongcheng, worship at Mount Tai, and construction of a Mingtang based on a Yellow Emperor-era design. He also built Jianzhang Palace, Taiye Pool with artificial islands, and the Shenming Terrace, all to attract gods. The calendar was revised, and the year was renamed Taichu. Alchemists continued to advise, leading to the construction of five cities and twelve towers. Emperor Wu made five pilgrimages to Mount Tai for sealing ceremonies, and sacrifices were made to various mountains and rivers.

During Emperor Zhao’s reign, he was too young to participate in sacrifices. Emperor Xuan honored Emperor Wu as Shizong, building temples where divine signs like white cranes and five-colored geese appeared. Emperor Xuan personally performed suburban sacrifices at Ganquan and Jiese, changing the era name to Sujue. He ordered annual sacrifices to the Yangtze River, sea, and Luoshui, and regular worship of the Five Mountains and Four Rivers. Alchemists advised building temples for various gods, including Suihou and Zhoukangbaoding. Doctor Liu Gengsheng was punished for failed alchemy. Jingzhao Yin Zhang Chang advised against alchemists, and many were dismissed. A tripod found in Meiyang was debated; Zhang Chang argued it was a Zhou dynasty artifact, not suitable for the ancestral temple. Phoenixes and other auspicious signs led to the construction of Bushou Palace and Phoenix Hall. Emperor Xuan died after a reign marked by frequent sacrifices and omens.

Emperor Yuan followed old rituals, performing suburban sacrifices every other year, but favored Confucianism. Ministers like Gong Yu and Wei Xuancheng proposed abolishing many temples, but Emperor Yuan reversed this after dreaming of divine condemnation. Emperor Cheng’s reign saw a major reform: Prime Minister Kuang Heng and Imperial Censor Zhang Tan argued that sacrifices should be moved to Chang’an’s northern and southern suburbs, citing ancient practices and the hardships of traveling to Ganquan and Fenyin. After debate, the move was approved, and many temples were abolished. However, after Emperor Cheng had no heirs, the Empress Dowager ordered the restoration of the old temples at Ganquan, Fenyin, and Yongcheng, blaming the move for the lack of descendants. Emperor Cheng later became obsessed with alchemists, but Gu Yong warned against them, citing historical failures. Du Ye also criticized the distant sacrifices. After Emperor Cheng’s death, the Empress Dowager restored the northern and southern suburbs.

Emperor Ai, ill, revived many temples, but after his death, the Empress Dowager again restored the Ganquan and Fenyin temples. Under Emperor Ping, Wang Mang reported extensively on sacrificial history, arguing for a return to the northern and southern suburbs and proposing a system where heaven and earth were worshiped together with ancestors. He established altars for the Five Emperors and various gods in Chang’an’s suburbs, and later added an official shrine for the god of grain. After usurping the throne, Wang Mang built the Eight Winds Tower and engaged in alchemy, with over 1,700 temples by his death. The text concludes with a commentary on the Han Dynasty’s ritual evolution, noting debates over the five virtues and the influence of alchemists, with Gu Yong’s criticisms deemed correct.
