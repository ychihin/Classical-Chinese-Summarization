Among the eight types of government officials in "Hong Fan," the third type is sacrificial officials. Sacrifice is performed to show filial piety, serve ancestors, and understand gods. Sacrifices are widespread among surrounding ethnic minorities, and even animals like jackals and otters perform them. The sage king established laws and rituals for sacrifices. If a spirit is single-minded, respectful, solemn, and wise, gods may come to him, designating men as 觋 and women as witches to reach the gods and provide sacrifices. Descendants of previous sages who understand mountains, rivers, worship etiquette, and divine affairs become wizards; those who understand pure-color livestock for seasonal sacrifices, sacrificial locations, and godly origins are called sect members. Thus, there are officials above the gods and below the people, each managing their own affairs without confusion. The people and gods have different roles, respecting each other without offense, so gods send good luck, people follow natural order, disasters do not come, and needs are met.

After the young Emperor Wu weakened, nine members of the Li family rebelled, causing chaos among people and spirits. Families worshiped witches and made unrestrained sacrifices, defiling the gods, who considered them unclean. Good luck did not come, disasters occurred frequently, and lives could not be completed. Zhuanxu accepted this and ordered Nan Zhengzhong to master astronomy to entrust the gods and Huo Zhengli to master geography to entrust the people, restoring normalcy without violating or defiling the gods. Since the Gonggong family dominated Kyushu, his son Julong could pacify the water and soil and was worshiped as the land god after death. Lieshan ruled the world, and his son Zhu produced hundreds of grains and was worshiped as a grain god after death. Worshiping land and grain gods in the countryside has a very early origin.

"Yu Shu" says Shun observed the Xuanji Jade Scale to organize the sun, moon, five stars, and seven political affairs. He offered sacrifices to heaven, six sects, mountains, rivers, and gods everywhere. He collected auspicious jade from princes, uncles, sons, and men, chose an auspicious month and day, met with pastors and guardians of princes, and awarded the jade. In February each year, he inspected the east and arrived at Daizong (Mount Tai), burning firewood and sacrificing by rank. He harmonized the four seasons, months, and dates, unified rhythm and weights, revised the five rituals and music, and used three silks, two life and one death as stinging rituals. In May, he inspected Nanyue (Hengshan); in August, Huashan; in November, Beiyue (Hengshan), all with the same etiquette as Daizong. Zhongyue is Mount Song. Inspections occurred every five years. Dayu continued this system.

Thirteen generations later, Emperor Kongjia had bad moral conduct and liked to worship gods, defiling them, causing two dragons to leave. Thirteen generations later, Tang conquered Xia Jie and planned to move Xia's land and sacred sites. When this failed, he made "Xia She," demoting Lieshan's son Zhu and sacrificing Zhou Guo's son to the Grain God instead. Eight generations later, Emperor Taiwu had two mulberry and grain trees growing in the courtyard, growing as thick as an arch overnight. Taiwu was frightened. Yi Zhi said monsters cannot defeat virtue. Taiwu practiced virtue, and the trees died. Yi Zhi informed Wu Xian. Thirteen generations later, Emperor Wuding got Fu Yue as prime minister, reviving the Yin Dynasty, and Wuding was named Gaozong. A pheasant crowed on a tripod, frightening Wuding. Zu Ji said to cultivate virtue. Wuding obeyed, and the throne remained peaceful. Five generations later, Emperor Yi neglected the gods and was struck by thunder and died. Three generations later, Emperor Zhou became promiscuous, and King Wu crusaded against him. From this, it seems that initially there was respect for the gods, but later it gradually declined.

The Duke of Zhou assisted King Cheng, harmonizing the king's way, establishing rituals and music. The emperor's places for worshiping heaven were called Mingtang and Piyong; princes' places were called Pangong. Sacrifices to Hou Ji in the suburbs matched offerings to heaven, and sacrifices to King Wen in the Mingtang clan matched offerings to heaven. Within the four seas, everyone assisted in sacrifices according to their positions. The emperor sacrificed to the world's famous mountains and rivers, inviting gods to appease them, all in order. The Five Mountains were treated like the three nobles, and the Four Sacred Mountains like princes. Princes only sacrificed to famous mountains and rivers within their territory, officials sacrificed to five places including gates and stoves, and common people only sacrificed to ancestors. Each had laws and etiquette; indulgence in sacrifice was prohibited. Thirteen generations later, the world declined, and rituals and music were abolished. King You lost his royal power and was defeated by Quanrong. King Ping moved eastward to Luoyi. Duke Xianggang of Qin attacked the Quan Rong, rescued the king of Zhou, was classified as a prince, lived in the west, represented the God of Shao Wu, built Xizhi, and sacrificed to the White Emperor with one horse, one colt, one ox, and one ram each.

For the next fourteen years, Duke Wen of Qin hunted eastward between Yi and Wei, divined his residence, and got good omens. He dreamed of a yellow snake hanging from the sky to the ground, its mouth resting on the hillside of Wandi. Shi Dun said this was a symbol of heaven, so he built a fortress and used three animals to worship Baidi in the suburbs. Before Yongzhi was established, there were Wuyang Wuzhi next to Yongcheng and good Zhizhi in Yongcheng and Cambodia, but they were abandoned. Some say Yongzhou has always been the residence of gods due to its high terrain, so altars were built to worship heaven in the suburbs, and temples of various gods gathered here. Sacrifices were held during the Huangdi period, and suburban sacrifices in the late Zhou Dynasty, but these words are not in the classics. Nine years after the palace was established, Duke Wen of Qin got a stone-like object and worshiped it in the city on the northern hillside of Chencang. Sometimes this god didn't come for a year, sometimes several times a year, often at night with lights like shooting stars from the east gathering in the temple city, and lights like roosters crowing loudly, attracting wild geese to crow at night. An animal was sacrificed, called Chen Bao. Seventy-one years after Chen Bao Temple was established, Qin Degong became emperor and lived in Yongcheng after divination. His descendants expanded territory to the Yellow River and established their capital in Yongcheng. The temples in Yongcheng flourished from this time. Three hundred animals were sacrificed in Yongzhi. A temple was built to worship. Dogs were divided and hung on the four gates to protect against evil spirits. Four years later, Qin Xuangong established a secret camp in Weinan to worship the Qing Emperor. Thirteen years later, Duke Mu of Qin became king. He lay ill for five days without waking. When he woke, he said he dreamed of heaven, and God ordered him to quell chaos in Jin. The historian recorded it and hid it in the mansion. Later generations said Duke Mu of Qin went to heaven.
