Shentu Gang, a native of Maoling, was an upright man who resented Wang Mang's isolation of Emperor Ping's maternal relatives. When recommended as a virtuous and upright man, he submitted a countermeasure criticizing the court for suppressing dissent and separating close relatives, arguing this violated the principles of good governance. He cited the examples of the Duke of Zhou and Huo Guang, warning that those with great power are often unsafe. He urged the emperor to welcome his grandmother and recall the Feng and Wei clans. Wang Mang had him dismissed from office. After Wang Mang usurped the throne, Shentu Gang fled. Later, when Wei Xiao considered betraying Han, Shentu Gang advised him to remain loyal to Emperor Guangwu, but Wei Xiao refused. In the seventh year of Jianwu, Shentu Gang was summoned. He again wrote to Wei Xiao, warning that isolation and refusal of advice lead to ruin, but Wei Xiao did not accept. Shentu Gang was appointed imperial censor and then ministerial order. He once stopped Emperor Guangwu from traveling by placing his head on the wheel. He repeatedly advised against the emperor's harsh treatment of officials and urged the appointment of virtuous guardians for the crown prince, but was not heeded. He was eventually released as the Pingyin Order, later recalled as Taizhong doctor, and died at home after resigning due to illness.

Bao Yong, a native of Tunliu, was the son of Bao Xuan, who was killed by Wang Mang. He was filial, once divorcing his wife for scolding a dog in front of his stepmother. Wang Mang sought to kill Bao Yong, but the prefect Gou Jian protected him. Bao Yong advised Gou Jian on reviving the Han. After Gou Jian's death, Bao Yong's brother was arrested, but the new prefect Zhao Xing released him. Bao Yong later exposed an impostor. During Gengshi's reign, he became a general and defeated Qingdu. After Gengshi's death, he submitted to Guangwu, dismissing his troops out of shame for serving Gengshi. He persuaded the governor of Huai County to surrender. As governor of Lu County, he killed the rebel generals Peng Feng and others. He later served as Yangzhou Mu, relaxing laws to pacify the people. As Sili Xiaowei, he impeached the emperor's uncle, King Liu Liang of Zhao, for disrespect, causing the court to be in awe. He wept at Gengshi's tomb, which initially displeased the emperor but was defended by a minister. He was later demoted for speaking out and then served as Yanzhou Mu until his death. The historian commented that Bao Yong's loyalty to his old master allowed him to serve a new one, and that upright words are difficult for people to understand.

Zhi Yun, a native of Runan, was a scholar who, during Wang Mang's reign, observed astronomical signs and predicted the Han would be restored. He advised a local official to follow this destiny but refused a minor post, citing historical precedents. He then wrote to Wang Mang, urging him to return the throne to the Liu family, citing heavenly warnings. Wang Mang imprisoned him, but he was released. Later, he served under General Fu Jun, advising him to stop looting tombs and mistreating corpses, which the general did. Zhi Yun resigned, ashamed of gaining office through military merit. He avenged his friend Dong Zizhang by killing his enemy and then turned himself in, but the magistrate refused to imprison him. He later served as a meritorious officer under Prefect Ouyang She. At a feast, he publicly denounced another official, Yao Yan, as corrupt, causing the prefect shame. After a period of conflict, Zhi Yun left the prefect's service. He later became a marquis and gatekeeper, once refusing to let Emperor Guangwu enter the city at night. He remonstrated with the emperor about hunting and later advised the crown prince after Empress Guo was deposed. He served as governor of Changsha, where he praised a filial son, and later as magistrate of Mang County before retiring to teach. He wrote eight books and died of illness.
