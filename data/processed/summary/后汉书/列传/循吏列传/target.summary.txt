**Summary of Historical Records on Governance and Notable Officials of the Eastern Han Dynasty**

Emperor Guangwu, having risen from the common people, understood the hardships of farming and the suffering of the people. After unifying the realm, he pursued peace, abolished the complex policies of Wang Mang's era, and restored the lenient Han methods. He dressed simply, avoided luxury, and showed no favoritism in his court. When foreign states presented valuable horses and swords, he ordered the horses used for carriage and the swords given to knights. He reduced officials, abolished hunting expeditions, and wrote imperial letters in small characters, fostering an atmosphere of diligence and frugality. He frequently consulted ministers and visited the people to learn of their grievances, leading to diligent governance and rest for the populace. Local governors competed in their efforts, with figures like Du Shi, Ren Yan, and Xiguang achieving notable political successes. However, during the Jianwu and Yongping eras, governance was overly strict and detailed, leading to frequent replacements of officials based on rumors. Despite sharp advice from Zhu Fu and others, their counsel was not adopted, leaving shortcomings in the restoration period. From the reigns of Emperor Zhang and Emperor He onward, officials like Lu Gong, Wu You, and Liu Kuan used benevolence and integrity to prevent deceit, while Wang Tang and Chen Chong appointed virtuous talents, promoting education. Others like Wang Huan and Ren Jun, as Luoyang magistrates, punished evil and improved officialdom, though they had imperfections in morality and etiquette.

Wei Sa, from a poor family, studied diligently and served as a magistrate and prefect. In Guiyang, he built schools, established marriage rites, and transformed local customs. He constructed roads and inns, reducing corvée labor, and regulated iron smelting, increasing annual revenue. He governed with consideration for the people, maintaining peace for ten years. Ren Yan, a learned scholar, served as a young prefect of Kuaiji, where he hired virtuous men and supported the poor. In Jiuzhen, he taught farming, established marriage regulations, and civilized the people, leading to prosperity and admiration from barbarian tribes. He later governed Wuwei, where he executed corrupt officials, defended against invaders, built irrigation systems, and promoted education, leaving a lasting legacy.

Wang Jing, a scholar of the *Book of Changes* and other subjects, was summoned to control floods. He successfully built canals and embankments over a thousand miles, using sluices to manage water flow, saving costs and preventing disasters. He later served as a prefect in Xuzhou and Lujiang, where he taught cattle plowing, reclaimed land, and guided sericulture, engraving laws for the people. Qin Peng, from a prominent family, governed Shanyang with etiquette and education, establishing moral precepts and agricultural classifications. He cultivated rice fields and implemented a grading system to prevent official corruption, later serving in Yingchuan with auspicious signs. Wang Huan, initially chivalrous, later studied Confucianism and served as a magistrate, eliminating cunning criminals and ensuring peace. As Luoyang magistrate, he resolved difficult cases and uncovered hidden evils, earning widespread praise. After his death, the people built a shrine for him, and even Emperor Huan later preserved it.

Xu Jing, as Guiyang prefect, established wedding and funeral rites and resolved a property dispute between brothers by taking responsibility himself, leading to their repentance. Mengchang, known for his integrity, cleared a widow's wrongful conviction, ending a drought. As Hepu prefect, he reformed pearl collection, restoring the pearl trade and reviving the local economy. He retired to farming, and despite recommendations, was never hired, dying at seventy. The Fifth Fang, a grandson of a former minister, governed Zhangye during a famine by opening granaries without awaiting orders, saving the people. He later served in Nanyang and as Colonel Protecting Qiang. Liu Chong, a descendant of royalty, governed with kindness, removing oppressive rules. When leaving Kuaiji, elders gave him coins, but he accepted only one from each. He held high offices but remained frugal, dying at home.

Qiu Lan, a quiet scholar, governed as a pavilion chief, promoting agriculture and education. He resolved a filial piety dispute by counseling a mother and son, transforming the son into a filial child. He later entered the Imperial College but avoided frivolous talk, maintaining strict personal conduct. Tong Hui, known for fairness, argued for his superior Yang Ci when others abandoned him. As a magistrate, he educated offenders, rewarded good deeds, and captured tigers harming people, executing one and releasing another. His governance led to peace and an influx of refugees.
