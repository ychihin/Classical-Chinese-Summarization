Liu Yan, a descendant of King Lu Gong, served as a local official and later as Nanyang Prefect, Zongzheng, and Taichang. During Emperor Ling's reign, rebellions spread due to weak governors. Liu Yan proposed appointing powerful governors to control regions, a suggestion initially ignored but later adopted after several governors were killed. He was sent to lead Yizhou as shepherd. In Yizhou, he faced a rebellion led by Ma Xiang, who claimed to be the Son of Heaven, but it was suppressed by Jia Long. Liu Yan appointed Zhang Lu, who later seized Hanzhong and killed the prefect. Liu Yan grew arrogant, built imperial carriages, and killed powerful locals, causing resentment. After losing two sons in a failed plot, he died of illness. His son Liu Zhang succeeded him but lacked authority, leading to internal strife and rebellion. Liu Zhang eventually surrendered to Liu Bei after a siege, and died later.

Yuan Shu, son of Sikong Yuan Feng, was known for chivalry but later changed. He fled to Nanyang after Dong Zhuo's coup and allied with Sun Jian. Conflicts arose with his cousin Yuan Shao over leadership. Yuan Shu declared himself emperor in 197 CE, citing prophecies and his family's lineage. His rule was marked by extravagance and cruelty, leading to widespread discontent. After defeats by Cao Cao and Lu Bu, his army weakened, and he died in 199 CE, vomiting blood in despair. His family sought refuge with Sun Ce.

Lu Bu, a skilled warrior from Wuyuan, served under Ding Yuan but killed him to join Dong Zhuo. He later killed Dong Zhuo with Wang Yun's persuasion but fled after Li Cui's attack. He allied with various warlords, including Yuan Shu and Liu Bei, but frequently betrayed them. Known for his prowess, he defeated Zhang Yan but alienated allies due to his brutality. He eventually surrendered to Cao Cao after a siege at Xiapi, but was executed along with his generals Chen Gong and Gao Shun. His life was marked by shifting loyalties and ultimate failure.
