In the Biography of the Eight Kings of Xiaoming, Emperor Xiaoming had nine sons, including Jia Gui, who became Emperor Zhang, and Yin Gui, who became Emperor Liu Chang of Liangjie. The mothers of the other seven kings were not recorded. Liu Jian, the Qiancheng Ai King, was granted his title in the third year of Yongping but died the following year without sons, leading to the dissolution of his kingdom. Liu Xian, Chen Jing Wang, was initially made Guangping King in the third year of Yongping. In the third year of Jianchu, he was ordered to his fiefdom, but Suzong, unable to bear separation, kept him in the capital. Later, his title changed to Xiping King, and after the emperor's death, a posthumous edict made him King Chen. He died at age 37, succeeded by his son Liu Jun.

Liu Gong, King Jing of Pengcheng, was titled King Lingshou in the ninth year of Yongping, later becoming Julu King, then Jiangling King, and finally Liu'an King before being posthumously made King Pengcheng. He was dignified and respected. In the sixth year of Yongchu, his son Anu was made a marquis. In the third year of the Yuan Dynasty, Liu Gong's son Liu Youfu committed suicide after angering him. Zhao Mu, the Prime Minister, falsely accused Liu Gong of treason, but an investigation cleared him, and Zhao Mu was imprisoned. Liu Gong died at 46, succeeded by his son Liu Daosi.

Liu Dang, the successful king of Jing, was titled King Chongxi in the ninth year of Yongping. He was intelligent and close to Suzong. After the emperor's death, he went to his fiefdom but broke the law by committing adultery with a palace woman named Ai Zhi and covering up the murder of her husband. He died at 25, succeeded by his son Liu Chongsi.

Liu Yan, King of Xia Pi Hui, was granted his title in the 15th year of Yongping. He had good looks and was often with Suzong. After the emperor's death, he went to his fiefdom. He later had heart problems, and his concubines fought over succession. Emperor He sent Liu Gong to resolve the matter. Liu Yan died at 54, succeeded by his son Liu Chengsi.

Liu Chang, the king of Liangjie, was made King of Runan in the 15th year of Yongping and was especially favored. He moved to Liang and added counties to his fiefdom. Intelligent but arrogant, he engaged in divination and was flattered by officials who said he should be emperor. In the ninth year of Yongyuan, he was reported for rebellion. The emperor spared him, only cutting off two counties. Liu Chang wrote a remorseful letter, offering to return lands and servants. The emperor refused his resignations. Liu Chang died at 27, succeeded by his son Liu Jian.

Liu Ribing, the king of Qing in Huaiyang, was made King of Changshan in the 15th year of Yongping, later moving to Huaiyang. He died 16 years after his establishment without an heir. Liu Chang, the mourning king of Jiyin, was granted his title in the 15th year of Yongping and died 13 years later in the capital without children, leading to the abolition of his fiefdom.
