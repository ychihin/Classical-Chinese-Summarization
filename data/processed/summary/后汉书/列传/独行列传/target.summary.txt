The text begins with a philosophical reflection on human nature, noting that while a mad person may be aggressive, a greedy person is unwilling to do bad things. It suggests that this is an imperfect approach, as people's temperaments differ, and what they do or refrain from doing is appropriate for each individual. In times of peace, many who deviate from the norm can become famous and upright, with ambitions as strong as gold or integrity as severe as winter frost. Some make friends willing to live and die together, risking their lives for justice. Though not perfect, these deeds are memorable, though their complexities make them hard to categorize. The text states that such exceptional conduct is recorded in the "Ding Xing Pian" to fill gaps in history.

The biography of Qiao Xuan follows. Born in Ba County, he was learned in the "Yi" and "Spring and Autumn." He became an official and was recommended for his integrity during a solar eclipse in Emperor Yongshi's reign. He criticized Emperor Cheng for neglecting state affairs and focusing on trivial matters, warning of dangers from unpreparedness. Despite frequent disasters, his advice was ignored, and he remained in a low position. He later resigned after his brother's death. During Wang Mang's usurpation, Qiao Xuan abandoned his post and hid. Gongsun Shu tried to recruit him with threats of poison, but Qiao Xuan refused, citing historical examples of integrity. His son offered money to spare his life, and Qiao Xuan hid until Gongsun Shu's death. He died in the eleventh year of Jianwu, and Guangwu later honored him.

Li Ye, from Guanghan, was ambitious and upright, studying the "Lu Shi." He refused to serve under Wang Mang, feigning illness. Forced by Prefect Liu Xian, he was imprisoned but released after a guest intervened. He later hid in the valley. Gongsun Shu tried to recruit him with threats of poison, but Li Ye refused, stating that a gentleman should not enter a chaotic state. He drank poison and died. Gongsun Shu, ashamed, held a funeral. After Shu was pacified, Guangwu honored Li Ye. Others like Wang Hao and Wang Jia also refused to serve Gongsun Shu, choosing death over submission.

Liu Mao, from Taiyuan, was known for filial piety. He studied the "Book of Rites" and taught students. He abandoned his post during Wang Mang's usurpation and hid in Hongnong Mountain. During a Red Eyebrow attack, he carried the prefect Sun Fu to safety, hiding and foraging for food for over a hundred days. Sun Fu praised his integrity, and Liu Mao was appointed Yilang. He later died in office. Other accounts describe Yan Shuo, who died fighting Xianbei invaders, and Suo Fu, who died saving a magistrate from thieves.

Wen Xu, from Taiyuan, was recommended as a talent and became a censor. He was captured by Gou Yu, who tried to persuade him to join a rebellion. Wen Xu refused, scolding the rebels and killing several before falling on his sword. Guangwu honored him with a grand funeral and appointed his sons as doctors. Peng Xiu, from Kuaiji, defended his father from robbers at age fifteen, gaining fame. As an official, he saved a chief clerk from execution by reasoning with the prefect. He later died shielding the prefect from thieves' arrows.

Suo Lufang, from Dongjun, taught the "Shang Shu" and served as a county magistrate. During the Gengshi period, he offered to die in place of a prefect condemned by an envoy, who pardoned them. He later became Luoyang Order, known for his ability. In his old age, Guangwu summoned him to the capital and honored him. Zhou Jia, from Runan, followed a prefect into battle against thieves. When the prefect was wounded, Zhou Jia shielded him and scolded the thieves, who released them. He later refused to marry a princess and died as prefect of Lingling.

Shi Shi, from Shanyang, was known for his faithfulness. He promised to visit his friend Zhang Shao after two years and kept his word. When Zhang Shao died, Shi Shi dreamed of him and traveled to attend his funeral, where the coffin moved only after his arrival. He later cared for another friend's family after his death. As governor of Jingzhou, he recognized a poor friend working as a laborer and offered him a position, but the friend refused. Shi Shi died as prefect of Lujiang.

Li Shan, from Nanyang, saved an orphaned infant, Li Xu, from murderous slaves by fleeing and raising him. He treated Li Xu with respect and later returned to reclaim the family property, punishing the slaves. Guangwu honored them. Li Shan later became governor of Rinan, visiting Li Yuan's tomb with great reverence. He died en route to Jiujiang. Wang Yi, from Guanghan, buried a stranger who died in his care, using gold from the deceased. Later, a horse and embroidered quilt led him to the stranger's father, who recognized his son's belongings. Wang Yi also solved a ghostly murder case at an inn.

Zhang Wu, from Wu County, mourned his father, who died fighting thieves. He often visited the site of his father's death with his sword. He was commended for filial piety but died of grief after his mother's death. Lu Yu, from Kuaiji, was known for his intelligence and loyalty. During a rebellion, he and others were tortured but refused to implicate the prefect. His mother's cooking helped identify her presence. He was pardoned and died of old age.

Dai Feng, from Jibei, was a devoted student who buried a classmate and returned his belongings. He once gave his remaining money to a thief, who returned his stolen goods. As a magistrate, locusts avoided his territory, and he prayed for rain by threatening self-immolation, which brought rain. He released prisoners to visit families, and all returned. He died in office. Li Chong, from Chenliu, was poor but refused to separate from his family. He expelled his wife for suggesting division. He later killed a tomb robber and refused official posts. He was respected by General Deng Zhi but criticized him openly. He died at eighty-eight.

Miao Xu, from Runan, resolved family disputes by self-reflection. He defended a magistrate and built a tomb for a prefect during a rebellion. As magistrate, he executed corrupt officials and died in office. Chen Chong, from Yuzhang, was generous, paying a colleague's debts and taking blame for a missing pair of pants. He resigned when a friend was deposed and died as a censor. Lei Yi, from Yuzhang, refused gold and took blame for a colleague's crime. He later feigned madness to avoid office and died as Nandun Order.

Fan Ran, from Chenliu, was a maverick who rejected世俗 norms. He refused to travel with a friend to avoid appearing sycophantic. He lived simply, selling divination and farming. During the party ban, he was imprisoned. He died at seventy-four, instructing a simple burial. Dai Jiu, from Kuaiji, endured brutal torture while refusing to falsely accuse a prefect. His resilience impressed the interrogator, who released him. He died of illness.

Zhao Bao, from Ganling, was ashamed of his eunuch relative. As prefect, he fought Xianbei invaders who captured his mother. She urged him to fight, and he did, but she was killed. He died of grief. Xiang Xu, from Hanoi, was eccentric, wearing disheveled hair and shouting. He taught disciples and later became Prime Minister of Zhao, where he was solemn. He was executed for allegedly conspiring with Zhang Jiao. Lang Fu, from Guanghan, prayed for rain by threatening self-immolation, which brought rain.

Liu Yi, from Yingchuan, was generous, giving his car to a mourner and helping the poor. He refused office but later served as chief minister during Emperor Xian's reign. He sold his horse to bury a stranger and starved to death helping friends. Wang Lie, from Taiyuan, was known for his virtue, encouraging a thief to reform by giving him cloth. He fled to Liaodong during the Yellow Turban rebellion and died there.
