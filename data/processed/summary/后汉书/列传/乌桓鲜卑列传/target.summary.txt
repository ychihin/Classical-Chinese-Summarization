The Wuhuan originated from the Donghu tribe. After the Xiongnu leader Maodun defeated Donghu in the early Han Dynasty, the survivors took refuge in Wuhuan Mountain and adopted the name as their tribal title. They were skilled horsemen and archers, primarily hunting birds and animals, and practiced nomadic pastoralism without fixed residences. Their felt tents faced east, and they subsisted on meat, cheese, and clothing made from animal hairs. They valued youth over the elderly, had fierce tempers, and would kill fathers and brothers in anger but never mothers, as mothers had their own tribes for revenge. Leaders were chosen based on bravery and strength, not heredity, with towns forming tribes under small leaders. They used carved wood as talismans for commands, lacked writing, and had no fixed surnames, adopting the names of strong leaders instead. Marriage involved abducting women, cohabiting for months, then paying betrothal gifts in livestock. Sons-in-law served their wives' families for one to two years before receiving dowries. They practiced levirate marriage, burying stepmothers and sisters-in-law with their original husbands. Women’s opinions were heeded in strategy, but men decided in battle. They shaved their heads, with women growing hair only after marriage, and wore headdresses decorated with gold and jade. Women embroidered leather and wove wool; men made bows, arrows, saddles, and smelted copper and iron for weapons. They cultivated crops like Biaozi and Aunt, which matured in ten months, and used the pregnancy of black beasts to distinguish seasons. They honored death in battle, used coffins, and conducted mourning rituals with singing and dancing, burning a fat dog with a colored rope along with the deceased’s possessions to guide the soul to Chishan, akin to the Central Plains’ Mount Tai. They sacrificed cattle and sheep to gods, ghosts, heaven, earth, and ancestors, burning the offerings completely. Disobedience to the leader was punishable by death; feuds could be settled with livestock. Wuhuan was located southwest of Dingling and northeast of Wusun, with many vipers.

After Maodun’s defeat, Wuhuan remained weak, often submitting to the Xiongnu and paying annual tribute. Emperor Wu moved them outside the five counties of Shanggu, Yuyang, Youbeiping, Liaoxi, and Liaodong to spy on the Xiongnu. Their leaders paid homage to the emperor annually, and Wuhuan captains were established to supervise them. During Emperor Zhao’s reign, Wuhuan grew stronger, desecrated a Xiongnu chanyu’s tomb, and was attacked by the Xiongnu. General Huo Guang sent Fan Mingyou to intercept, but the Xiongnu had retreated; Fan then attacked Wuhuan, killing over 6,000. Under Emperor Xuan, Wuhuan gradually surrendered and guarded the border. When Wang Mang usurped the throne, he conscripted Wuhuan troops, holding their families hostage, leading to rebellion and hatred. The Xiongnu lured Wuhuan leaders to join them. In the early Guangwu period, Wuhuan and Xiongnu jointly invaded, devastating border counties. In the 21st year of Jianwu, Ma Yuan attacked Wuhuan but achieved little. In the 22nd year, Wuhuan defeated the weakened Xiongnu, and the emperor bribed them with money and cloth. In the 25th year, Hao Dan and 922 others paid tribute, and the emperor appointed 81 Wuhuan leaders as princes and commanders, settling them in border counties to scout for the Han. Later, Wuhuan captains were re-established to supervise them. From Emperor Ming to Emperor He, Wuhuan guarded the frontier peacefully. During Emperor An’s reign, Wuhuan invaded Daijun and Shanggu, and later Yanmen Wuhuan joined Xianbei and Southern Xiongnu in attacking Wuyuan, defeating the Han. The court sent He Xi and Liang Dong to defeat them, and Wuhuan gradually became closer to the Han. Under Emperor Shun, Wuhuan invaded Yunzhong, but Geng Ye repelled them. In the 5th year of Yonghe, Wuhuan leaders rebelled but were defeated by Zhang Dan. During Emperor Huan’s Yongshou period, Wuhuan in Shuofang rebelled but were pacified by Zhang Huan. In the 9th year of Yanxi, Wuhuan, Xianbei, and Southern Xiongnu invaded nine counties, but Zhang Huan drove them out. Under Emperor Ling, leaders like Nanlou and Qiu Liju claimed kingship. In the 4th year of Zhongping, Zhang Chun rebelled, joined Qiu Liju, and invaded four provinces. Liu Yu pacified the north. During Emperor Xian’s Chuping period, Tadun succeeded Qiu Liju and controlled three counties. Yuan Shao granted Tadun and others Chanyu seals. Later, Yan Rou killed the Wuhuan captain and replaced him. When Yuan Shang failed, he fled to Tadun. Cao Cao conquered Wuhuan in the 12th year of Jian’an, killed Tadun, captured 200,000, and moved the remaining people to the Central Plains.

Xianbei, also a Donghu branch, relied on Xianbei Mountain. Their customs were similar to Wuhuan, but they shaved their heads for marriage and gathered by the Raole River in spring. Their region had unique animals like wild horses and horned oxen, whose horns were used for bows. Mink and mole furs were valuable. In the early Han, Xianbei was defeated by Maodun and fled to Liaodong, with no contact with the Han. In the early Jianwu period, they invaded the north. In the 21st year, Ji Xu defeated them, and they later exchanged envoys with Han. Ji Xu bribed them to attack the Northern Xiongnu, and they received rewards. During the Ming and Zhang reigns, they guarded the border peacefully. Under Emperor He, Dou Xian defeated the Xiongnu, and Xianbei occupied their territory, absorbing 100,000 Xiongnu remnants and growing strong. They invaded Liaodong, Youbeiping, and Yuyang, with mixed results. During Emperor An’s Yongchu period, Yan Liyang paid tribute, and the court built facilities for trade and hostages. Xianbei alternately surrendered and rebelled, clashing with Xiongnu and Wuhuan. In the 2nd year of Yuanchu, they surrounded Wulu County but gained nothing. In the 4th year, Lianxiu invaded, but Wuhuan leader Yu Zhiju defeated him. In the 5th year, 10,000 Xianbei cavalry invaded Daijun, burning government offices. The court mobilized troops to guard Shanggu. In the 6th year, Deng Zun defeated Xianbei. In the 1st year of Yongning, Wulun and Qizhi Tar surrendered. In the 1st year of Jianguang, Qizhi Tar rebelled, invaded Juyong, and killed the prefect. Geng Kui relieved the siege. Qizhi Tar grew stronger, invading Yanmen, Dingxiang, and Taiyuan. In the 2nd year of Yanguang, he invaded the east, killing over 1,000. In the 3rd year, he invaded Gaoliu. Under Emperor Shun, Qizhi Tar invaded Dai County, killing the prefect. Zhang Guo defeated him in the 2nd year of Yongjian. Xianbei from Liaodong invaded but were repelled by Geng Ye. In the 3rd and 4th years, they invaded Yuyang and Shuofang. In the 6th year, Geng Ye defeated them. In the 1st year of Yangjia, Geng Ye sent Rong Zhumo to attack Xianbei successfully. Xianbei later invaded Liaodong, and Geng Ye moved to Wulu City. In the 2nd year, Zhao Chou defeated Xianbei. Under Emperor Huan, Tanshihuai emerged. His father Touluhou doubted his birth, but his wife claimed a miraculous conception. Tanshihuai grew brave and resourceful, recovering stolen cattle and becoming leader. He established a royal court by the Geqiu River, subdued tribes, and expanded territory from Dingling to Wusun, covering 14,000 miles east-west. In the 2nd year of Yongshou, he invaded Yunzhong. In the 1st year of Yanxi, Zhang Huan killed 200 Xianbei. In the 2nd year, they invaded Yanmen. In the 6th year, they invaded Liaodong. In the 9th year, tens of thousands invaded nine counties, but Zhang Huan drove them out. The court tried to make Tanshihuai king, but he refused and intensified raids. He divided Xianbei into three parts: east, middle, and west. Under Emperor Ling, Xianbei invaded the three provinces of Youzhou, Bingzhou, and Liangzhou annually. In the 3rd year of Xiping, Xia Zhi defeated them. In the 5th year, they invaded Youzhou. In the 6th year, they invaded three borders. Xia Yu proposed a winter-spring campaign, but the court disagreed. Tian Yan sought to lead an expedition, but Cai Yong advised against it, citing past failures and current poverty. The court ignored him, sending Xia Yu, Tian Yan, and Zang Man with 10,000 cavalry each. Tanshihuai’s forces defeated them, killing 70-80% of the troops. The generals were imprisoned. In the 1st year of Guanghe, Xianbei invaded Jiuquan. Tanshihuai, facing food shortages, attacked the Japanese eastward, capturing over 1,000 to fish. He died at 45, succeeded by his son Helian, who was less capable and lost half his people. Helian was killed by a Han archer. His son Qianman was young, and his nephew Kuixi succeeded. Later, Qianman competed with Kuixi, dividing the tribe. Budugen succeeded after Kuixi’s death. From Tanshihuai, leadership became hereditary. The text concludes that Wuhuan and Xianbei caused continuous border troubles, with no effective strategies to control them.
