The text describes the historical and cultural evolution of the Eastern Yi peoples, beginning with their origins and characteristics. The Yi, meaning "root," were considered docile and easily governed by morality, leading to the emergence of a "country of gentlemen" and a "country of immortality." They were divided into nine branches, and Confucius once expressed a desire to live among them. Early interactions with Chinese dynasties included Yao sending Xizhong to Woyi and the Yi submitting to Xia rulers, though rebellions occurred under tyrants like Jie. Over centuries, the Yi fluctuated between submission and revolt, with notable events such as Xu Yi usurping the title of emperor and attacking Zongzhou, only to be defeated by King Wen of Chu. Later, during the Zhou and Han dynasties, the Yi peoples, including Huaiyi and Siyi, were scattered, conquered, or assimilated, with periods of tribute and conflict under rulers like Wang Mang and Emperor Wu of Han.

The text then details specific Yi groups, starting with Fuyu, located north of Xuantu. Fuyu was a flat, fertile land producing horses, jade, and mink. Its people were strong, brave, and kind, using bows and swords, and practicing rituals like the "Yinggu" festival. They had severe punishments, including execution for adultery and theft, and practiced levirate marriage. Burial customs involved jade boxes for kings, provided by the Han court. Fuyu engaged in tribute and conflict with Han, including invasions under King An and later defeats by Gongsunyu.

Yilou, northeast of Fuyu, was a mountainous, cold region where people lived in caves and wore pigskin. They were brave archers using poisoned arrows and were known for plundering. Gaoguli, south of Fuyu, had five tribes and a history of conflict with Han, including invasions and tribute missions. Their customs included sacrifices to heaven, singing and dancing, and severe punishments. Woju, near Gaoguli, was fertile and known for farming and foot combat. Their burial practices involved large wooden coffins and bone collection.

Korea, originally granted to Jizi by King Wu of Zhou, had a culture of etiquette and prohibitions. Over time, it was influenced by Chinese refugees and eventually conquered by Emperor Wu of Han, who established four commanderies. The text describes the three Han states: Mahan, Chenhan, and Benchen, each with distinct customs, including farming, iron trade, and rituals. Japan, southeast of Korea, comprised over a hundred island states, with Yamatai as the leading kingdom. Japanese customs included tattooing, polygamy, and divination. The text concludes with a reflection on Jizi's influence on Korean customs and the gradual decline of these traditions due to Chinese contact.
