Based solely on the provided text, here is a summary of the biographies of several individuals:

Zhang Sheng, named Yanzhen, was from the Chenliuwei clan. He was well-read but willful and unrestrained. He made friends based on shared ambition, regardless of social status, and believed that fate determined life and death. As an official, he executed any official who accepted bribes. When criticized for his harshness, he cited Confucius's actions as a precedent, stating a gentleman's official career requires loyalty. He was later killed at age forty-nine, leaving sixty writings.

Zhao Yi, named Yuanshu, was from Hanyang. He was tall and relied on his talent, leading to arrogance and rejection. After committing crimes, he was rescued by friends. He later went to the capital and impressed Situ Yuan Feng by not bowing like others. Yuan Feng praised him highly. Zhao Yi also visited Yin Yangzhi, initially being refused entry, but eventually gaining an audience through persistence. Yang Zhi and Yuan Feng jointly recommended him, making him famous. When visiting Prefect Huangfu Gui, he was not announced and left. Huangfu Gui apologized in a letter, but Zhao Yi's reply was critical of Gui's arrogance. Zhao Yi refused all subsequent official summons and died at home. He wrote sixteen pieces.

Liu Liang, named Manshan, was from Dongping. He lost his father young and sold books to survive. He resented snobbish friends and wrote "On Breaking the Group," which was compared to Confucius's "Spring and Autumn." He also wrote "The Theory of Debian and Tongzhi." He became Chief of Beixin City, where he prioritized teaching over administrative duties, gathering hundreds of disciples. He was later promoted to Shangshu Lang and Ye Wang Ling, dying of illness during the Guanghe era.

Bian Yi, named Wenli, was from Chenliu. He was knowledgeable, good at debating, and wrote with satirical intent. General He Jin recruited him by deception. Bian Rang impressed guests with his fortune-telling. Cai Yong recommended him to He Jin, praising his unparalleled talent and arguing he should hold a high position. Bian Rang was later promoted to Prefect of Jiujiang but did not show his talents. During the Chuping era, he resigned and returned home. He insulted Cao Cao, who later ordered his execution. His writings are lost.

Li Yan, named Wensheng, was from Fanyang. He was talented in literature and music, with a quick tongue. He was ambitious but unsatisfied with official appointments. He suffered from madness, and during an illness, his mother's death caused him to become severely ill. He frightened his wife to death and was imprisoned. He died in prison at age twenty-eight.

Hou Jin, named Ziyu, was from Dunhuang. He was orphaned and poor, studying by firelight after work. He was summoned many times but declined due to illness. He wrote "On Correcting the World" and "Ying Guest Difficulty." He also wrote thirty chapters of "Huang De Biography" based on "Han Ji." He was respected in Hexi and called "Hou Jun."

Gao Biao, named Yifang, was from Wu County. He was poor and studied at Taixue. He stuttered. He wrote to Ma Rong after being refused an audience, shaming Ma Rong into apologizing. He was later promoted as a filial and honest man and became a doctor. He wrote satirical poems praised by Emperor Ling. He wrote a proverb at a farewell banquet that was praised by Cai Yong. He was later promoted to Huangling, where he governed well and died in office.

Zhang Chao, named Zibing, was from Hejian. He had literary talent and served as Sima under Zhu Chi against the Yellow Turbans. He wrote nineteen pieces and was also skilled in cursive calligraphy.

Mi Heng, named Zhengping, was from Pingyuan. He was good at debating and arrogant. He visited Xuchang but refused to see most officials, only respecting Kong Rong and Yang Xiu. Kong Rong recommended him to Cao Cao in a letter praising his talent. Mi Heng refused to meet Cao Cao and insulted him. Cao Cao made him a drum official to humiliate him, but Mi Heng humiliated Cao Cao by stripping naked. Cao Cao then sent him to Liu Biao. Mi Heng insulted Liu Biao, who sent him to Huang Zu. Mi Heng served as Huang Zu's secretary and impressed him. He later insulted Huang Zu, who ordered his execution. He was twenty-six years old.
