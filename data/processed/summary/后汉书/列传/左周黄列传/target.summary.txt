# Summary of Historical Biographies

## Zuo Xiong's Biography

Zuo Xiong, courtesy name Bohao, was a native of Nieyang, Nanyang. During Emperor An's reign, he was elected as Xiaolian and became governor of Jizhou. He kept his door closed to avoid those who sought connections through back channels and dealt harshly with corrupt officials. In the early Yongjian years, Emperor Shun appointed him as Yilang. Zuo Xiong submitted several fierce memorials, and Minister Pushe Yu Xu recommended him for his loyalty and uprightness.

Zuo Xiong was promoted to minister and submitted a memorial discussing governance. He advocated for tolerant policies toward remote areas and peaceful methods inland, emphasizing that peace for the people required appointing wise men with proper rewards and punishments. He cited historical examples from the Wen and Jing eras through Emperor Xuan's reign, noting that careful selection of officials and long tenure led to good governance. He criticized the current state of affairs where officials were transferred too frequently, corruption flourished, and supervision was ineffective. He proposed that officials with outstanding achievements should be promoted on the spot and not transferred unless for parental death, while those who disobeyed laws should be banned from office for life.

Emperor Shun was moved by Zuo Xiong's words but never implemented them due to eunuch power. In the third year of Yongjian, earthquakes occurred, and in the fourth year, floods struck. Zuo Xiong warned of impending rebellion, and soon thieves arose in Qingzhou, Hebei, and Yangzhou. He later petitioned with Guo Qian about bandits but was not accepted. He also advocated for respecting classics and building the Imperial Academy, which Emperor Shun approved.

In the first year of Yangjia, the Taixue was completed. Zuo Xiong proposed that those under forty should not be recommended as Xiaolian unless they had special talents, and they should first study at government offices. Emperor Shun accepted this. When Xu Shu of Guangling was elected underage, Zuo Xiong questioned him and dismissed him. Several officials, including Hu Guang, were dismissed for election errors, while only about thirty people like Chen Fan and Li Ying were approved. Thereafter, officials feared recommending Xiaolian carelessly.

Zuo Xiong also requested recruiting famous Confucian scholars as doctors and increasing salaries for the virtuous. He petitioned to appoint twelve-year-old Xie Lian and Zhao Jian as boy scholars, attracting many students to the capital.

Zuo Xiong secretly wrote against granting titles to wet nurse Song E and Liang Ji, citing Emperor Gao's rules and past disasters from such grants. He advised following ancient laws and not ruining national ceremonies for small favors. He repeatedly advised against granting titles, warning of disasters. Song E eventually asked to resign her title, but the emperor still made her Lord of Shanyang. She later lost her title due to conspiracy.

When Chief Minister Liu Zhi was beaten for dereliction, Zuo Xiong argued that beating Jiuqing was improper, and the emperor stopped the practice. Zuo Xiong was promoted to Sili Xiaowei. He recommended Zhou Ju as minister, and when Zhou Ju later impeached Zuo Xiong for Feng Zhi's crimes, Zuo Xiong praised Zhou Ju's integrity. He died in the third year of Yonghe.

## Zhou Ju's Biography

Zhou Ju, courtesy name Xuanguang, was from Ruyang, Runan, son of Chenliu Prefect Zhou Fang. He was short and ugly but erudite, respected by Confucianists. In the fourth year of Yanguang, he served in Situ Li He's mansion. When eunuch Chen Chan suggested moving Empress Dowager Yan to separate residence, Zhou Ju advised Li He to respect the Queen Mother, citing historical examples. Li He submitted this, and Emperor Shun later made peace with the Queen Mother.

When Sun Cheng and others were exiled for competing for merit, Zhou Ju urged Zhu Qian to advise the emperor, saying they should not kill meritorious officials. Zhu Qian's advice was accepted. Zhou Ju was later promoted to Maocai and appointed Pingqiu Ling. He wrote passionately about governance, impressing Shangshu Guo Qian and Ying He.

As governor of Taiyuan, Zhou Ju abolished the custom of prohibiting fire during Jie Zitui's death month, which caused many deaths from cold food. He posted messages explaining this was not a sage's intention, and customs gradually changed.

In the third year of Yangjia, Zuo Xiong recommended Zhou Ju as minister. He worked with Pushe Huang Qiong, gaining court respect. During a severe drought, the emperor asked about governance. Zhou Ju criticized the emperor for following Qin's luxurious path, having many resentful women in the palace, and lacking a crown prince. He blamed eunuchs for harming families and recommended releasing excess women, rectifying injustices, and eliminating eunuch expenses. The emperor summoned him and others to discuss governance.

Zhou Ju was promoted to Sili Xiaowei. In the first year of Yonghe, when disasters occurred, the emperor asked about burying the Marquis of Beixiang with royal rites. Zhou Ju argued against giving him a posthumous title, and most officials agreed. He was later transferred to Shu County governor but dismissed.

General Liang Shang recommended Zhou Ju as Zhonglang. When Liang Shang died, he recommended Zhou Ju as an admonishing official. Zhou Ju was sent as one of eight envoys to inspect customs, known as the "Eight Heroes." He was promoted to Dahonglu. When the Empress Dowager Liang wanted Emperor Shang's temple order below Emperor Shun's, Zhou Ju argued for proper order, and the Queen Mother agreed. He died in the third year of Jianhe, and the court mourned deeply.

## Zhou Xie's Biography

Zhou Xie, courtesy name Jusheng, liked Xuanxu studies. He resigned from office due to his father's connections. When his father's old subordinate summoned him, Zhou Xie secluded himself. He refused Liang Ji's conscriptions three times and later declined recruitment due to illness. He lived in seclusion for over ten years. In the second year of Yanxi, he opened his door to guests. Liang Ji was killed that autumn, and Zhou Xie died at year's end, aged fifty.

## Huang Qiong's Biography

Huang Qiong, courtesy name Shiying, was from Anlu, Jiangxia, son of Huang Xiang. He initially refused appointments due to illness. During Yongjian, he was recruited but refused until officials reported him as disrespectful. Li Gu wrote to him, advising him to enter politics. Huang Qiong became Yilang and was promoted to Minister of Public Affairs.

During disasters, Huang Qiong advised Emperor Shun to consult records, recruit Confucian scholars, and dismiss the unworthy. He recommended several scholars. During a severe drought, he advised examining political measures, practicing frugality, and releasing prisoners. The emperor accepted his memorial.

Huang Qiong petitioned to resume the emperor's plowing ceremony, which had been abandoned. He argued it was a grand national ceremony and cited historical examples. The emperor accepted. He was promoted to minister and proposed increasing filial piety subjects. He defended the reexamination method for officials against Zhang Sheng's proposal to abolish it.

During Emperor Huan's reign, when the emperor wanted to commend Liang Ji, Huang Qiong argued that Liang Ji's rewards should be comparable to Deng Yu and Huo Guang, not the Duke of Zhou. Liang Ji held a grudge. Huang Qiong was dismissed during an earthquake but later reappointed. He refused to appoint those Liang Ji recommended.

After Liang Ji was killed, Huang Qiong became Taiwei and was granted a marquisate, which he repeatedly declined. When five princes took power, Huang Qiong excused himself due to illness. He was dismissed several times due to disasters. In his final illness, he submitted a memorial criticizing the court for eunuch power, corruption, and the deaths of loyal officials like Li Gu and Du Qiao. He died at seventy-nine, posthumously titled Zhonghou.
