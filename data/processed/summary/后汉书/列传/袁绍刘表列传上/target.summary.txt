Yuan Shao, courtesy name Benchu, was from Ruyang, Runan, and the grandson of Situ Yuan Tang. His father Yuan Cheng served as a lieutenant general. Yuan Shao began his career as a Langguan and later became Chief of Puyang. After his mother's death, he resigned to observe mourning, and later also mourned his father. He then moved to Luoyang, where his elegant demeanor and generosity attracted many scholars, leading to resentment from court eunuchs. His uncle Yuan Wei reprimanded him, but Yuan Shao did not change his ways.

Yuan Shao served under General He Jin as a subordinate officer and later as Colonel of the Eight Xiuyuan. After Emperor Ling's death, he advised He Jin to eliminate the eunuchs, but He Jin was killed. When Dong Zhuo arrived, Yuan Shao opposed his plan to depose the young emperor. After a heated argument, Yuan Shao fled to Jizhou. Dong Zhuo initially offered a reward for his capture but later pardoned him and appointed him Governor of Bohai and Marquis of Yunxiang.

In the first year of Chuping, Yuan Shao raised troops in Bohai, joining forces with Yuan Shu, Han Fu, and others to crusade against Dong Zhuo. They formed an alliance with Yuan Shao as leader. Dong Zhuo killed Yuan Shao's uncle and clan members in the capital. Yuan Shao then took control of Jizhou from Han Fu through a combination of military pressure and persuasion, becoming its governor. He appointed Ju Shou as his advisor and expanded his territory.

Yuan Shao defeated Gongsun Zan in battle, killing Yan Gang and pursuing him to the boundary bridge. Despite a near-capture by Gongsun Zan's cavalry, Yuan Shao's forces prevailed. He later suppressed rebellions in Wei County and defeated the Black Mountain bandits, killing tens of thousands. Yuan Shao also killed his general Qu Yi for insubordination.

When Emperor Xian was in danger, Ju Shou advised Yuan Shao to welcome the emperor, but Yuan Shao hesitated due to advice from Guo Tu and Chunyu Qiong. He eventually declined. Yuan Shao had three sons: Yuan Tan, Yuan Xi, and Yuan Shang. He favored Yuan Shang due to his wife's influence, adopting Yuan Tan as his brother's heir and sending him to Qingzhou, against Ju Shou's warnings.

In the first year of Jian'an, Cao Cao welcomed the emperor to Xu County and blamed Yuan Shao for not aiding the emperor. Yuan Shao responded with a lengthy letter defending his actions, citing his loyalty and efforts against Dong Zhuo and others. The court appointed him Taiwei and Marquis of Ye, but he refused until Cao Cao yielded the position of General. Later, he was granted command of four provinces.

Yuan Shao attacked Gongsun Zan again, pacifying Youzhou. With control of four provinces and hundreds of thousands of troops, he grew arrogant. He planned to attack Xu County, ignoring Ju Shou's advice to rest and farm. Instead, he followed Guo Tu and Shen Pei's counsel to attack immediately. Tian Feng's warnings led to his imprisonment.

In the fifth year of Jian'an, Yuan Shao marched south after Liu Bei's defection. He issued a proclamation condemning Cao Cao's crimes. His forces initially succeeded but were defeated at Guandu after Xu You defected and revealed the location of Yuan Shao's grain supplies at Wuchao. Cao Cao attacked and destroyed the supplies, leading to Yuan Shao's defeat. Yuan Shao fled with 800 cavalry, while many of his troops were killed or surrendered. Ju Shou was captured and executed after attempting to return to Yuan Shao.

Yuan Shao appeared generous but was proud and unable to accept advice, leading to his downfall. After his defeat, he killed Tian Feng. He fell ill and died in the seventh year of Jian'an. His succession was disputed: Shen Pei and Feng Ji supported Yuan Shang, while others favored Yuan Tan, leading to internal conflict.
