**Summary of Historical Text on Classical Scholars**

During the reigns of Wang Mang and Gengshi, the world fell into chaos, causing the collapse of rituals and music and the loss of documents. Emperor Guangwu, who favored classical studies, gathered scholars and restored texts. Scholars from across the land brought their books to the capital. The state established fourteen doctors for the Five Classics, each teaching according to their family methods. In the fifth year of Jianwu, the Imperial Academy was built, and later, structures like Piyong, Mingtang, and Lingtai were erected. Emperor Ming personally performed rituals, lectured on classics, and established separate schools for descendants of meritorious officials. The Xiongnu even sent their children to study. During Emperor Zhang’s reign, Confucians gathered at the White Tiger Hall to discuss texts, resulting in the "White Tiger General Meaning." However, under Empress Deng’s regency, scholarly activity waned. Emperor An neglected arts and literature, leading to the decay of school buildings. Emperor Shun rebuilt the academy, adding 240 rooms and 1,850 houses, and recruited students. By the time of Empress Dowager Liang, the number of students exceeded 30,000, but scholarly rigor declined. After the execution of party members, corruption and textual alterations spread. In the fourth year of Xiping, Emperor Ling ordered the Five Classics to be engraved on stone tablets in multiple scripts. When Dong Zhuo moved the capital, many texts were lost or destroyed, with only seventy carts remaining after transport to Chang'an, half of which were lost. Later, all were burned in the chaos of Chang'an.

The text records notable scholars. Liu Kun, a descendant of King Xiao of Liang, studied rituals and taught over 500 disciples during Wang Mang’s time. He performed ceremonies with great care, but Wang Mang arrested him for private gatherings. After Wang Mang’s failure, Liu Kun was spared. He later served as a county magistrate, where his prayers stopped fires and his benevolent governance caused tigers to leave. Emperor Guangwu appointed him as a tutor for the crown prince. Wa Dan, from Nanyang, taught the "Mencius Yi" and refused office under Wang Mang. He became a doctor under Guangwu and wrote seven chapters of "Yi Tong Lun." Ren An, from Guanghan, studied the "Mencius Yi" and mastered several classics, but refused official appointments due to illness. Yang Zheng, from Jingzhao, studied "Liang Shiyi" and was known for his eloquent explanations. He once pleaded for Fan Sheng’s release by appearing before the emperor, enduring wounds. He was known for his boldness and friendship with powerful figures. Zhang Xing, from Yingchuan, taught "Liangqiu Yi" and had nearly ten thousand disciples. Dai Ping, from Runan, studied "Jing's Book of Changes" and debated with scholars, earning a reputation for his expertise. He served as a minister and was known for his outspokenness. Sun Qi, from Jiyin, studied "Jingshi Yi" and "Guwen Shangshu," supporting his mother by raising pigs. His virtue inspired others, and even Yellow Turban bandits spared his home.

The text also covers scholars of the "Shangshu." Ouyang She, from Le'an, inherited the family tradition of teaching "Fu Sheng Shangshu" and served as a governor. He was imprisoned for corruption, but his student Lizhen pleaded to die in his place. Mou Chang, from Le'an, taught "Ouyang Shangshu" to over a thousand students and wrote "Mou Shi Zhang Ju." Song Deng, from Chang'an, taught "Ouyang Shangshu" and served as a prefect, known for his honest governance. Zhang Xun, from Jiyin, taught "Da Xia Hou Shangshu" and served as a minister. Yin Min, from Nanyang, studied various classics and corrected prophecies, but fell out of favor with the emperor. Zhou Fang, from Runan, studied "Guwen Shangshu" and wrote "Shangshu Miscellaneous Notes." Kong Xi, from Lu, inherited the tradition of "Guwen Shangshu" and "Mao Shi." He was accused of slandering Emperor Wu but defended himself in a letter, and the emperor appointed him as Lantai Order. Yang Lun, from Chenliu, studied "Guwen Shangshu" and taught over a thousand disciples. He criticized corruption and was dismissed but later pardoned. He refused further appointments and died at home.

The text concludes by noting that during Guangwu’s reign, scholars like Mou Rong, Wang Liang, and Huan Rong taught various versions of the "Shangshu." Huan Rong’s teachings flourished in Tokyo, while Du Lin’s "Gu Wen Shangshu" became prominent through the works of Jia Kui, Ma Rong, and Zheng Xuan.
