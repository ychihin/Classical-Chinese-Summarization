# Summary of Historical Biographies

## Zhang Chun's Biography

Zhang Chun, courtesy name Boren, was from Duling in Jingzhao. His great-great-grandfather Zhang Anshi served as General Sima Wei under Emperor Xuan and was granted the title of Marquis Fuping. His father Zhang Fang served Emperor Cheng. Zhang Chun inherited his father's title young and served as minister under Emperors Ai and Ping, and continued under Wang Mang. When Wang Mang usurped the throne, Zhang Chun retained his title due to his honesty and compliance with law.

In the early Jianwu years, he became a doctor in Taizhong and led troops to farm in Nanyang. Emperor Guangwu refused to abolish his position, renaming him Wu Shihou with half of Fuping County's salary. Zhang Chun was well-versed in old affairs and decided many rituals including sacrifices, weddings, and funerals. The emperor appointed him Huben Zhonglang General, introducing him sometimes four times daily.

Zhang Chun and Zhu Fu memorialized about ancestral temple matters, arguing for proper ritual order. They proposed abolishing the current Qin Temple and following old scriptures. The emperor consulted ministers who recommended replacing the ancestral temple with five emperors and four generations. The emperor complied. Zhang Chun later became imperial servant, then Great Sikong. He opened the Yang Canal for water transportation, benefiting the people. He established regulations for sacrificial ceremonies every three and five years. He proposed building schools and discussed establishing Piyong and Mingtang. In the 30th year, he submitted a memorial about holding a Zen ceremony. In the first Zhongyuan year, the emperor visited Mount Tai with Zhang Chun, who died in March and received the posthumous title Jiehou.

## Cao Bao's Biography

Cao Bao, courtesy name Shutong, was from Xue in Lu State. His father Cao Chong studied the "Qingshi Rites" and became a doctor during Jianwu, establishing various rituals. After Xianzong ascended, Cao Chong discussed ritual and music matters with the emperor, who appreciated his views and appointed him as servant.

Cao Bao was ambitious and generous, inheriting his father's studies at twenty. He was knowledgeable and fond of etiquette, often feeling the imperial system was incomplete. He studied morning and night, even forgetting directions while walking. He was first promoted to Xiaolian, then magistrate of Yuxian County, governing with etiquette. When five thieves were captured, he refused to kill them despite orders, saving their lives. He was dismissed for this but later recruited as a doctor.

When Suzong wanted to formulate rituals and music, Cao Bao presented a memorial. The emperor appointed him as doctor. Later, the emperor ordered him to work on "Han Yi" in Nangong and Dongguan. Cao Bao wrote 150 articles on ritual systems from emperor to commoners. After the emperor died, Cao Bao was promoted to various positions. Some officials complained about his "Han Rites," which was not implemented. He bought land to bury unclaimed coffins, visited patients during epidemics, and saved many lives. He became governor of Hanoi, where rain fell during drought and harvests were abundant. He died in office in fourteen years, having written numerous works including "Tongyi" and "Exemplary Classics," teaching over a thousand students.

## Zheng Xuan's Biography

Zheng Xuan, courtesy name Kangcheng, was from Gaomi in Beihai. His eighth-generation ancestor Zheng Chong was minister of public affairs under Emperor Ai. As a boy, Zheng Xuan worked as a small official but preferred studying. He studied at Taixue in Beijing under various teachers, becoming proficient in multiple classics. He later studied under Ma Rong, who had over 400 students. Zheng Xuan studied diligently for three years before meeting Ma Rong personally, asking difficult questions before returning home.

After more than ten years of study, he returned to farm in Donglai with hundreds of students. During the party imprisonment disaster, he was imprisoned but continued studying. He refuted He Xiu's theories, impressing He Xiu. When the party ban was lifted, General He Jin recruited him, but Zheng Xuan escaped after one night. At sixty, he had thousands of students. He declined various official positions.

During the Yellow Turban uprising, he took refuge in Xuzhou. In the first Jian'an year, he returned to Gaomi, encountering Yellow Turban troops who bowed and agreed not to enter the county. When seriously ill, he wrote to his son about his life, studies, and hopes for his son's cultivation. Yuan Shao invited him as a guest, where he impressed everyone with his knowledge. He declined Yuan Shao's recommendation as Zuo Zhonglang General. The imperial court conscripted him as chief minister of agriculture, but he requested to return home due to illness. Forced by Yuan Shao's son to join the army, he died at seventy-four in Yuancheng County, buried simply with over a thousand mourners.

His students compiled his answers to questions into "Zheng Zhi." He annotated numerous classics including "The Book of Changes," "Shangshu," "Mao Shi," and "The Analects," writing over one million words. He was respected as a master in Qilu, and his students became famous in their regions.
