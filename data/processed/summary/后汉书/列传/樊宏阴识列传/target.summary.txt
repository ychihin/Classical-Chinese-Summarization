The text summarizes the biographies of Fan Hong and Yin Shi, two figures from the early Eastern Han dynasty.

Fan Hong, a native of Huyang, Nanyang, was the uncle of Emperor Guangwu (Shizu). His ancestor was granted the title of Fan. His father, Fan Zhong, was a kind, law-abiding farmer and businessman who expanded the family's wealth to over 300 hectares of land, building grand estates with ponds and canals. He provided relief to his clan and village, and once settled a property dispute among his grandsons by giving them two hectares of land. Upon his death at eighty, his son Fan Hong burned the deeds of debtors, refusing repayment. During Wang Mang's reign, rebels attacked Huyang, and Fan Hong's wife was captured, but she was spared due to the family's reputation for kindness. Fan Hong declined a generalship under Gengshi, instead building a defensive camp that sheltered over a thousand people. When Red Eyebrow bandits threatened, he sent them gifts, and they spared him due to his benevolence. Under Emperor Guangwu, he became a high-ranking official, was enfeoffed as Changluohou, and later as Hou Zhang. He was known for his modesty, caution, and advice to his son against excessive wealth. He often arrived early at court and destroyed drafts of his memorials. On his deathbed, he requested a simple burial, which the emperor honored, granting him the posthumous title of Marquis Gong.

Yin Shi, a native of Xinye, Nanyang, was the brother of Empress Guanglie. His ancestor was Guan Zhong, whose descendant changed his surname to Yin. During Liu Bo's uprising, Yin Shi abandoned his studies in Chang'an to join him, leading over a thousand followers. He became a captain and later a general under Gengshi, conquering five counties. Under Emperor Guangwu, he was made a Cavalry Captain and Marquis of Yin Township. He humbly declined additional titles, stating the world was newly settled. He served as a captain and later returned home for his mother's funeral. He was enfeoffed as Luhou and served as a guardian to the crown prince, often staying to guard the capital during the emperor's travels. He was discreet about state affairs and employed virtuous scribes. Upon Emperor Ming's ascension, he became Zhijinwu. He died in the second year of Yongping, receiving the posthumous title of Zhenhou.
