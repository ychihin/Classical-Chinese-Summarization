Zhu Fu, courtesy name Shu Yuan, was from Xiao, Pei State. He initially served under Guangwu, followed him to break Handan, and was later appointed General Youzhou Mu to guard Jicheng and settle the northern border. In the second year of Jianwu, he was made Marquis of Wuyang. Zhu Fu sought to revive his reputation by recruiting famous scholars and former officials of Wang Mang, which angered Peng Chong, the prefect of Yuyang, who refused to comply. Zhu Fu slandered Peng Chong, leading to resentment. Zhu Fu reported Peng Chong for various offenses, and Peng Chong, enraged, raised troops to attack Zhu Fu. Zhu Fu reproached Peng Chong in a letter, criticizing his rebellion and comparing his merits to a Liaodong pig. Peng Chong attacked more fiercely, and despite Zhu Fu’s pleas for imperial aid, the court only sent Deng Long to assist. Zhu Fu fled after his city fell, and his wife was killed. The emperor replaced him as Jinwu and later as Marquis of Chengdu. After Peng Chong and Zhang Feng failed, Zhu Fu wrote a memorial criticizing the emperor’s harsh governance, advocating for slower, more careful administration. The emperor agreed, reducing mobilizations. Zhu Fu later served as a servant and proposed expanding the selection of doctors for the Imperial Academy, which the emperor approved. In the Yongping period, Zhu Fu was sentenced to death on false accusations, but Fan Wei interceded, and the emperor repented. The historian noted that Zhu Fu’s criticism of haste led to his death.

Feng Li, courtesy name Xiaosun, was from Huyang, Nanyang. During Wang Mang’s reign, he gathered guests and built a camp. He once saved Shentu Ji from Yu Duwei’s cousin, earning respect. Guangwu summoned him in the third year of Jianwu, making him magistrate of Yu County, where he governed with prestige. Later, as magistrate of Jiaxian, he fought bandits led by Yan Bao, who surrendered after Guangwu’s arrival. Feng Li pardoned them, and they served as informants, pacifying the county. He later served as prefect of Wei County and was appointed imperial servant of Zhao. In the Zhongyuan period, he became Sikong and Marquis of Guannei. After Guangwu’s death, he built the original mausoleum. In Yongping, he was dismissed for scrutinizing Deng Rong but later resumed duties. He served as King Wu of Yin Song, giving loyal advice. He retired due to old age in Jianchu and died at eighty-six.

Yu Yan, courtesy name Zi Da, was from Chenliu. Born with an auspicious sign, he was strong and tall. As a youth, he arrested a noble’s guests, earning resentment. During Wang Mang’s chaos, he defended his relatives. He adopted his cousin’s abandoned infant. As magistrate of Xiyang County, he let prisoners go home for the new year; they returned as promised. He resigned after advising Prefect Fu Zong against extravagance; Fu Zong was later executed. Guangwu, impressed, appointed Yu Yan as governor. He served as Luoyang magistrate, punishing the Yin family’s guest Ma Cheng despite pleas. He later became Nanyang governor. In Yongping, he recognized Deng Yan’s lack of manners, leading to Deng Yan’s shame. Yu Yan served as Taiwei and Situ, but when Liu Ying of Chu rebelled, Yu Yan failed to report contacts, leading to his suicide. His family was left poor.

Zheng Hong, from Kuaiji, was a misfit as a youth but was recognized by Prefect Wu Lun. He studied under Jiao Kuang, who was implicated in Liu Ying’s rebellion. When Jiao Kuang died, Zheng Hong shaved his head and pleaded for his family, gaining fame. As magistrate of Zuxian, he governed benevolently. He became Shangshu Ling and proposed that Shangshu Lang supplement Thousand Stone Orders, which the emperor accepted. As chief agricultural officer, he opened mountain roads in Lingling and Guiyang, saving three trillion in assets. He reduced contributions and corvee fees during drought. As Taiwei, he deferred to the Fifth Lun, and the emperor set up a screen between them. He reported Zhang Lin and Yang Guang for corruption, but Dou Xian accused him of leaking secrets. Zheng Hong resigned and died, leaving instructions for a simple burial.

Zhou Zhang, from Nanyang, was a meritorious official. He advised the prefect against privately visiting Dou Xian, cutting the horse’s leather to stop him. When Dou Xian was executed, the prefect was spared. Zhou Zhang became Guang Luxun and later Sikong. He criticized Zheng Zhong and Cai Lun’s power. After Emperor He’s death, Empress Dowager Deng made Liu Sheng king of Pingyuan and later Emperor An. Zhou Zhang plotted to kill Deng Zhi, Zheng Zhong, and Cai Lun, depose the queen, and make Liu Sheng emperor. The plot was discovered, and Zhou Zhang committed suicide, leaving no wealth. The historian warned against such usurpation, citing Mencius.
