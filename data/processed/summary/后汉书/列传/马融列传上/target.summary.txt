Ma Rong, courtesy name Ji Chang, was born in Maoling, Fufeng, as the son of Ma Yan. He was handsome, talented, and eloquent. He studied under the recluse Zhixun in Nanshan, mastering the classics, and Zhixun married his daughter to him. In the second year of Yongchu, General Deng Zhi summoned him, but Ma Rong refused and lived as a guest in Liangzhou. During a Qiang rebellion, famine struck, and Ma Rong, facing hardship, regretted his refusal, citing the value of life from Laozi and Zhuangzi, and accepted Deng Zhi's call. In the fourth year, he became a calligrapher at Dongguan Dian School, collecting secret books.

During Empress Dowager Deng's regency, Confucian scholars advocated abolishing martial arts, leading to widespread banditry. Ma Rong disapproved, arguing that civil and military affairs were both essential. In the second year of Yuanchu, he wrote "Guangcheng Ode" to satirize this policy, suggesting that the emperor should engage in hunting and martial training to promote harmony. This offended Deng, and he was confined to Dongguan for ten years without promotion. After his nephew's death, he resigned and returned home, angering the Empress Dowager, who banned him from office. After her death, Emperor An recalled him, and he served as chief of the stables for the King of Hejian. He wrote "Ode to the Eastern Tour" for the emperor's pilgrimage to Mount Tai, earning a promotion to doctor.

Later, as governor of Wudu during a Xiqiang rebellion, Ma Rong proposed a swift military campaign to defeat the rebels, but his advice was ignored. He accurately predicted the subsequent invasions. Under Emperor Huan, he became prefect of Nanjun but was framed by General Liang Ji, exiled to Shuofang, and later pardoned. He returned to writing at Dongguan before retiring due to illness.

Ma Rong was a renowned scholar with thousands of students, including Lu Zhi and Zheng Xuan. He was skilled in music, lived luxuriously, and taught with a red gauze curtain, separating male and female students. He annotated many classics, including the "Classic of Filial Piety," "Analects," and "Book of Songs." However, he was criticized for drafting a memorial for Liang Ji to frame Li Gu. He died at home at age 88, with a simple burial. The historian noted his initial integrity but later indulgence in luxury, reflecting the tension between valuing life and maintaining moral principles.
