Ban Biao, courtesy name Shupi, was born in Anling, Fufeng. His grandfather Ban Kuang served as a captain of the Yue Cavalry School, and his father Ban Zhi was prefect of Guangping. Ban Biao had a serious personality and valued traditional culture. In his twenties, during the chaos following Gengshi's defeat, he followed Wei Xiao to Tianshui. When Wei Xiao asked whether the Warring States period's vertical and horizontal alliances would recur or if a single ruler would unify the realm, Ban Biao explained that the Zhou Dynasty's feudal system led to weak central control and allowed such alliances, while the Han Dynasty's prefecture and county system centralized power. He argued that the Han's decline stemmed from above, not below, and that after Wang Mang's usurpation, people longed for the Han, leading to widespread uprisings under the Liu family banner. Wei Xiao disagreed, citing the Qin collapse as an unpredictable event. Ban Biao, disturbed by Wei Xiao's arrogance, wrote "On the King's Mandate" to argue that Han virtue succeeded Tang Yao, but Wei Xiao remained unmoved, so Ban Biao fled to Hexi.

There, Dou Rong employed him as an official, treating him with great respect. Ban Biao advised Dou Rong to serve the Han and resist Wei Xiao. When Dou Rong returned to the capital, Emperor Guangwu learned of Ban Biao's role and summoned him, promoting him to Secretary Maocai and appointing him as Order of Xuzhou, though illness prevented him from taking the post. Ban Biao later served under the Three Dukes.

Ban Biao was talented in writing and focused on historical records. After Sima Qian's "Historical Records," no comprehensive history existed for the period after Taichu. Ban Biao collected historical materials and wrote dozens of later biographies, critiquing their merits and flaws. He noted that various states had their own histories, such as "Mu Shou Chu," "Cheng," and "Spring and Autumn." He praised Sima Qian's narrative skill but criticized his bias toward Huang-Lao thought, neglect of the Five Classics, and superficial discussions. Ban Biao emphasized the need for strict factual verification and stylistic consistency in history writing.

Ban Biao also advised on the selection of tutors for princes, citing historical examples like King Cheng of Zhou and the Han's use of scholars like Chao Cuo and Jia Yi. He recommended choosing prestigious Confucian scholars as tutors and ensuring proper staffing for the Eastern Palace and other kingdoms. The emperor accepted his advice. Ban Biao later served as commander of the capital under Situ Lian, beloved by officials and people. He died in office at age 52, leaving nine works including fu, treatises, and memorials.
