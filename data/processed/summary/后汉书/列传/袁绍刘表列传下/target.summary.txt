Liu Biao, courtesy name Jingsheng, was a descendant of King Lu Gong from Gaoping, Shanyang. Over eight feet tall with a gentle appearance and burly build, he was implicated in the "Ba Gu" party affair and escaped arrest. After the party ban was lifted, he served as an assistant to General He Jin. In the first year of Chuping, he was appointed Governor of Jingzhou following Sun Jian's murder of Governor Wang Xu. Unable to reach Jingzhou due to bandits and Yuan Shu's troops blocking the road, Liu Biao rode alone to Yicheng and consulted Kuai Yue and Cai Mao. Following Kuai Yue's advice, he lured and killed fifteen clan bandit leaders, then pacified their tribes. The Jiangxia thieves Zhang Hu and Chen Li surrendered after negotiations, and the region south of the Yangtze River was subdued. Liu Biao organized troops to observe the situation.

Yuan Shu and Sun Jian attacked Liu Biao, but Sun Jian was killed by a stray arrow during the siege of Xiangyang. Liu Biao sent tribute to Emperor Xian and was appointed General of Zhennan, Shepherd of Jingzhou, and Marquis of Wu. When Zhang Ji attacked Lucheng and was killed, Liu Biao accepted condolences and recruited Zhang Ji's troops. He suppressed rebellions by Zhang Xian and others, expanding his territory to connect with Wuling in the south and Han in the north, commanding over 100,000 armored warriors. Liu Biao pacified bandits, supported scholars, established schools, and commissioned commentaries on the Five Classics.

During Cao Cao and Yuan Shao's conflict at Guandu, Liu Biao remained neutral despite advice from Han Song and Kuai Yue to submit to Cao Cao. He sent Han Song to observe Cao Cao, but after Han Song praised Cao Cao and urged submission, Liu Biao imprisoned him. Liu Bei defected to Jingzhou in the sixth year of Jian'an, but Liu Biao did not utilize him. In the thirteenth year, Cao Cao marched south, and Liu Biao died of illness in August after nearly twenty years in Jingzhou.

Liu Biao favored his son Liu Qi initially but later favored Liu Cong due to his wife Cai's influence. Liu Qi, feeling unsafe, sought advice from Zhuge Liang and requested to govern Jiangxia. When Liu Biao fell ill, Liu Qi was prevented from visiting him, and Liu Cong succeeded. Liu Cong surrendered Jingzhou to Cao Cao, who appointed him Governor of Qingzhou and granted marquisates to others. Cao Cao later released Han Song and appointed him Da Honglu. Liu Biao's alliance with Yuan Shao was criticized by Deng Yi, who resigned. Cao Cao was defeated at Chibi, and Liu Bei recommended Liu Qi as Jingzhou's historian; Liu Qi died the following year. The text concludes that Liu Biao's indecision and favoritism led to his downfall.
