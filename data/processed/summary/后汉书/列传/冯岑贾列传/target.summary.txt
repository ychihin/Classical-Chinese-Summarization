Feng Yi, also known as Gongsun, was from Fucheng County, Yingchuan. He was well-read in the Zuo's Spring and Autumn Annals and Sun Tzu's Art of War. When the Han army rose, he served as a county magistrate and defended the city for Wang Mang alongside Miao Meng. Guangwu (Liu Xiu) captured Yingchuan but could not take Fucheng. Feng Yi was captured by Han soldiers while traveling. His cousin Feng Xiao and others recommended him to Guangwu. Feng Yi offered to return to the city and secure five counties in exchange for service. He persuaded Miao Meng to surrender to Guangwu, noting that Guangwu did not plunder like other generals. When Guangwu passed by, Feng Yi and Miao Meng opened the gates and welcomed him with gifts. Guangwu appointed Feng Yi as a principal, and Feng Yi recommended several others for positions.

Feng Yi advised Guangwu to build ties with Cao Xu, which proved helpful when Guangwu crossed the Yellow River northward. After Guangwu's brother Bosheng was killed, Feng Yi comforted him and urged him to act independently, perform kindness, and win over the people. When Wang Lang rebelled, Guangwu fled south, and Feng Yi provided him with bean porridge and other food during the harsh journey. Feng Yi was later made a partial general and granted the title of Marquis Ying after defeating Wang Lang. He was known for his humility, often hiding under a tree during discussions of merit, earning the nickname "General Big Tree." His troops were loyal to him, and Guangwu valued him highly.

Feng Yi wrote to Li Yi, a general under Gengshi, urging him to surrender by citing historical examples of those who changed allegiances. Li Yi, though hesitant due to past conflicts with Guangwu, agreed to stop fighting. This allowed Feng Yi to pacify several counties. Guangwu later revealed Li Yi's letter to Zhu Kun, who assassinated Li Yi in anger. Zhu Kun then attacked Feng Yi but was defeated. After Gengshi's fall, Feng Yi advised Guangwu to ascend the throne, citing a dream as divine will. In the second year of Jianwu, Feng Yi was made Marquis of Yangxia and sent to pacify the three auxiliary regions, where he defeated Chimei and other bandits.

Feng Yi faced Chimei in Huayin, fighting for over sixty days. Despite initial setbacks, he used an ambush strategy, dressing his troops like Chimei to cause confusion, and defeated them, forcing 80,000 to surrender. He then pacified Guanzhong, defeating various bandit leaders like Yancen and Zhang Han. Feng Yi governed with justice, recruiting people and redressing grievances. When rumors arose that he was acting arrogantly, Guangwu showed him the accusations, and Feng Yi wrote a humble apology, reaffirming his loyalty. Guangwu reassured him of their bond.

In the sixth year of Jianwu, Feng Yi visited the capital and was honored by Guangwu, who recalled his past aid. Feng Yi requested that Guangwu remember their shared hardships. He later defended the city of Xun against Wei Xiao's forces, using a surprise attack to defeat them. He continued to pacify the north, defeating Lu Fang and the Xiongnu. In the ninth year, he took command of Ji Zun's troops and fought against Wei Xiao's son and Gongsun Shu's forces. Feng Yi fell ill and died in the army, receiving the posthumous title of Jiehou. His descendants inherited his title, though some were later revoked and restored.

Cen Peng, courtesy name Junran, was from Jiyang County, Nanyang. During Wang Mang's reign, he served as a county magistrate. After the Han army rose, he was captured but later fled to Wan, where he defended the city with Yan Shuo. When food ran out, he advocated surrender. He was spared and made a marquis, serving under various leaders. He eventually joined Guangwu, who pardoned him after he explained his past. Cen Peng persuaded others to surrender and was appointed as an assassin general, helping to pacify Hebei.

After Guangwu became emperor, Cen Peng was made a court lieutenant and marquis. He besieged Luoyang with other generals and persuaded Zhu Kun to surrender by promising safety. Zhu Kun surrendered, and Guangwu kept his word. Cen Peng then attacked Jingzhou, capturing several cities. He fought against Qin Feng and Deng Feng, eventually defeating them with Guangwu's help. Cen Peng was made Marquis of Wuyin and continued to campaign in the south, defeating Tian Rong and other rebels. He established control over Jiangnan, receiving tributes from various prefects.

In the sixth year of Jianwu, Cen Peng was summoned to the capital and honored. He later fought in the west against Wei Xiao, but the campaign stalled due to food shortages. In the ninth year, Gongsun Shu's forces attacked, and Cen Peng built warships to counter them. In the eleventh year, he led a large force to Jingmen, using fire ships to destroy the enemy's pontoon bridges. He advanced into Shu, winning battles and winning over the people. However, he was assassinated by a fake slave at his camp. He was given the posthumous title of Zhuanghou, and the Shu people built a temple in his honor. His descendants inherited his title, though some were later revoked.

Jia Fu, courtesy name Junwen, was from Champion County, Nanyang. He studied the Book of Documents and was recognized as having the potential to be a general. During Wang Mang's reign, he gathered followers and joined Liu Jia, but left to join Guangwu in Hebei. Guangwu appointed him as a general, and he proved his bravery in battle, often leading charges. He was seriously wounded but recovered, and Guangwu promised to unite their families through marriage. Jia Fu fought in many campaigns, including against Zhu Kun and Chimei, and was known for his courage, though he rarely boasted of his achievements.

After Guangwu became emperor, Jia Fu was made a marquis and continued to fight, defeating Yin Zun and others. He was known for his humility and did not claim credit. In the thirteenth year of Jianwu, he was granted the title of Marquis of Jiaodong. He later retired from military affairs to promote Confucianism, as Guangwu sought to end warfare. Jia Fu was one of the few meritorious officials allowed to discuss state affairs. He died in the thirty-first year of Jianwu, receiving the posthumous title of Ganghou. His descendants inherited his title, though some were later revoked and restored.

The historian comments that among the ZTE generals, Feng Yi and Cen Peng achieved the greatest feats, particularly in the west and south. Feng Yi and Jia Fu were humble and did not take credit, while Cen Peng's loyalty inspired his troops. The text notes that Feng Yi avoided disaster by leaving a place with an ominous name, while Cen Peng was unable to do so and met his end, suggesting that fate may play a role in such matters.
