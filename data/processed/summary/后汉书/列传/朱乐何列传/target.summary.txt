# Summary of Historical Biographies

**Zhu Hui and Zhu Mu**

Zhu Hui, courtesy name Wen Ji, was from Wan, Nanyang. His father died when he was young. At thirteen, during the chaos of Wang Mang's fall, he defended his grandmother's family from bandits with a sword, earning their respect. Emperor Guangwu, having studied with Zhu Hui's father, summoned Zhu Hui as a minister after ascending the throne. Zhu Hui soon resigned due to illness and studied at Taixue. He was known for his reserved temperament and strict observance of etiquette.

When Xinyang Houyin admired Zhu Hui and sent gifts, Zhu Hui refused to see him. As a county official, he refused to sell a maid to Prefect Ruan Kuang but later sent generous gifts after Ruan's death. Liu Cang, the Hussar General, treated him with respect. During a New Year ceremony, when jade could not be found, Zhu Hui cleverly obtained it from a chief clerk and delivered it to Liu Cang. The emperor, impressed by his bravery, appointed him as Guard Order and later Prefect of Linhuai.

Zhu Hui enforced laws strictly, seeking justice for the wronged and punishing the guilty. Officials feared him, singing: "If you are strong, you will succeed, Zhu Ji of Nanyang." He was later removed from office for breaking the law. During a famine, he distributed his property to the poor. He also secretly provided for Zhang Kan's widow after Zhang's death, fulfilling a promise. He recommended Chen You over his own son for an official position.

During the Yuanhe period, Suzong summoned Zhu Hui as a minister. He later became Taishan Prefect but requested to remain in the capital. He wrote memorials on political matters, which were praised. When grain prices rose, Zhu Hui opposed Zhang Lin's proposal to control salt and currency, arguing it would harm the people. The emperor initially rejected his advice, and Zhu Hui asked to be imprisoned. After being pardoned, he claimed illness and refused to participate in politics. He died soon after.

Zhu Mu, courtesy name Gongshu, was known for filial piety at age five. He studied diligently, sometimes forgetting his appearance. During Emperor Shun's reign, he was recommended to General Liang Ji to suppress thieves. When Emperor Huan ascended, Zhu Mu advised Liang Ji to support the royal family, warning of disasters and recommending virtuous officials. Liang Ji partially heeded his advice.

As Imperial Censor, Zhu Mu wrote essays criticizing contemporary customs. He admonished Liang Ji about his arrogance and corruption, warning of potential rebellion. Liang Ji ignored him. During a flood, Zhu Mu became Governor of Jizhou, where he impeached corrupt officials and executed bandit leaders. He even exhumed eunuch Zhao Zhong's father's tomb for illegal burial items. The emperor imprisoned him, but scholars petitioned for his release.

Zhu Mu later served as Minister, deeply hating eunuchs. He petitioned to dismiss them and restore old Han regulations, but the emperor refused. Zhu Mu fell to the ground in protest. After repeated slander from eunuchs, he returned home and died of illness at sixty-four. He lived frugally, leaving no wealth. The court posthumously honored him.

**Le Hui**

Le Hui, courtesy name Boqi, was from Changling, Jingzhao. At eleven, he cried daily at the county magistrate's gate to save his father from execution, succeeding. He studied under Dr. Jiao Yong and became a renowned Confucian. He refused gifts from Xinyang Houyin. As a minor official, he alone attended his prefect's funeral after the prefect was executed. He promoted his slanderer's son as a filial candidate.

Le Hui served as a minister and remonstrated against Dou Xian's expedition against the Xiongnu. He impeached officials connected to Dou Xian. When Dou Xian's brother sought his friendship, Le Hui refused. He wrote memorials warning against relatives holding power. When his advice was ignored, he claimed illness and retired. Dou Xian persecuted him, and Le Hui poisoned himself.

**He Chang**

He Chang was from Pingling, Fufeng. His ancestor Bigan studied Shangshu and served as Tingwei during Emperor Wu's reign. He Chang served under Taiwei Song You, offering high-level discussions. He warned against treating strange birds and plants as auspicious signs. He advised Song You to reduce excessive rewards and relieve the poor during floods and droughts.

When Liu Chang was murdered by Dou Xian's agents, He Chang investigated despite opposition, earning praise. As Imperial Censor, he remonstrated against Dou Xian's military campaigns and his brothers' extravagant building projects. He later became Taifu of Jinan, assisting King Liu Kang with moral principles. As Prefect of Runan, he governed with leniency, repaired irrigation canals, and increased cultivated land. After the Dou family fell, he was dismissed due to his son's friendship with them. He later served as Wugan Zhonglang General but was falsely accused by Cai Lun and died at home.
