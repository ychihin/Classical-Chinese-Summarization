# Summary of the Biography of Dang Guo and Related Figures

Confucius stated that human natures are similar but habits diverge greatly, meaning education and environment shape conduct. Virtuous people teach others to cultivate character, overcome dissolute behavior, and be cautious in making friends. During times of chaos, customs became superficial, and some used benevolence and loyalty as tools for personal gain. By Emperor Gaozu's rise, martial advocates emerged, inheriting the style of the four lords—Xinling, Pingyuan, Chunshen, and Mengchang—where people valued loyalty and vengeance. Under Emperor Wu, Confucianism flourished, with scholars debating the Six Arts at Shiqu Pavilion, forming factions based on agreement or disagreement.

When Wang Mang usurped the Han throne, loyal scholars refused to serve him, preferring poverty and seclusion. Although Guangwu revived the Han, the issue of serving in office remained critical. During Emperors Huan and Ling's reigns, eunuchs controlled the government, and scholars considered it shameful to associate with them. Commoners became indignant, and upright scholars freely discussed governance, praising each other and criticizing ministers. This righteous atmosphere spread widely, but eventually led to the disaster of the Party禁锢, as seen with Fan Pang and Zhang Jian.

The party incident began when Emperor Huan, formerly Marquis of Liwu, studied under Zhou Fu and later promoted him. Yin Fangzhi of Henan was also famous, and their guests ridiculed each other, forming factions that divided Ganling into northern and southern parts. Later, Prefect Zongzi of Runan appointed Fan Pang, and Prefect Cheng Jin of Nanyang appointed Cen Ri. Ballads spread to the Imperial College, where over 30,000 students followed leaders Guo Linzong and Jia Weijie, respecting Li Ying, Chen Fan, and Wang Chang. Rumors praised Li Yuanli as a model, Chen Zhongju as fearless, and Wang Shumao as outstanding. Others like the Bohai clan and Fufeng Wei Qiqing dared to speak against the powerful.

Zhang Cheng of Hanoi, skilled in fortune-telling, predicted an amnesty and had his son kill someone. Li Ying, as Henan Yin, arrested and executed Zhang Cheng despite the amnesty. Zhang Cheng's disciple falsely accused Li Ying and others of forming cliques, slandering the court. The emperor arrested over 200 party members, with some escaping. The next year, officials petitioned for reconsideration, and the arrested were sent home but banned from office for life. Thereafter, upright people were abandoned, while the wicked colluded. Those pursuing high integrity praised each other, creating labels: Sanjun (Three Monarchs), Bajun (Eight Heroes), Bagu (Eight Supports), Baji (Eight Guides), and Bachu (Eight Assistants). Dou Wu, Liu Shu, and Chen Fan were Sanjun; Li Ying, Xun Yi, Du Mi, Wang Chang, Liu You, Wei Lang, Zhao Dian, and Zhu were Bajun; Guo Linzong, Zongci, Basu, Xia Fu, Fan Pang, Yin Xun, Cai Yan, and Yangzhi were Bagu; Zhang Jian, Cen Rizhi, Liu Biao, Chen Xiang, Kong Yu, Yuan Kang, Tanfu, and Zhai Chao were Baji; Du Shang, Zhang Miao, Wang Kao, Liu Ru, Hu Muban, Qin Zhou, Fan Xiang, and Wang Zhang were Bachu.

Zhu Bing, a native of Zhang Jian's hometown, wrote to accuse Zhang Jian and twenty-four others of being cronies. Emperor Ling issued an edict to arrest them, but Zhu Bing's name was omitted for secrecy. Cao Jie signaled officials to arrest former party members, and over 100 died in prison. Others escaped or died before capture. Anyone with personal grudges was classified as a clique, and six to seven hundred people were killed, exiled, or banned. In the fifth year of Xiping, Cao Luan wrote to defend party members but was beaten to death. An edict then examined party disciples, old subordinates, and relatives, banning five generations from office. In the second year of Guanghe, He Haishu argued that imprisoning five clans violated laws, and the emperor lifted all prison terms. In the first year of Zhongping, the Yellow Turban rebellion arose, and Lu Qiang warned that long imprisonment might cause party members to join the rebels. The emperor pardoned them, but the government later collapsed. The party incidents lasted over twenty years, affecting all good people in the world.

Liu Shu, from Hejian, studied the Five Classics and taught hundreds of students. He declined multiple invitations but was eventually carried to Luoyang, where he answered the emperor's questions and was appointed Yilang. He advised dismissing eunuchs, but the emperor did not adopt it. After Emperor Ling ascended, eunuchs falsely accused Liu Shu of conspiring with Dou Wu, and he committed suicide in prison.

Li Ying, from Yingchuan, was aloof and regarded Xun Shu and Chen Chen as mentors. He was recommended as Xiaolian and became governor of Qingzhou, where corrupt officials fled upon hearing of his arrival. He later served as Colonel Hu Wuhuan, defeating Xianbei invaders. As Henan Yin, he arrested and executed Zhang Cheng despite the amnesty, angering the emperor. During the party disaster, Li Ying was arrested and tortured to death at age sixty. His wife and children were exiled, and his students were banned from office.

Du Mi, from Yingchuan, was deep and simple. He served as prefect of Daijun and Taishan, arresting eunuchs' sons who committed evil acts. He discovered Zheng Xuan's talent and sent him to study. After the party broke out, he was dismissed and later committed suicide during a party summons.

Liu You, from Zhongshan, was familiar with history and skilled in writing. He exposed crimes of Liang Ji's disciple and removed corrupt officials in Hedong. As Si Li Xiaowei, he confiscated lands occupied by powerful ministers, angering Emperor Huan, who sentenced him to labor. After pardon, he returned to seclusion and died at home, spared from the party massacre.

Wei Lang, from Kuaiji, avenged his brother's murder and studied at Taixue. He served as Jiuzhen Captain, defeating thieves and beheading 2,000. As prefect of Hanoi, his governance was exemplary. During the party disaster, he was dismissed and later committed suicide at Niuzhu.

Xia Fu, from Chenliu, was upright and did not associate with wealthy families. He was framed by eunuchs as a party leader. While fleeing, he cut his beard, changed his appearance, and worked as a servant in a smelting family for years. His brother discovered him, but Xia Fu left at dawn and died before the party ban was lifted.

Zongci, from Nanyang, refused multiple government summons. He served as a military commander but abandoned his post when the prefect was corrupt. He died on the road while being summoned as Yilang.

Basu, from Bohai, conspired with Dou Wu and Chen Fan to kill eunuchs. After their failure, he was banned from office. Cao Jie arrested him, and Basu surrendered himself, saying he would not escape punishment. He was killed, and the governor carved a stone to record his deeds.

Fan Pang, from Runan, was appointed envoy to investigate thieves in Jizhou. He exposed corrupt officials and was praised for his integrity. During the party disaster, he was imprisoned and tortured. After release, he returned south but was later arrested again. His mother encouraged him to die for a good reputation, and he was executed at age thirty-three.

Yin Xun, from Henan, adhered to integrity despite his family's power. He participated in killing Liang Ji and was granted marquis. He wrote to explain Fan Pang's case but was later imprisoned and committed suicide due to Dou Wu's incident.

Cai Yan, from Runan, was proficient in Confucian classics and mediated village disputes. He refused to nominate a eunuch's relative and impeached corrupt officials. He died of illness after Emperor Ling ascended.

Yangzhi, from Taishan, was upright and knowledgeable. He reported corruption cases and recommended honest officials. He suppressed powerful people as Henan Yin but was removed during the party disaster and died at home.

Zhang Jian, from Shanyang, exposed Hou Lan's crimes but was framed by Zhu Bing. He fled for his life, with many houses sheltering him despite the risk of execution. Dozens were killed for hiding him. After the party ban was lifted, he returned home and shared his wealth with villagers. He died at age eighty-four during Jian'an.

Cen Hao, from Nanyang, studied at Taixue and befriended Guo Linzong. He served as a meritorious officer under Wang Jin, arresting and killing over 200 corrupt individuals. Emperor Huan imprisoned Wang Jin, and Cen Hao fled, later dying in the Jiangxia Mountains.

Chen Xiang, from Runan, reported Liang Ji's disrespect and was promoted to governor of Yangzhou. He expelled corrupt officials but was imprisoned during the party disaster. He was exonerated and died at home.

Kong Yu, from Lu, studied "Shang Shu" and refused Liang Ji's summons. He was banned from office due to the party disaster and later died at home after serving as Luoyang magistrate.

Yuan Kang, from Bohai, was strict as Taishan prefect, returning stolen lands. He arrested Hou Lan's clan guests hiding in Taishan. Hou Lan falsely accused him, and Yuan Kang was sentenced to death but later pardoned. He died at home.

Tanfu, from Shanyin, was poor but had high ambitions. He taught hundreds of students and refused multiple summons. He died at home at age eighty.

Liu Ru, from Dongjun, was intelligent despite stuttering. He wrote ten articles on governance but was imprisoned and committed suicide after Dou Wu's incident.

Jia Biao, from Yingchuan, was known for his integrity. As Xinxi magistrate, he punished those who did not raise children and stopped infanticide. During the party disaster, he appealed to Dou Wu and Huo, leading to the party members' pardon. He refused to shelter Cen Hao and was banned from office, dying at home.

He Yong, from Nanyang, avenged his friend's father and fled after Chen Fan and Li Ying's failure. He secretly helped many escape the party disaster. After the disaster ended, he was recruited but refused Dong Zhuo's offer. He conspired to kill Dong Zhuo but died of sorrow and anger after arrest. He once praised Cao Cao as the one to pacify the world.
