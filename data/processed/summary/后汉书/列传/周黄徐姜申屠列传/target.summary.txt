The text presents biographies of several virtuous men from the Later Han period, emphasizing their moral integrity, reclusiveness, and resistance to officialdom.

**Zhou Xie**, from Runan, was born with a disfigured face that his mother wanted to abandon, but his father kept him, foreseeing his future prosperity. He was a precocious child, mastering classics like the *Book of Songs* and *Analects* by age ten, and later specializing in the *Book of Rites* and *Book of Changes*. He lived self-sufficiently, farming and fishing, and refused to eat what he did not produce. Despite being recommended for office multiple times, he feigned illness to avoid service. Eventually, he accepted a summons but soon returned home, citing the difficulty of moving with the times. He died in his seventies.

**Huang Xian**, also from Runan, was born into a poor family, his father being a cattle doctor. At fourteen, he impressed the scholar Xun Shu, who declared him a teacher. His profound character was noted by contemporaries: Dai Liang felt humbled in his presence, and Chen Fan admitted that seeing Huang Xian curbed his own stinginess. Guo Linzong compared him to vast, immeasurable waters. Huang Xian was recommended for office but never took up a post, dying at forty-eight.

**Xu Zhen**, from Yuzhang, was a poor farmer who refused to eat unearned food. He briefly served as a merit officer under Prefect Chen Fan, who set up a special couch for him. He repeatedly declined official summons, including from Emperor Huan. After the death of Taiwei Huang Qiong, Xu Zhen traveled to mourn anonymously. He was known for his subtle acts of respect, such as leaving a bundle of grass at Guo Linzong's mother's funeral. He died at seventy-two.

**Jiang Gong**, from Pengcheng, was renowned for filial piety and brotherly love, sleeping with his brothers even after marriage. He taught the Five Classics to over 3,000 students but refused all official positions. When encountering bandits, he and his brother vied to die for each other, moving the thieves to repentance. Later, he feigned illness to avoid being painted for the emperor and fled to the coast to escape eunuch-dominated politics, living in obscurity until his death at seventy-seven.

**Shen Tu Pan**, from Chenliu, mourned his father excessively, abstaining from meat and wine for over a decade. At fifteen, he successfully pleaded for a woman avenging her father. He lived in poverty, working as a painter, and was praised by Cai Yong. He refused official posts, including from Taiwei Huang Qiong, and predicted the disaster of the Partisan Prohibitions, which he escaped by living in seclusion. He died at seventy-four, maintaining his principles through turbulent times.
