# Summary of Wu Han and Associated Generals

Wu Han, courtesy name Ziyan, was born into poverty in Wan County, Nanyang County, and served as a county pavilion chief. During Wang Mang's final years, he fled to Yuyang after his guests broke the law, supporting himself by selling horses and befriending heroes throughout Yan and Ji regions. When Gengshi ascended the throne, envoy Han Hong appointed Wu Han as magistrate of Anle County. During Wang Lang's uprising, Wu Han sought to join Guangwu, attempting to persuade Prefect Peng Chong to ally with Guangwu against Handan. When officials favored Wang Lang, Wu Han fabricated a letter from Guangwu and sent a Confucian scholar to convince Peng Chong, who ultimately agreed. Wu Han led troops south, killing Wang Lang's generals, and after pursuing Guangwu to Guang'a, was appointed partial general. Following the conquest of Handan, he was made Marquis Jiance.

Wu Han was loyal but lacked literary talent. Deng Yu recommended him, and Guangwu trusted him deeply. When Guangwu needed a general for Youzhou, Deng Yu praised Wu Han's bravery and resourcefulness. Wu Han was appointed general and sent north with a talisman to mobilize troops from ten counties. When Gengshi's Youzhou governor Miao Zeng refused to comply, Wu Han captured and killed him, seizing his troops. All cities in Beizhou submitted. Wu Han then led his forces south to join Guangwu at Qingyang, presenting the roster of his troops to Guangwu, which shamed other generals who had doubted his willingness to share command.

When Xie Gong, a Gengshi official, occupied Handan with his troops, Guangwu outwardly praised him while secretly planning against him. After Xie Gong was defeated by bandits at Qingdu, Guangwu sent Wu Han and Cen Peng to attack Ye. Wu Han sent debaters to persuade Chen Kang, the Wei county governor, to surrender, which he did, opening the gates to Han forces. When Xie Gong returned, he was captured and killed. Guangwu later attacked bandits in the north with Wu Han leading five thousand assault cavalry as vanguard. After Hebei was pacified, Guangwu ascended the throne and appointed Wu Han Grand Sima, granting him the title Marquis of Wuyang.

In subsequent campaigns, Wu Han defeated the Tanxiang bandits, earning the title Marquis of Guangping. He conquered multiple cities in Nanyang County and defeated Qin Feng. He fought alongside Geng Yan and Gai Yan against Qingdu, forcing his surrender. During the siege of Guangle, Wu Han was wounded but rallied his troops, defeating Zhou Jian and Su Mao's forces. He later helped besiege Liu Yong in Suiyang, and after Liu Yong's death, both cities surrendered. Wu Han continued defeating bandits across multiple regions, including Linping, Dongjun, Qinghe, and Pingyuan. He pacified Li County by diplomatic means, convincing the five surnames to surrender without battle.

When Wei Xiao rebelled, Wu Han garrisoned Chang'an. In the eighth year of Jianwu, he disobeyed Guangwu's order to dismiss troops, leading to defeat when Gongsun Shu's forces arrived. In the eleventh year, Wu Han led forces against Gongsun Shu. After Cen Peng's assassination, Wu Han took command. Despite Guangwu's warnings, Wu Han advanced recklessly toward Chengdu, establishing two camps separated by twenty miles. When Gongsun Shu's forces attacked, Wu Han was defeated and surrounded. He secretly joined forces with Liu Shang in Jiangnan, defeated the Shu army, killing Xie Feng and Yuan Ji. After eight consecutive victories, Wu Han's forces killed Gongsun Shu in battle, and Chengdu surrendered.

Wu Han was known for his strong character and composure during crises. He distributed his wealth to relatives rather than acquiring land. On his deathbed in the twentieth year of Jianwu, he advised Guangwu not to pardon criminals easily. He was given the posthumous title Zhonghou.

The text also records the careers of Gai Yan, Chen Jun, and Zang Gong, who served under Guangwu with distinction in various campaigns, including against Liu Yong, Dong Xian, and Gongsun Shu. The historian's commentary notes that Guangwu's defensive strategy was cautious and far-reaching, rejecting proposals for aggressive campaigns against the Xiongnu in favor of allowing the people to recuperate.
