**Summary of Historical Biographies**

Du Du, styled Jiya, was from Duling, Jingzhao. His great ancestor was Du Yannian, a censor under Emperor Xuan. Though knowledgeable, Du Du was not well-regarded locally. After a conflict with Meiyang Ling, he was arrested and sent to the capital. While imprisoned, he wrote an edict for Guangwu, who praised his writing, gave him silk, and pardoned him. Du Du later argued against rebuilding Luoyi, writing a "Fu on the Capital." He served as a county literary official but did not go to the capital for over twenty years due to eye disease. He lamented his family's decline, noting his ancestors' achievements contrasted with his own lack of appointment. He died in battle serving as a lieutenant under Ma Fang. His works include eighteen articles and fifteen chapters of "Lun on the Ming Dynasty." His son Du Gu was a heroic figure.

Wang Long, styled Wenshan, from Yunyang, served as Lang under Wang Mang, then fled to Hexi. Under Jianwu, he became magistrate of Xinji County. He wrote twenty-six poems and other works. Cen Zixiao of Pei State was also famous for his articles under Wang Mang.

Xia Gong, styled Jinggong, from Liang State, studied "Han Shi" and "Mencius' Book of Changes," teaching thousands. During Wang Mang's reign, he defended his area with troops. Guangwu appointed him doctor, then captain of Taishan. He wrote twenty works, including "Encouraging Learning," and died in office at forty-nine, posthumously named "Xuanmingjun." His son Xia Ya wrote forty poems.

Fu Yi, a literary scholar under Suzong, served as Lantai Lingshi and doctor, correcting books with Ban Gu and Jia Kui. He wrote ten "Xianzong Odes" to honor Emperor Xiaoming. He served under Ma Fang, but was dismissed after Ma's defeat. Later, he worked for Dou Xian. He died young, leaving twenty-eight works.

Huang Xiang, styled Wenqiang, from Jiangxia, was known for filial piety after losing his mother at nine. At twelve, Prefect Liu Hu summoned him as a "filial son." He was poor but studious, earning the saying "Jiangxia Huangtong is unparalleled in the world." He served as doctor, Shangshulang, and Shangshuling, often discussing court affairs. He declined a post as prefect of Dongjun, citing his inadequacy. The emperor retained him, increasing his salary. He handled border affairs well and saved many in a rumor case. He later became prefect of Wei County, where he rented out gardens to avoid competing with the people, and distributed wages during a flood. He died at home after dismissal. He wrote five poems and other works.

Liu Yi, son of King Jing of Beihai, was known for literary debate. He published "Lun on Han De" and "Lun on Constitution," earning praise and a grant from Emperor An.

Li You, styled Boren, from Guanghan, was famous for his articles. Jia Kui recommended him to Emperor He, and he served as Lantai Lingshi and admonishing official. He co-authored "Han Ji" and remonstrated against deposing the prince. He died at eighty-three, leaving twenty-eight works. Li Sheng, also from his county, wrote dozens of poems.

Su Shun, styled Xiaoshan, from Jingzhao, was known for talent and learning under Emperors He and An. He favored health techniques and lived in seclusion, later becoming a doctor. He wrote sixteen works. Cao Zhong and Bo Shi of Fufeng were also talented, writing four works. Cao Shuo wrote four chapters of "Ode to the Han Dynasty."

Liu Zhen, styled Qiusun, from Nanyin, was eager to learn. He served as a servant under Empress Dowager Deng, collating classics. He wrote biographies of famous officials and served as Zongzheng and Weiwei. He wrote seven works and thirty chapters of "Shiming."

Ge Gong, styled Yuanfu, from Liang State, was known for writing under Emperor He. He served as magistrate of Dangyin and Linfen, achieving political success. He wrote twelve works.

Wang Yi, styled Shuyuan, from Nanjun, served as school secretary and servant under Emperor Shun. He authored "Chu Ci Chapters and Sentences" and 123 chapters of "Chinese Poems." His son Wang Yanshou wrote "Ode to Lingguang Palace" and died young.

Cui Qi, styled Ziwei, from Zhuo County, was known for extensive articles. He admonished Liang Ji for illegal acts, writing "Admonitions to Foreign Relatives" and "White Bird Ode." Liang Ji sent an assassin, but the assassin spared Cui Qi. Liang Ji later arrested and killed him. He wrote fifteen works.

Bian Shao, styled Xiaoxian, from Chenliu, was famous for writing and teaching. He was quick-witted, once defending his daytime sleep with a clever rhyme. He served as Prime Minister of Linying, governor, and Shangshu, dying in office. He wrote fifteen works.
