# Summary of Historical Biographies

## Zheng Xing

Zheng Xing, courtesy name Shaogan, was born in Kaifeng, Henan. He studied "Gongyang Chunqiu" in his youth and later "Zuo Shi Zhuan," becoming so knowledgeable that his classmates regarded him as their teacher. During the Tianfeng period, he taught Liu Xin the meaning of "Zuo Shi Zhuan." When Gengshi ascended the throne, Zheng Xing served as chief historian and advised the emperor to move west to Chang'an rather than stay in Luoyang, arguing that the people missed Han virtues and that delaying would cause alienation and rebellion. Gengshi appointed him as an admonishment official and later governor of Liangzhou.

After being dismissed following a rebellion in Tianshui, Zheng Xing joined Wei Xiao. When Wei Xiao considered declaring himself king, Zheng Xing dissuaded him by citing historical examples of King Wen of Zhou and Emperor Gaozu of Han, warning that such ambition would bring disaster. He also advised against establishing excessive official positions. When Wei Xiao refused to let Zheng Xing return home to bury his parents, Zheng Xing argued that using his parents as leverage was improper, and Wei Xiao eventually relented.

In the sixth year of Jianwu, Du Lin recommended Zheng Xing to the emperor, praising his knowledge and virtue. Zheng Xing was recruited as Taizhong doctor. After a solar eclipse, he submitted a memorial discussing celestial omens and advising the emperor to select officials based on merit rather than personal grudges. The emperor once asked Zheng Xing about using prophecies for suburban sacrifices, but Zheng Xing admitted he did not understand prophecies, which limited his advancement. In the ninth year, he was sent to supervise military campaigns and later demoted for privately purchasing slaves. He died at home after retiring from official service.

## Fan Sheng

Fan Sheng, courtesy name Bianqing, was from Daijun. Orphaned young, he studied "Liangqiu Yi" and "Laozi." During Wang Mang's reign, he criticized the heavy taxes and military conscription, warning that the policies would lead to rebellion. His advice was ignored, and he later joined the Han forces.

In the second year of Jianwu, Emperor Guangwu appointed him as a doctor. Fan Sheng declined, recommending two older scholars instead, but the emperor refused. He debated against establishing "Zuo Shi Chunqiu" as an official study, arguing it lacked proper lineage and that establishing it would open the door to other heretical texts. He presented fourteen discrepancies in "Zuo Shi" and thirty-one instances where it contradicted the "Five Classics." Later imprisoned after accusations by his divorced wife, he was released and returned home. He served as Liaocheng Order during Yongping but was dismissed and died at home.

## Chen Yuan

Chen Yuan, eldest grandson of his family, was born in Guangxin, Cangwu. He inherited his father Chen Qin's study of "Zuo Shi Chunqiu" and made annotations for it. During the early Jianwu period, he was considered an authentic scholar alongside Huan Tan, Du Lin, and Zheng Xing.

When Fan Sheng opposed establishing "Zuo Shi" as a doctoral study, Chen Yuan submitted a memorial arguing that "Zuo Shi" was personally taught by Confucius to Zuo Qiuming, unlike "Gongyang" and "Guliang." He refuted Fan Sheng's criticisms as nitpicking and argued that later emperors need not follow predecessors' decisions. After ten debates, the emperor established "Zuo Shi" studies, and Chen Yuan became the first of four doctors. However, when Li Feng died, the study was abandoned again. Chen Yuan later served as Sikong Litong's assistant and advised against establishing a supervisory office over the three highest officials, arguing it would undermine trust. He died at home due to old age.

## Jia Kui

Jia Kui, courtesy name Jingbo, was born in Pingling, Fufeng. He inherited his father Jia Hui's studies, mastering "Zuo Shi Zhuan," "Guoyu," "Zhou Guan," and ancient texts. At twenty, he could read "Zuo Shi Zhuan" and the "Five Classics." He wrote fifty-one chapters of exegesis on "Zuo Shi Zhuan" and "Guoyu," which Emperor Xianzong valued and stored in the secret hall.

When a divine bird appeared, Jia Kui explained it as an omen of Hu people's surrender. Emperor Suzong appreciated his lectures and asked him to demonstrate how "Zuo Shi Zhuan" surpassed "Gongyang" and "Guliang." Jia Kui presented thirty examples promoting proper君臣 relations and argued that "Zuo Shi" alone contained texts proving the Liu family descended from Emperor Yao. The emperor rewarded him generously and ordered talented students to study the Four Classics, which became popular worldwide. Jia Kui wrote over a million words of exegesis and various literary works. He died at seventy-two, and the court appointed his sons as officials.

## Zhang Ba

Zhang Ba, courtesy name Bo Rao, was from Chengdu, Shu County. Known for filial piety and humility from childhood, he was called Zhang Zengzi by villagers. He studied "Yanshi Gongyang Chunqiu" and extensively read the "Five Classics." During Yongyuan, he became prefect of Kuaiji, where he promoted talented individuals and encouraged moral cultivation, leading thousands to study scriptures.

Zhang Ba simplified Fan Min's edition of "Yan's Spring and Autumn Annals" to 200,000 words, calling it "Zhang's Studies." He pacified thieves through rewards without military force. After three years, he requested sick leave, citing the principle that fullness leads to decline. Later promoted to Attendant, he declined friendship with the powerful Deng Zhi. He died at seventy, instructing his sons to bury him simply where he died rather than transport his body home. His disciples posthumously titled him Xianwen.
