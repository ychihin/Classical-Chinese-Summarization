Based solely on the provided text, here is a summary of the biographies of several historical figures.

Fu Zhan, also known as Hui Gong, was a native of Dongwu. He was a descendant of the scholar Fu Sheng. Fu Zhan was a filial and learned man who taught his father's teachings to hundreds of students. During the chaos of the Gengshi era, he served as the Prefect of Pingyuan, where he remained calm, ate coarse grains, and used his salary to relieve the starving people. He beheaded a powerful governor who planned to raise troops, which stabilized the county. When Guangwu came to the throne, Fu Zhan was appointed to high office, eventually becoming the Grand Situ and Marquis of Yangdu. He advised the emperor against personally conquering the rebel Peng Chong, arguing that the emperor should first secure the central territories. The emperor heeded his advice. A thief named Xu Yiqing, who could not be defeated by officers, surrendered specifically to Fu Zhan. Fu Zhan was later dismissed from his post for a minor fault. He was later recommended for reinstatement but died of heatstroke before he could take office.

Hou Ba, courtesy name Junfang, was from Mi County. He was a serious and dedicated student of the "Guliang Spring and Autumn Annals." As a county magistrate, he killed powerful criminals and arrested bandits, bringing peace to the region. As the Grand Administrator of Huaipei, he was known for his capable governance. When Wang Mang failed, he defended the county. The people begged for him to stay when he was summoned by Gengshi. Under Guangwu, he became a minister and established many good governance laws. He succeeded Fu Zhan as Grand Situ. He was known for being clear-minded and selfless. After his death, the emperor posthumously named him Marquis Ai of Zixiang, and the people of Linhuai built a temple for him.

Song Hong, courtesy name Zhongzi, was from Chang'an. He served under several emperors and Wang Mang. When Chimei entered Chang'an, he attempted suicide by drowning to avoid serving them but was rescued. Under Guangwu, he became Grand Sikong and was known for his honesty, distributing his rent among his clan. He once recommended Huan Tan to the emperor, but later criticized Huan Tan for playing secular music at court banquets, which Song Hong felt was inappropriate. When the emperor tried to arrange a marriage between Song Hong and his widowed sister, Princess Huyang, Song Hong refused, stating that a friend from humble times should not be forgotten, and a wife from poverty should not be cast aside. He was later dismissed from his post.

Cai Mao, courtesy name Zili, was from Hanoi. He was a renowned Confucian who refused to serve under Wang Mang. He took refuge with Dou Rong. Under Guangwu, he became the Grand Administrator of Guanghan. He wrote a memorial criticizing the illegal behavior of imperial relatives and praising the uprightness of Luoyang's magistrate, Dong Xuan. He later became Situ and was known for his honesty and frugality. He died in office.

Feng Qin, courtesy name Wei Bo, was from Fanyang. He was tall and capable with arithmetic. He served as a meritorious officer under Yaoqi and was recommended to Guangwu. He was valued for his skill in negotiating military supplies and was put in charge of examining the fiefs of the princes, a system he established. He later became Situ. The emperor warned him to be a loyal minister, using the example of Zhu Fu. Feng Qin served with respect and conscientiousness. His mother was honored by the emperor. He died in office.

Zhao Xi, courtesy name Boyang, was from Wan. As a youth, he sought revenge for his cousin but delayed the act until his enemies recovered from illness. He was recruited by Gengshi to persuade the Li family of Wucheng to surrender. He fought at Kunyang and was wounded. After Gengshi's failure, he helped friends and Gengshi's family escape, protecting them from bandits. He was falsely accused of conspiracy but was cleared by the emperor. He served as a county magistrate and governor, suppressing bandits and punishing the powerful. He was known for his mercy, moving surrendered bandits to other counties. He became Taiwei and managed frontier affairs, ordering immigrants to return to their original lands. He presided over the funeral of Emperor Guangwu and established proper court etiquette for the princes. He died at age eighty-four.

Mou Rong, courtesy name Ziyou, was from Beihai. He was a professor of the "Great Xia Hou Shangshu." As a county magistrate, he had no jail cases. He served in various high offices, including Sili Xiaowei, Dahonglu, and Chief Agricultural Officer. He was well-versed in classics and had great talent for discussion. He became Taiwei under Emperor Suzong and died in office.

Wei Biao, courtesy name Mengda, was from Pingling. He was a descendant of the prime minister Wei Xian. He was known for his filial piety, mourning his parents for three years. He was a learned Confucian who lived in poverty. He was summoned by Emperor Xianzong and became the Prefect of Wei County. He later served as Da Honglu. He submitted a memorial arguing that the selection of officials should prioritize filial piety and conduct over talent. He also advised the emperor to reduce harsh punishments and select officials with experience as state governors. He was known for his frugality and charity, leaving his family with no money. He wrote a twelve-chapter work titled "Wei Qingzi."
