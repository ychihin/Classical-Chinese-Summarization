The Xiqiang people, originally from Sanmiao, a branch of the Jiang surname, lived in a region near Nanyue, stretching from the Cizhi River to the borders of Shu and Han in the south and Shanshan and Cheshi in the northwest. They were nomadic, migrating with water and grass, relying primarily on livestock rather than grain. Their society lacked fixed clans, with tribes named after paternal or maternal lines. After twelve generations, intermarriage occurred, and customs included marrying stepmothers after a father's death and widows after an elder brother's death, leading to rapid population growth. They had no central ruler; power determined leadership, with violence and plunder common. Their soldiers excelled in valley combat but not on plains, valued death in battle over illness, and endured harsh conditions.

Historically, the Xiqiang's relationship with Chinese dynasties fluctuated. During the Xia Dynasty, Taikang's loss of control led to barbarian rebellion, but later rulers like Houxiang and Houxie subdued them. Under the Yin Dynasty, Wuding conquered Xirong and Guifang, but Wu Yi's brutality provoked Dog Soldier invasions. The Zhou Dynasty saw King Wen and King Wu command Xirong, while King Mu campaigned against Quanrong. As Zhou weakened, Rong tribes invaded, leading to conflicts with Qin and Jin. During the Spring and Autumn period, Rong groups like Yiqu and Dali grew powerful, building cities and claiming kingship. Qin gradually absorbed them, with King Zhao of Qin destroying Yiqu in the 4th century BCE.

Under the Han Dynasty, the Xiqiang faced expulsion and relocation. Emperor Wu expanded borders, building fortresses and separating Qiang from Xiongnu. In 42 BCE, Xianling Qiang allied with Xiongnu, attacking Lingju and Paohan, but were suppressed by General Li Xi. Later, Zhao Chongguo defeated them in 60 BCE. During Wang Mang's reign, Qiang lands were seized, but after his fall, they rebelled. In 25 CE, Guangwu restored the Qiang Protectorate, but conflicts persisted. Ma Yuan defeated Qiang rebellions in 35 CE, moving them to Tianshui and Longxi. By 58 CE, Dianwu's rebellion was crushed, and Qiang were resettled in Sanfu.

The Qiang continued to resist Han rule. In 77 CE, Miwu led a revolt, defeating Han forces. Fu Yu, the Qiang Protector, was killed in 87 CE. Zhang Yu retaliated by poisoning Qiang leaders, but Miwu's son Mitang continued raids. Deng Xun and later officials used bribery to divide the Qiang, but conflicts resumed. In 107 CE, the Qiang rebellion under Dianling and others devastated border regions, costing billions and causing massive displacement. Ren Shang and Deng Zun campaigned against them, but corruption and mismanagement plagued efforts. By 118 CE, the Qiang were largely subdued, though sporadic revolts occurred.

In later years, the Qiang faced further suppression. Ma Xian defeated them in 141 CE, but his death in battle in 144 CE led to renewed chaos. Zhao Chong and others campaigned, but the Qiang declined by the 2nd century CE. During the Han dynasty's decline, Qiang allied with Yellow Turban rebels in 184 CE. By the 3rd century CE, the Qiang had fragmented into 150 tribes, with the Zhong Qiang being the most powerful, fielding over 100,000 warriors. The Yuezhi Hu, a related group, intermarried with Qiang and lived in Huangzhong.

Despite costly campaigns by generals like Deng Lu and Ren Shang, the Han failed to achieve lasting peace.
