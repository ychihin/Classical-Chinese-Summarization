**Summary of Historical Biographies**

**Fifth Lun (Biography 5)**
Fifth Lun, courtesy name Boyu, was from Changling, Jingzhao, descended from the Tian clan of Qi. Upright from youth, he built fortresses during Wang Mang's era to defend against bandits like Bronze Horse and Red Eyebrow, successfully repelling attacks. He served under Yin Xianyu Bao, who recognized his talent, and later became a village official, gaining popularity through fair tax collection and dispute mediation. He moved to Hedong, changed his name to Wang Boqi, and worked as a salt carrier. Recommended by Xianyu Bao, he became chief bookkeeper under Jingzhao Yin Yanxing, where he rectified market scales and coins, earning public trust. Promoted to Xiaolian in 51 CE, he served as chief medical officer for Huaiyang State. Emperor Guangwu, impressed by his governance insights, appointed him governor of Fuyi, then Kuaiji. In Kuaiji, he banned superstitious cattle sacrifices and witch fraud, restoring order. Transferred in 62 CE for legal violations, he was pursued by weeping locals. After release, he farmed privately until appointed magistrate of Danqu County, then governor of Shu Commandery, where he dismissed corrupt officials and promoted virtuous ones, ending bribery. As Sikong under Suzong, he warned against the power of Empress Dowager Mingde's Ma relatives, citing their lavish gifts and potential corruption. He also opposed Ma Fang's Western Qiang campaign, fearing nepotism. Known for strictness but leniency, he criticized harsh governance, advocating for benevolent officials. He retired in 86 CE with honors and died over eighty, receiving imperial burial gifts.

**Zhongli Yi (Biography)**
Zhongli Yi, courtesy name Zi'a, from Shanyin, Kuaiji, began as a county governor. During a plague in 38 CE, he personally provided medicine to victims. Promoted to Xiaolian, he served under Grand Situ Hou Ba, showing compassion by arranging clothes for a sick disciple. As Xiaqiu magistrate, he privately forgave an official's theft, leading the thief's father to force his son's suicide. As Tangyi magistrate, he allowed a prisoner, Guang, to bury his mother, later securing his pardon. Under Xianzong, as minister, he refused tainted pearls from corrupt official Zhang Hui, earning imperial praise and a reward. He criticized the emperor's frequent hunts at Guangcheng Garden, urging frugality during a drought. He also defended a clerk's clerical error, taking blame himself. Known for bold remonstrance, he rescued ministers from punishment and advised leniency in governance. As prime minister of Lu for five years, he promoted moral education, dying in office. His final words urged gentle rule, and the emperor granted burial funds.

**Song Jun (Biography)**
Song Jun, courtesy name Shuxiang, from Nanyang, became a doctor at fifteen via his father's appointment. Proficient in classics, he served as Chen Yang's assistant, establishing schools and banning improper sacrifices. He led 3,000 troops to rescue General Liu Shang but arrived after his death. With General Ma Yuan, he faced disease and blockades, so he forged an imperial edict to accept barbarian surrender, dispersing their forces. Emperor Guangwu pardoned his unauthorized action, rewarding him. As Shangcai magistrate, he refused to enforce strict funeral bans, prioritizing justice. As Jiujiang governor, he attributed tiger attacks to corrupt officials, removing traps and promoting virtue, causing tigers to leave. He ended a marriage sacrifice custom in Junqiu County. Promoted to prime minister of East China Sea, he was later dismissed but recalled due to public demand. As minister, he defended his principles against imperial anger, earning praise for his integrity. As Hanoi governor, he was beloved by the people. He retired due to illness, receiving honors. He advocated lenient governance, blaming harsh laws for social ills, and died in 76 CE.

**Han Lang (Biography)**
Han Lang, courtesy name Boqi, from Xue, Lu, survived abandonment as an infant during chaos. A Confucian scholar, he taught *Shang Shu* and served as imperial censor during Yongping. In the Chu prison case, he investigated four marquises implicated by false confessions, finding no evidence. Despite imperial anger, he argued against mass accusations, warning of wrongful deaths. His plea led to the release of over a thousand prisoners. He later imprisoned himself after the accused died, but was amnestied. Under Suzong, he was appointed Yizhou magistrate, then Luoyang magistrate, resigning after his mother's death. In 87 CE, he was promoted to Situ Prefecture, then Qinghe governor, dismissed for legal violations. Recommended as a doctor in 109 CE, he died at eighty-four.
