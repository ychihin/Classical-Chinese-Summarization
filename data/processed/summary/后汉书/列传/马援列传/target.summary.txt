Ma Yuan, styled Wenyuan, was a native of Maoling, Fufeng. His ancestor Zhao She was a general of Zhao, and his descendants adopted the surname Ma. During Emperor Wu's reign, the family moved from Handan to Maoling. Ma Yuan's great-grandfather Ma Tong was granted the title of Chonghehou, but his brother was implicated in a rebellion and killed, preventing Ma Yuan's grandfather and father from holding prominent positions. Ma Yuan's three elder brothers were all talented and served as officials worth two thousand shi under Wang Mang. Orphaned at twelve, Ma Yuan was ambitious and unconventional. He left his studies to farm and herd in border counties, eventually amassing great wealth, which he distributed to his brother and friends, living frugally.

During the end of Wang Mang's reign, Ma Yuan served as a county official and later fled to the north after releasing a prisoner. He was pardoned and became a successful farmer and herder. He joined Wei Xiao, who respected him and made him General Suide. Ma Yuan was sent to observe Gongsun Shu in Shu but was unimpressed by his pompous behavior, calling him a "frog in a well." He then went to Luoyang to meet Emperor Guangwu, who received him warmly. Ma Yuan praised the emperor's magnanimity and wisdom, comparing him favorably to Gaozu. He returned to Wei Xiao but eventually sided with Guangwu, writing letters to persuade Wei Xiao's generals to defect. Ma Yuan's military strategies were highly valued, and he led campaigns against the Qiang and other rebels, achieving significant victories.

Ma Yuan served as Governor of Longxi, where he focused on spreading favor and trust, appointing capable officials, and encouraging agriculture and animal husbandry. He successfully pacified the region and proposed minting five-baht coins, which was implemented. He later led campaigns against the Wuxi barbarians in Wuling, but died of illness at age 62. After his death, he was falsely accused of bringing back treasures from the south, leading to the emperor's anger and the revocation of his marquis title. His family was disgraced until a letter from Zhu Bo, a former associate, helped clear his name. Ma Yuan's daughter later became empress, and he was posthumously honored as Marquis Zhongcheng. His sons included Ma Liao, Ma Fang, Ma Guang, and Ma Keqing, the latter dying young. The historian notes Ma Yuan's rise to fame and his tragic fall due to slander, reflecting on the perils of fame and the importance of self-reflection.
