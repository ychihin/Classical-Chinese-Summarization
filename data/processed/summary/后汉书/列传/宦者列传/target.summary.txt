The preface to the biographies of eunuchs in the *Book of the Later Han* explains that eunuchs have served in imperial courts since ancient times, as evidenced by celestial signs and classical texts like the *Book of Changes* and the *Rites of Zhou*. Initially, they were employed for their perceived lack of evil thoughts and suitability for managing the inner palace. However, over time, their roles expanded. While some, like Bo Diao and Guan Su, made contributions to states, others caused disasters, such as in Qi and Song. During the Han Dynasty, the system continued, with eunuchs like Zhang Qing serving Empress Lü and later emperors. Their power grew significantly during the Eastern Han, starting with Zheng Zhong, who helped Emperor He eliminate the Dou Xian faction and was enfeoffed, marking the beginning of eunuch influence. This trend continued under subsequent rulers, with eunuchs like Sun Cheng playing key roles in supporting emperors, leading to their increased authority and the granting of titles and lands.

Cai Lun, a eunuch known for his talent and integrity, served under several emperors and is credited with inventing paper. He supervised the production of imperial instruments and later oversaw the collation of classics. However, he was implicated in a past conspiracy and committed suicide after being ordered to confess. Sun Cheng, another prominent eunuch, led a coup to establish Emperor Shun, killing rival eunuchs and officials. He and eighteen others were enfeoffed as marquises for their efforts. Despite later being dismissed and recalled, Sun Cheng’s legacy included the formalization of eunuch adoption rights. Other eunuchs like Cao Teng served four emperors without error, recommending worthy officials and maintaining a reputation for integrity, even when falsely accused.

The power of eunuchs peaked during the reign of Emperor Huan, when five eunuchs—Shan Chao, Xu Huang, Ju Yuan, Zuo Xuanguan, and Tang Heng—helped the emperor eliminate the powerful Liang Ji family. They were all enfeoffed as marquises on the same day, becoming known as the "Five Marquises." Their influence led to widespread corruption, with their relatives and associates plundering the people and causing chaos. Despite some attempts at reform, the eunuchs’ power only grew, leading to the persecution of upright officials and the establishment of party prisons. The historian notes that while Yuan Shao later killed all eunuchs, this merely replaced one form of violence with another, and the Han dynasty ultimately fell due to its reliance on eunuchs.

The biographies of individual eunuchs detail their rise and fall. Hou Lan, a favorite of Emperor Huan, amassed immense wealth through bribery and corruption, with his brother Hou Shen committing atrocities. Hou Lan was eventually impeached by Zhang Jian, but he retaliated by having Zhang Jian killed. He later committed suicide after being stripped of his titles. Cao Jie, who served under Emperor Shun and Huan, helped Emperor Ling ascend the throne and was instrumental in the massacre of the Dou Wu and Chen Fan factions. He and his associates, including Wang Fu, were granted extensive titles and lands, leading to widespread corruption and abuse of power. Despite some opposition, Cao Jie died in office, and his adopted son inherited his title.

Lu Qiang, a eunuch known for his honesty, advised Emperor Ling to eliminate corrupt officials and grant amnesty to party members during the Yellow Turban rebellion. However, he was falsely accused by other eunuchs and committed suicide. Other eunuchs like Zhao and Li Xun were noted for their learning and integrity, but they were exceptions. Zhang Rang and Zhao Zhong, who served under Emperor Ling, were among the most powerful eunuchs. They controlled the court, collected exorbitant taxes, and built lavish palaces. Their corruption and cruelty drove many to join the Yellow Turbans. After Emperor Ling’s death, Yuan Shao led a massacre of eunuchs, killing thousands. Zhang Rang and others fled with the emperor but eventually committed suicide by drowning.

The historian concludes that the fall of the Eastern Han was due to the gradual accumulation of eunuch power, which corrupted the court and alienated the people. While some eunuchs were loyal and capable, the majority were self-serving and destructive. Their influence, combined with the weakness of young emperors and the machinations of female regents, led to the dynasty’s collapse. The historian emphasizes that the eunuchs’ ability to gain trust and manipulate the court, coupled with their lack of familial ties, made them particularly dangerous. The decline was not sudden but a slow process, as warned in the *Book of Changes*: "Walking on frost, solid ice will come."
