In the final years of the Jianwu era, Feng Yan submitted a memorial reflecting on historical figures. He compared Gaozu’s talents and Chen Ping’s wisdom, noting how slander could alienate Chen Ping while praise could draw him closer. He also referenced Emperor Wen’s wisdom and Wei Shang’s loyalty, observing that justice could turn people into criminals while virtue could make them heroes. Feng Yan lamented the tragedies of loyal officials like Dong Zhongshu, who was jealous of Gongsun Hong, and Li Guang, who was rejected by Wei Qing despite his efforts against the Huns. Feng Yan considered himself a humble minister without the recommendations of Wei Wuwu or Feng Tang, nor the talents of Dong Zhongshu or Li Guang, making it difficult to avoid slander and resentment.

Feng Yan recounted his ancestor Feng Shen’s loyalty and the troubles it caused. During disturbances, Feng Yan avoided seeking personal gain, and his servants and generals had no evil plans. He associated with Wei Wei Yinxing, who was respectful and cautious, and who offered money to help Feng Yan establish a livelihood, but Feng Yan refused, believing his talents were insufficient. He noted that during the Gengshi period, he held a position for over twenty years but his property decreased, leaving him poor. In the Qingming period, he faced resentment and ridicule, acknowledging that it is easier for the rich to do good than the poor.

After his memorial was ignored due to past mistakes, Feng Yan retired to write poems. He reflected on virtue, comparing it to jade or stone, and emphasized adapting to changing times. He criticized rigid adherence to laws and customs, advocating for moral truth over worldly fame. He lamented that his strategies were unused and that he remained a minor official for years, unable to display his abilities. His property diminished over twenty years, and he suffered hunger and cold, even losing his eldest son.

Feng Yan chose a cemetery east of Xinfeng, on high ground with broad views, and retreated into seclusion. He mourned his ancestors’ virtues and the disorder of their tombs, planning to repair them and conduct sacrifices. He closed his door to lecture on morality, studying Confucius and Laozi, and sought spiritual blessings. He wrote a poem titled "Xian Zhi" to express his feelings, but after Xianzong ascended the throne, he was accused of exaggeration and abandoned. Feng Yan married a jealous woman who prevented him from raising a young wife, leaving him impoverished in old age. Despite this, he maintained his ambitions, refusing to seek high status or wealth. He died poor and old, leaving behind fifty works valued by Suzong. His son was Feng Bao.
