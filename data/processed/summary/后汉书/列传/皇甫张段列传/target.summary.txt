# Summary of the Biographies of Huangfu Gui, Zhang Huan, and Duan Jiong

Huangfu Gui, courtesy name Weiming, was from Anding County. His grandfather served as a Liao general and his father as Commander-in-Chief of Fufeng. During the sixth year of Yonghe, when Western Qiang forces invaded Chang'an and surrounded Anding, General Ma Xian failed to defeat them. Huangfu Gui, though a civilian, recognized Ma Xian's impending defeat and wrote to the court about it. After Ma Xian was defeated, the county governor appointed Huangfu Gui as a meritorious officer. He led 800 soldiers against the Qiang, killing several enemies and causing the bandits to retreat. He was subsequently recommended as a planner.

When Qiang soldiers later gathered to attack Longxi, Huangfu Gui petitioned the emperor, explaining that the Qiang rebellion resulted from border generals failing to properly govern, instead invading peaceful territories for small gains, falsely reporting victories, and concealing defeats. He requested command of troops from two battalions and two counties to lead 5,000 soldiers against the Qiang, but the emperor did not use him at that time. During the reigns of Emperors Chong and Zhi, when Empress Dowager Liang held power, Huangfu Gui was praised as virtuous and upright. He criticized the court for allowing treacherous ministers to accumulate wealth, sell official positions, and disturb the world. He specifically condemned General Liang Ji and others for their misconduct, urging the dismissal of corrupt officials. Liang Ji hated Huangfu Gui for mocking him and appointed him as a doctor, but Huangfu Gui excused himself due to illness and returned home, teaching over 300 students for fourteen years.

After Liang Ji was killed, the court summoned Huangfu Gui five times but he did not respond. He was later appointed as the prefect of Taishan to deal with bandit Sun Wuji, and he successfully pacified all the bandits. In the fourth year of Yanxi, when Qiang forces invaded Guanzhong, Huangfu Gui, familiar with Qiang affairs, petitioned to serve. He was promoted to Zhonglang General and defeated the Qiang, beheading 800 people. Over 100,000 Qiang people surrendered due to his prestige. However, Huangfu Gui reported the crimes of corrupt officials, including the governor of Anding and others, leading to their removal or execution. This made him enemies both inside and outside the court, who falsely accused him of bribing the Qiang. Huangfu Gui defended himself, explaining that his actions saved over 100 million in costs and that his expenditures were documented. Despite his merits, eunuchs Xu Huang and Zuo Xiang demanded bribes from him, and when he refused, they framed him. He was sentenced to hard labor but was released after an amnesty.

Huangfu Gui later recommended General Zhang Huan to replace him as commander. He was appointed as General Conquering Liao, but repeatedly requested retirement due to illness. When party affairs broke out, Huangfu Gui, considering himself a hero of Xizhou, felt ashamed not to be implicated and wrote to claim involvement, but the court did not hold him accountable. He died at age seventy-one, leaving twenty-seven written works. The historian commented that Huangfu Gui's words matched his actions, and he achieved success in military affairs without greed or arrogance.

Zhang Huan, courtesy name Ranming, was from Dunhuang County. He studied at Sanfu and later reduced a lengthy text from over 450,000 words to 90,000 words. After serving as a gentleman, he was transferred to Anding. When Southern Xiongnu and Eastern Qiang forces invaded with 7,000 men, Zhang Huan led only 200 troops but stationed at the Great Wall, gathered soldiers, and lured the Eastern Qiang to make peace. Together they attacked and defeated the invaders. The Qiang marshal presented him with twenty horses and eight gold taels, but Zhang Huan returned them, saying gold and horses could not be kept. He was promoted to Xiongnu Zhonglang General. When Xiutuge and Wuhuan rebelled, Zhang Huan calmly sat in his tent while others panicked, secretly lured Wuhuan to make peace, and defeated the rebels. After Liang Ji was executed, Zhang Huan was dismissed as his subordinate. Huangfu Gui recommended him seven times. He later served as prefect of Wuwei, where he changed the custom of killing children born in certain months. He was appointed General of Liao Dynasty, and for several years the border was peaceful.

When Xianbei invaded, Zhang Huan was recalled to defend against them. Over 200,000 Xiongnu and Wuhuan surrendered. He was later deceived by eunuch Cao Jie into surrounding General Dou Wu, who committed suicide. Zhang Huan was devastated and refused the marquis title granted for this action. He later petitioned to rebury Dou Wu and Chen Fan and vindicate those imprisoned for their association, but eunuchs opposed this. Zhang Huan was eventually banned from office due to party crimes. He educated thousands of disciples and wrote over 300,000 words. He died at age seventy-eight, leaving instructions for a simple burial. His sons were famous for cursive calligraphy.

Duan Jiong, courtesy name Jiming, was from Wuwei County. He learned horse racing and archery as a youth but later turned to ancient learning. He served as a captain and used a clever ambush to capture Xianbei invaders. He was appointed Zhonglang General to suppress bandits in Taishan, killing their leaders and gaining over 10,000 heads. He was promoted to Colonel Protecting Qiang and fought numerous battles against various Qiang groups, killing thousands and capturing tens of thousands. He was imprisoned due to a governor's false accusations but was later released and appointed governor of Bingzhou. He was again appointed Colonel Protecting Qiang and defeated the Qiang, beheading 23,000 and capturing tens of thousands. He was granted a marquis title.

When Emperor Huan asked about dealing with Eastern Qiang, Duan Jiong argued that the Qiang could not be won over with kindness and would rebel again after surrendering. He proposed using 5,000 cavalry, 10,000 infantry, and 3,000 chariots to completely defeat them in two or three years. The emperor agreed. Duan Jiong fought 180 battles, beheading over 38,600 enemies and capturing over 427,500 animals, at a cost of 4.4 billion. He was granted a county marquis title. He later relied on eunuchs and unjustly killed several people. He served as Taiwei but committed suicide in prison after being implicated in a murder. The three men were collectively known as "Liangzhou Sanming."
