# Summary of Historical Biographies

**Zhang Hao and Zhang Gang**

Zhang Hao, courtesy name Shuming, was from Wuyang, Qianwei County. His sixth-generation ancestor Zhang Liang served as prince's young master under Emperor Gao and was granted the title of Liuhou. Zhang Hao studied in the capital as a youth, later served as an official, and was promoted five times to Shangshu Pushe before becoming prime minister of Pengcheng. In Yongning's first year, he became Tingwei. Though not a Legalist, he emphasized proper punishment and often debated with ministers to correct doubts. When Emperor An deposed the crown prince, Zhang Hao argued unsuccessfully with officials. He later submitted a memorial citing historical examples of wrongful accusations and urging the selection of virtuous advisors for the young prince, but his advice was rejected. Under Emperor Shun, Zhang Hao became Sikong and promoted many talents. When Zhao Teng and others were arrested for criticizing the government, Zhang Hao admonished the emperor, citing ancient practices of accepting remonstrance, and persuaded him to reduce their sentences. He was removed from office due to natural disasters, reinstated as Tingwei in Yangjia's first year, and died in office at age eighty-three.

Zhang Hao's son Zhang Gang, courtesy name Wen Ji, was proficient in Confucian classics and strictly maintained integrity. He became imperial censor under Emperor Shun. Distressed by the emperor's indulgence of eunuchs, Zhang Gang submitted a memorial contrasting the frugal governance of earlier Han emperors with current corruption, urging reform. The emperor did not accept it. In Han'an's first year, when eight envoys were selected to inspect customs, Zhang Gang alone buried his carriage wheel in Luoyang, declaring he would investigate the powerful rather than minor officials. He submitted a report condemning General Liang Ji and his relatives for corruption and tyranny. Though the emperor knew Zhang Gang was truthful, he did not act due to Liang Ji's influence. Liang Ji then appointed Zhang Gang as prefect of Guangling, hoping the bandit troubles there would destroy him. Zhang Gang went alone with few officials to meet the bandit leader Zhang Ying, persuading him to surrender through sincere discussion of loyalty and consequences. Over ten thousand people surrendered. Zhang Gang died at forty-six, with the people mourning deeply and Zhang Ying leading over five hundred in mourning attire to build his tomb.

**Wang Gong and Wang Chang**

Wang Gong, courtesy name Bo Zong, was from Gaoping County, Shanyang. He served as Qingzhou governor, impeaching corrupt officials, and was promoted to Shangshu. He became Runan prefect, where he promoted moderate governance and introduced talented individuals like Huang Xian and Chen Fan. When Chen Fan left a note claiming illness after not being immediately received, Wang Gong initially wanted to dismiss him but was persuaded otherwise by meritorious official Yuan Lang. Wang Gong later became Taiwei, maintaining strict propriety and appointing respected elders. Concerned about eunuch power, he submitted a memorial requesting their banishment. Eunuchs retaliated by falsely accusing him, and Emperor Shun ordered him to confess. Li Gu wrote to General Liang Shang urging intervention, citing historical precedents, and the matter was resolved. Wang Gong served five years before retiring due to age and illness, dying at home.

Wang Gong's son Wang Chang, courtesy name Shumao, was known for honesty. He served in various positions including Yuyang prefect, where he was praised for seriousness and shrewdness. After being dismissed, he was recommended by Taiwei Chen Fan and became minister, then Nanyang governor. He harshly punished corrupt officials, demolishing houses and cutting down trees of those who concealed stolen goods. Meritorious official Zhang Chang advised leniency, citing historical examples of benevolent governance. Wang Chang accepted this advice and implemented lenient governance. He personally practiced frugality to correct extravagant habits. When advising young Liu Biao, Wang Chang emphasized moderation and cited historical examples of integrity. He later became Sikong but was dismissed due to floods, dying at home the following year.

**Zhong Hao, Zhong Dai, Zhong Fu, and Zhong Shao**

Zhong Hao, courtesy name Jing Bo, was from Luoyang County. After his father died, he used his inheritance to help the poor. He was recommended as filial and incorrupt by Wang Chen, who recognized his potential. Zhong Hao served as censor under Emperor Shun, impeaching corrupt officials recommended by Liang Ji. When eunuch Gao Fan came to meet the crown prince, Zhong Hao prevented him from entering without proper authorization, earning imperial praise. As Yizhou governor, he extended Han influence to foreign tribes. He exposed a prefect who presented gold snakes to Liang Ji, earning Liang Ji's enmity. After being framed by Liang Ji, he was pardoned due to Li Gu's intervention. He later served as Liangzhou governor, winning great popularity, and as Hanyang prefect, where he taught Qiang and Hu people. As general against the Xiongnu, he secured border peace through sincere persuasion. He became Situ in Yanxi's fourth year, recommending capable ministers, and died at sixty-one, mourned by border residents and even the Xiongnu.

Zhong Hao's son Zhong Dai, courtesy name Gongzu, loved learning but refused official appointments, dying of illness. His brother Zhong Fu, courtesy name Yingbo, served as Wan County magistrate, where he shamed corrupt officials into reform. He became Sikong but was dismissed due to earthquakes. When Li Chu and Guo Si rebelled, Zhong Fu fought to the death in the palace, dying alongside his son Zhong Shao. Zhong Shao, courtesy name Shenfu, served as an admonishing official. He confronted Dong Zhuo, forcing him to retreat. After Dong Zhuo seized power, Zhong Shao refused high office, declaring he could not face the emperor while his father's murderers remained unpunished. He joined Ma Teng and others to attack Li Chu and Guo Si but died in battle.

**Chen Qiu**

Chen Qiu, courtesy name Bo Zhen, was from Huaipu County, Xiapi. He was learned in Confucianism and law. As Fanyang magistrate, he refused to send bribes to the Wei prefect, who could not dismiss him due to his good reputation. As Lingling prefect, he defeated bandits and suppressed a rebellion, using innovative defensive tactics. He later became Nanyang governor but was slandered by powerful families and returned home. As Tingwei, he argued successfully for Empress Dowager Dou to be buried with Emperor Huan, despite opposition from eunuchs Cao Jie and Wang Fu. He later became Sikong but was dismissed due to earthquakes. He secretly plotted with Liu to kill eunuchs, but his wife's relative Cheng Huang betrayed them to Cao Jie. The emperor imprisoned Chen Qiu, who died in prison at sixty-two.
