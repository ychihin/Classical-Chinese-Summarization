The text contains biographies of several individuals from the Han dynasty. Liu Ping, originally named Kuang, was from Pengcheng. During Wang Mang's time, he was a county official. During the Gengshi period, when thieves arrived, he fled with his mother, abandoning his own son to save his orphaned niece. He later encountered a hungry thief who wanted to eat him, but Liu Ping begged to feed his mother first and promised to return. The thief, moved by his sincerity, let him go. Liu Ping kept his promise and returned, but the thief spared him. In the early Jianwu years, he risked his life to protect the wounded governor Sun Meng, suffering seven stab wounds and even offering his own blood to the thirsty Sun Meng. Liu Ping was later promoted to Xiaolian and became Prime Minister of Jiyin County. He was later recommended by Zhongliyi and served as Yilang and Zongzheng, recommending famous scholars. He died at home after requesting retirement due to old age and illness.

Zhao Xiao, named Changnian, was from Peiguo. His father served under Wang Mang. Zhao Xiao was known for his humility, traveling in plain clothes. During a famine, his brother Zhao Li was captured by hungry thieves. Zhao Xiao bound himself and went to the thieves, offering his own fatter body in exchange. The thieves released them. He was later recruited and served as an admonishing official and Changle Guardsman. His brother Zhao Li also became an official. The emperor favored them, but Zhao Li died a few years later. Zhao Xiao died at home after returning from leave.

Chunyu Gong, named Meng Sun, was from Beihai. He was a quiet man who studied Laozi. When someone stole his fruit, he helped them harvest it. When he saw a thief stealing seedlings, he hid to avoid shaming him. During a famine, he offered to replace his brother Chunyu Chong who was about to be cooked by bandits, and both were spared. He raised his brother's children, disciplining them by beating himself. He continued to farm despite chaos. He refused multiple calls to serve, living in seclusion for decades. Emperor Suzong eventually summoned him, made him a counselor, and later promoted him. He died in office, and the emperor ordered a stone carving to commend him.

Jiang Ge, named Cweng, was from Linzi. He fled with his mother on his back during chaos, picking wild fruits to survive. When thieves tried to kidnap him, he cried and begged, saying his mother depended on him, and the thieves spared him. He worked to support his mother in poverty. After returning home, he pulled his mother in a cart himself, earning the nickname "Jiang Da Duozi." He refused official invitations due to his mother's age. After her death, he slept by her tomb. He was later promoted to Xiaolian and served as Taipu of Chu, but abandoned the post. He later served as Sikong Changshi and General of the Five Features. The emperor treated him with great respect. He refused gifts from nobles. He requested retirement and was made an admonishing doctor. The emperor later issued an edict praising his filial piety and ordered the county to give him gifts.

Liu Ban was the great-great-grandson of Emperor Xuan. His father Liu Yu raised his younger brother Liu Ping after their mother died, and died of grief after Liu Ping's death. Liu Ban lived with his mother during Wang Mang's failure. He studied diligently despite his family's concerns. In the eighth year of Jianwu, he moved to Luoyang to study. He was later enfeoffed as a marquis. The emperor commended him for his good conduct. He served as Jinwu and captain of the cavalry school. He advised against establishing the Changpingcang, arguing it would harm the people. He also argued against a ban on farmers engaging in fishing and hunting, and against officials inflating land cultivation figures. He died at age sixty.

Zhou Pan, named Jianbo, was from Runan. He studied classics and was known for his good conduct. He left his official post to return home when he missed his mother. After his mother died, he lived next to her grave and taught thousands of disciples. He refused multiple government calls, saying he had no need for external things after his mother's death. At age seventy-three, he predicted his death, gave his sons instructions for a simple burial with a copy of the "Yao Canon," and died without illness.

Zhao Zi, named Wenchu, was from Dongjun. He was known for his filial piety. When robbers came to his house, he asked them to leave some food for his sick mother. The robbers, ashamed, left. He was later summoned to be an official and became Prime Minister of the East China Sea. He was known for his frugality. Before his death, he instructed his son to use a simple, thin coffin to allow his body to decay quickly and return to the earth. He wrote a long suicide note criticizing lavish burials and advocating for simplicity, citing ancient examples. His son followed his instructions.
