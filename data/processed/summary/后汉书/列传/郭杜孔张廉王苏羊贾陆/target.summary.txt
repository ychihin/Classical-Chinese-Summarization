# Summaries of Historical Biographies

## Guo Ji (Guo Xihou)

Guo Ji, from Maoling, Fufeng, was born into a family with a knightly ancestor and a prefect father. During the reigns of Emperor Ai and Emperor Ping, he served in the Da Sikong Mansion and became Yuyang Duwei. Under Wang Mang, he was Great Yin of Shanggu. During the Gengshi era, when the three auxiliaries were invaded, Gengshi appointed him Zuo Fengyi to pacify the people. After Shizu ascended, Guo Ji became Yongzhou's pastor and later a minister offering loyal advice. In the fourth year of Jianwu, he became prefect of Zhongshan, then governor of Yuyang after Peng Chong's defeat. There, he eliminated bandits through punishment and reward, and organized defenses against the Xiongnu, who ceased border incursions. After five years, household registrations doubled. In the ninth year, he became prefect of Yingchuan to suppress bandits, successfully recruiting Zhao Hong and Zhao Wu to surrender. Later appointed Bingzhou pastor, he advised selecting officials from worthy candidates nationwide rather than exclusively from Nanyang. He was known for keeping promises to children who welcomed him on horseback. He served long in Bingzhou due to Lu Fang's troubles, eventually forcing Lu Fang to flee to the Xiongnu. He resigned due to age and illness, became Taizhong doctor, distributed his wealth among his clan, and died at eighty-six.

## Du Shi (Du Jungong)

Du Shi, from Ji County, Hanoi, was talented from youth. He served as a county meritorious official, then joined the Da Sima Mansion. In the first year of Jianwu, he became Shi Yushi and executed the violent General Xiao Guang, receiving ceremonial halberds from Liu Xiu. He later killed the traitor Yang Yi and others. As magistrate of Chenggao, then captain of Pei and Runan, he governed well. In the seventh year, he became Nanyang Prefect, known for frugality and honesty. He invented a water-powered piston bellows for iron smelting, making agricultural tools more efficiently. He repaired ponds and expanded farmland, enriching the county. People compared him to former prefect Zhao Xinchen. Feeling uneasy about his merit, he requested demotion, arguing that troops needed rest and that meritorious generals should be rewarded with county positions. The emperor refused. Du Shi recommended talented people and proposed using tiger talismans for troop mobilization to prevent forgery, which the emperor accepted. After seven years, he was recalled due to a case involving his brother's revenge. He died poor without land or house; the emperor provided funeral expenses.

## Kong Fen (Kong Junyu)

Kong Fen, from Maoling, Fufeng, studied the Zuo Commentary on the Spring and Autumn Annals under Liu Xin. During Wang Mang's rebellion, he fled to Hexi with his mother and brother. In the fifth year of Jianwu, Dou Rong appointed him as chief aunt. He became Marquis of Guannei. Despite Guzang's wealth, Kong Fen's property did not increase in four years. He served his mother filially, eating vegetables with his wife while others laughed at his frugality. After Long and Shu were pacified, when other Hexi governors left with满载 goods, Kong Fen rode alone with nothing. The people offered him cattle, horses, and utensils worth over ten million, but he accepted nothing. In the capital, he became Commander of Wudu. When thieves captured his wife and son as hostages, he pursued relentlessly, resulting in their deaths. The emperor commended him and made him prefect of Wudu, where he governed with clarity. He resigned due to illness and died at home. His younger brother Kong Qi and son Kong Jia both wrote works on the Zuo Commentary.

## Zhang Kan (Zhang Junyou)

Zhang Kan, from Wan, Nanyang, was orphaned early and shared his father's wealth with his brother and son. At sixteen, he studied in Chang'an, where classmates called him "Holy Boy." Shizu praised him early on. After ascending, the emperor appointed him as an official. He delivered silk and horses to Wu Han for attacking Gongsun Shu, becoming prefect of Shu County. When the Han army had only seven days of food and planned retreat, Zhang Kan persuaded Wu Han to stay, leading to Gongsun Shu's defeat. After capturing Chengdu, Zhang Kan inspected the treasury without taking anything. He comforted the people, who were delighted. Later, as Governor of Yuyang, he defeated a Xiongnu invasion of 10,000 cavalry with only 1,000 horsemen. He opened over 8,000 hectares of rice fields, enriching the county. The people sang his praises. After eight years, the Xiongnu dared not invade. A Shu County commander testified that when Gongsun Shu was defeated, Zhang Kan left with only a quilt bag despite mountains of treasure.

## Lian Fan (Lian Shudu)

Lian Fan, from Duling, was a descendant of Zhao general Lian Po. His family had served as frontier county guards for generations. After his father died in Shu Han, fifteen-year-old Lian Fan went to retrieve his body. The prefect offered money, which he refused. When the coffin boat sank, Lian Fan clung to it and was rescued. He studied under Dr. Xue Han. When Xue Han was killed for involvement in King Chu Ying's rebellion, Lian Fan alone collected his body. The emperor questioned him but pardoned him for his courage. As governor of Yunzhong, when the Huns invaded with overwhelming numbers, Lian Fan ordered soldiers to light multiple torches, making the enemy think reinforcements had arrived. He pursued at dawn, killing hundreds. The Huns never attacked Yunzhong again. As prefect of Shu County, he discouraged frivolous debate and abolished night work restrictions, instead requiring water storage for fire prevention. The people sang his praises. He was known for generosity, once giving his horse to a mourning official without revealing his identity. He died at home.

## Wang Tang (Wang Jingbo)

Wang Tang, from Guanghan, was promoted from county magistrate due to his governance. During Yongchu, when Western Qiang rebels troubled Ba County, Wang Tang became prefect, killed over a thousand Qiang captives, and pacified the region. The people built a shrine for him. As You Fufeng, he refused to accommodate the powerful wet nurse Wang Sheng and eunuch Jiang Jing during Emperor An's western expedition, closing his gate to them. Falsely accused, he was vindicated after the emperor's death and Jiang Jing's execution. He later served as Prime Minister of Lu, then prefect of Runan, where he delegated authority to capable subordinates like Chen Fan and Ying Si, achieving good governance. He was hated by General Liang Shang and Minister Yuan Tang, who falsely accused him and had him dismissed. He died at eighty-six, requesting a simple burial.

## Su Zhang (Su Ruwen)

Su Zhang, from Pingling, Fufeng, was an eighth-generation descendant of General Su Jian. His grandfather Su Chun was a respected scholar. Su Zhang was erudite and capable of writing. During Emperor An's reign, he became Yilang for his virtuous countermeasures. As magistrate of Wuyuan, he opened granaries during famine, saving over 3,000 households. As governor of Jizhou, he invited the corrupt prefect of Qinghe for dinner, discussing good deeds before publicly punishing him. He was known for selflessness. As governor of Bingzhou, he was dismissed for opposing the powerful. He hid in the countryside and refused to serve as Henan Yin. He died at home.

## Yang Xu (Yang Xingzu)

Yang Xu, from Pingyang, Taishan, was a descendant of officials. He served as a doctor and joined General Dou Wu's mansion. After Dou Wu's failure, he was imprisoned for over ten years due to party affiliation. When the ban was lifted, he served in the Taiwei Mansion and became prefect of Lujiang. When Yellow Turban bandits attacked Shu, Yang Xu mobilized all men over twenty to fight, defeating the bandits. He later defeated the Anfeng thief Dai Feng, beheading over 3,000 and giving farming tools to the rest. As prefect of Nanyang, he traveled incognito to gather information, then attacked and killed the rebel Zhao Ci. He lived frugally, driving an old horse and broken carriage. When offered fish, he hung it in the court to discourage further gifts. He refused to let his wife and son stay with him, sending them home with only basic necessities. He was nominated for Taiwei but failed due to refusing bribes. He died at forty-eight, requesting a simple burial.

## Jia Wangzong (Jia Mengjian)

Jia Wangzong, from Liaocheng, Dongjun, was promoted to the capital for his political achievements. Jiaozhi was rich in precious products, but corrupt governors exploited the people, causing rebellion. In the first year of Zhongping, Emperor Ling sent Jia Wangzong as governor of Jiaozhi. He investigated, found heavy taxes caused the rebellion, and issued proclamations to settle the people, exempt corvee, execute harmful generals, and select good officials. Within a year, the territory was pacified. The people sang his praises. After three years, he became the best among thirteen states. As governor of Jizhou, he ordered the removal of curtains from his carriage, saying the governor should observe openly. Corrupt officials fled. He died in office as General of Liao Dynasty.

## Lu Kang (Lu Jining)

Lu Kang, from Wu County, was known for righteousness. As magistrate of Gaocheng County, he governed with kindness and trust, eliminating bandits. As prefect of Guiyang and Le'an, he was praised. When Emperor Ling wanted to cast bronze figures and tax land ten yuan per mu during floods and droughts, Lu Kang remonstrated, citing historical examples of how excessive taxes caused disasters. Concubines falsely accused him of disrespect, and he was sent to court in a prison cart. He was dismissed but later became Yilang. When the Lujiang thief Huang Rang gathered over 100,000 people, Lu Kang became prefect of Lujiang, defeated them, and the rest surrendered. During Emperor Xian's reign, he sent tribute to court despite chaos. When Yuan Shu demanded soldiers and armor, Lu Kang refused, preparing defenses. Yuan Shu sent Sun Ce to attack; Lu Kang held out for two years before the city fell. He died a month later at seventy. His youngest son Lu Ji served Wu as governor of Yulin and was known for filial piety, once taking oranges from Yuan Shu's table for his mother.
