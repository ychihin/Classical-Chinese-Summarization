Li Tong, courtesy name Ciyuan, was from Wan County, Nanyang. His family was renowned for business, and his father Li Shou was a tall, imposing man who served Wang Mang. Li Tong worked for General Wuwei and later became magistrate of Wu County, known for his competence. During Wang Mang's late reign, Li Tong heard his father's prophecy about the Liu family's revival with the Li family as assistants. Wealthy and disinclined to serve, he resigned and returned home. When rebellions erupted in Nanyang, Li Tong and his cousin Li Yi discussed joining Liu Boxheng (Liu Yan) and his brother Guangwu (Liu Xiu) to restore the Han. Li Tong sent Li Yi to greet Guangwu, and they formed a pact. They planned to kidnap officials Zhen Fu and Liang Qiuci to spark an uprising. Li Tong sent his cousin's son Li Ji to inform Li Shou in Chang'an, but Li Ji died en route. Li Shou, learning the secret, tried to flee but was advised by Huang Xian to request leave. The plot was discovered; Wang Mang arrested Li Shou and, despite Huang Xian's pleas, executed both and Li Shou's family in Chang'an. Nanyang also killed sixty-four of Li Tong's clan members. Li Tong joined Guangwu and Li Yi in Jiyang, attacking and killing Zhen Fu and Liang Qiuci. Under Gengshi, Li Tong became General of Zhuguo and later King of Xiping. He married Guangwu's sister, Princess Ningping. After Guangwu ascended, Li Tong served as Grand Minister of Agriculture, guarding the capital during campaigns. In Jianwu 5-6, he led troops against Hanzhong bandits and Gongsun Shu, defeating them. As peace prevailed, Li Tong sought to retire due to illness, but Guangwu refused. He became Grand Sikong but remained humble, often taking sick leave. Guangwu favored him, granting his son Li Xiong a marquisate. Li Tong died in Jianwu 18, posthumously titled Gonghou. His lineage continued, and he alone among his kin lived with honor.

Wang Chang, courtesy name Yanqing, from Wuyang, Yingchuan, fled to Jiangxia after avenging his brother. He joined Wang Feng and Wang Kuang in the Lulin uprising, becoming a supporting general. After defeats, he regrouped in Ruoxi Valley and revived his forces, defeating Jingzhou Mu and reaching Nanyang. When Han forces were defeated at Xiaochang'an, Bosheng and Guangwu sought Wang Chang's alliance. Wang Chang, convinced of the Han's destiny, persuaded his reluctant generals Cheng Dan and Zhang to join, citing the people's longing for Han. They united, defeated Zhen Fu and Liang Qiuci, and supported Gengshi. Wang Chang became a court official and marquis, later serving as Nanyang prefect under Gengshi. After Gengshi's fall, he surrendered to Guangwu, who forgave him and appointed him Zuocao. Wang Chang campaigned against various rebels, including Deng Feng and Wei Xiao, and pacified regions. He died in Jianwu 12, posthumously titled Jiehou. His son Wang Guang succeeded but lost the fief due to involvement in a rebellion.

Deng Chen, courtesy name Weiqing, from Xinye, Nanyang, married Guangwu's sister Liu Yuan. During Wang Mang's reign, he heard a prophecy about Liu Xiu becoming emperor and bonded with Guangwu. When Han forces rose, Deng Chen joined Guangwu at Jiyang. After a defeat, Guangwu fled with his sister Boji, but Liu Yuan and her daughters were killed. Deng Chen's home was defiled, yet he remained loyal. Under Gengshi, he became a partial general, fighting alongside Guangwu. He served as Changshan governor, supporting Guangwu during Wang Lang's rebellion. After Guangwu's ascension, Deng Chen became a marquis. He governed Zhongshan and Runan, building irrigation systems that enriched the region. He died in Jianwu 25, posthumously titled Hui. His lineage continued until it ended without heirs.

Lai She, courtesy name Junshe, from Xinye, was the son of an admonishing official who married Guangwu's aunt. His sister married Liu Jia, King of Hanzhong. After Gengshi's fall, Lai She persuaded Liu Jia to join Guangwu, who appointed him Taizhong doctor. Lai She volunteered to negotiate with Wei Xiao, securing his son as hostage. When Wei Xiao hesitated to attack Shu, Lai She angrily confronted him, nearly assassinating him. Wei Xiao imprisoned him but released him due to his reputation. In Jianwu 8, Lai She attacked Lueyang, holding it against Wei Xiao's siege until Guangwu relieved him. He later campaigned against Gongsun Shu and Qiang tribes, pacifying Longxi. In Jianwu 11, he was assassinated by Shu agents. Before dying, he reprimanded General Gai Yan for weeping and dictated a memorial recommending Duan Xiang and pleading for his brother. Guangwu mourned deeply, granting him posthumous honors. His son Lai Xi succeeded, and his brother was made a marquis. The historian praised Lai She's integrity as an envoy, noting his sincerity earned trust without selfish ambition.
