The text details the lives and fates of Emperor Zhang's eight sons, focusing on the lineages of several kings. Liu Qing, King Xiao of Qinghe, was born to Noble Consort Song. His mother and aunt were favored by Emperor Zhang but were framed by Empress Dou after the death of Empress Dowager Ma, leading to their forced suicide. Liu Qing was deposed as crown prince in favor of his half-brother Liu Zhao (Emperor He). Despite this, Liu Qing maintained a close and respectful relationship with Emperor He, who treated him with great favor and trust. Liu Qing was known for his filial piety, caution, and grief over his mother's unjust death, often making private sacrifices. He died at age 29 and was buried in Guangqiu. His son Liu Hu succeeded him but died without heirs, leading to the appointment of Liu Yanping, son of Liu Chong, as King of Qinghe. Later, Liu Qing was posthumously honored as Emperor Xiaode, and his mother as Empress Jingyin.

Liu Shou, King Hui of Jibei, was born to Noble Consort Shen. He was granted his kingdom in the second year of Yongyuan. Emperor He, following his father's customs, kept his brothers in the capital, and they were very close. Liu Shou died after 31 years as king. His descendants included Liu Deng, Liu Duo (who died without sons), and Liu Anguo, who was appointed king. Liu Ci, son of Liu Anguo, was noted for his extreme filial piety during his father's mourning, for which Empress Dowager Liang granted him an additional 5,000 households. The Jibei line ended when Liu Zheng died without sons in the eleventh year of Jian'an.

Liu Kai, King Xiao of Hejian, was granted his kingdom in the second year of Yongyuan. He was known for abiding by the law and was respected by his subordinates. He died after 42 years as king. His son Liu Zheng was arrogant and cruel but was reformed by the strict Prime Minister Shen Jing. Liu Zheng's descendants included Liu Jian, Liu Li, and Liu Ye, who was made Marquis of Chongde when Wei accepted the Han abdication. Liu Yi, a son of Liu Kai, was initially favored by Empress Dowager Deng but was later framed and demoted. He eventually became the father of Emperor Huan, who honored Liu Kai as Emperor Xiaomu. Liu Shu, another son of Liu Kai, was posthumously honored as Emperor Xiaoyuan by his son, Emperor Ling.

The text also briefly mentions other sons of Emperor Zhang: Liu Quan, King of Qiancheng, who died without heirs; Liu Shu, King Huai of Chengyang, who died without children; Liu Wansui, King Ai of Guangzong, who died the same year he was enfeoffed; and Liu Sheng, King Huai of Pingyuan, who died young without heirs. The historian's commentary notes that the descendants of Emperor Zhang, known for their virtue and honesty, became the rulers of the Han dynasty, fulfilling an ancient saying.
