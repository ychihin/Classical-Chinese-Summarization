# Summary of Chen Fan's Biography

Chen Fan, courtesy name Zhongju, was born in Pingyu, Runan. His grandfather served as Governor of Hedong. At fifteen, he lived in a dirty room. When his father's friend Xue Qin asked why he didn't clean it, Chen Fan replied that a real man should sweep away the world's rubbish, not just his own room. Xue Qin admired his ambition.

Chen Fan began as a county official, was elected Xiaolian, and became a doctor. After his mother's death, he resigned to mourn. When the mourning period ended, Governor Zhou Jing summoned him, but Chen Fan left due to differing opinions. He later refused government recruitment. Taiwei Li Gu recommended him, and he became Prefect of Le'an. He remained in office while others fled from strict Governor Li Ying. Chen Fan recruited the reclusive Zhou Chu, treating him with great respect. He exposed Zhao Xuan's false filial piety—Zhao had lived in a tomb for over twenty years but fathered five children there—and punished him for deceiving the world.

When General Liang Ji sent a messenger requesting private favors, Chen Fan beat the messenger to death and was sentenced to military service. He later became a minister. When mountain bandits threatened Lingling and Guiyang, Chen Fan argued against sending troops, stating that corrupt officials caused the unrest and that replacing them with virtuous men would resolve the problem without military action. He also criticized the expedient recommendation of talents. Because he offended the emperor's associates, he was sent to be Governor of Yuzhang.

Chen Fan was promoted to Dahonglu. When Emperor Huan wanted to execute Li Yun for blunt remonstrance, Chen Fan wrote to save him and was dismissed. He was later reinstated as Guangluxun. He advised Emperor Huan against excessive titles and rewards, particularly for Deng Zun and Huang Jun, warning that improper enfeoffments caused cosmic disorder, crop failure, and poverty. He noted that thousands of palace maids impoverished the country and urged their release. Emperor Huan adopted some advice, releasing five hundred maids and limiting titles.

Chen Fan advised against Emperor Huan's hunting at Guangcheng Garden, citing the "Three Empties"—empty fields, court, and warehouses—and ongoing wars. The emperor ignored him. As Guangluxun, Chen Fan and Huang Wan conducted elections without favoring the powerful, leading to false accusations and his dismissal. He was soon recalled as a servant and later became Taiwei, though he declined, citing his inferiority to Hu Guang, Wang Chang, and Li Ying.

Chen Fan defended Li Ying and others punished for violating the emperor's will, weeping as he pleaded. He argued for leniency toward officials who executed eunuch associates despite pardons, comparing them to loyal ministers of old. Emperor Huan grew angrier and ignored him. The eunuchs hated Chen Fan, but because of his reputation, they dared not harm him directly.

When Li Ying was imprisoned for party affiliation, Chen Fan wrote a strong remonstrance, comparing the persecution to Qin's book burning. He warned that punishing the innocent would cause chaos and noted that droughts and famines plagued the land while the palace hoarded wealth. Emperor Huan dismissed Chen Fan for allegedly selecting inappropriate talents.

After Emperor Huan's death, Empress Dowager Dou appointed Chen Fan as Grand Tutor. When ministers feigned illness during the succession crisis, Chen Fan reproached them, and they returned to court. Emperor Ling granted Chen Fan the title of Marquis of Gaoxiang with three hundred households, but Chen Fan declined ten times.

Chen Fan and General Dou Wu planned to eliminate the eunuchs. Chen Fan warned the Empress Dowager about eunuch corruption, but she ignored him. When the plot was exposed, Cao Jie forged orders to kill Dou Wu. The seventy-year-old Chen Fan led over eighty officials and students into the palace, shouting against the eunuch rebellion. He was arrested, kicked and stepped on by eunuch soldiers, and killed that same day. His family was exiled, and his clan and disciples were imprisoned. His friend Zhu Zhen collected and buried his body, hiding Chen Fan's son Chen Yi. Zhu Zhen endured torture without revealing the son's location. Chen Yi later became Prime Minister of Lu.

The historian commented that during Emperors Huan and Ling's reigns, Chen Fan established a reputation for moral integrity, speaking out against corruption and competing with eunuchs despite great danger. Though his plans failed, his faith sustained the people, and the Han dynasty survived over a hundred years due to his efforts.
