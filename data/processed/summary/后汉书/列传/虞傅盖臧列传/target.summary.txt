Yu Xu, from Wuping in Chen, was known for his integrity. His grandfather, a prison officer, handled cases with compassion. Yu Xu was orphaned young and cared for his grandmother, refusing an official post until after her death. He later served as a doctor. When General Deng Zhi proposed abandoning Liangzhou due to Qiang rebellions, Yu Xu argued against it, stating it would expose the imperial tombs and that Liangzhou's people were a vital defense. He suggested controlling local leaders by giving their sons official posts to prevent rebellion. Deng Zhi, resentful of Yu Xu's opposition, sent him to govern Chaoge, a bandit-ridden area. Yu Xu used deception, recruiting former bandits to ambush others and marking thieves' clothes with silk threads, leading to their dispersal. Promoted to Wudu's prefect, he again used deception against the Qiang, increasing his campfires to feign strength and marching rapidly. He then used a strategy of weak arrows to lure the Qiang into range of his strong crossbows, defeating them. He improved the county by building fortifications, opening waterways for transport, and increasing the population. As Sili Xiaowei, he impeached powerful officials, earning their hatred. He was framed and sentenced to hard labor, but eunuch Sun Cheng defended him, leading to his release and promotion. He later abolished the practice of "righteous money" and argued for fair treatment of petitioners. He remained upright despite nine reprimands and three punishments, dying as Shangshu Ling. On his deathbed, he regretted killing many bandits at Chaoge, believing it brought divine punishment.

Fu Xie, from Lingzhou, was known for his caution in speech and action. He served as Sima under Huangfu Song against Zhang Jiao's Yellow Turbans. Before the campaign, he warned the emperor that the real threat was internal, from eunuchs like Zhao Zhong, and that evil and good cannot coexist. Despite his merits, Zhao Zhong framed him, but the emperor spared him. Later, when Situ Cui Lie proposed abandoning Liangzhou during a Xiqiang rebellion, Fu Xie vehemently opposed him, arguing that Liangzhou was the empire's shield and that abandoning it would empower the barbarians. The emperor agreed. Fu Xie refused to curry favor with the powerful eunuch Zhao Zhong, even when offered a marquisate. As Hanyang's governor, he pacified the Qiang. He warned Governor Geng Bi against attacking Han Sui with untrained troops, predicting disaster. Geng Bi ignored him and was killed. When Hanyang was besieged, Fu Xie refused to abandon the city despite his son's pleas and the offer of safe passage from grateful Hu cavalry. He chose to die in battle, cursing a defecting official. His posthumous title was Zhuangjiehou.

Gai Xun, from Dunhuang, was a Xiaolian and Hanyang's chief historian. He refused to take revenge on an enemy, Su Zhenghe, when the governor wanted to kill him to cover for a corrupt prefect, arguing it was wrong to kill a good man. He later remonstrated with Governor Zuo Chang for embezzling funds, but Zuo Chang sent him to a dangerous post. Gai Xun survived and later rescued Zuo Chang from a siege by Bian Zhang. He criticized Governor Song Xiao's plan to teach the *Classic of Filial Piety* to pacify the border, calling it impractical. During a battle against Qiang rebels, he was wounded but refused to flee, cursing his captors. The Qiang, respecting his courage, released him. As a famine relief official, he used his own grain to save over a thousand people. Summoned by Emperor Ling, he blamed eunuchs for the world's chaos. He planned with Liu Yu and Yuan Shao to kill the eunuchs, but was made Jing Zhaoyin instead. There, he prosecuted a corrupt official despite pleas from nobles. After Emperor Ling's death, he wrote to Dong Zhuo, criticizing his deposition of the young emperor. He openly defied Dong Zhuo in court, refusing to bow. He died of illness at 51, refusing Dong Zhuo's funeral gifts.

Zang Hong, from Guangling, was made a Dong Zilang due to his father's military merits. After Dong Zhuo killed the emperor, Zang Hong urged Prefect Zhang Chao to raise an army. He helped organize an alliance at Suanzao, where he gave a passionate oath, calling for the destruction of Dong Zhuo. The alliance, however, failed to act decisively. Later, Zang Hong served Yuan Shao as Qingzhou's governor, pacifying the region. When Cao Cao besieged Zhang Chao, Zang Hong begged Yuan Shao for troops to rescue him, but Yuan Shao refused. Zhang Chao was killed. Enraged, Zang Hong broke with Yuan Shao, who then besieged him. Despite a letter from Chen Lin urging surrender, Zang Hong refused, accusing Yuan Shao of betraying allies and killing loyal men. He stated he would rather die for righteousness. During the siege, he shared his last food with his soldiers and even killed his concubine to feed them. The city fell, and Zang Hong was captured. He defiantly cursed Yuan Shao for his treachery and was executed. His subordinate Chen Rong also chose to die with him.
