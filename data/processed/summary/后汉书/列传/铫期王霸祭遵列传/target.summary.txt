# Summary of Historical Records: Yao Qi, Wang Ba, and Ji Zun

**Yao Qi** (courtesy name Ci Kuang) was born in Jiaxian County, Yingchuan. Standing eight feet two inches tall with a striking appearance, he was known for solemnity and majesty. After his father Yao Meng, Governor of Guiyang, died, Yao Qi observed three years of mourning, earning village praise. When Guangwu raided Yingchuan, he recruited Yao Qi to attack Ji. During Wang Lang's rebellion, Yao Qi cleared crowded roads by brandishing his halberd and shouting, broke through a closed city gate, and later served under Deng Yu. Deng Yu recognized Yao Qi's capability, making him a partial general with 2,000 soldiers. Yao Qi captured several counties, fought bravely against Wang Lang's forces—killing over fifty enemies despite a forehead wound—and was appointed General Huya after Wang Lang's death. He advised Guangwu to consolidate Hebei's elite forces, and Guangwu smiled at his boldness. Yao Qi fought the Bronze Horse rebels, killing many despite initial setbacks, and later defeated bandits attacking his baggage, sustaining three wounds. After Guangwu ascended the throne, Yao Qi became Marquis of Ancheng with 5,000 households. As Prefect of Wei County, he suppressed Zhuo Jing's rebellion, beheading over 600, and pacified the region. When Li Lu plotted rebellion, Yao Qi showed mercy to Li Xiong, leading Li Lu to suicide from shame. Yao Qi served as Taizhong大夫 and Weiwei, known for integrity and remonstrating with the emperor, once stopping Guangwu from careless travel. He died in the tenth year of Jianwu, receiving posthumous honors as Marquis Zhong.

**Wang Ba** (courtesy name Yuan Bo) was from Yingyang, Yingchuan. His family valued legal traditions, and he worked as a jailer before studying in Chang'an. When Guangwu passed through Yingyang, Wang Ba joined his army, fighting at Kunyang. Despite others leaving, Wang Ba remained loyal; Guangwu praised him, saying "The strong wind knows the strong grass." During Wang Lang's rebellion, Wang Ba falsely claimed the Hutuo River was frozen to calm frightened troops, and the ice miraculously formed, allowing crossing. He became a military commander and Marquis of Guan Nei, later capturing Wang Lang's seal. Known for comforting soldiers, he was made a partial general after Guangwu's ascension. In the fourth year of Jianwu, Wang Ba employed strategic patience against Su Mao, closing his camp and refusing battle until the enemy tired, then launching a surprise attack that routed them. He served as General Against Captives, fought Xiongnu forces at Gaoliu, and became Prefect of Shanggu. For over twenty years, he managed border defenses, built roads and barriers, and advocated peace with the Xiongnu. He died in the second year of Yongping, having been granted Marquis of Huailing.

**Ji Zun** (younger brother's grandson) was from Yingyang, Yingchuan. Though wealthy, he was respectful and frugal. After killing a bullying government official, he joined Guangwu. As a military commander, he executed Guangwu's own son for breaking the law, earning Guangwu's respect. He became a partial general and Marquis of Yingyang. Ji Zun fought bravely despite arrow wounds, defeated Zhang Man by cutting his food supply, and captured him alive. He attacked Zhang Feng, whose rebellion stemmed from a Taoist priest's deception about a jade seal. Ji Zun resisted Peng Chong for over a year, and after Peng's death, pacified his territory. In the sixth year of Jianwu, he argued for direct attack against Kui Xiao, serving as vanguard. He remained steadfast when others retreated, dying in the army in the ninth year of Jianwu. Ji Zun was known for self-denial, giving rewards to soldiers, owning no private property, and wearing plain clothes. Guangwu mourned deeply, personally attending his funeral. Posthumously named Marquis Cheng, Ji Zun was praised for his purity, loyalty, and Confucian conduct—selecting talents by Confucian standards, reciting poetry, and establishing a Confucian temple. He had no children, so his title was abolished.
