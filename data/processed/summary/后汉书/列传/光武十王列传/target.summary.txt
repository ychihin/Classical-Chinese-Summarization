Emperor Guangwu had eleven sons. Queen Guo bore him several sons, including Liu Qiang, who was initially made crown prince. In the 17th year of Jianwu, Empress Guo was deposed, causing Liu Qiang great anxiety. He repeatedly expressed his wish to step down as crown prince and become a vassal king. Emperor Guangwu eventually agreed in the 19th year, naming him King of Donghai. The emperor, believing Liu Qiang was not at fault, granted him an exceptionally large fief of 29 counties, along with special honors like tiger-head talismans and palace bells and drums. Even after arriving at his fief, Liu Qiang repeatedly tried to return lands, but the emperor refused. He was later allowed to make Lu his capital. Liu Qiang died in the 1st year of Yongping. On his deathbed, he wrote a grateful and humble letter to the emperor, expressing his unworthiness of the honors and his concern for his son. The emperor was deeply saddened and ordered a grand funeral with special rites, attended by many nobles. To honor Liu Qiang's modesty, the emperor decreed a simple burial.

Liu Fu, another son, was made King of Pei. He was known for his dignified conduct and love for the classics, authoring the "Treatise on the Five Classics," which was called the "Pei Wang Tong Lun." He was cautious and disciplined, earning the title of a wise king. He died after 46 years of rule.

Liu Ying, King of Chu, was the son of a less favored concubine. He was fond of Huangdi and Laozi and studied Buddhism. In the 8th year of Yongping, he offered silk to atone for his sins, but the emperor returned it. Later, he was accused of treason for making talismans and fabricating prophecies. The emperor deposed him and moved him to Danyang, where he committed suicide. His kingdom was abolished. The ensuing investigation implicated many, leading to thousands of executions and exiles.

Liu Kang, King An of Jinan, was known for not following laws and socializing with guests. He was accused of conspiracy, but the emperor, due to their kinship, only reduced his fief. He later became extravagant, with many slaves, horses, and private lands. A governor admonished him, but he did not change his ways. He died after 59 years of rule.

Liu Cang, King Xian of Dongping, was intelligent and well-read. He served as General of Hussars under Emperor Xianzong and helped establish ritual systems. He often advised the emperor, once persuading him to cancel a hunting trip. He repeatedly asked to resign from his high post, which the emperor eventually allowed. He was known for his virtue and was greatly respected. The emperor frequently sought his advice on state matters. He died in the 2nd year of Jianchu, and his son succeeded him.

Liu Yan, King Zhi of Fuling, was arrogant and harsh. He was accused of making prophecies and was demoted to Marquis of Fuling. He later plotted rebellion again and was demoted again. He was eventually restored as king by Emperor Zhanghe.

Liu Jing, King Si of Guangling, was impatient and insidious. After Emperor Guangwu's death, he wrote a seditious letter to Liu Qiang, urging him to rebel. Liu Qiang reported him. The emperor concealed the matter. Liu Jing later consulted fortune tellers and claimed to resemble the late emperor, leading to further investigations. He eventually committed suicide.

Liu Heng, Duke Huai of Linhuai, died before becoming king, and his state was abolished.

Liu Yan, King Jian of Zhongshan, was the youngest son of Empress Dowager Guo and stayed in the capital. He was favored by the emperor. He once hanged his concubine and was punished by losing a county. He died in the 2nd year of Yongyuan.

Liu Jing, King Xiao of Langya, was respectful and filial, fond of Confucian classics. He was especially favored by Emperor Xianzong. He built magnificent palaces and wrote poems praising virtues. He died after 31 years of rule.
