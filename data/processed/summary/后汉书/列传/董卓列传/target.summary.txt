Dong Zhuo, a man of rough and scheming temperament from Lintao, Longxi, rose to prominence through his interactions with Qiang leaders, distributing rewards to soldiers and gaining a reputation for bravery. He served as a military commander under Zhang Huan, defeating rebellious Qiang forces, and was later appointed to various posts, including Governor of Bingzhou and Hedong. During the Zhongping era, he was tasked with suppressing rebellions, including those led by Bian Zhang and Han Sui, but faced defeats. Despite setbacks, he managed to preserve his army and was granted the title of Marquis of Lianxiang.

After Emperor Ling's death, Dong Zhuo was summoned to the capital by He Jin and Yuan Shao to intimidate the Empress Dowager. He entered Luoyang with a small force but cleverly expanded his army by integrating defectors, including troops from He Jin and He Miao. He deposed the young emperor, established Emperor Xian, and assumed the role of Prime Minister, wielding immense power. His rule was marked by brutality, including the massacre of civilians, looting, and the destruction of the economy through coinage reforms. He also moved the capital to Chang'an, forcing millions to relocate, causing widespread death and destruction.

Dong Zhuo's tyranny led to a coalition of regional governors, including Yuan Shao and Sun Jian, rising against him. He was eventually assassinated in a plot by Wang Yun and Lü Bu in 192 AD. His death was followed by chaos, as his former subordinates Li Jue and Guo Si seized control of Chang'an, leading to further violence and famine. The capital was devastated, with widespread cannibalism and depopulation.

The aftermath saw continued infighting among Dong Zhuo's former allies and the rise of Cao Cao, who eventually took control of the imperial court.
