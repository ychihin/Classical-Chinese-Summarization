**Summary of Historical Records: Liang Tong and Liang Ji**

Liang Tong, courtesy name Zhongning, was from Anding Wushi. His ancestor Liang Yi'er was a Jin Dynasty doctor. His grandfather Zidu moved from Hedong northward, and his son Liangqiao relocated to Maoling with great wealth, returning to Anding during the reigns of Emperors Ai and Ping. Liang Tong had a resolute character and loved law. He began his official career in state and county posts. In the second year of Gengshi's reign, he was summoned as General Zhonglang and appointed Governor of Jiuquan. After the reform failed, the Red Eyebrow Army entered Chang'an. Liang Tong, Dou Rong, and other governors raised troops to defend their territory. When they discussed establishing a commander-in-chief, all recommended Liang Tong, but he firmly declined, citing his lack of virtue and ability. Thus, Dou Rong was elected General of Hexi and Governor of Wuwei. Liang Tong's strict political views earned him prestige in neighboring counties.

In the fifth year of Jianwu's reign, Liang Tong and others sent envoys to the imperial court with Dou Rong's chief minister Liu Jun to pay tribute. The emperor conferred on Liang Tong the title of General Xuande. In the eighth year, Emperor Guangwu personally conquered Kui Xiao, and Liang Tong and Dou Rong led soldiers to join him. After Kui Xiao's defeat, Guangwu granted Liang Tong the title of Marquis of Chengyi, and his brother Liang Xun and cousin Liang Teng were also made marquises. In the twelfth year, Liang Tong and Dou Rong came to the capital as marquises. Liang Tong was granted the title of Gaoshan Marquis, appointed Taizhong大夫, and his four sons were named Lang. While in court, Liang Tong repeatedly reported beneficial matters. He believed the law was too light, increasing crime, and advocated for stricter punishments according to old laws. He submitted a memorial arguing that lenient punishments under Emperors Yuan and Ai had led to lawlessness and increased crime. He cited historical precedents from the Five Emperors, Three Kings, and Han emperors, urging the adoption of stricter measures. However, the matter was referred to the Sangong and Tingwei, who deemed harsh punishments unnecessary and refused to change the old law. Liang Tong wrote again, clarifying he did not seek severe punishment but adherence to old rules. He argued that mitigating punishment had caused rampant theft and rebellion, citing recent outbreaks of banditry. His memorial was sent but not reported. Later, Liang Tong served as Prefect of Jiujiang and was granted the title of Marquis of Lingxiang. He achieved great governance in the county, earning the fear and love of officials and people, and died in office.

Liang Ji, courtesy name Bozhuo, had a hunched back like an eagle's wings and upright eyes like a jackal. He was idle and unruly from childhood, fond of drinking, archery, gambling, and racing. He began as Minister of Huangmen and rose to various military and police posts. In the first year of Emperor Shun's Yonghe reign, he was appointed Yin of Henan, where he was brutal and illegal. His father Liang Shang's friend, Lu Fang, criticized Liang Ji, leading Liang Ji to have Lu Fang assassinated. He then framed Lu Fang's enemies and had Lu Fang's brother capture them, killing over a hundred people. After Liang Shang's death, Emperor Shun appointed Liang Ji as General and his brother Liang Buyi as Yin of Henan. When Emperor Shun died, the Queen Mother ordered Liang Ji, Taifu Zhao Jun, and Taiwei Li Gu to manage government affairs. Liang Ji became more extravagant and cruel. After Emperor Chong died, Liang Ji welcomed Emperor Zhi, who was young and intelligent. When Emperor Zhi called Liang Ji a "domineering general," Liang Ji poisoned him. He then welcomed Emperor Huan and framed Li Gu and Du Qiao.

Emperor Huan granted Liang Ji 13,000 households and increased his staff. Liang Buyi and others were also enfeoffed. In the first year of peace, Liang Ji received another 10,000 households, totaling 30,000. His wife Sun Shou was granted the title of Lord of Xiangcheng. Sun Shou was beautiful and seductive, and Liang Ji doted on and feared her. Liang Ji had an affair with You Tongqi, a former concubine of his father, and she bore him a son. Sun Shou killed You Tongqi's family, and Liang Ji hid the child. Liang Ji also favored a servant named Qin Gong, who had an affair with Sun Shou. Liang Ji and Sun Shou amassed immense wealth through extortion and bribery, confiscating property from wealthy families. They built grand mansions with lavish decorations, gardens, and rare animals. Liang Ji's power grew unchecked, with all court matters decided by him. He killed officials who opposed him, including Wu Shu and Hou Meng. A young doctor named Yuan Zhu wrote to the emperor criticizing Liang Ji's power, but Liang Ji had him beaten to death. Liang Ji also killed many others, including Hao Xie and Hu Wu's families. Liang Buyi, who loved scriptures and treated others well, was estranged by Liang Ji and resigned. Liang Ji's family included seven marquises, three queens, six nobles, two generals, and many other officials. Liang Ji held power for over 20 years, dominating the court. Emperor Huan, angered by Liang Ji's arrogance, plotted with eunuchs to kill him. In the first year of Yanxi, after a series of events, Emperor Huan ordered the arrest of Liang Ji. Liang Ji and Sun Shou committed suicide. Over 300 of his officials and guests were dismissed. His property, worth over three billion, was confiscated and used to reduce taxes. His gardens were given to the poor.
