# Summary of Historical Biographies

**Zheng Taichuan (Zheng Gongye)** was born in Kaifeng, Henan, as the great-grandson of Sinong Zheng Zhong. Talented from youth, his mother foresaw chaos in the final years of Emperor Ling's reign and secretly befriended heroes. Despite owning 400 hectares of land, he often lacked food. He was renowned throughout Shandong. Initially recommended for office, he declined multiple appointments. When General He Jin recruited him as Minister of the Secretariat and Censor, Zheng warned against recruiting Dong Zhuo, predicting his violent ambition would endanger the court. He Jin ignored this advice, so Zheng resigned. After He Jin was killed and Dong Zhuo rebelled, Zheng conspired with others to oppose Dong Zhuo. When Dong Zhuo wanted to attack the rebels, Zheng argued that governing depends on virtue, not military might. Fearing Dong Zhuo's anger, he fabricated a flattering analysis listing ten reasons why Dong Zhuo would defeat the rebels. Dong Zhuo appointed him general, but others warned of Zheng's cunning, so Dong Zhuo revoked the command. Zheng later plotted to kill Dong Zhuo, but the plan leaked. He escaped to Yuan Shu, who appointed him Governor of Yangzhou, but he died en route at age forty-one.

**Kong Rong**, a descendant of Confucius, showed exceptional intelligence as a child. At ten, he cleverly gained an audience with Li Ying by claiming family friendship through Confucius and Laozi. When Chen Wei dismissed him, Kong retorted sharply. At thirteen, his father's death caused such grief he needed support to stand. At sixteen, he sheltered the fugitive Zhang Jian, leading to his brother's execution when the family competed to take blame. Kong Rong became famous and was recruited as an official. He criticized corrupt eunuch relatives, offended He Jin, and later opposed Dong Zhuo's deposition of the Young Emperor. Dong Zhuo sent him to troubled Beihai as punishment. There, Kong Rong built cities, schools, promoted Confucianism, and recommended worthy men. When surrounded by Yellow Turban rebels, Liu Bei rescued him, amazed that Kong Rong knew of him. Kong Rong refused to ally with Yuan Shao or Cao Cao, knowing they would usurp the Han. After six years, Liu Bei recommended him as Qingzhou governor. Yuan Tan attacked; though outnumbered, Kong Rong remained calm, reading and laughing until the city fell. He fled, but his wife was captured. Later serving Emperor Xian, Kong Rong often made court decisions. He opposed corporal punishment, argued against publicizing Liu Biao's treason, and criticized Cao Cao's massacre of Yecheng and his son's marriage to Yuan Xi's wife. He mocked Cao Cao's campaigns and opposed his alcohol ban. Cao Cao, though outwardly tolerant, grew jealous. Eventually, Cao Cao had Kong Rong executed for treason at fifty-six. His children were also killed; his seven-year-old daughter and nine-year-old son calmly accepted death, saying a destroyed nest cannot have unbroken eggs.

**Xun Yu**, grandson of Magistrate Xun Shu, was recognized as extraordinary by He Yong. Recommended as Xiaolian, he abandoned office when Dong Zhuo rebelled. He warned his clan to flee Yingchuan, predicting war; those who stayed were later killed. He joined Yuan Shao but, seeing the Han's chaos, left for Cao Cao, who called him "my Zhang Liang." When Zhang Miao and Chen Gong rebelled, Xun Yu defended Zhen City, outsmarting their deception and personally meeting Guo Gong to prevent his alliance with rebels. He secured three cities for Cao Cao's return. Xun Yu advised Cao Cao to stabilize Yanzhou before attacking Xuzhou, leading to Lu Bu's defeat. He urged Cao Cao to welcome Emperor Xian to Xuchang, arguing this would bring legitimacy. Xun Yu recommended talented men like Xun You, Zhong Yao, and Guo Jia. He predicted Yuan Shao's defeat despite his strength, correctly assessing his generals' weaknesses. During the Guandu stalemate, when Cao Cao wanted to retreat, Xun Yu insisted on holding position, leading to Yuan Shao's defeat. He advised against attacking Liu Biao before pacifying Yuan Shao's remnants. When Cao Cao wanted to restore the ancient Jiuzhou system, Xun Yu warned it would cause panic, and Cao Cao abandoned the plan. Cao Cao praised Xun Yu's strategies as turning defeat into victory. When Dong Zhao proposed making Cao Cao Duke of Jin, Xun Yu opposed it, saying Cao Cao should remain loyal to Han. Cao Cao grew resentful. During a southern campaign, Cao Cao kept Xun Yu at his camp, then sent him an empty food box. Xun Yu took poison and died at fifty. The next year, Cao Cao declared himself Duke of Wei.
