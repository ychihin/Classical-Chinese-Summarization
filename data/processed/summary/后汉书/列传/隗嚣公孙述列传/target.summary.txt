### Summary of the Historical Text on Kui Xiao and Gongsun Shu

Kui Xiao, courtesy name Jimeng, was from Chengji County, Tianshui. He began his career as a local official and later served under Wang Mang's advisor Liu Xin. After Liu Xin's death, Kui Xiao returned home. His uncle, Kui Cui, a bold and chivalrous figure, planned to raise troops to support the Han dynasty after hearing of Gengshi's proclamation and Wang Mang's defeat. Kui Xiao initially advised against it, warning of the dangers of war, but Kui Cui proceeded, gathering thousands to attack Pingxiang. The group then appointed Kui Xiao as general due to his reputation and knowledge of scriptures. Kui Xiao accepted on condition that his advice be heeded.

Following Fang Wang's counsel, Kui Xiao built a temple to honor Han emperors Gaozu, Taizong, and Shizong, where he and his allies performed rituals and swore an oath to assist Liu Zong, invoking divine punishment for betrayal. They issued a proclamation condemning Wang Mang for crimes against heaven, earth, and humanity, including usurpation, tyranny, and exploitation. Kui Xiao then led 100,000 troops, killing Yongzhou shepherd Chen Qing and capturing Anding after executing Wang Xiang. He later seized Longxi, Wudu, Jincheng, and other commanderies.

In the second year of Gengshi, Kui Xiao was summoned to Chang'an and appointed Right General. After his uncle and brother plotted rebellion, Kui Xiao reported them, leading to their deaths, and was made Imperial Censor. When Chimei rebels threatened, Kui Xiao suggested Gengshi delegate power to Liu Liang, but was ignored. He later participated in a plot to kidnap Gengshi, which failed, forcing him to flee to Tianshui, where he declared himself General of Xizhou. He attracted many scholars and officials, gaining fame in the west.

Under Guangwu, Kui Xiao initially cooperated, defeating Chimei and aiding General Feng Yi. Guangwu praised him in letters, offering rewards and urging unity against Gongsun Shu. However, Kui Xiao's generals, Wang Yuan and Wang Jie, advised him to maintain independence, citing the dangers of submitting to Guangwu. Kui Xiao agreed, sending his son as a hostage but secretly planning to dominate the region. Over time, his support waned as Guangwu's forces grew.

In the sixth year of Jianwu, Guangwu ordered Kui Xiao to attack Shu, but he refused, citing difficult terrain. Guangwu then led a campaign against him, but Kui Xiao resisted, leading to defeats. He apologized but remained defiant. Guangwu's attempts to recruit him failed, and Kui Xiao allied with Gongsun Shu, who named him King of Shuoning. Kui Xiao invaded Anding but was repelled. Many of his generals, including Wang Zun, defected to Guangwu.

In the eighth year, Guangwu's forces besieged Kui Xiao, who fell ill and died of anger. His son Kui Chun was made king but later surrendered. Kui Xiao's legacy was marked by his initial righteousness but eventual failure due to isolation and overreliance on natural defenses.

Gongsun Shu, courtesy name Ziyang, from Maoling, rose to power during Wang Mang's decline. He defeated local rebels and declared himself King of Shu, later emperor, citing prophecies and omens. He expanded his territory, gathering troops and resources. Despite Guangwu's letters urging surrender, Gongsun Shu refused, believing in his destiny. His rule was marred by cruelty and poor governance, leading to internal dissent.

In the eleventh year, Guangwu's generals attacked, defeating Gongsun Shu's forces. He was wounded in battle and died, leading to the fall of his regime. Guangwu condemned the subsequent massacre of Gongsun Shu's family and officials. Historians noted that Gongsun Shu's downfall stemmed from his reliance on geography rather than virtue, echoing past failures.
