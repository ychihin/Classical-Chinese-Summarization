Cui Xiang, courtesy name Tingbo, was from Anping, Zhuo County. His great-great-grandfather Cui Chao served Youzhou under Emperor Zhao, advising the governor against associating with King Yan Ci. After failing to assassinate the king, he was promoted to Shi Yushi. His son Cui Shu governed four counties capably. Cui Xiang, Cui Shu's youngest son, was summoned by Ming Jing during Wang Mang's era. When Taibao Zhen Feng appointed him infantry captain, Cui Xiang refused, stating that conquering a country does not require benevolent people or Confucian scholars, and resigned. Wang Mang, who hated dissent, favored Cui Xiang's brother Cui Fa, making him Da Sikong. Cui Xiang's mother was a Confucian scholar honored as Mrs. Yicheng. Later, Cui Xiang was forced to become Grand Yin of Jianxin. He reluctantly served, claiming illness and neglecting duties for three years. After releasing over 2,000 prisoners through fair trials, he resigned, saying he would rather die than harm innocents. In the early Jianwu years, he refused a recommendation due to shame over his clan's ties to Wang Mang, living in Xingyang and writing sixty-four chapters of the "Book of Changes" before his death.

Cui Xiang's son Cui Yi was reclusive due to illness. Cui Yi's son Cui Xiang (the younger) was proficient in classics at thirteen, studied at Taixue, and was famed alongside Ban Gu and Fu Yi. He wrote "Da Zhi" to defend his reclusive nature. During the Yuanhe period, he praised Emperor Suzong with "Ode to the Four Patrols." The emperor admired his work and urged Dou Xian to meet him. Cui Xiang became Dou Xian's guest, but after the emperor's death, he warned Dou Xian against arrogance, citing historical examples. When Dou Xian became general, he appointed Cui Xiang as a courier. Cui Xiang repeatedly admonished Dou Xian for his excesses, leading to alienation. He was made magistrate of Changling but refused and died at home in 92 AD, leaving numerous writings.

Cui Yuan, courtesy name Ziyu, lost his father early and studied under Jia Kui, mastering classics and astrology. He avenged his brother's murder by fleeing, later returning after an amnesty. Poor, he lived with brothers for decades. At forty, he became a county official but was imprisoned. During imprisonment, he studied the "Book of Rites." After release, he served under generals Deng Zun and Yan Xian. When Empress Dowager Yan regented, Cui Yuan advised Yan Xian to depose the young emperor and install King Jiyin, but Yan refused. After Emperor Shun's rise, Yan Xian was killed, and Cui Yuan was ostracized. He later served as county magistrate, cultivating rice fields for seven years. Recommended as a virtuous scholar, he became prime minister of Jibei. Accused of embezzlement, he defended himself and was released. He died at sixty-six, requesting a simple burial in Luoyang. He wrote fifty-seven works, including "Nanyang Literary Official Chronicle," and was known for his hospitality despite poverty.

Cui Chen, courtesy name Zizhen, was quiet and studious. After his father's death, he mourned at the tomb. Refusing official invitations, he wrote "Political Commentary" criticizing contemporary governance. He argued for adapting policies to circumstances, citing historical examples like Emperor Xuan's strict laws versus Emperor Yuan's laxity. He advocated severe punishment for troubled times, comparing it to medicine. He served as governor of Wuyuan, teaching weaving to locals and strengthening defenses against Xiongnu. Later, as Yilang, he reviewed classics but was imprisoned for ties to Liang Ji. After release, he became governor of Liaodong but resigned due to his mother's death. He died during the Jianning period, impoverished, with his burial funded by officials. He wrote fifteen works.
