# Summary of Historical Records: Huan Tan and Feng Yan

Huan Tan, courtesy name Junshan, was from Xiangxian County, Peiguo. He was appointed as a minister through his father's connections. He was knowledgeable in the Five Classics, wrote excellent essays, and favored ancient learning. He debated with Liu Xin and Yang Xiong, and enjoyed criticizing secular Confucian scholars, which led to his ostracism. During the reigns of Emperor Ai and Emperor Ping, his status remained low.

Fu Yan, Marquis of Kongxiang, valued Huan Tan. When Emperor Ai favored Dong Xian and his sister Zhaoyi over Empress Fu, Huan Tan warned Fu Yan of potential danger, advising him to dismiss guests and remain humble. Fu Yan followed this advice, and when Dong Xian later attempted to incriminate the Fu family, no evidence was found, saving them. Dong Xian later sought Huan Tan's friendship, but Huan Tan refused, criticizing Dong Xian's inability to protect himself through proper governance. During Wang Mang's usurpation, Huan Tan remained silent while others praised Wang Mang.

After Emperor Guangwu (Shizu) ascended, Huan Tan served as a郎官. He submitted memorials on governance, emphasizing the importance of wise advisors, harmony between ruler and ministers, and adapting policies to circumstances. He criticized harsh laws, private revenge killings, usury by merchants, and inconsistent legal punishments. He advocated for unified laws, agricultural development, and suppressing commerce. His proposals were not accepted.

The emperor believed in prophecies. Huan Tan submitted another memorial denouncing prophecies as absurd, urging the emperor to follow the Five Classics instead. He also criticized inadequate rewards for surrendered rebels, which prolonged military campaigns. The emperor became displeased. When later asked about using prophecies to decide the location of the Lingtai, Huan Tan refused, arguing prophecies were irrational. The emperor ordered his execution, but Huan Tan kowtowed until bleeding and was instead demoted to Liu'an County Magistrate. He died en route, over seventy years old. He wrote the "New Treatise" in twenty-nine chapters and other works.

Feng Yan, courtesy name Jingtong, was from Duling, Jingzhao. A prodigy, he could recite the "Book of Songs" at nine and was well-read by twenty. During Wang Mang's reign, he declined official recommendations. He served under Lian Dan, advising him to garrison troops and wait for opportunities rather than fight recklessly. Lian Dan refused and died in battle. Feng Yan later served Bao Yong, advising him to prepare defenses in Bingzhou, cultivate virtue, and select talented officers.

After Emperor Guangwu's ascension, Feng Yan wrote to Tian Yi, urging him to remain loyal to the Gengshi regime. Tian Yi responded defiantly, criticizing Bao Yong and Feng Yan for their failures. Feng Yan and Bao Yong eventually surrendered. The emperor initially dismissed Feng Yan for his late surrender, but Bao Yong's intercession led to Feng Yan's appointment as Quyang Magistrate, where he suppressed bandits.

Feng Yan later submitted a memorial on eight matters during a solar eclipse. However, former enemies slandered him, preventing imperial audience. He befriended influential relatives but was implicated when the emperor punished their associates. Feng Yan confessed and was pardoned, then returned home, isolating himself from former acquaintances.
