**Summary of Su Jing and Yang Hou**

Su Jing, courtesy name Bo Kuang, was a scholar from Pingling, Fufeng, proficient in the Book of Changes and skilled in drawing. During Wang Mang’s reign, he compiled books with Liu Xin and served as Lieutenant of Daijun, where he kept the northern frontier safe from Hunnic raids. Under Emperor Guangwu, he became Governor of Daijun and strengthened defenses against the Xiongnu. In the fifth year of Jianwu, Lu Fang plundered the north; Su Jing, gravely ill, handed his troops to his brother and went to the capital to apologize. He was appointed a servant but dismissed months later due to illness.

While in Nanyang, Su Jing wrote to Liu Gong, Liu Xin’s nephew, who served as an adviser to the bandit Deng Zhongkuang. He urged Liu Gong to abandon rebellion, citing historical examples like Zhiguo and Chen Ping, who wisely avoided harm. He argued that the Han dynasty, despite Wang Mang’s usurpation, was revived by Guangwu, and that celestial signs—such as irregular movements of Taibai and Yinghuo—foretold doom for rebels like Dong Xian and Peng Chong. He warned against following false prophecies and urged surrender, comparing Liu Gong’s potential choice to the virtuous actions of Duke Zhou’s uncle Kang and King Jibei Liu Zhi. Su Jing also wrote to Deng Zhongkuang, who eventually surrendered with Liu Gong. Su Jing died at home at age seventy, leaving writings like *Records of Teachings*.

Yang Hou, courtesy name Zhonghuan, was from Guanghan. His grandfather Yang Chunqing, skilled in prophecy, served Gongsun Shu but committed suicide after Han’s victory, instructing his son Yang Tong to study secret records. Yang Tong learned prophecy and astronomy, served as a magistrate, and prayed for rain during droughts. He wrote two volumes on prophecy and died at ninety. Yang Hou, his son, studied these arts. In Emperor An’s third year, when Taibai entered Beidou and Luoyang flooded, Yang Hou advised sending princes back to their states; the queen mother complied, and the omens ceased. He was appointed a middle man but dismissed after displeasing the queen mother with his prophecies. Later, he refused official posts, citing illness. In Emperor Shun’s reign, he predicted disasters like locust plagues and barbarian revolts, which occurred. He declined gifts from the powerful Liang Ji and retired to teach Huang-Lao arts to over 3,000 disciples. He died at eighty-two, posthumously named Wen Fu, with a temple built by his students.
