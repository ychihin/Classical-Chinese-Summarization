**Summary of Historical Biographies**

**Xuan Bing, Zhang Zhan, Wang Dan, and Wang Liang**

Xuan Bing, known for his integrity, fled into seclusion during Wang Mang's usurpation, refusing all calls to serve. After Guangwu's ascension, he held high offices including Yushi Zhongcheng and Sili Xiaowei, known for frugality—wearing plain clothes and eating simple food. The emperor praised his poverty as surpassing that of ancient sages. He used his salary to support relatives and died in office.

Zhang Zhan was a man of strict propriety, whose dignified conduct was emulated in the capital. Accused of hypocrisy, he admitted to "deceiving only for good." He served under Wang Mang and later as Zuo Fengyi under Guangwu, where he established rituals and regulations. Known for his humility, he would dismount and walk when passing his home county's gate. As Guang Luxun, he frequently admonished the emperor, who remarked, "The white horse is coming to admonish me again." He resigned due to illness and later died at home.

Wang Dan, a wealthy recluse, aided the poor during farming seasons, offering wine and food to diligent workers and shaming the lazy into reform. He personally cared for the sick and bereaved. He refused friendship with the powerful Chen Zun and declined an official post, citing illness. When his son wished to travel for a funeral, Wang Dan whipped him and sent only silk, warning that friendships rarely endure. He was later appointed Crown Prince Tutor but died at home.

Wang Liang, a scholar of the *Shangshu*, refused service under Wang Mang and taught over a thousand students. As an admonishing official, he offered loyal advice. As Da Situ Sizhi, he was so frugal that his wife, clad in a cloth skirt, was mistaken for a servant while gathering firewood. He later resigned due to illness and died at home. The historian notes that while Xuan Bing and Wang Liang were praised for integrity, their sincerity was questioned, unlike Zhang Zhan's honest self-awareness and Wang Dan's cautious view of friendship.

**Du Lin**

Du Lin, a learned scholar, fled with his family during the chaos of Wang Mang's fall. When threatened by bandits, his companion Meng Ji's speech about divine retribution saved them. He later served under Wei Xiao but refused to compromise his integrity. After his brother's death, Wei Xiao allowed him to return east but sent an assassin, who was moved by Du Lin's filial piety and fled. Guangwu appointed him as censor, and he became renowned for his erudition, particularly in ancient texts. He preserved a rare copy of the *Guwen Shangshu*, sharing it with scholars like Wei Hong and Xu Xun, reviving ancient learning. As Guang Luxun, he advocated for lenient laws, arguing that harsh penalties breed deceit. He served as tutor to the Crown Prince and later as Da Sikong, dying in office at age 73. The historian praises his loyalty and sincerity, which moved even an assassin.

**Guo Dan, Wu Liang, Chenggong, Zheng Jun, and Zhao Dian**

Guo Dan, orphaned young, studied in Chang'an, vowing not to leave the pass until he rode in an official carriage. He served Gengshi faithfully, mourning his death and returning his seal. As Situ, he was known for honesty and justice, dying at 87.

Wu Liang, a county official, once stopped a subordinate from flattering the prefect. He served under Prince Liu Cang, who recommended him for his integrity. As chief minister, he upheld law, even against the emperor's relative. He died in office.

Chenggong, a poor herdsman as a child, studied the *Spring and Autumn* while gathering firewood. He later farmed in seclusion, once giving away his millet to a claimant. As a doctor, his integrity was feared by courtiers and known to the Xiongnu. He refused to meet a Xiongnu envoy, citing his plain appearance, and died in 76 AD.

Zheng Jun, a follower of Huang-Lao, dissuaded his brother from corruption by working to repay stolen money. He cared for his widowed sister-in-law and orphans. Refusing official posts, he was later appointed Yilang, known for loyal advice. The emperor praised his poverty and chastity, granting him gifts. He died at home.

Zhao Dian, a frugal scholar, admonished the emperor against expanding Hongchi and criticized granting titles without merit. He distributed rewards to the poor. Dismissed for remonstrance, he defied a ban on attending the emperor's funeral, riding to the capital. He was pardoned and later died, receiving posthumous honors.
