# Summary of Historical Biographies

## Wang Chong

Wang Chong, courtesy name Zhongren, was from Shangyu, Kuaiji, though his ancestors had moved from Yuancheng, Wei County. His father died when he was young, and he was praised for his filial piety. He studied at Taixue in the capital under Ban Biao of Fufeng. Despite his family's poverty and lack of books, he frequently visited Luoyang bookstores, memorizing texts after a single reading, which allowed him to master various schools of thought. He later returned home to teach and served as a meritorious official, but left due to disagreements with his superiors after repeatedly offering advice.

Wang Chong criticized Confucian scholars for clinging to textual details while losing deeper meanings. He secluded himself, placing writing tools throughout his home, and composed 85 chapters of "Lunheng" (over 200,000 words) to explain phenomena and correct contemporary misconceptions. Governor Dong Qin recruited him, but he resigned. His friend Xie Yiwu recommended him to the court, and Emperor Suzong issued an edict for his service, but illness prevented him from traveling. Near age seventy, with failing memory, he wrote sixteen chapters of "The Book of Cultivation of Nature" on self-discipline and spiritual preservation. He died at home during the Yongyuan period.

## Wang Fu

Wang Fu, courtesy name Xin, was from Linjing, Anding. Studious and ambitious, he befriended Ma Rong, Dou Zhang, Zhang Heng, and Cui Yuan. Because he was born to a secondary wife, his village looked down on him. During the reigns of Emperors He and An, when powerful families recommended each other for office, Wang Fu's uprightness prevented him from becoming an official. Distressed, he secluded himself and wrote over thirty books criticizing contemporary politics, calling his work "The Theory of Qianfu" to remain anonymous.

His writings denounced societal ills. He argued that officials should govern justly, as heaven loves the people, and that stealing official positions for personal gain was worse than robbery. He criticized the neglect of agriculture, noting that in Luoyang, merchants outnumbered farmers tenfold, creating widespread poverty. He condemned extravagant dress, wasteful religious practices, and lavish funerals where families spent fortunes on coffins and burials while neglecting living parents. He advocated for proper talent selection, arguing that wise men existed but were improperly used, and criticized judicial corruption where the poor could not obtain justice against wealthy officials. He opposed frequent amnesties, which he believed emboldened criminals while harming the virtuous.

Wang Fu never served as an official and died at home. His contemporary Huangfu Gui famously demonstrated respect for him by rushing to greet him while ignoring a wealthy former governor, leading people to say that "a governor with two thousand stones is not worth a common man."

## Zhong Changtong

Zhong Changtong was from Gaoping, Shanyang. Studious and well-read, he was considered extraordinary by his peers. When visiting Gao Gan, the governor of Bingzhou, he warned that Gao had great ambitions but insufficient talent and poor judgment in choosing associates. Gao ignored this advice and later rebelled and was defeated.

Zhong Changtong was unconventional and outspoken, earning the nickname "Kuang Sheng" (Mad Scholar). He frequently declined government summons by claiming illness. He valued personal freedom over official service, describing an ideal life of leisure with fertile land, comfortable housing, family, friends, entertainment, and philosophical contemplation. He wrote poems expressing his desire to transcend worldly concerns. Minister Xun Yu appointed him, and he later served under Prime Minister Cao Cao. He wrote "Chang Yan," consisting of 34 chapters and over 100,000 words, expressing his critical views on contemporary society. He died at age forty-one during the year Emperor Xian abdicated.
