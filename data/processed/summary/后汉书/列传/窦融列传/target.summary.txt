Dou Rong, courtesy name Duke of Zhou, was from Pingling County, Fufeng. His seventh-generation ancestor Dou Guangguo was the younger brother of Queen Xiaowen. Orphaned young, he served as Sima under Wang Jun during Wang Mang's reign, earning the title Jianwu Nan for military merit. He was known for chivalry and filial piety, caring for his mother, elder brother, and younger sibling.

During the chaos at the end of Wang Mang's rule, Dou Rong initially served under Wang Yi but later surrendered to Gengshi's general Zhao Meng. Seeking stability, he requested and received the position of Captain of Zhangye's Vassal State, moving his family to Hexi. There, he befriended local heroes, harmonized with ethnic minorities, and gained widespread support. After Gengshi's fall, Dou Rong was elected General of the Five Counties in Hexi, overseeing a peaceful and prosperous region with tolerant governance and strong defenses against Qiang and Hu invasions.

When Guangwu ascended the throne, Dou Rong considered allegiance but was courted by Wei Xiao, who urged independence. After consulting with local leaders, Dou Rong decided to support Guangwu, citing prophecies and the strength of Luoyang. He sent an envoy with gifts, and Guangwu responded by appointing him General of the Five Counties and later Liangzhou Mu. Dou Rong rebuffed Wei Xiao's attempts at alliance, writing letters to persuade him to submit, but Wei Xiao refused. Dou Rong then prepared for military action against Wei Xiao.

Dou Rong joined Guangwu's campaign against Wei Xiao, leading troops from five counties. After Wei Xiao's defeat, Dou Rong was enfeoffed as Marquis of Anfeng. Despite his high rank, he remained humble and repeatedly sought to resign, but Guangwu refused. His family became powerful, with multiple marriages to royalty, but later fell from grace due to misconduct. Dou Rong died at seventy-eight, posthumously honored as Marquis of Dai. His descendants continued, though some faced imprisonment and loss of titles.

The historian comments that Dou Rong rose from obscurity to high office through merit, wisely yielding power when at his peak. While not a great strategist, his conduct in advancing and retreating was exemplary.
