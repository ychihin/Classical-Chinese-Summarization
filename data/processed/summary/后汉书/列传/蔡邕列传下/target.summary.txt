Cai Yong, courtesy name Bo Zhe, was a native of Chenliuyu County. His sixth-generation ancestor Cai Xun served under Emperor Ping but refused to serve Wang Mang, fleeing into the mountains with his family to maintain loyalty to Han. Cai Yong's father, Cai Leng, was known for his integrity. Cai Yong himself was filial, tending to his ill mother for three years without rest, and after her death, he built a hut by her tomb and observed strict mourning rituals. He lived harmoniously with his uncle and brothers for three generations, earning local praise. Erudite from youth, he studied under Hu Guang and excelled in literature, mathematics, astronomy, and music.

During Emperor Huan's reign, eunuchs like Xu Huang and Zuo Guan summoned Cai Yong to play the qin, but he feigned illness and returned home. He wrote "Explanation" to encourage himself, inspired by Dongfang Shuo and others. In the third year of Jianning, he served under Situ Qiaoxuan. Later, as Yilang, he noted errors in the classics and, in the fourth year of Xiping, petitioned Emperor Ling to correct the Six Classics. He personally wrote the text on steles, which were set up outside Taixue, attracting over a thousand visitors daily.

Cai Yong submitted a memorial criticizing the "three mutual laws" that prevented officials from serving in related regions, arguing they hindered governance in You and Ji provinces. He urged their abolition to select talented officials promptly, but his advice was not adopted. He also addressed disasters and Xianbei invasions, proposing seven reforms: proper imperial sacrifices, recruiting virtuous scholars, rewarding loyal officials like Zhang Wen, empowering honest governors, and ending corrupt recruitment practices. His memorial was leaked by Cao Jie, leading to false accusations from enemies like Yang Qiu. Cai Yong was imprisoned and sentenced to death, but Lu Qiang interceded, and his sentence was commuted to exile in Shuofang. Despite assassination attempts by Yang Qiu, he survived.

After nine months, a general amnesty allowed his return, but he fled to Wuhui after offending Wang Zhi. He lived in Wu for twelve years, once crafting a famous "Jiaowei Qin" from a burnt tung tree. In the sixth year of Zhongping, Dong Zhuo forced him into service, promoting him rapidly. Cai Yong advised Dong Zhuo against excessive honors but grew disillusioned with his tyranny. After Dong Zhuo's execution, Cai Yong expressed regret, angering Wang Yun, who imprisoned him. Despite pleas from scholars like Ma Rishidan, Wang Yun executed him, fearing his historical writings would slander the court. Cai Yong died at sixty-one, mourned by many. His works included poems, inscriptions, and 104 other pieces, though much was lost in chaos. The historian noted his tragic fate, bound by loyalty and circumstance.
