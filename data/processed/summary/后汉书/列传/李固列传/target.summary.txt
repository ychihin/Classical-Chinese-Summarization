Li Gu, courtesy name Zijian, was from Nanzheng, Hanzhong, and the son of Situ Li Hui. He had a peculiar appearance with bones on his head protruding like a tripod foot and turtle inscriptions on his feet. He was an eager learner who traveled thousands of miles to find teachers, befriended many wise men, and attracted students from across the land. He was promoted as Xiaolian but did not take office.

In the second year of Yangjia, after earthquakes and landslides, the emperor asked Li Gu for advice. Li Gu replied that if the royal way works, Yin and Yang are harmonious; if politics are disobedient, disasters occur. He criticized that officials today only seek wealth and power, and that harsh officials are promoted while kind ones are ostracized. He advised against enfeoffing the emperor's grandmother, citing the danger of excessive power for relatives, and warned against the influence of the Liang family and eunuchs. He urged the emperor to select virtuous officials, dismiss eunuchs, and follow the laws of sages. Emperor Shun adopted many of his suggestions, but eunuchs framed Li Gu, leading to his dismissal. He later became a magistrate but resigned and returned home.

Liang Shang asked Li Gu to be a lieutenant. Li Gu wrote to Liang Shang, criticizing the corruption of previous reigns and urging him to rectify morals, reduce palace concubines, and establish a crown prince. He warned against building new ancestral halls and noted that disasters and eclipses indicated conspiracies within the palace. Liang Shang did not listen. During the Yonghe period, Li Gu was appointed governor of Jingzhou, where he pardoned bandits and pacified the region within half a year. He reported corrupt officials, who bribed Liang Ji, leading to Li Gu's transfer to Taishan. There, he dismissed most soldiers and used kindness to subdue bandits within a year.

Li Gu was promoted and recommended several scholars. He later became Taiwei under Emperor Chong. When the emperor died, Li Gu insisted on immediate mourning, opposing the Empress Dowager's delay. He advocated for establishing an older, virtuous emperor, but Liang Ji appointed the young Liu Zuan as Emperor Zhi. Li Gu reduced burial costs for the emperor. He dismissed over a hundred improperly appointed officials, who then slandered him to Liang Ji, accusing him of nepotism, extravagance, and lack of mourning. The Empress Dowager spared Li Gu from death. Liang Ji later poisoned Emperor Zhi. Li Gu cried over the emperor's body. When establishing a new emperor, Li Gu advocated for Liu Lian, the King of Qinghe, but Liang Ji, persuaded by eunuchs, chose Liu Zhidang (Emperor Huan) instead. Liang Ji later falsely accused Li Gu of plotting to make Liu Lian emperor and imprisoned him. Despite public support, Liang Ji had Li Gu killed at age 54. Before death, Li Gu wrote to Hu Guang and Zhao Jie, blaming them for following Liang Ji and causing the decline of the Han dynasty. His sons died in prison, and his body was exposed. His disciple Guo Liang and others mourned him, and the Empress Dowager eventually allowed his burial.

Du Qiao, courtesy name Shurong, was from Linli, Hanoi. He served as a prefect and later as a servant. He reported that Li Gu had the best political performance and that others, including Liang Ji's relatives, had embezzled money. He was promoted to Minister of Agriculture. He remonstrated against granting titles to Liang Ji's relatives and eunuchs without merit, warning it would cause chaos. He refused Liang Ji's requests to borrow evidence and attend his daughter's funeral, earning Liang Ji's hatred. As Guang Luxun, he opposed lavish gifts for Liang Ji's sister becoming queen and refused to appoint a corrupt official. After Li Gu was deposed, Du Qiao remained serious and unyielding. He was dismissed due to an earthquake. Eunuchs and Liang Ji framed him for opposing the emperor's ascension and colluding with Li Gu. He was arrested and died in prison. His body was exposed, but his former subordinate Yang Kuang mourned him and requested burial, which the Empress Dowager granted. The historian commented that Li Gu and Du Qiao sacrificed their lives for righteousness, unlike Hu Guang and Zhao Jie.
