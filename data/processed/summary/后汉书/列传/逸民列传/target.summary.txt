**Summary of Biographies of Yimin (Preface and Selected Entries)**

The preface explains that seclusion has been a respected tradition since ancient times, as seen in figures like Chao Fu and Xu You, who refused Yao’s throne, and Boyi and Shuqi, who rejected Zhou’s rule. The motivations for seclusion vary: some seek to fulfill ambitions, perfect moral character, suppress impatience, avoid danger, establish integrity, or preserve innocence. While some criticize such acts as seeking fame, the preface argues that true hermits transcend worldly pursuits, as Xunzi noted: high ambitions despise wealth, and strong morals disdain princes. During Wang Mang’s usurpation, many abandoned office, and under Emperor Guangwu, hermits like Yan Guang and Zhou Dang were recruited but often refused to serve. The text records those who lived in seclusion or withdrew after serving.

Xiang Changchuan, a hermit from Hanoi, refused Wang Mang’s summons, studied the *Book of Changes*, and after his children married, he severed ties with his family and disappeared into the Five Mountains. Feng Meng, from Beihai, left his post as a pavilion chief, studied the *Spring and Autumn Annals*, and fled to Liaodong after Wang Mang killed his son. He later refused Guangwu’s summons, claiming old age and disorientation, and died in seclusion. Zhou Dang, a wealthy man from Taiyuan, studied in Chang’an, fought a duel over an old insult, and later refused to serve Wang Mang. Under Guangwu, he was summoned but declined, and the emperor defended his choice, granting him silk. Zhou Dang wrote books and died in Mianchi.

Wang Ba, also from Taiyuan, abandoned his official post during Wang Mang’s reign and refused to serve Guangwu, citing the emperor’s lack of respect for his subjects. Yan Guang, a childhood friend of Guangwu, changed his name and fished in seclusion. The emperor found him, but Yan Guang refused to serve, famously putting his feet on Guangwu’s belly while sleeping. He later farmed in Fuchun Mountain and died at home. Jing Dan, a scholar from Fufeng, was known for his pride and refused invitations from princes. When tricked into a banquet by a relative of the empress, he criticized the host’s meager food and left, living in seclusion thereafter.

Liang Hong, from Fufeng, studied in Taixue, worked as a pig herder, and compensated a neighbor for a fire by labor. He married Meng Guang, who dressed simply and shared his life of seclusion. They fled to Baling Mountain, then to Wu, where Liang Hong worked as a rice pounder. His wife served him with respect, raising the tray to her eyebrows. Liang Hong died in Wu and was buried near the martyr Yaoli. Gao Feng, a scholar from Nanyang, was so absorbed in reading that he let rain ruin his wheat. He mediated disputes and refused official summons, pretending to have a lawsuit to avoid service. Tai Tong, from Wei County, lived in a cave, collected herbs, and refused summons, stating his life was peaceful compared to officials’ toil.

Han Kang, from Jingzhao, sold herbs at a fixed price for thirty years. When a girl recognized him, he fled to Baling Mountain and refused Emperor Huan’s summons, escaping halfway. Jiao Shen, from Fufeng, studied Huang-Lao, lived in a cave, and refused to marry. He predicted his death and later was seen in Dunhuang, rumored to be immortal. Dai Liang, from Runan, imitated his mother’s braying for fun, and after her death, he ate meat while mourning, arguing that rituals should not suppress genuine emotion. He refused office and fled to Jiangxia, marrying off his five daughters with simple dowries.

Fa Zhen, a great Confucian from Fufeng, refused a prefect’s offer of office, saying he would rather be north of Beishan or south of Nanshan. He was recommended to Emperor Shun but declined, living in seclusion and earning the title Mr. Xuande. The Hanyin Old Man, during Emperor Huan’s visit, continued plowing and criticized the emperor for indulging in leisure while the people suffered. The Chenliu Old Man, during the party insurrection, told weeping officials that crying was useless, as the world’s traps were everywhere. Pang Gong, from Xiangyang, refused Liu Biao’s summons, saying he left peace to his descendants, not danger. He and his wife later retreated to Lumen Mountain.
