# Summary of Historical Biographies

**Yang Zhong** (courtesy name Zishan), from Chengdu, Shu County, became a county official at thirteen. The prefect, impressed by his talent, sent him to study the *Spring and Autumn Annals* in the capital. During Emperor Xianzong's reign, he served at Lantai as school secretary. In the first year of Jianchu, during a severe drought and high grain prices, Yang Zhong submitted a memorial arguing that excessive imprisonment and military settlements in remote areas caused widespread suffering. He cited historical precedents, including the Qin dynasty's harsh policies and Emperor Gaozu's reforms, urging the emperor to show mercy. When officials debated his proposals, Yang Zhong defended his position by referencing historical examples of wise rulers who changed harmful policies. The emperor eventually accepted his advice, recalling exiles and ending border settlements. Yang Zhong also advocated for scholarly examinations following the Shiqu Pavilion model, leading to the Baihu Temple conference. Despite imprisonment, he was pardoned and participated. He later edited the *Tai Shi Gong Shu* and wrote on the *Spring and Autumn Annals*. He died in the twelfth year of Yongyuan.

**Li Fa** (courtesy name Bodu), from Nanzheng, Hanzhong, was well-read and upright. In the ninth year of Emperor Yongyuan's reign, he became a doctor and later Shizhong and Guanglu doctor. He criticized harsh government policies, excessive eunuch power, and favoritism toward concubines, which led to his removal from office. He returned home and lived in seclusion for eight years, refusing to discuss politics with friends. He later served as Yilang and admonished officials, maintaining his principles. As governor of Runan, he achieved political success before returning home and dying.

**Zhai Youfu** (courtesy name Zichao), from Luo, Guanghan, was skilled in the *Book of Songs*, *Laozi*, astronomy, and calendar calculation. After avenging his uncle, he fled to Chang'an, worked as a fortune teller, and later shepherded sheep in Liangzhou. He eventually became a court official. When a vacancy arose, he manipulated the examination process out of fear of competition. He submitted a memorial criticizing the emperor for granting excessive power to relatives and eunuchs, warning of historical disasters from such practices. He advocated for frugality and wise counsel. His memorial was ignored, and he became feared by the emperor's favorites. As prefect of Jiuquan, he defeated Qiang rebels, beheading 900. He later served as Jing Zhaoyin and Guanglu doctor. He was falsely accused, imprisoned, and eventually cleared. He wrote twelve chapters on classical texts and advocated for restoring the Imperial Academy, which the emperor approved.

**Ying Feng** (courtesy name Shishu), from Nandun, Runan, was exceptionally intelligent, memorizing everything he read. As governor, he visited forty-two counties and accurately recalled prisoner details. He wrote the *Preface to the Book of Han*. During a Wuling Man rebellion, he was appointed prefect and successfully pacified them through diplomacy. He later served as Sili Xiaowei, known for his severity. When Emperor Huan wanted to make Tian Guiren queen, Ying Feng advised against it, citing historical disasters from such choices. The emperor accepted his advice. He later resigned due to illness and wrote thirty chapters commemorating Qu Yuan.

**Ying Shao** (courtesy name Zhongyuan), studious and well-read, served as a messenger under Emperor Ling. During border conflicts, he argued against recruiting Xianbei soldiers, citing their unreliability and past betrayals. His view prevailed. As prefect of Taishan, he defeated 300,000 Yellow Turban rebels. After failing to protect Cao Song, he fled to Yuan Shao. He wrote extensively on legal matters, compiling works like *Han Yi* and *Customs of Customs*. He refuted legal precedents, arguing that punishment should follow established principles. His writings totaled 136 works, and he interpreted the *Book of Han*. He died in Ye.

**Huo Chen** (courtesy name Shuzhi), from Ye, Wei County, was a young scholar. When his uncle Song Guang was falsely accused and imprisoned, fifteen-year-old Huo Chen wrote to General Liang Shang, arguing for leniency based on the *Spring and Autumn Annals* principle of judging intent. Liang Shang pardoned Song Guang, and Huo Chen became famous. He served as prefect of Jincheng, respected by the Qiang and Hu. After his mother's death, he resigned for mourning. He later served as prime minister of Beihai and minister, courageously opposing Liang Ji's power. After Liang Ji's death, Emperor Huan honored him. He died in office.

**Yuan Yan** (courtesy name Jiping), from Chenliu, was poor but studious. He served as a village official, known for benevolent governance. When Emperor Huan asked what kind of ruler he was, Yuan Yan replied he was a "Lord of Hanzhong," explaining that while Chen Fan handled affairs well, eunuchs interfered. The emperor appointed him to various positions. Yuan Yan warned against favoring unworthy associates, citing historical disasters from such practices. He urged the emperor to distance himself from flatterers and eunuchs. He later retired due to illness and died.

**Xu Chu** (courtesy name Mengyu), from Guangling, was learned and served as governor of Jingzhou. He prosecuted corrupt officials, including Zhang Zhong, despite pressure from the Empress Dowager. He defeated Yellow Turban rebels but was falsely accused by eunuchs and dismissed. He later served as prime minister of Donghai. When Emperor Xian moved the capital, Xu Chu was kidnapped by Yuan Shu but refused to serve him. After Yuan Shu's defeat, Xu Chu recovered the imperial seal and returned it. He served as Taichang and died in office.
