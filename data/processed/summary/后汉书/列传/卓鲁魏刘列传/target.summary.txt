**Summary of Historical Biographies**

**Zhuomao Biography:** Zhuomao, a native of Wan in Nanyang County, studied in Chang'an under Dr. Jiang Weng, mastering the "Poetry," "Li," and Calendar, and was called a Tongru for exhausting his teacher's scholarship. He was tolerant and respectful. Once, a man mistakenly claimed Zhuomao's horse as his own; Zhuomao gave it to him without dispute. When the man later found his own horse, he returned Zhuomao's horse with apologies. As magistrate of Mi County, Zhuomao governed diligently, treating the people as his sons. When a man admitted to giving gifts to a pavilion chief, Zhuomao explained that such exchanges were part of human relationships, not illegal coercion, and the people accepted his teachings. During a locust plague under Emperor Ping, locusts did not enter Mi County, a fact confirmed by the prefect. Under Wang Mang, Zhuomao was appointed minister of the capital but later resigned due to illness. When Gengshi was established, Zhuomao served briefly but resigned, knowing the political turmoil. Guangwu later visited him, praising his integrity. Zhuomao and six comrades, including Kong Xiu and Liu Xuan, refused to serve Wang Mang. Kong Xiu committed suicide when Wang Mang usurped the throne. Liu Xuan hid in the mountains until Jianwu. The historian noted that Zhuomao, a small county magistrate over seventy, was honored by Guangwu, akin to ancient kings honoring virtuous men.

**Lu Gong Biography:** Lu Gong, from Pingling, Fufeng, was orphaned at twelve. He and his younger brother Lu Pi mourned their father with exceptional propriety. They studied "Lu Shi" at Taixue, ignoring external affairs. Lu Gong let his brother achieve fame first, feigning illness to avoid office. He later served as a county official and was summoned by Suzong to discuss at Baihu Temple. As magistrate of Zhongmu, he focused on moral transformation, not punishment. When a pavilion chief refused to return a borrowed cow, Lu Gong prepared to resign, prompting the chief to return it. During a borer infestation, pests did not enter Zhongmu, and a child showed benevolence by not catching a pheasant hatching chicks. Lu Gong later became imperial censor and admonished Emperor He against attacking the Xiongnu, citing poor harvests, empty granaries, and the need for mourning. He argued that virtue conquers, not force, and warned of disaster. The emperor ignored him. Lu Gong later served as a doctor of "Lu Shi" and as a servant, speaking frankly. He became Situ but was dismissed due to a clan brother's affairs. He later served as Changle Guard and again as Situ. He remonstrated against harsh punishments in summer, advocating for seasonal justice. He died at eighty-one, known for his humble nature and reliance on classics.

**Wei Ba Biography:** Wei Ba, from Jiyinjuyang, was known for family etiquette. He lived with his brothers harmoniously. As prefect of Julu, he governed with simplicity and forgiveness, praising officials' merits and ignoring shortcomings, which curbed false accusations. He oversaw construction of Shunling under Emperor He, comforting officials despite harsh palace envoys. He later served as Taichang and Guanglu doctor, dying in office.

**Liu Kuan Biography:** Liu Kuan, from Huayin, was known for his magnanimity. When a man mistakenly claimed his cow, Liu Kuan gave it without argument; the man later returned it with apologies. As Nanyang prefect, he governed with gentleness, using only light whips for punishment and attributing merit to others. He taught filial piety and brotherhood, inspiring virtue. Under Emperor Ling, he served as Taiwei and lecturer. Once drunk at court, he claimed worry, not drunkenness. He disliked bathing, and when a maid spilled soup on him, he asked if she was hurt. He was called an elder. He died at sixty-six, with his son Liu Song succeeding him.
