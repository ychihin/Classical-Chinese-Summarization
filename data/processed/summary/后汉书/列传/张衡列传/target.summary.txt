Zhang Heng, styled Pingzi, was a native of Xi'e County, Nanyang. His grandfather, Zhang Kan, served as the prefect of Shu County. Zhang Heng excelled in writing from a young age, studied in Sanfu and the Imperial Academy, and mastered the Five Classics and Six Arts. Despite his exceptional talent, he remained humble and indifferent, avoiding interactions with common people. During the Yongyuan period of Emperor He, he was recommended as a Xiaolian but declined multiple government recruitments. Observing the extravagance of the time, he spent ten years composing "Ode to Two Capitals" as a satire, inspired by Ban Gu's work. General Deng Zhi admired his talent but Zhang Heng ignored his summons.

Skilled in mechanics, astronomy, yin-yang, and calendar calculation, Zhang Heng admired Yang Xiong's "Tai Xuan Jing." Emperor An appointed him as a doctor and later as Taishi Ling, where he studied yin-yang, built an armillary sphere, and wrote "Lingxian" and "Suanwang Lun." In the first year of Yangjia, he created a seismoscope made of fine copper, eight feet in diameter, with eight dragons holding copper balls and toads beneath them. When an earthquake occurred, a dragon would release a ball into a toad's mouth, indicating the direction. This device proved accurate, even detecting a distant earthquake in Longxi that others initially doubted.

As political affairs declined, Zhang Heng advised the emperor against sharing power with subordinates, warning that disasters stemmed from moral failings. He criticized prophecies as false, noting they emerged only after the Cheng and Ai periods and contradicted classical texts. He urged their suppression to prevent confusion.

Later, as Prime Minister of Hejian, Zhang Heng curbed the king's arrogance and lawlessness, earning a reputation for integrity. After three years, he requested retirement and died at sixty-two. He authored thirty-two works, including poems and essays. He also sought to correct historical records, arguing that Wang Mang's biography should focus on usurpation and that Gengshi's title should precede Guangwu's, but his proposals were not accepted. The historian praised his profound wisdom and mechanical ingenuity, noting that his virtue was not diminished by his skills.
