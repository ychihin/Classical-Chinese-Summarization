Deng Yu, courtesy name Zhonghua, was from Xinye County, Nanyang. At thirteen, he could recite poems and studied in Chang'an, where he befriended Guangwu, recognizing his extraordinary nature. After returning home, he refused offers from other heroes when the Han army rose. He told Guangwu he wished to serve him and achieve lasting fame. Guangwu kept him close, and Deng Yu advised that while Gengshi held Guanxi, instability in Shandong and bandit groups like the Red Eyebrows threatened order. He urged Guangwu to recruit heroes, win over the people, and build a great cause. Guangwu appointed him General Deng and consulted him on strategies.

Deng Yu recruited troops for Guangwu, defeating enemies like the bronze horses in Qingyang. When the Red Eyebrows threatened, Guangwu entrusted Deng Yu with the Western Expedition, appointing him Former General with 20,000 elite troops. In the first year of Jianwu, Deng Yu entered Hedong, defeating Gengshi generals and pacifying the region. Guangwu ascended the throne and made Deng Yu Chief Minister, praising his loyalty and achievements. At twenty-four, Deng Yu crossed into Xiayang, defeating more enemies as the Red Eyebrows entered Chang'an. His disciplined troops attracted many followers, and he became famous in Guanxi.

Deng Yu advised against directly attacking Chang'an, citing the Red Eyebrows' strength and advocating a strategy of gathering resources in the north to observe their weaknesses. He led his army north, defeating Red Eyebrow camps and gaining surrenders. However, internal conflicts among his generals led to setbacks. In the second year of Jianwu, Deng Yu was made Marquis of Liang. He fought the Red Eyebrows but faced defeats, and Guangwu recalled him, advising caution. Deng Yu, ashamed, continued fighting but was ultimately defeated in the third year, returning to Yiyang with few men and resigning his posts. He was later reinstated as Right General and achieved victories against Yan Cen.

In the 13th year of Jianwu, Deng Yu was made Marquis of Gaomi. He was known for his filial piety, modesty, and avoidance of fame and power. He had thirteen children, each skilled in a craft, and lived frugally on his fiefdom taxes. He served as Acting Situ again and accompanied the emperor to Mount Tai. Under Xianzong, he was made Grand Tutor and treated with great respect. He died at fifty-seven in the first year of Yongping, posthumously titled Yuan Hou. His sons inherited his titles, and his descendants continued in service, though some faced downfall due to political incidents.

Kou Xun, courtesy name Ziyi, was from Changping, Shanggu, from a prominent family. He served as a county officer under Prefect Geng Kuang. When Gengshi sent envoys, Kou Xun ensured Geng Kuang retained his position by persuading an envoy to return the seal. When Wang Lang rose, Kou Xun advised Geng Kuang to ally with Guangwu, citing his virtue. He attacked Handan's envoys and joined Guangwu, who made him a partial general. Deng Yu admired him, and Guangwu entrusted him with guarding Hanoi, comparing him to Xiao He.

Kou Xun fortified Hanoi, supplying Guangwu's army with arrows, horses, and grain. When Zhu Tuna attacked Wen, Kou Xun led a swift defense, defeating Su Mao and Jia Qiang with reinforcements. Guangwu praised him and ascended the throne. Kou Xun continued supplying the army, but heeded advice to feign illness to avoid suspicion. He later served as Prefect of Yingchuan, suppressing bandits, and was made Marquis of Yongnu. He avoided conflict with General Jia Fu, reconciling with him for the nation's sake.

Kou Xun governed Runan, building schools and teaching the Spring and Autumn Annals. He pacified Yingchuan again when bandits rose, and the people begged for his continued service. He advised Guangwu against personally attacking Gaoping, but when the campaign stalled, he persuaded Gao Jun to surrender by executing his advisor. Kou Xun was known for his virtue, generosity, and learning, and was considered prime minister material. He died in the 12th year of Jianwu, posthumously titled Wei Hou. His descendants continued his legacy, and his family remained influential. The historian praised his ability to control his emotions and avoid grudges, likening him to Boyi and Shuqi.
