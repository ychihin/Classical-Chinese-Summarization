The biography of Guo Gong, styled Zhongsun, was from Yangzhai, Yingchuan, in a family of officials for generations. His father, Guo Hong, studied "Xiao Du Lv" and served as a judge for Prefect Kou Xun, handling cases for Cao Cao. Guo Hong worked in prison for thirty years with fair methods, and those he judged felt no resentment, earning comparisons to Donghai Yugong. He died at ninety-five. Guo Gong inherited his father's work, teaching hundreds of disciples, then became a county official. During the Yongping period, he was summoned to court to review a case involving Dou Gu and Qin Peng. While others agreed with Dou Gu, Guo Gong argued that Qin Peng should be beheaded according to law, citing military urgency and Han system precedents. The emperor accepted his view. In another case of brothers committing murder, the emperor initially ruled the elder brother be severely sentenced, but Guo Gong advised leniency for a clerk, Sun Zhang, who mistakenly conveyed an imperial edict, distinguishing between intentional homicide and manslaughter. The emperor agreed. Guo Gong became Tingweizheng but was later dismissed for breaking the law. Promoted three times, he became Tingwei in the third year of Yuanhe. He advocated mercy in sentencing, reporting forty-one cases for mitigation, all implemented. In the first year of Zhanghe, he successfully petitioned to extend a pardon to escaped criminals, saving many lives. He died in the sixth year of Yongyuan. His son, Guo Rizhi, also understood law and served as Nanyang prefect.

Chen Chong, styled Zhaogong, was from Pei Kingdom. His great-grandfather, Chen Xian, served as Minister of Law under Emperors Cheng and Ai but resigned during Wang Mang's usurpation, hiding legal texts in walls. Chen Chong learned family law, served as a county official, and later worked for Situ Bao Yu, writing seven volumes of "Resignation and Litigation Bi" to handle lawsuits fairly. Promoted three times, he became minister under Emperor Suzong. He advocated leniency, arguing against harsh punishments in a memorial, and the emperor adopted his views, abolishing cruel punishments and fixing over fifty legal items. Chen Chong also debated the timing of executions, supporting winter executions based on classical texts, and the emperor accepted his reasoning. He served as prefect of Taishan and Guanghan, where he appointed good officials, reduced lawsuits, and investigated mysterious cries, burying unburied bones to stop them. He refused to flatter Dou Xian, later becoming Tingwei in the sixth year of Yongyuan. He cleaned up laws, proposing to reduce death sentences to 200 and total punishments to 3,000, but this was not implemented. He served as Sikong and died after three years.
