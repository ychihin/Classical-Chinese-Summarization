**Summary of Historical Records: Lang Yi and Xiang Kai**

Lang Yi, courtesy name Yaguang, was born in Anqiu, Beihai. His father, Lang Zong, studied the "Jing's Book of Changes," excelled in divination and astrology, and supported himself by fortune-telling. Emperor An summoned him, and his responses set a standard for Confucian scholars. He later became magistrate of Wu County, where he accurately predicted a fire in the capital through divination. The imperial court summoned him with a doctor's title, but Lang Zong, ashamed of being known for fortune-telling, fled by night, hanging his seal in the county court, and never served as an official again.

Lang Yi inherited his father's studies, lived in seclusion by the sea, and taught hundreds of disciples. During Emperor Shun's reign, disasters occurred frequently. In the first month of the second year of Yangjia, Lang Yi was summoned to court. He submitted a memorial attributing disasters to the ruler's governance, urging the emperor to practice frugality, reflect on faults, and reform policies. He warned that luxury, excessive construction, and harsh punishments caused celestial anomalies like prolonged cloudiness, cold spells, and fires. He cited classical texts to argue that the emperor should reduce palace expenditures, release palace maids to restore harmony, and dismiss incompetent officials. He also predicted earthquakes, floods, and invasions if reforms were not enacted.

In a dialogue with the minister, Lang Yi elaborated on seven urgent matters: cemetery fires due to extravagance, hypocritical officials causing yin-yang imbalances, impending droughts and floods, the need to establish an heir, white rainbow portending coups, and the necessity to change the calendar and official titles. He recommended recruiting virtuous men like Huang Qiong and Li Gu to stabilize the state. Despite his detailed warnings, Lang Yi resigned from his appointed doctor position and returned home. His predictions of earthquakes, droughts, and barbarian invasions later proved accurate. He was eventually killed by a local knight-errant, Sun Li.

Xiang Kai, courtesy name Gongju, was from Xiyin, Pingyuan. During Emperor Huan's reign, eunuchs dominated, punishments were brutal, and disasters were frequent. In the ninth year of Yanxi, Xiang Kai submitted a memorial citing astronomical anomalies—Mars and Venus invading key stars—as warnings against harsh rule and unjust executions. He criticized the killing of loyal officials like Li Yun and Du Zhong, the neglect of heirs due to excessive palace maids, and the decline of education. He noted omens like a dead dragon and falling stars, linking them to past dynastic collapses. He urged the emperor to reform punishments, release prisoners, and promote virtue.

Emperor Huan initially ignored Xiang Kai's reports. When summoned, Xiang Kai condemned eunuchs, stating their proliferation caused the lack of an heir. The minister of corrections accused him of slander, but the emperor spared him, citing the astronomical basis of his words. Xiang Kai later refused official posts, including one offered by Grand Tutor Chen Fan, and died at home during the Zhongping period.
