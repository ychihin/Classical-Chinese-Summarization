Ban Gu, courtesy name Meng Jian, was a precocious scholar who could compose sentences and recite poems at age nine. As an adult, he mastered classical texts and philosophical schools without adhering to a single teacher, focusing on general principles rather than textual minutiae. He was generous and unassuming, earning admiration from fellow Confucians.

During the early Yongping era, King Cang of Dongping, serving as a general, opened the East Pavilion to recruit talents. Twenty-year-old Ban Gu addressed him, praising his extraordinary achievements and urging him to emulate ancient sage-kings in selecting virtuous officials. Ban Gu recommended six exceptional individuals: a retired Confucian elder, Jingzhao wine-offerer Jin Feng, Fufeng scholar Li Yu, Jingzhao governor Guo Ji, Jingzhou officer Wang Yong, and Hongnong clerk Yin Su. He emphasized the importance of recognizing talent without bias, citing historical examples like Bian He and Qu Yuan to warn against neglecting worthy men. King Cang adopted his advice.

After his father Ban Biao died, Ban Gu sought to complete his father's incomplete historical work. However, he was accused of secretly altering national history and imprisoned. His brother Ban Chao successfully petitioned the emperor, explaining Ban Gu's intentions. Ban Gu was subsequently promoted to court positions and tasked with writing about heroes and compiling historical records. He authored the *Book of Han*, covering 230 years from Emperor Gaozu to Wang Mang's death, completing it over twenty years. The work was highly regarded by contemporaries.

Ban Gu later served under General Dou Xian during campaigns against the Xiongnu, acting as a counselor and envoy. After Dou Xian's downfall, Ban Gu was dismissed. He failed to discipline his sons, who often broke laws. A grudge held by Luoyang magistrate Zhong Jing, stemming from an incident with Ban Gu's drunken slaves, led to Ban Gu's arrest and death in prison at age sixty-one. The court later condemned Zhong Jing. Ban Gu's writings, including various literary forms, comprised forty-one works.
