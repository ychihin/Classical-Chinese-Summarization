# Summary of Historical Biographies

**Xun Shu (Jihe)** was a native of Yingyin, Yingchuan County, and the eleventh-generation grandson of Xun Qing. From youth, he possessed noble virtues and profound knowledge, though he disliked detailed textual analysis and was looked down upon by vulgar scholars. During Emperor An's reign, he was recruited as a doctor and later promoted to Tu Chang, but resigned to return home. The famous sages Li Gu and Li Ying respected him as their teacher. When Empress Dowager Liang governed, Xun Shu was promoted to answer imperial questions, where he criticized the emperor's noble relatives and favored people. General Liang Ji, jealous of him, transferred him to serve as Marquis of Langling. Known for his upright governance, he was called the "divine king." He soon resigned and returned home, distributing family property among clan members. He died at sixty-seven in the third year of Jianhe. Li Ying mourned him for three years. Both counties built temples in his honor. Xun Shu had eight sons—Jian, Nian, Jing, Tao, Wang, Shuang, Su, and Zhuan—all famous, known as the "Eight Dragons." Their neighborhood was renamed Gaoyangli after the ancient Gaoyang family's eight talented members. Xun Jing, a virtuous man who refused office, died at fifty and was named Mr. Xuan Xing. Xun Shu's sons Xun Yu and Xun Tan both hated evil and sought to eradicate eunuchs, killing even minor offenders among eunuch branches. Xun Yu later died with General Dou Wu and Li Ying, while Xun Tan was imprisoned for life.

**Xun Shuang (Ciming)** was eager to learn from childhood, understanding "Spring and Autumn" and "The Analects" by age twelve. Taiwei Du Qiao praised him as capable of being a teacher. Xun Shuang delved deeply into the scriptures, ignoring village celebrations and funerals. In the ninth year of Yanxi, he was recommended as filial and incorruptible, becoming a doctor. In response to an imperial question, he discussed the Han Dynasty's virtue of fire, filial piety, and the importance of proper mourning rites. He criticized officials who failed to observe three-year mourning periods and advocated for correcting funeral etiquette. He also addressed the improper subordination of husbands to wives in marriage rituals, arguing it violated cosmic principles. He condemned the excessive number of palace women—five to six thousand—which drained the treasury and caused disasters, urging their release to restore harmony. He further criticized ministers who usurped imperial privileges in dress and luxury. After submitting his memorial, he abandoned his post. Later, due to conflicts with eunuchs, he lived in seclusion for over ten years, writing books and becoming a great Confucian. After the party ban was lifted, he was recruited multiple times but refused. When Yuan Feng died, Xun Shuang mourned him for three years, setting a custom. He later served under General He Jin and then Dong Zhuo, rising to Sikong within ninety-five days. Seeing Dong Zhuo's cruelty, he conspired with Wang Yun but died of illness at sixty-three. He wrote numerous works, including "Li," "Yizhuan," and "Hanyu," totaling over a hundred chapters, many now lost.

**Xun Yue (Zhongyu)** was the son of Xun Jian, who died early. At twelve, Xun Yue could explain "Spring and Autumn." Despite poverty and lack of books, he could recite most texts after reading them at others' homes. During Emperor Ling's reign, when eunuchs held power, he feigned illness and lived in seclusion. He later served under Cao Cao and became Minister of Huangmen. Emperor Xian, fond of literature, conversed with Xun Yue and Kong Rong daily. As political power shifted to Cao Cao, Xun Yue wrote five chapters of "Shenjian" on governance, emphasizing benevolence and righteousness. He identified four troubles—hypocrisy, selfishness, debauchery, and luxury—and five policies: promoting agriculture, distinguishing good from evil, spreading culture, establishing military force, and enforcing rewards and punishments. He also criticized the marriage system for placing women above men and advocated for historical recording. The emperor, finding Bangu's "Hanshu" too lengthy, ordered Xun Yue to write thirty chapters of "Han Ji" in the style of "Zuo Shi Zhuan." Xun Yue's work was concise yet detailed. He also wrote "Chongde" and "Zhenglun." He died in the fourteenth year of Jian'an at age sixty-two.

**Han Shao (Zhonghuang)** was from Wuyang County, Yingchuan. He served as a county official and was recruited into the Situ Mansion. When Taishan bandit Gongsun Ju caused trouble, Han Shao was appointed county magistrate. The bandits, hearing of his reputation, avoided Ying County, but other counties suffered. Many fled to Ying County, and Han Shao opened granaries to feed over 10,000 families, despite objections from supervisors. He died in office. Li Ying, Chen Chen, Du Mi, and Xun Shu erected monuments praising him. His son Han Rong became a servant under Emperor Xian and died at seventy.

**Zhong Hao (Ji Ming)** was from Changshe County, Yingchuan, from a family proficient in law. Praised for honesty, he lived in seclusion teaching poetry to over a thousand students. He befriended Chen Chen and served as the county's meritorious officer. Recruited nine times by the government, he never accepted. Both Zhong Hao and Xun Shu were admired by scholar-bureaucrats. Li Ying said Xun Shu's knowledge was hard to match, but Zhong Hao's virtue could be a teacher's example. Zhong Hao's nephew Zhong Jin married Li Ying's sister. Zhong Hao died at sixty-nine at home. Confucian scholars praised his virtue. His grandson Zhong Yao became Sili Xiaowei.

**Chen Chen (Zhonggong)** was from Xu County, Yingchuan, of humble origin. From childhood, he was respected by peers. He served as a county official and studied at Taixue. When falsely accused of murder, he was tortured but released. Later, as postal supervisor, he treated his accuser with courtesy, earning admiration. As a meritorious officer, he refused to appoint an unsuitable candidate recommended by eunuch Hou Lan, taking the blame himself. He served as Chief of Taiqiu, governing with virtue and quietness. During the party arrests, he willingly imprisoned himself. He mourned for Zhang Rang's father, later saving many lives during renewed party persecutions. In his village, he settled disputes fairly. When a thief entered his home, he admonished him and gave him silk, ending burglaries in the county. He repeatedly refused high positions, closing his door to retire. He died at eighty-four in the fourth year of Zhongping. Over 30,000 mourners attended his funeral. His posthumous title was Mr. Wen Fan. His sons Chen Ji and Chen Chen were also virtuous. Chen Ji served under Dong Zhuo but opposed moving the capital, warning of destruction. He later became Dahonglu and died at seventy-one. Historians praised Chen Chen's virtue, noting that while imperial teachings declined, integrity survived among the people.
