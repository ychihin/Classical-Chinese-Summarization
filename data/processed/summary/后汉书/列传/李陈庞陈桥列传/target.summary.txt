# Summary of Historical Biographies

## Li Xun's Biography

Li Xun was born in Linjing, Anding, and studied "Han Shi" from a young age, teaching hundreds of students. He was appointed as a meritorious official by Prefect Li Hong of Yingchuan but failed to take up the post initially. After Li Hong's death, Li Xun served three years of mourning at his grave. He later served in Situ Huanyu's mansion and was promoted to imperial censor, traveling to Youzhou and Beidi to declare the emperor's favor. He documented mountains, rivers, and villages in over a hundred volumes. Emperor Suzong appointed him governor of Yanzhou, where he lived frugally with sheepskin mats and quilts. He was later promoted to governor of Zhangye with a prestigious reputation.

When General Dou Xian stationed troops in Wuwei, Li Xun refused to participate in corrupt gift-giving practices and was dismissed. He was later recruited as a visitor and made captain of the Western Regions. Despite receiving many valuable gifts from foreign envoys and merchants, he refused all of them. When the Northern Xiongnu cut off routes to Cheshi and Yiwu, Li Xun offered rewards, resulting in the enemy commander's beheading. The roads became clear, and his kindness and power spread. He later became prefect of Wuwei but was dismissed and walked home, living in seclusion and weaving mats. When the Xiqiang rebelled, he was captured but released due to his reputation. During a famine, he refused grain from officials, moved to Xin'an Pass, and ate acorns. He died at age ninety-six.

## Chen Chan's Biography

Chen Chan, courtesy name Jishan, was from Anhan, Ba County. As a county meritorious officer, he punished evil and was feared by the people. After being recommended as filial and incorruptible, he served in the state government. When the governor was accused of bribery, Chen Chan was arrested for investigation but remained composed under torture, maintaining his confession. General Deng Zhi summoned him. When barbarians rebelled in Hanzhong, Chen Chan became governor, and they surrendered upon hearing his reputation. He was promoted to Zuo Fengyi and became an admonishing doctor.

In the first year of Yongning, the Shan Kingdom presented performers who could spit fire and perform illusions. In the second year of Yuanhui, Emperor An watched these performances. Chen Chan protested, citing Confucius's execution of dwarfs at the Qi-Lu meeting and arguing that barbarian skills were inappropriate for court. Shangshu Chen Zhonggai defended the performances as legitimate tribute and requested Chen Chan's impeachment. The emperor demoted Chen Chan to Xuantuhou Chengzhaowei. When the Northern Huns invaded Liaodong, Chen Chan became governor. The Hu people retreated in fear, and Chen Chan used moral influence to persuade the Chanyu, who sent treasures. After Deng Zhao's death, Chen Chan was dismissed as Deng's former official. He later served under Yan Xian and was promoted to Sili Xiaowei under Emperor Shun, dying in office the following year.

## Pang Can's Biography

Pang Can, courtesy name Zhongda, was from Fengshi, Henan. He began as an obscure official until Henan Yin Pangfen promoted him to Xiaolian and Zuo Xiaoling. After breaking the law and being imprisoned, during the Yongchu period when the Zhongqiang people rebelled, Pang Can sent his son with a memorial advising against distant military campaigns and advocating for gathering troops to support the people. The imperial censor Fan Zhunshang recommended him, comparing him to Wei Shang. Empress Dowager Deng promoted him to visitor and sent him west to supervise military camps.

Four years later, when the Qiang rebelled again, Pang Can advised abandoning the Western Regions and moving border counties to Zhuling. Though Deng Zhao agreed, many opposed, so the plan stopped. As prefect of Hanyang, Pang Can understood the cryptic advice of recluse Ren Tang to be upright, attack powerful clans, and pity orphans. He governed by restraining the strong and helping the weak. Promoted to Qiang Protector captain, many Qiang surrendered. When ordered to lead troops against the Xianling Qiang, he was defeated and jailed for falsely claiming illness. Ma Rong petitioned for his release, citing historical precedents. Later pardoned, Pang Can became governor of Liaodong, then general of Liao Dynasty, and finally Da Honglu. Recommended by Yu Xu, he became Taiwei under Emperor Shun. Despite his reputation for loyalty, he was slandered and imprisoned. After being cleared, he was restored but later accused of killing his ex-wife's son. He was dismissed but later reinstated as Taiwei in the fourth year of Yangjia, dying at home in the first year of Yonghe.

## Chen Gui's Biography

Chen Gui, courtesy name Shuzhen, was from a border general family in Shangdang. He learned archery and horsemanship as a child, dominating Beizhou. During the Yongjian period, he became Xiaolian and later prefect of Wuyuan. In the fifth year of Yonghe, he became Zhonglang General envoy to the Xiongnu. When the Southern Xiongnu's left wing rebelled, Chen Gui blamed the Chanyu and was jailed but spared execution. As Jing Zhaoyin, he punished powerful clans and brought justice. When Qiang and Hu invaded, Emperor Huan appointed him general who conquered Liao. Chen Gui advised the emperor on border policies, recommending rewards for good officials and punishment for corrupt ones. The emperor selected new governors and reduced taxes. Chen Gui's administration so frightened Xianbei that they avoided the borders, saving millions annually. General Liang Ji, who had a long-standing quarrel with Chen Gui, accused him of destroying national prestige. Chen Gui was recalled and later became a minister. As Liang Ji's tyranny grew, Chen Gui accused him and requested his death. When the emperor ignored him, Chen Gui starved himself for seven days and died. People in the Western Regions, Bingzhou, and Liangzhou mourned him.

## Qiao Xuan's Biography

Qiao Xuan was from Suiyang, Liang State. His ancestor Qiao Ren wrote forty-nine chapters of the "Book of Rites." Qiao Xuan began as a county meritorious official. When Governor Zhou Jing visited Liang State, Qiao Xuan accused Chen Xiang Yangchang of crimes and investigated thoroughly despite Yangchang's friendship with General Liang Ji. Qiao Xuan became famous. After being promoted to Xiaolian and Luoyang Zuowei, he resigned when Liang Buyi became Henan Yin. He later became governor of Shanggu and Hanyang, punishing corrupt officials severely. When recluse Jiang Qi refused to serve, Qiao Xuan threatened to marry his mother, but relented after persuasion. During Emperor Huan's reign, when Xianbei, Southern Xiongnu, and Gaogouli rebelled, Qiao Xuan became general who conquered Liao. He rested his troops and defeated the rebels, bringing border peace for three years.

Under Emperor Ling, he became Henan magistrate, then Sikong and Situ. Despite quarreling with Chen Qiu, he recommended him as court captain. Qiao Xuan later became minister and accused official Gai Sheng of corruption, but the emperor protected Gai Sheng. Qiao Xuan resigned due to illness. When kidnappers took his ten-year-old son and demanded property, Qiao Xuan refused and ordered troops to attack, resulting in his son's death. He then requested an edict that hostage-takers be killed without ransom. This law ended hostage-taking among nobles. Qiao Xuan died at seventy-five. He was humble and thrifty, leaving little property. When Cao Cao was young and unknown, Qiao Xuan recognized his potential, saying Cao would stabilize the world. Cao Cao later visited Qiao Xuan's tomb and wrote a memorial poem honoring him.
