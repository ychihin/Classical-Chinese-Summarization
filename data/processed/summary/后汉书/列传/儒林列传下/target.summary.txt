The text summarizes the transmission and scholarly traditions of classical Chinese texts, particularly the *Shi* (Poetry) and other classics, during the Han dynasty. It begins by noting that the *Shi* of Lu, Qi, and Han schools each had established doctors, while the *Mao Shi* did not. The biographies of several scholars follow, detailing their studies, teachings, and official careers.

Gao Xu, from Pingyuan, studied the *Lu Shi* and served as a doctor, but feigned blindness to avoid serving Wang Mang. Under Emperor Guangwu, he held various posts, including Grand Secretary of Agriculture, and died in office. Bao Xian, from Kuaiji, studied *Lu Shi* and the *Analects* under a doctor. Captured by Red Eyebrow rebels, he was released for chanting scriptures. He later taught the crown prince, wrote chapters on the *Analects*, and held high offices, receiving generous rewards from Emperor Xianzong. Wei Ying, from Rencheng, studied *Lu Shi* and taught hundreds of students. He became a doctor, minister, and later Grand Herald, known for his moral purity and large following. Fu Gong, from Langfu, inherited his uncle Fu An's teachings on *Qi Shi*, served as a county magistrate, and became a doctor. He simplified his uncle's complex chapters and was honored as Sikong. Ren Mo, from Shu, studied *Qi Shi* and gained fame for personally transporting his friend's funeral. Jing Luan, from Guanghan, studied *Qi Shi* and other texts, writing extensively on various classics. Xue Han, from Huaiyang, studied *Han Shi* and was a leading authority on poetry and prophecies, but died in prison. Du Fu, from Wuyang, studied under Xue Han and taught *Han Shi* to many disciples. Zhao Xun, from Jiujiang, studied *Han Shi* and served as a cavalry captain and tutor to princes. Yang Ren, from Ba, studied *Han Shi* and served as a doctor, known for his advice on governance. Zhao Ye, from Kuaiji, studied *Han Shi* under Du Fu and refused official posts.
