Before the Xia and Shang dynasties, records of the concubine system were sparse. "The Rites of Zhou" describes the emperor's harem hierarchy: a queen, three wives, nine concubines, twenty-seven wives, and eighty-one female concubines, each with specific duties. The queen held equal status to the emperor, while wives assisted her in women's ethics. Concubines taught women's virtues, speech, appearance, and merits. Female officials managed rituals, dining, and sleeping arrangements, with a female historian recording merits and demerits. This system aimed to maintain order and prevent corruption, as seen in Queen Kang of Zhou's virtuous conduct. However, after the Zhou capital moved to Luoyang, etiquette declined. Dukes like Huan of Qi and Xian of Jin indulged in multiple wives, leading to succession conflicts and state ruin. During the Warring States period, moral decay worsened, causing widespread destruction.

Qin Shihuang unified China but imitated the six kingdoms' palaces, recruiting beauties. The Han dynasty initially lacked a formal harem system, leading to lax morals under Emperor Gaozu and favoritism under Emperor Xiaowen. From Emperor Wu onward, concubine numbers swelled to three thousand, with fourteen grades, fostering corruption and foreign relatives' power. Emperor Guangwu of Eastern Han simplified the system, reducing titles to queen and noble, with minimal salaries. He sent officials to select virtuous girls aged 13-20 for the harem. Emperor Ming upheld these standards, emphasizing moral character, but failed to prevent promiscuity. From ancient times, emperors relied on governors, not women, to rule. The Qin dynasty's Queen Mother Mi set a precedent for regency, which Han emperors followed, leading to power struggles. Eastern Han saw four emperors with regent empresses, like Empress Dowager Zhang Di and Hexi Deng, who controlled state affairs, causing disasters. Empress Dowager Huansi Dou was exiled, and her father executed, illustrating the cycle of ruin.

The text then details specific queens. Queen Guangwu Guo, from Zhending, married Guangwu during his campaign. She became queen and bore Prince Liu Qiang, but her favor waned, leading to her deposition as Queen Mother of Zhongshan. Her brother Guo Kuang was honored but later implicated in a rebellion, with his family exiled. Historians note that favor and disfavor follow natural laws, and Queen Guo's decline was due to lost affection, yet she retained honor. Queen Guanglie Yin, from Nanyang, was admired by Guangwu for her beauty. She married him and became noble, refusing the queen title due to Guo's son. After Guo's deposition, Yin became queen, praised for her humility and virtue. She died at sixty, buried with Guangwu. Emperor Ming mourned her deeply.

Empress Ma of Mingde, daughter of General Ma Yuan, lost her parents young and managed household affairs at ten. She entered the crown prince's palace at thirteen, serving Queen Yin with grace. She raised Emperor Zhang as her own, despite having no children, and was made queen. She wore simple clothes, avoided luxury, and advised Emperor Ming on legal matters, reducing punishments. As empress dowager, she refused to ennoble her brothers, citing historical precedents of foreign relatives' downfall. She maintained frugality and moral standards, dying at forty. Jia Guiren, mother of Emperor Zhang, was not honored as queen, as Emperor Zhang considered the Ma family his maternal relatives. Empress Zhang De Dou, from Fufeng, was chosen for her talent and beauty. She became queen but, lacking children, grew jealous, causing the deaths of Song and Liang nobles. After Emperor Zhang's death, she served as regent, but her brothers' rebellion led to her posthumous demotion, though she was buried with Emperor Zhang.

Queen Yin, great-niece of Queen Guanglie, was deposed for witchcraft and died in sorrow. Queen Hexi Deng, granddaughter of Tai Fu Deng Yu, was known for her intelligence and virtue. She entered the palace at sixteen, serving Queen Yin humbly. After Yin's deposition, she became queen, banning luxury and refusing to ennoble her family. As empress dowager, she ruled during crises, reducing expenses, pardoning exiles, and promoting education. She died at forty-one, buried with Emperor He. Historians criticize her for holding power too long, leading to resentment, but acknowledge her efforts to stabilize the state.
